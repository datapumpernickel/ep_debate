QUESTIONS TO COUNCIL
Question no 10 by Panagiotis Beglitis (H-0019/05)
 Subject: Violation of Greek territorial waters off the islets of Imia by Turkish vessels
 

On 11 January 2 Turkish port authority patrol boats illegally entered Greek territorial waters near the islets of Imia, remained there for many hours and obstructed Greek fishermen in the area. This fresh provocation by Turkey openly calls into question the exercise of national sovereignty and sovereign rights by Greece and Greek citizens and constitutes a flagrant violation of the principles of international law and international treaties which constitute an inalienable part of the European acquis. At the same time, Turkey is continuing to violate Greek national airspace, thereby creating tension in the Aegean.

Since the European Council decided very recently - in December - to commence accession negotiations with the candidate country Turkey in October 2005, what measures does it intend to take in respect of Turkey to dissuade it from taking similar actions in future and to prevail upon it to live up to its commitments as an applicant country? How does it intend to assess Turkey's continuing provocative behaviour towards Greece, an EU Member State, through the mechanism for monitoring and evaluating the performance of candidate countries? How does it intend to express its solidarity with Greece, as it is obliged to do?

 
  
 

(FR) The European Council in Helsinki in 1999 had already declared that applicant countries must share the values and objectives of the European Union as set out in the Treaties. In this respect the European Council had insisted on the principle of the peaceful settlement of differences in accordance with the United Nations Charter, and had urged the applicant countries to make every effort to settle any border disputes they might have, and any other issues of a similar kind.

It is in that spirit that the Council has constantly monitored developments in the relations between Turkey and its neighbours.

As the honourable Member is aware, on 16 and 17 December 2004, the European Council, emphasising the need for an unequivocal commitment to good neighbourly relations, noted with satisfaction the improvement in Turkey’s relations with its neighbours. The European Council also welcomed the fact that Turkey was prepared to continue to cooperate with the Member States concerned with a view to settling unresolved border disputes while observing the principle of the peaceful settlement of differences set out in the United Nations Charter. The European Council, in line with its previous conclusions, and in particular those which it adopted on this matter in Helsinki, has taken stock of the situation with regard to the continuing differences and welcomes the exploratory contacts which have taken place with this end in view. On this subject, it has reiterated its view that any unresolved differences which have repercussions on the accession process should if necessary be referred to the International Court of Justice with a view to their settlement. The European Council has emphasised that it should be kept up to date on the progress achieved, which it will examine as and when appropriate.

 

Question no 11 by Adamos Adamou (H-0022/05)
 Subject: Clearance of mines from the buffer zone and the opening of Ledra Street
 

On 18 November 2004, the Republic of Cyprus unilaterally began the process of clearing all mines from the buffer zone which separates Greek Cypriot territory from the occupied territories of Cyprus.

On 16 December 2004, the Greek Cypriots also submitted a proposal to open Ledra Street, withdraw the guard post at the end of the street and, at the same time, abolish the Turkish guard post which had been set up 50 metres from the Green Line. The government's proposal was forwarded to the Turkish side through the United Nations with the aim of removing all obstacles to traffic along Ledra Street by opening the border crossing-point.

What is the Council's assessment, from a political perspective, of these events?

 
  
 

(FR) The Council has repeatedly confirmed its commitment to solving the Cyprus question and its hope that all Cypriots will soon be able to come together again as citizens of a united island of Cyprus within the European Union. In this context, the Council has always supported any efforts made, in particular by the Secretary-General of the United Nations, towards the overall settlement of the problem. It has also recognised that a crucial contribution towards this goal must result from a gradual rapprochement between the two communities.

It is in this spirit that the Council welcomes, both politically and generally, any measure which could help to establish a climate of confidence and reconciliation. Moreover, without going into details concerning the specific measures or proposals to which the honourable Member refers, it should be noted that the Union is already giving financial support to mine-clearance operations.

 

Question no 12 by Brian Crowley (H-0023/05)
 Subject: Asylum policy
 

Would the Council outline the intentions of the Luxembourg Council Presidency with regard to the introduction of a common EU asylum policy, applicable in all 25 Member States, in accordance with the clear priorities set out in the 'Tampere Programme'?

 
  
 

(FR) Following the adoption of ‘The Hague Programme’, the organisation of future work in the area of Justice and Home Affairs will be structured around an action programme which the Commission has been instructed to draw up and which should be ready for approval by the Council in the course of the next six months.

In more detailed terms, the Council expects the Commission to submit the following proposals on asylum to it in the months to come:

a proposal for a directive on acquisition by refugees of the status of third-country nationals who are long-term residents,

a proposal for the establishment of a single procedure to evaluate applications for international protection,

an action plan on one or more regional pilot programmes of protection based on the principles of solidarity and an equitable distribution of responsibilities,

a proposal for a reinstallation programme for refugees (designed to leave Member States free to decide whether or not they wish to participate).

In order to help the Commission in this context, the Presidency decided to schedule a debate on the external dimension of European asylum policy. This debate took place at the informal meeting of Justice and Home Affairs Ministers in Luxembourg on 27-29 January 2005.

 

Question no 13 by Liam Aylward (H-0025/05)
 Subject: The Kyoto Agreement
 

Can the Council outline clearly the political initiatives that it will undertake during the Luxembourg Council Presidency to convince more countries, in particular the United States of America, to sign up to the provisions of the Kyoto Protocol?

 
  
 

(FR)The entry into force of the Kyoto Protocol on 16 February 2005 will provide an additional effective legal basis for international efforts to combat climate change and should demonstrate the effectiveness of global cooperation involving all countries. The fact that 136 States have so far ratified the Protocol is a clear signal that this binding legal instrument, based on specific quantified commitments to limit or reduce emissions, is perceived by the majority as the only multilateral instrument capable of tackling climate change.

As the Council pointed out in reply to an earlier question put by the honourable Member, the European Union is ready to do everything in its power to persuade the United States to rejoin the multilateral process on climate change, in particular by stepping up scientific and research cooperation and encouraging national policies and measures within the framework of a frank dialogue on future climate change policy.

The Luxembourg Presidency, for its part, raised this issue at the meeting with President Bush on 22 February 2005 and will bring it up again in its bilateral meetings with certain countries that have a key role in curbing climate change. Its efforts are part of an action programme which will be continued during the second half of the year by the future United Kingdom Presidency.

In this context, the Conference of Parties will start examining commitments for the period after 2012 at least seven years before the end of the first commitment period (2008-2012), in practice this will be in November 2005. It is important that all the Parties to the UN Framework Convention on climate change should be involved in these negotiations in order to reach a post-2012 climate regime for the whole planet.

Finally, the Luxembourg Presidency is also counting on the support of European Parliament in continuing to explain the merits of the Protocol and all its potential benefits to the parties that have not yet ratified it, in particular the United States.

 

Question no 14 by Eoin Ryan (H-0027/05)
 Subject: Millennium Development Goals - Education
 

Can the Council state exactly its political and financial commitments towards the promotion of primary education in Africa, in accordance with the Millennium Development goals?

 
  
 

(FR) The central importance of education in fighting poverty and aiding development was clearly defined under paragraph 19 of the Millennium Declaration, adopted by the United Nations General Assembly in September 2000.

The European Union's commitments in terms of promoting education in Africa and other developing regions are based on sectoral budgetary aid that also targets the areas of health, politics, water and sanitation.

The EU's strategy is rooted in the context of the Millennium Development Goals for education and the wider objective of Education For All (EFA), adopted by the international community in Dakar in April 2000.

In its Resolution of May 2002 entitled 'Education and Poverty', the Council stated that a substantial share of the ODA increase resulting from the Monterrey/Barcelona commitments should be used to support social development in developing countries, with special emphasis on improving health and education outcomes, and that this should be followed up accordingly. In the education sector, this should translate into additional support for the MDG/EFA goals to ensure universal primary completion, for boys and girls alike, by 2015.

According to a Commission evaluation, contributions to national programmes and projects in the field of education totalled EUR 258m in 2003, EUR 70m of which went to ACP States. These projects cover primary, vocational and higher education and are all integrated into the national programmes and development strategies of partner countries.

The Council supports the EFA Fast Track Initiative (FTI), in which the Commission plays a key role in coordination at EU Member State level and examines the possibilities of financial support. This initiative aims to help low-income countries with sound policies but insufficient resources to reach the Millennium Development Goal of giving all children a full primary education by 2015.

 

Question no 15 by Seán Ó Neachtain (H-0029/05)
 Subject: Environmentally-friendly fishing methods
 

Would the Council confirm that the principle of promoting more environmentally-friendly fishing methods has already been approved by the Council and indicate whether it intends to take forward the ideas on this matter, set out in COM(2004)0438/final, during the Luxembourg Council Presidency?

 
  
 

(FR) The issue raised by the honourable Member was addressed in Council conclusions adopted in June 2004, which were based on a communication from the Commission. These conclusions recognise that making fishing activities more environmentally friendly will contribute to a more sustainable fishing industry. In its conclusions, the Council emphasised the importance of research in areas such as more environment-friendly types of fishing gear and marine ecosystems. It confirmed its support for pilot projects on discards.

The Council also invited the Commission to take a series of measures in this field, and in particular:

to assess, by the end of 2005, the potential for the provision of financial and non-financial incentives to encourage fishermen to employ more environment-friendly fishing methods;

to propose, after consultation with the relevant Regional Advisory Councils, simplified technical conservation measures for the Atlantic and the North Sea.

As regards the Luxembourg Presidency, environment-friendly technical conservation measures are among the priorities of the joint work programme adopted by that Presidency and the future British Presidency for 2005. The proposal on the Mediterranean is a top priority in this regard, and the Council looks forward with interest to Parliament's opinion on this subject. The Presidency hopes, if possible, to adopt this proposal by the summer.

The Luxembourg Presidency also plans to launch discussions on the proposal on technical conservation measures in the Baltic Sea. It can therefore assure the honourable Member that it will devote the utmost attention to this important matter, which is crucial to arriving at a sustainable fisheries approach.

The results of the elections in Palestine open up real hopes that the longed-for peace process will become a reality. Could the Council say what steps it intends to take?

 
  
 

(FR) The Council agrees with the honourable Member’s view that the Palestinian presidential elections and the measures taken by Israel which made it possible to facilitate the electoral process have given rise to a great deal of hope for real success in the peace process – something which has been so often hoped for.

As for the constructive proposals and measures which are the subject of the honourable Member’s question, the Council will be working out the details of the short-term action plan which was adopted by the European Council last November, covering the aspects of security, reform, the economy and elections. The meeting in London, which will be held on 1 March 2005 at the invitation of the United Kingdom, will give the international community a chance to support the efforts of the Palestinian President. Moreover, the Council has confirmed that the European Union, in collaboration with the other members of the Quartet and Egypt, will support the implementation of the withdrawal from the Gaza Strip and from certain parts of the Northern West Bank, as being the first stage in the overall process in accordance with the conditions defined by the European Council of March 2004.

Question no 19 by Nikolaos Vakalis (H-0095/05)
 Subject: Strategic enlargement of the EU
 

In its conclusions of 13 December 2004, the Council stressed the strategic importance of Ukraine as a neighbour and partner of the EU and expressed its wish that cooperation with that country should be strengthened after the free and democratic election of a new president. On 17 December 2004, the European Council expressed its wish for the gradual intensification and strengthening of political and economic relations between the EU and Ukraine. The new President of Ukraine, Viktor Yushenko, has since stated that Ukraine aspires to full membership of the EU.

Does the Council have a strategic plan in regard to the geographical limits of enlargement, in addition to the Copenhagen criteria and Article 6(1) of the Treaty on European Union and, if so, what is that plan?

Does the enlargement process affect the pace of integration of the Union as a whole and, if so, how?

 
  
 

(FR) The Council is closely monitoring the development of the situation in Ukraine. The Council wholeheartedly welcomed the organisation of a second round of presidential elections in Ukraine, conducted on a free and proper basis. Question no 20 by Katerina Batzeli (H-0045/05)
 Subject: Disruption of the market in agricultural products
 

It has been established that, in the EU in general and in the individual Member States, there is low demand for agricultural products which are mainly associated with very low market prices. Cereals are one of the products which have suffered the worst fall in market prices and demand.

A typical example is Greece where market prices for most agricultural products, such as rice, cereals, peaches, tobacco and cotton are unprecedentedly and unjustifiably low, which is causing significant problems for the rural economy and farmers' incomes.

How does the Council, in cooperation with the Member States, propose to tackle this difficult commercial situation on the market in agricultural products which, apart from a possible increase in production, is significantly affected by the implementation of bilateral trade agreements, the pre-accession agreements and, above all, by the period pending implementation of the new CAP in the Member States which is creating uncertainty among producers and the individual sectors concerned?

 
  
 

(FR) The Council’s attention has indeed been drawn to the risk of disruptions on the markets for certain products in several regions of the Union. The Council has noted the explanations supplied on this issue by the Commission, which is responsible for the management of the markets.

It should be remembered that, in 2003 and 2004, the Council adopted a major reform of the common agricultural policy, which was implemented on 1 January 2005 in most Member States, and which will be implemented with effect from 1 January 2006 in the remaining Member States. All products which are alleged to have been affected by low demand and low prices have in recent years been the subject of the above-mentioned reform. The key element of that reform is the replacement of market and price support by income support for farmers, which will give the market more of a role in guiding production. One of the objectives of the single farm payment system is precisely to give farmers stability of income, particularly in cases where the markets are disrupted.

After such a general reform, while being aware, at this stage, of the need to allow time for the implementation and application of the new policy so as to have the perspective which is essential for a proper assessment of the new mechanisms, the Council will be sure, nevertheless, to examine closely any legislative proposal from the Commission which seeks to put in place any adjustments which may be necessary.

 

Question no 21 by Daniel Caspary (H-0047/05)
 Subject: Fraudulent use of Turkish lira for purchases from vending machines
 

According to an AFP report, which also appeared in the Turkish daily newspaper 'Hürriyet', there has been an increase in cases of fraud involving vending machines selling cigarettes and confectionery in Germany since the new Turkish lira was introduced on 1 January 2005. Apparently, many vending machines in the Federal Republic of Germany and in other Western European countries cannot differentiate between a coin with a face value of one Turkish lira - which is worth about 55 euro cent - and one with a face value of two euro. This has apparently led to losses running into seven figures. Is the Council aware of this abuse? If so, what action does it plan to take? How is it that 300 000 vending machines could be suitably adapted in good time in Austria but not in the other countries affected?

 
  
 

(FR) Cases of fraud involving vending machines using Turkish lira have not been brought to the attention of the Council. The Council has not, therefore, adopted a position.

The Council, in collaboration with the Commission, established the legal framework for the introduction of the euro coins, after long consultations with all the parties concerned, including the automatic vending machine sector. The Council even made an adjustment to the specifications for the 50 cent coin before it was brought into circulation, in order to respond to the only explicit request to that effect from the distribution sector.

The Council’s role is restricted to defining the technical specifications for the coins. Protection against this type of fraud is chiefly the responsibility of the national authorities and, as far as possible, of the economic operators themselves.

Finally, the Council would ask the honourable Member to refer to the Commission’s Communication on the introduction of euro notes and coins following one year’s experience, which refers explicitly to the vending-machine sector at national level, in that particular case in Germany.

 

Question no 22 by Johan Van Hecke (H-0049/05)
 Subject: Protectionist imposition by France of duty on alcoholic beverages containing more than 35 g sugar
 

Since 1 January, a new law has been in force in France (Law No 2004/806 of 9.8.2004), which imposes high levels of duty on alcoholic beverages containing more than 35 g sugar. However, the law provides for so many exceptions for French products such as wines, liqueurs and ciders that the duty applies only to non-French products. The effect is to double the price of non-French products in comparison with French ones, and this must accordingly unequivocally be regarded as a protectionist measure. The law contravenes both Directive 92/12/EEC(1) on the general arrangements for products subject to excise duty and Article 90 of the EC Treaty.

Belgian and Luxembourgish fruit beers are particularly affected, and this has severely damaged their producers. The new duty has doubled their price, so that their sale has ceased in France. A complaint has already been submitted to the Commission. Will the Council raise this matter with France as a matter of urgency and ask it to repeal the law immediately?

 
  
 

(FR) The honourable Member’s question is concerned with the application of Community law and, more precisely, with Article 90 of the EC Treaty concerning the taxation of products in circulation within the Community. Verification of the compatibility of the law in question with the Treaty and with Directive 92/12/EEC, which is concerned with the free circulation of products subject to excise duty, is primarily the responsibility of the Commission. It is therefore not the task of the Council to express an opinion on this question.

Question no 23 by Jean Lambert (H-0051/05)
 Subject: The destruction of Babylon, Iraq, by US and Polish troops
 

According to a report by the British Museum's John Curtis, Babylon has been severely contaminated, and parts of it have been irreparably damaged by the military presence of US and Polish troops using the ancient city as a base. What will the European Union do to help repair the damage caused to Babylon? Will the EU call for an investigation into the conduct of the US and Polish troops and into their reasons for setting up the 'security' camp in Babylon?

Others have been brought to trial and convicted for such atrocities committed in the past, for example Miodrag Jocik for shelling the Old Town of Dubrovnik. What will the European Union do to ensure that the guilty parties are also brought to justice in this instance? Will the EU also call for Babylon to be designated a world heritage site?

 
  
 

(FR) The Council has not specifically discussed the destruction that has occurred on the site of Babylon. However, it wishes to stress that it attaches great importance to the conservation of the rich legacy of the world’s cultural heritage.

(EN) Annex III 2b of Council Directive 91/676/EEC(2), concerning the protection of waters against pollution caused by nitrates from agricultural sources, provides for the possibility of derogating from the limit of 170 kg N/ha from livestock manure, in the case where the different amounts fixed do not prejudice the objectives specified in Article 1 of the Directive and are justified on the basis of objective criteria. Question no 102 by Othmar Karas (H-0101/05)
 Subject: Distortion of competition between Europe and Asia in the circuit board industry as a result of customs barriers
 

In 2002 the European circuit board industry accounted for about 16% of the world market. Partly because of the current market consolidation, but mainly because of significant locational disadvantages and customs barriers, this branch of industry is threatening to migrate to Asia. There is, for example, a significant imbalance in the duty charged on products. European purchasers do not pay any customs duties for finished products (circuit boards) from Asia. On the other hand, customs duties of 5-7% are charged on primary materials, which according to information from the industry can account for up to 50% of manufacturing costs, when imported to Europe from Asia. Since these products are essential for domestic producers, however, the customs charge puts them at a distinct disadvantage.

Do these arrangements amount to discrimination against Community manufacturers of circuit boards and distort competition with Asian competitors?

 
  
 

(EN) The honourable Member refers in his question to circuit boards which can be imported duty free from third countries. Printed circuits without any active electrical elements fall with CN code 8534 00. Such products have been duty free since 1 January 2000 due to the Information Technology Agreement (ITA). Populated printed circuit boards, get ITA duty free treatment when they are classified as parts of finished products such as personal computers.

The production of these goods mainly requires chemicals for edging (CN Chapter 29, duty rate 6,5%), plastics (CN Chapter 39, duty rate 6,5%) and copper foil (CN Chapter 74 duty rate 5,2%) as primary materials. However, in cases where the EU industry imports some of these primary materials, it has the possibility to apply for the customs procedure called ‘transformation under customs control’. By this procedure Community producers can be authorised, when it is economically justified, to use imported materials duty free in the production of goods which are not subject to import duties when imported from a third country. Given this situation it is very unlikely that import duties on raw materials are a prime cause for the transfer of such industries to third countries.

The Commission considers therefore that issues other than tariffs are much more likely to influence the decision where to locate such factories. These could be labour costs and, for the most sophisticated multilayered boards the availability of advanced technology. Equally important may be investment aids that can attract foreign direct investment into some parts of Asia, as well as environmental considerations.
