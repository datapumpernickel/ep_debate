Verbatim report of proceedings
Monday, 12 December 2011 - Strasbourg OJ edition

18. One-minute speeches on matters of political importance
Video of the speeches
PV
 

  President. – The next item is the one-minute speeches on matters of political importance (Rule 150 of the Rules of Procedure).

 
  
 

  Seán Kelly (PPE). – (GA) Mr President, thank you for giving me the opportunity to say a few words about the conduct of banks in Ireland.

As everybody knows, the banks were primarily responsible for many of the woes, particularly in my own country. They have been bailed out, but they have shown no such interest in bailing out many of their clients who are now in an unfortunate position because of the recession, which the banks were instrumental in causing.

Particularly I want to highlight the activities of those who have been put into receivership and how they are being treated by the receivers. At the weekend I was contacted by four different people about the behaviour of these receivers who go into people’s yards – particularly agricultural contractors and farmers who live in isolated areas – frighten the living daylights out of them, ignore all protocol, take machinery etc., turn out the lights, and do enormous damage and a great deal of threatening, both in language and indeed in physical activity. It is not good enough and I want to highlight it.

 
  
 

  Andrés Perelló Rodríguez (S&D). – (ES) Mr President, whilst the European Union announces that it is going to devote EUR 80 000 million to research, the Government of the Valencian Community in Spain has withdrawn funding, to the point of it being exhausted, from the Prince Felipe Research Centre for biomedical research, a centre that came into being through European Union Structural Funds.

I am reporting this here as it has become an indecent paradox, as whilst EUR 5 million cannot be found for this research centre, for its research programmes into cancer, Alzheimer’s, and Parkinson’s, there is EUR 15 million for Formula 1 and EUR 3.5 million for a golf tournament.

Maybe this Parliament should indicate to the Member States that the crisis cannot be used to justify everything, the actions of inhumane governments even less so, and certainly that austerity should not be the door to politicians committing indecencies.

These are the paradoxes we cannot tolerate, and the European Union should take these into account, so that these types of issues do not occur in some Member States and regions.

 
  
 

  Graham Watson (ALDE). - Mr President, the European Food Safety Authority is investigating health claims on food products, the so-called Article 13 claims. This is very important for citizens. Their NDA Panel which does this work has rejected, however, 95% of claims for plant-based foods – maybe in many cases with good reason – but among the claims rejected is the claim that prunes have a laxative effect. Now according to the NDA there is no cause and effect between eating prunes and normal bowel function.

I have asked the Commission if it is satisfied with the criteria and the methodology used for testing such claims because I know that prunes contain two substances, sorbitol and dihydrophenylisatin, which have laxative effects. But most of our constituents do not require a scientific test. So I would ask you, Mr Vice-President, if you would, on our behalf, challenge Commissioner Dalli to a prune-eating contest so that he can see for himself.

 
  
 

  President. – Yes, Mr Watson, but I imagine that you do not want either of us to start a tussle with Commissioner Dalli. In any event, we will ask him to undertake the test, but without competing with the parliamentarians.

 
  
 

  Valdemar Tomaševski (ECR). – (LT) Mr President, I would like to draw attention to restrictions on rights experienced by municipalities under the current conservative and liberal coalition governing Lithuania. Due to the recession and government inaction, some municipalities are trying to provide their residents with financial assistance by themselves. Unfortunately, last week a government representative demanded that Vilnius district municipality revoke its 2009 decision under which residents’ heating costs are subsidised. This is government interference in the competence of municipalities and a gross violation of the European Charter of Local Self-Government, and unfortunately not the first one. Municipalities in my country are so far unable to use finance and land of their own accord. The use of even the smallest plot of land for municipal purposes requires explicit government permission. I would like to use this platform as an MEP to ask the Committee of the Regions and other local bodies to defend municipalities’ rights, which have been disregarded by the current Lithuanian Government.

 
  
 

  Nikolaos Salavrakos (EFD). – (EL) Mr President, 2011 has ended, leaving behind a feeling of uncertainty about the future, the future of our citizens, the future of Europe and the future of the euro. Last year, the institutions of the European Union were replaced by an informal German-powered management team supported by the French. Political leaders and the media have presented the problems of Mediterranean Europe as the result of the laziness and corruption of its inhabitants. We call the Greeks lazy, but it turns out that they are the hardest-working Europeans. As a Greek, I refute these accusations, accusations that, unfortunately, by dint of constant vilification, have ripped the people apart.

We need to be careful, we need to remedy this and, more importantly, we need to send out a message: we respect Berlin, we respect Paris, but the European Union is alive and kicking.

 
  
 

  Corneliu Vadim Tudor (NI). – (RO) Mr President, economists are people who teach us what to do with money we do not have. Everybody is talking about the crisis as though it were inevitable. There are some who even say: ‘the alternative to the crisis is war: you choose!’ Well, this is not really the case. Perhaps this reasoning was valid up to 1945, when imbalances were dealt with brutally by force, in the sense that a big scandal was covered up by an even bigger one. In the meantime, however, the European Union came into being, that organisational structure whose duty it is to avoid war and prevent or drastically limit the crisis.

However, what the founding fathers of the European Union failed to take into consideration was human greed. While the entire wealth of the Kennedy clan 60 years ago was USD 400 million, today we are confronted by fabulous riches. On our planet there are around 1 500 dollar billionaires.

I will close with a meditation from Saint Augustine: the rich will go to heaven when the poor bring them there.

 
  
 

  Georgios Koumoutsakos (PPE). – (EL) Mr President, if your house catches fire, your first thought is to try and put it out with any fire-fighting appliances at your disposal. You do not think about how to improve the workings of the fire department.

That is common sense, unlike the decisions adopted at the summit on 9 December. The summit reversed the order of priorities, which is why, three days later, we are again discussing a partial, rather than an integrated solution to the crisis. The proof lies in the fact that the markets are already sending out discouraging messages and time is running out. However necessary they may be, economic governance and fiscal austerity alone cannot put out the fire. We need immediate and decisive involvement on the part of the European Central Bank.

Mr Sarkozy spoke about the birth of a different Europe based on converging economies, fiscal rules and taxation. The problem with that is we may ultimately give birth to another Europe: a Europe of recession, unemployment, homelessness, soup kitchens, crime and despair, a Europe that, unfortunately, many Greek citizens have already started to experience. That is not the Europe of our dreams; it is the Europe of our worst nightmares.

 
  
 

  Alexander Mirsky (S&D). – (LV) Mr President, ladies and gentlemen, a week ago I returned from Russia, where I could see in person how the parliamentary elections were held. Errors and irregularities could not drastically have affected the results. However, demonstrations are being held in Russia today and a resolution of the European Parliament is being prepared. Prior to taking a decision, it has to be known that the demonstrations in Russia have been organised by two former prime ministers, and their goal is not democracy, but only power. However, they have already been in power and have become very wealthy people. We should not interfere in the internal affairs of Russia. It is stupid and we have to find a different way. Thank you for your attention.

 
  
 

  Sergej Kozlík (ALDE). - (SK) Mr President, last year, without the agreement of neighbouring states, Hungary unilaterally adopted a non-standard form of nationality, which is granted on the basis of ethnicity, with no real link of the citizen to the state in question. Since Hungary has rejected and continued to postpone a communication on this matter, the Slovak Republic has passed an appropriate amendment to nationality. This includes the principle of withdrawal of Slovak nationality where there is a voluntary acquisition of nationality of another state. This principle is not contrary to European standards. Quite the reverse – it is included in the European Convention on Nationality as well as in the legislation of many EU Member States. I would therefore like to recommend to Hungarian colleagues in the European Parliament that, rather than hysterical nationalistic speeches, they put pressure on the government in Budapest to begin correct negotiations over this issue with neighbouring states.

 
  
 

  Charles Tannock (ECR). - Mr President, I wish to express my concern about two important elections which took place this week.

In the Democratic Republic of Congo, there have been serious concerns about the activities of the CENI, the electoral commission, with accusations of widespread fraud in favour of the incumbent, President Joseph Kabila, and a lot of serious violence has broken out. International observers on the ground are demanding more transparency and the publication of the results at local level, and the High Representative must insist on this if the EU is to unequivocally recognise the results.

Similarly in Russia – parliamentary elections – there have been widespread protests at the allegations of massive fraud, again carried out in favour of Putin’s United Russia party. The scale is so large that there are grounds to believe, a bit like in Ukraine in 2005, that the outcome of the election would have been completely different. So there is also reason to demand now a re-run of these parliamentary elections in Russia, otherwise the presidential election next year will be a total farce.

 
  
 

  Andreas Mölzer (NI). – (DE) Mr President, just a few weeks ago the European Commission placed the Baltic-Adriatic axis on the list of the major projects to be supported in the coming years. This north-south axis, which is nearly 2 000 km in length, links up nearly 50 million people in ten EU Member States. It covers 85 conurbations and commercial centres, 138 sea ports and internal waterways as well as 28 border crossings with third countries, and with a freight volume of around 24 million tonnes per year is one of the main Alpine crossing points. These facts make it very clear what a positive effect the Baltic-Adriatic axis will have for the countries concerned. It will bring the eastern Member States of the EU closer to the centre of Europe and provide them with better links, which will result not only in economic growth in the adjoining countries but also in a strengthening of the internal market. In addition to a further opportunity for shifting freight from road to rail, it will also dramatically cut travel times for passenger transport. This opens up new prospects for tourism, and thus offers us all a chance to get to know each other better.

 
  
 

  Danuta Jazłowiecka (PPE). – (PL) Mr President, on 11 October 2011 the former Prime Minister of Ukraine, Yulia Tymoshenko, was found guilty and sentenced to seven years in prison. In addition, the court banned her from all public functions for a period of three years and obliged her to pay compensation to Naftohaz. Since that day, the former Prime Minister has been remanded in prison waiting for further court proceedings. On 1 December 2011, the Court of Appeals rejected Tymoshenko’s appeal, and also denied her release for health reasons.

Subsequent court rulings show that Ukraine’s President and his advisors have decided not to respond to the European Union’s proposals regarding, inter alia, changes to criminal law and the dismissal of Tymoshenko’s case or the granting of her appeal. The guilty verdict and her remand in custody shows that domestic policy issues are more important to the Party of Regions. Keeping the former Prime Minister in prison means Ukraine is effectively isolated in the West. However, the refusal by Parliament, under the control of Yanukovych, to amend the criminal code, along with the subsequent court proceedings and the rejection of the appeal, show that the authorities have no intention of releasing the former Prime Minister, who is being held in prison under scandalous conditions, with limited access to her.

Neither the European Commission nor the European Parliament should forget about this matter, and from Brussels and Strasbourg …

(The President cut off the speaker)

 
  
 

  Kriton Arsenis (S&D). - Mr President, just yesterday morning in Durban a decision was made in the climate negotiations for a second commitment period for the Kyoto Protocol. The developed countries – the Annex I countries – have now to commit to the new commitments for emission reductions by May 2012. We have to bridge a big emission gap.

The developing countries have delivered. We asked them for a road map for a legally binding global agreement. They delivered this road map. By 2015 there will be an agreement, but now it is up to us, the developed countries. Now it is up to the EU to deliver as well. We have to commit to a 30% reduction of our greenhouse emissions by 2012. This will allow us to create jobs and to stay globally competitive.

 
  
 

  Kristiina Ojuland (ALDE). - Mr President, the Kremlin deceived the people of Russia in a number of ways at the State Duma elections a week ago. Firstly, the people were not granted free elections as provided for in the constitution, because Putin’s government refused to register a number of political parties that wished to participate in the elections. Secondly, the counting of the votes on the election day was fraudulent. There are reports which say that United Russia received much less than 49.54% of the votes.

International organisations of which Russia is a member cannot approve that. The people of Russia are entitled to demand their constitutional rights, namely that the government adheres to the principles of democracy, which ought to be a cornerstone for every member of the Council of Europe and the OSCE, including Russia. Therefore the results of the State Duma elections in Russia should be deemed null and void.

 
  
 

  Csanád Szegedi (NI). – (HU) Mr President, ladies and gentlemen, allow me to take this opportunity to also respond to the provocative speech delivered by Mr Kozlík. Already after the enactment of the Slovakian language law and the maintenance of the Beneš decrees I proposed here in Strasbourg, in this House, that the European Union apply some form of sanctions against Slovakia. Now, Slovakia, not having been subjected to any sanctions, is continuing its rampage. And let us say it: the intention of Slovakia and the Slovakian Government is to erase the Hungarian population of almost half a million living in the historic region of Upper Hungary from the map. In order to achieve this they even go as far as withdrawing the Slovakian citizenship of one Hungarian in Upper Hungary after another. The inhumane nature of the actions of the Slovakian Government is also demonstrated by last week’s incident where a 100-year-old Hungarian teacher was deprived of her Slovakian citizenship.

And Slovakia will keep up this chauvinistic, anti-Hungarian, racist attitude as long as it is not subjected either to financial penalties or to any other sanction by the European Union, be it even the temporary suspension of its EU membership. Thank you for your attention.

 
  
 

  Anna Záborská (PPE). - (SK) Mr President, the debt crisis is a crisis of confidence. Investors on financial markets are afraid to lend more money to governments that increase expenditure in an irresponsible way. Failure to implement reforms has increased public sector debt. As EU Member States’ economies are interlinked, it is necessary to find the correct balance between the responsibility of states for running their affairs and solidarity, when some Member States are threatened with economic and social breakdown. The massive intervention of the European Central Bank must not, however, become a standard measure to be used by states every time they get into trouble through bad economic policies. That would have catastrophic consequences not only for Europe, but for the whole world. We must make use of the existing legal framework based on the Lisbon Treaty. Opting for a new convention and a series of negotiations requires far more time than Europe now has available.

 
  
 

  Vasilica Viorica Dăncilă (S&D). – (RO) Mr President, the ruling party in Romania has taken the decision, by assuming responsibility for the Government in Bucharest, and without even consulting the other parties or holding a debate on the subject, to merge the local elections with parliamentary elections.

The bill in question is illegal and an abuse of power because the postponement of local elections infringes several provisions in force which stipulate that the date of local elections may be delayed in the event of war or disaster, but Romania is in neither of these situations.

The postponement of local elections is profoundly undemocratic, given that electoral standards produced at European level rule out postponing elections, as stipulated by the 2010 Venice Convention on electoral standards, rights and freedoms.

I believe that this unprecedented situation, which is out of step with democracy, is an attack on European values and must be responded to by Romanian and EU civil society and other international bodies which believe in freedom and democracy.

 
  
 

  Mairead McGuinness (PPE). - Mr President, tomorrow we will debate the outcome of the summit of Thursday and Friday, but in advance of that could I suggest that we need to change the public narrative around solving our current difficulties, because I believe that this will not happen in one step or one meeting, which is what is portrayed in the general public debate, but rather by incremental change, which is what has been happening over time. I think we need to say that very openly to our citizens, rather than believing that each meeting will be the final solution to a crisis which has taken ten years to make and therefore will take a lengthy time to actually be resolved.

So let me say to the media commentators and the market analysts who in a headline or in 500 words declare that this must be solved this week: it will not happen. It will happen slowly and we will have to do it working together. Europe united can and will come to a solution.

 
  
 

  Ivailo Kalfin (S&D). - Mr President, the decisions of the European Council last week did not reassure the financial markets. This is not surprising, given the slow speed with which the right decisions are presented and the lack of political courage and vision of the European leaders. The very concept of how to tackle the issue of financial stability is going in the wrong direction. Confidence and stability suggest quick action. There is no time to create new institutions and unclear procedures and even less to change the European Union Treaty.

The right approach is not to restrict the circle of countries who endeavour to stabilise the euro, but the opposite, to expand that circle. Countries like the United States, Japan, China and Brazil, which really have an interest in the stability of the euro currency, should be involved in this effort. The IMF is able to provide a platform for such an intervention. It should be allowed to conclude agreements to finance the countries with balance of payments problems. In parallel, the restricted use of Eurobonds, the European Stability Mechanism and the activity of the European Central Bank can provide the necessary robust financial resource which is needed.

 
  
 

  Gianni Vattimo (ALDE). – (IT) Mr President, I would once again like to draw this House’s attention to the increasingly serious situation of the Turin-Lyon railway and the works being carried out in the Susa Valley.

As I have often pointed out, European funds have been granted to the Italian Government, as Europe’s contribution, based on entirely false information. It has been said that the local communities have been duly consulted, but that is not true. It has also been said that the works have begun, but that is not true either. The only thing that has been done is to militarise the Susa Valley, which has recently been declared an area of strategic interest, almost as if it were a military occupation.

Plenty of valley dwellers have presented a good many petitions calling on the European Parliament to stop trusting the information from the Commission and the Council, because they have been aiding and abetting the Italian Government in this whole manoeuvre.

The local population quite rightly does not want this railway line for a variety of reasons, which have been set out in extremely important expert studies. Parliament should now send a fact-finding committee to the Susa Valley to examine the situation as it really is.

 
  
 

  Claudiu Ciprian Tănăsescu (S&D). – (RO) Mr President, I was unpleasantly surprised by the scandal involving fake organic products and especially by the size of the chain that sells these products throughout Europe. It is a wake-up call not only for Romania and Italy, the countries from which the raw materials were obtained, but for the EU as a whole.

Given that around 700 000 tonnes of fake products labelled as organic were sold across Europe, I fear the impact this will have on consumers, and I am particularly concerned about how this incident will affect the entire food sector.

We consistently advocate the need for correct and complete information for consumers, but this time the measures we are going to take will need to reassure consumers that this type of incident will not be repeated. Tighter control and stricter monitoring of transactions involving organic product are required within the EU. Moreover, from now on we must be much more careful to ensure that such incidents do not occur in other sectors as well.

 
  
 

  Rovana Plumb (S&D). – (RO) Mr President, I wish to highlight that the Romanian Government has decided today to legislate on merging local and parliamentary elections in 2012 by taking on the responsibility without holding a debate in Parliament and, in so doing, has extended the mandate of local elected officials without justification.

Organising local and parliamentary elections on the same date goes against the spirit of European recommendations. What is more, the undemocratic nature of this move is also clear from European conventions, and the Venice Commission clearly states that the legislation cannot be amended one year before the elections take place.

Given that we have so many problems in Europe, we must find solutions for ending this crisis. In such a volatile global climate, we need your support and the close monitoring of events in Romania, in the knowledge and awareness that we are interested in having the legitimacy of the governments in the European Union.

 
  
 

  Luis Yáñez-Barnuevo García (S&D). – (ES) Mr President, the Portuguese Government has just introduced the compulsory toll on the motorway that links the Portuguese region of the Algarve with the Spanish region of Andalusia, which has led to displeasure and rejection across the board on both the Portuguese and Spanish sides, given that it is a road that is used a great deal by Europeans from both nations and those from third countries, who use this area, privileged in terms of nature and climate, as their regular residence or for holidays.

The measure has caused astonishment as the construction of the aforementioned motorway was funded by European funds, and also for the very negative effect on transport, trade, and tourism, which has caused the President of the Andalusian Regional Government, José Antonio Griñán, to express his displeasure and to protest.

From here within the European Commission, we urge the new Spanish Government and the Portuguese Government, both PPE, to revoke the measure, or at least adopt compensation actions for the small and medium-sized companies and workers that are affected.
