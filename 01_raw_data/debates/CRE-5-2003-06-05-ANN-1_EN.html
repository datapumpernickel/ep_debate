Question no 14 by Rodi Kratsa-Tsagaropoulou (H-0334/03)
 Subject: People with disabilities and Community policies
 

2003 is the Year of People with Disabilities. In its proposal to the Member States concerning the general guidelines on employment, the Commission included full employment for people with disabilities as part of social integration. The Council will adopt its final position on the guidelines in the near future.

Does the Council intend to promote and implement these political commitments in its final position? Is it the Council's intention to ask the Commission to put forward new initiatives to promote the integration of people with disabilities into the labour market and to secure the other fundamental rights? Is the Presidency satisfied with the progress made with priorities in this area?

 
  
 

(EL)

1. As the honourable Member already knows, pending the European Parliament's opinion on the Commission proposal, which it is hoped will be returned by plenary on 5 June 2003, the Council approved a general approach for a decision on guidelines for the employment policies of the Member States at its meeting on 3 June. At the same time, the Council affirmed that access to the labour market is a major priority in the case of people with disabilities who, it is estimated, number approximately 37 million in the EU, many of whom are willing and able to work.

2. In the preamble to the decision, the Council recognises that the real integration of people with disabilities into the labour market will result in greater social integration and higher employment rates and will improve the viability of social protection systems. Therefore, the policy applied must respond to negative discrimination, provide an individualised approach to individual needs and create jobs by giving employers recruitment incentives.

3. Point 7 of the guidelines therefore calls on the Member States to promote the integration of and combat the discrimination of people at a disadvantage in the labour market, by increasing their recruitment potential, increasing the number of jobs and preventing all forms of discrimination. Within these frameworks, it is calculated that applicable national policies will need to have as their objective a significant reduction by 2010 in each Member State of the unemployment differential of people with disabilities, in accordance with all the national objectives and definitions.

4. At the same time, the Council has also adopted a resolution concerning the promotion of employment and social integration of people with disabilities. This resolution calls on the Member States and the Commission, within the framework of their respective powers, to continue efforts to remove barriers to the integration and the participation of people with disabilities in the labour market.

5. The resolution calls in particular on the Member States to ensure the transposition and implementation of Directive 2000/78/EC establishing a general framework for equal treatment in employment and occupation before the deadline is agreed. It also calls on the Member States and the Commission to reflect on the need for further measures to promote employment and social integration and to consider the possibility of taking measures, at national and European level, consistent with the objectives of the European Employment Strategy.

6. The resolution also encourages the social partners to promote the integration of people with disabilities, in particular in the mainstream labour market, through their actions and their collective agreements at all the relevant levels of social dialogue.

7. The Presidency believes that these initiatives are absolutely in keeping with the concerns expressed by the honourable Member. They are also in line with the political declaration by the Ministers responsible for integration policies for people with disabilities made in Malaga on 8 May 2003, which affirms that one of the main aims of the next decade is to improve the quality of life of people with disabilities and their families.

Finally, the Presidency would like to reiterate, as part of the undertaking it has made in this sector, that this political undertaking was also confirmed by the Ministers at the informal Council meeting in Nafplio on 23 and 24 January 2003, which emphasised the need for more extensive inclusion of disability issues in employment and social protection policies.

 

Question no 15 by James (Jim) Fitzsimons (H-0336/03)
 Subject: Consumer and health initiatives
 

Will the Council state what positive developments it considers have taken place during the Greek Presidency to strengthen the role of consumer policy and create greater awareness of the importance of preventive measures with regard to illnesses, and which consumer and health dossiers will it be recommending to the next Presidency for further action?

 
  
 

(EN)

1. As regards consumer policy issues, the Greek Presidency has continued actively to work. On 19 May 2003, the Council held an exchange of views on the Regulation on sales promotion and a policy debate took place on the Directive on consumer credit. Guidance for future work on these files was established, in order to progress further during the second semester of 2003. Furthermore, the Greek Presidency continued promoting the dialogue with consumers' organisations.

2. At the same Council on 19 May 2003, two major steps on foodstuffs legislation were marked, which will have significant impact on the protection of consumers' health.

Firstly, the Directive on the conditions of use for the food additive 'E 425 konjac' which has caused the death of some human beings in third countries was adopted at first reading.

Secondly, a political agreement was reached on the modification of Directive 94/35/EC on sweeteners, regarding notably the authorisation of new products and the monitoring of authorised sweeteners, particularly the reduction of the intake of cyclamates.

Final adoption on this issue as well as on the labelling of allergenic ingredients, which is also an important issue for consumers' health protection, should be reached, with due regard to the European Parliament competencies, before the end of 2003.

3. During the second semester of 2003 the Commission is expected to present a certain number of proposals in sectors such as safety of services, E-confidence, enforcement of consumer rights, unfair commercial practices, fortified food, dietetic food. The Council is looking forward receiving them in order to engage the process of examination.

4. As regards developments, which have occurred during the Greek Presidency leading to greater awareness of the importance of preventive measures with regard to illnesses, the following can be mentioned:

response to the SARS epidemic,

progress made with the WHO's Framework Convention on Tobacco Control

agreement on a Council common position on the proposed human tissues and cells Directive.

The Council has already reported in this Assembly the results of the extraordinary meeting of the Council on 6 May 2003, in connection with SARS. The Council, at its meeting on 2 June 2003, held a further exchange of views on the SARS epidemic in which the importance of close and rapid co-ordination between Member States was underlined and delegations reiterated their support for the Commission's forthcoming proposal.

On 21 May 2003, the 56th World Health Assembly adopted the text of the World Health Organisation's Framework Convention on Tobacco Control. The 'EPSCO' Council on 2 June 2003 agreed on the adoption of a Council Decision on the signing of the Convention.

The Council also reached political agreement on a common position concerning a Directive of the European Parliament and of the Council on setting standards of quality and safety for the donation, procurement, testing, processing, storage and distribution of human tissues and cells.

Furthermore, the Council also draws the attention of the Honourable Parliamentarian to the Council Conclusions on combating stigma and discrimination in relation to mental illness, as a result of the EU Conference on 'Mental Illness and Stigma in Europe: facing up to the challenges of Social Inclusion and Equity' held in Athens on 27-29 March 2003.

Finally, the Council at its meeting on 2 June 2003 reached agreement on two of the three proposals making up the 'review of Community pharmaceutical legislation': the proposal

for a Regulation on medicinal products and the proposal for a Directive on medicinal products for human use. It took note of a progress report on the third proposal; the proposal for a Directive on medicinal products for veterinary use.

5. The Italian Presidency intends to focus work in particular on:

collaborating closely with the Parliament on the second reading of the human tissues and cells Directive;

examining the Commission's forthcoming proposal to establish a European Centre for Disease Prevention and Control with a view to reaching an early agreement thereon;

securing the adoption of a Council Recommendation on cancer screening, for which the Commission's proposal has recently been received;

securing the adoption of Council Conclusions on Healthy Life Styles, Education, Information and Communication following the Conference to be held on this theme in Milan on 3-4 September 2003.

achieving a political agreement on the proposed Directive relating to medicinal products for veterinary use in order to finalise the package on 'the review of the Community pharmaceutical legislation' with a view to transmitting the Common Position on all 3 texts to the European Parliament.

if possible achieving a political agreement on the proposed Directive related to traditional herbal medicinal products.

 

Question no 16 by Liam Hyland (H-0338/03)
 Subject: Young farmers
 

Given the difficult international economic conditions and taking into account the importance of the agriculture sector for economies throughout the EU, what new initiatives does the Council consider can be taken to safeguard the agricultural sector, including providing a real future in farming for Europe’s young farmers?

 
  
 

(EL) The current regulation for financing farming development measures makes provision for special measures for young farmers in the form of a EUR 25 000 subsidy for the establishment of young farmers under 40 years of age who meet certain additional preconditions, together with a 5% increase in the limit of the total value of aid, expressed as a percentage of the eligible investment figure.

In the proposal to amend Regulation (ΕC) No 1257/99, the Commission did not propose new measures or the amendment of current measures. However, within the framework of technical discussions on this proposal, there was talk of the problem of young farmers and the issue of introducing additional measures was raised, especially in the form of an increase in the amount of the start-up subsidy or an increase in the maximum financing percentage (currently 45% and 55% for disadvantaged areas), together with the possibility of derogating from the application of the reference period in order to determine the amount to be paid to newly established farmers within the framework of the support regimes. Similarly, the issue was touched on of special treatment for newly established farmers, so that they are less affected by modulation measures and, consequently, by the gradual reduction in aid.

In the absence of any relevant proposals from the Commission, however, the Council is focusing its examination on the proposals for special measures submitted to it by the Commission. It is not possible at this stage for anyone to anticipate the results of the work and, hence, the impact which the reform will have on young farmers.

 

Question no 17 by Niall Andrews (H-0340/03)
 Subject: Guaranteeing independence of EU development aid from trade and security goals
 

What is the Council’s reaction to growing concerns that in the future the least developed countries which benefit enormously from the EU’s development policy and aid budget would seriously lose out by being obliged to meet EU trade and security demands in return for humanitarian aid and will it give assurances that no such link between the EU’s development policies and foreign and trade issues will be permitted?

 
  
 

(EN) At its November 2002 meeting, the Council (GAERC) adopted its conclusions on Trade and Development, following the Commission communication on the same subject. These conclusions are in line with the joint Council-Commission Statement on the EC Development policy of November 2000, which highlights Trade and Development as a priority area of Community development activities contributing towards the overarching goal of reducing poverty.

In its conclusions, the Council pays particular attention to the Least Developed Countries (LDCs) inter alia by

reaffirming its commitment to assist LDCs in taking full advantage of the 'Everything But Arms' (EBA) initiative;

supporting the Commission's proposal to pay particular attention to the needs of LDCs in the allocation of resources for trade-related assistance;

inviting the Commission to consider further practical measures to facilitate exports from developing countries and especially from LDCs, particularly where such exports are confronted with administrative and bureaucratic procedures and problems that may create unintended obstacles;

inviting the Commission to work for an accelerated and streamlined WTO accession process for the LDCs.

In conformity with the Council conclusions of May 2002 which stressed the need to ensure greater coherence between EU development assistance policies and other EU policies of an external dimension (such as trade and security policies), the Council has invited the Commission to make systematic and thorough analysis of any direct and indirect effects of policy proposals on developing countries and their impact on sustainable development, and it has welcomed the Commission's intention to present a general review of the EU's trade-related assistance in 2005.

 

Question no 18 by Erik Meijer (H-0342/03)
 Subject: Easier application for long-term visas
 

As an instance of the problems raised by visas for the EU, I would draw your attention to the fact that in Turkey a visa costs 5 % of an average annual income. Having to apply for one several times a year has a discouraging effect.

Does the Council agree that for non-EU citizens who intend to visit the EU regularly for work, research or conference reasons, it ought to be possible to set up a special arrangement whereby a document valid for a number of years permits them to make short visits of this kind to European countries?

Does the Council agree that such an arrangement would save such people a great deal of practical trouble and expense?

 
  
 

(EL) The honourable Member's attention is drawn to the fact that, at the initiative of the Hellenic Republic, the competent Council services are currently examining an amendment to Annex 12 of the Common Consular Instructions with a view to harmonising fees levied for examining visa applications. One of the results of this measure will be that the fee levied for multiple entry visas valid for one to five years will be greatly reduced.

The Member is also reminded that, on 27-28 February 2003, the Council adopted conclusions on flexibility in issuing visas to persons participating regularly as national representatives in actions to promote Euro-Mediterranean cooperation, which state that, in order to facilitate the issue of visas, in accordance with the Schengen acquis, to persons participating regularly as national representatives in actions to promote the Euro-Mediterranean cooperation, the acting Presidency must also ensure that it informs, on the one hand, the other Member States about the meetings scheduled on each occasion and, on the other hand, the local authorities of the method of application of the relevant rules in order to facilitate the procedure.

 

Question no 19 by Brian Crowley (H-0344/03)
 Subject: Protecting employee pension schemes
 

Taking into account the necessity for future pension protection, has the Council discussed or investigated the possibility of new forms of protection for employee pension schemes in the event of company failure?

 
  
 

(EN) As the honourable Member is certainly aware, pension policy is considered to be a national competence. However, a comprehensive approach on pensions is being developed in the framework of the open method of co-ordination (OMC). This method is a mutually learning process including a periodical monitoring of the progress made by each Member State on the basis of commonly agreed indicators. Applied to pension reforms, it seeks to co-ordinate the action of policy makers whereby mutual learning is seen as an instrument to identify best practices. This process involves the three pillars of pensions (public state schemes, occupational pensions and personal or private pensions).

Following the Joint Report by the Commission and the Council on adequate and sustainable pensions, the Spring European Council invited the Council and the Commission to maintain the momentum for co-operation within the open method of co-ordination framework in the area of pensions by delivering special studies focusing on common challenges for pensions systems.

In response to this mandate, the Economic Policy Committee (EPC) and the Social Protection Committee (SPC) have both outlined a programme of work to the Council.

As the honourable Member rightly points out, the growth of occupational pensions exposes individuals and institutions to a number of risks and challenges, including company insolvency risks. These risks may quite severely affect the individual's retirement income.

In this context, it is envisaged that the topics for further study by the Social Protection Committee would involve regulatory policies to ensure sustainability and effectiveness of the second (and third) pillar pensions schemes. In addition, the Economic Policy Committee should examine the feasibility of further studies on such issues as guarantees of private pension schemes.

 

Question no 20 by John Walls Cushnahan (H-0346/03)
 Subject: Illegal immigration
 

As the Thessaloniki Council will discuss measures for fighting illegal immigration relating to the management of external borders, what specific plans will the Council consider to tackle this problem on Europe's maritime borders? Bearing in mind that European waters are also used for other forms of trafficking and have also experienced major environmental disasters, would the Council give consideration to commissioning a study on the feasibility of establishing an EU coastguard?

 
  
 

(EN) The Council would like to point out to the honourable Member that, following the adoption of the Comprehensive plan to combat illegal immigration and the trafficking of human beings by the Council in February 2003, the Commission was requested to carry out a feasibility study on improving sea border control (point 63). This study is currently being carried out by the Commission, which is expected to present a final report of this feasibility study at the Justice and Home Affairs Council meeting on 5 and 6 June 2003. The Council is therefore not yet in a position to comment on future measures to be taken with regard to the control of sea borders nor on the feasibility of establishing a European Coast Guard.

The Council would like to recall that, in the framework of the Plan for the Management of the external borders of the Member States of the European Union, various joint operations were carried out to control and reduce illegal immigration by sea. A final report on the implementation of the above-mentioned Plan, including i.a. an evaluation of the results of these operations, will be presented at the meeting of the Justice and Home Affairs Council on 5 and 6 June 2003 with a view to its submission to the Thessaloniki European Council.

As usual, the Council will report to Parliament on these developments in due time.

 

Question no 21 by Astrid Thors (H-0350/03)
 Subject: Institutional care in Latvia
 

Institutionalised childcare is for the time being the most widespread form of social care for children in Latvia. Since 1989 the number of children in all kinds of social care institutions has grown fourfold. This is largely due to the increasing number of families in economic difficulty. Unfortunately, the foster-family service is rarely used, one reason being that the local authorities do not have enough social workers who could work professionally with the child's family to facilitate the child's earliest return to his or her family.

Has the Council taken any measures in cooperation with Latvia to promote the development of long-term solutions in the form of community-based alternatives and the development of professional social work in local municipalities? What has been done to support families and to enable them to care for their children, as the family should have the primary responsibility for the child according to the CRC?

 
  
 

(EL) The Council attaches the utmost importance to compliance on the part of candidate countries with the political accession criteria laid down by the European Council in Copenhagen and, specifically, democracy, the rule of law, human rights and the protection of minorities. Therefore, protection of the child constitutes a requirement for all the candidate countries and the Council has already had an opportunity to reply to relevant questions in relation to some of them.

In the present case of Latvia, however, the Council must point out that the Commission's regular report for 2002 on Latvia's progress towards accession only refers in passing to this specific issue, in order to record a positive development: the ratification by Latvia of the European Convention on Recognition and Enforcement of Decisions concerning Custody of Children. However, this issue is not included in the priorities laid down in the revised accession partnership for Latvia approved by the Council in 2002 or in other related unification documents, such as the Union's positions in view of the more recent meetings of the bodies of the relevant European Agreement, namely the Association Council and the Association Committee.

Without, therefore, excluding the possibility that social problems exist in Latvia, the Council calls on the honourable Member to address her question preferably to the Commission.

Question no 33 by Richard Howitt (H-0326/03)
 Subject: Commission participation in the EU CSR Forum
 

Welcoming the commitment of the European Commission to promote a genuine multi-stakeholder approach in its CSR Forum, will the Commission now authorise Commission officials to play a more pro-active role and in particular directly contribute to discussions? What is the Commission’s preliminary assessment of progress made by the Forum so far?

 
  
 

(EN) The Commission strongly believes that the Union success in promoting Corporate Social Responsibility (CSR) is ultimately based on widespread ownership of CSR principles by business, social partners and civil society. In this context, the Union Multistakeholder Forum on CSR has been set up to allow stakeholders to develop common answers to the challenges raised by CSR.

The Commission is part of the process as it chairs and facilitates the meetings, promoting constructive dialogue and consensus building in the identification of drivers, challenges, obstacles and recommendations for action. Also, it should be stressed that with the Forum the Commission has not given up its right to take the initiative on CSR: at the end of 2004, and on the basis of the analysis of the report detailing the conclusions of the work of the Forum, it will decide how CSR can best be promoted in the future.

With the Forum, the Commission has taken a 'gamble' on the willingness and ability of the parties (employers, trade unions, civil society organisations, business networks) to work together in a constructive way and to develop a common approach to CSR. This challenge has been accepted by all invited actors.

The Commission is satisfied with the progress made so far: each of the four Round Tables of the Forum has met at least once, the quality of case-studies presented has been good, and participating stakeholders have shown strong commitment as well as an open mind as discussions have moved forward in a constructive and cooperative climate.

In the coming months, the Forum, meeting at plenary level, will review the progress of the first meetings and prepare the next wave of Round Table meetings, which should enter into more focused and analytical discussions in order to allow for concrete outcomes to the exercise. These should include a clear roadmap recommending actions to be undertaken not only by the Union, but also by the Forum members themselves - whether alone or in partnership with others.

 

Question no 34 by Konstantinos Alyssandrakis (H-0327/03)
 Subject: Hundreds of workers made redundant as businesses go to the wall
 

Recent bankruptcies, the selling-off of companies in Greece and the transfer of other, profitable businesses to countries with particularly low labour costs and more favourable conditions for exploiting workers are giving rise to a wave of redundancies, justifiable anger on the part of workers and feelings of insecurity intensified by the news that other companies will be going down the same path. Workers at numerous companies, including 'Sisser-Palko', 'Yvonni', 'Yioula', 'Krikas', 'Ippokampos', 'Petalouda', 'Triumph', 'Afoi Antonopouloi', 'Afoi Avgerinopouloi', 'AGNO', 'Alako' etc., are demonstrating, with the support of all the country's workers, against the employers' unaccountability. The situation is also exacerbated by the fact that the policy of relocating to neighbouring countries paying wretched wages is encouraged by companies being offered the same financial subsidies that are paid within those countries.

Does the Commission believe that such measures can resolve the problem of unemployment and the shrinkage of the productive base in Greece and other Member States and, if not, what measures will it take to change this situation?

 
  
 

(EN) The Commission can assure the honourable Member that it is extremely concerned that all corporate restructuring be undertaken having full regard to its social consequences. This is why, in January 2002, it launched a first stage consultation of the social partners on the social aspects of corporate restructuring. The intention is to establish, at European level, a set of principles to be followed in restructuring situations.

Two of the issues highlighted in the consultation document are relevant to the circumstances described by the honourable Member: the need to fully involve employees in the restructuring process through proper information and consultation procedures and the need to have regard to the local consequences of any restructuring proposals.

Directive 2002/14/EC of the Parliament and of the Council of 11 March 2002 establishing a general framework for informing and consulting employees in the European Community(1) provides for information and consultation of employees in such cases.

There are other existing Community provisions which apply. Under the terms of Council Directive 98/59/EC dealing with collective redundancies, an employer contemplating collective redundancies is obliged to begin consultations with the workers' representatives in good time. Such consultations should cover ways of avoiding or reducing the number of workers affected and mitigating the consequences. Any alleged breach of the national legislation implementing the collective redundancies Directive would, however, have to be pursued at national level.

The same could be said of the European Works Council Directive, adopted in September 1994, which provides for worker involvement in management decisions within multinational corporations. This Directive constitutes a valuable instrument to ensure that workers' interests are fully taken into account in circumstances like the ones mentioned by the honourable Member, when decisions have a transnational scope.

The Commission hopes that the European social partners decide to fully address the issues raised in the Corporate Restructuring consultation document, by jointly agreeing a set of guiding principles which would govern socially responsible restructuring of business throughout Europe.

Industrial change has economic implications and an impact on employment and social cohesion. However, it also provides opportunities when anticipated, prepared for and managed.

Dialogue, transparency, proper worker involvement, anticipation, risk prevention and the development of employability are the key elements of a European answer to this phenomenon.

 
 

(1) OJ L 80 of 23.05.2002

 

Question no 35 by Rodi Kratsa-Tsagaropoulou (H-0335/03)
 Subject: People with disabilities and Community policies
 

2003 is the Year of People with Disabilities. In its proposal to the Member States concerning the general guidelines on employment, the Commission included full employment for people with disabilities as part of the process of social integration. The Council will adopt its final position on the guidelines in the near future.

In the light of the above, does the Commission intend to take specific initiatives on this matter? Does it have details of the take-up rate of funding for the social integration programmes (employment, education, vocational training) forming part of the National Action Plans for each Member State and for Greece, in particular, which has a high percentage of people with disabilities? Will it promote other policies in general to integrate people with disabilities into the employment market?

 
  
 

(EN) The European Employment Strategy has the leading role in supporting the implementation of the employment and labour market objectives of the Lisbon Strategy at national level. Within the Employment Strategy, special attention is paid to the situation of people with disabilities, ethnic minorities and other disadvantaged groups. One aim is to develop appropriate forms of preventive and active policies to promote their integration into the labour market. The proposed new set of Employment Guidelines also includes a specific target aimed at significantly reducing the unemployment gaps for people at a disadvantage in each Member State.

Within the framework of the European Employment Strategy, the Union also calls on Member States to secure adequate financial provision to support the implementation of the guidelines. However, no systematic information has been incorporated in Member States' National Action Plans on Employment.

The concrete implementation of employment policies remains a matter for the Member States. However, the Union promotes the labour market integration of people with disabilities mainly through the European Social Fund. During the present programming period 2000-2006, EUR 9.3 billion has been set aside for promoting equal opportunities for all in the labour market for the Union as a whole or EUR 650 million for Greece alone. This policy field targets all disadvantaged groups, which of course includes people with disabilities. The money is used to support a range of actions including work experience wage subsidies, work experience schemes, temporary sheltered employment and other intermediate labour market steps. In Greece, support is also provided for self employment, including the development of co-operatives for people with mental and severe physical disabilities. The mid-term evaluation of the Structural Funds, due by the end of 2003, will provide a more complete picture of both the financial allocation to people with disabilities and the effectiveness of the measures.

As people with disabilities would greatly benefit from a level playing field which would allow them to participate in society on an equal footing with non-disabled persons, the Commission is actively promoting a mainstreaming approach. Other policies and programmes therefore also deal with aspects related to the special needs of people with disabilities. The Social Inclusion process, in particular, recognises that disability is one of the factors that may cause social exclusion and poverty. The Common Objectives underpinning the Social Inclusion Process call on Member States to promote the social integration of women and men at risk of persistent poverty due to their disability. This requires policy responses across a broad range of policies including housing, health, education and training, and transport.

 

Question no 36 by Marie Anne Isler Béguin (H-0290/03)
 Subject: Dealing with and prohibiting animal meal within the Community
 

Having been blacklisted, animal meal was banned and set to be destroyed. Burning it in kilns at cement works ensures neither the short-term elimination of the mountains of animal meal, nor the complete destruction of the prions that could be released into the atmosphere and redeposited in the environment.

Has the Commission drawn up an exhaustive list of centres where these stocks of animal meal are stored, looking at the dangerousness and volatility of these products in terms of dissemination during transport, monitoring silos and the safeness of waste from the incinerators.

On the basis of what level of consultation with nearby residents, what scientific considerations on the risk from the presence of prions in the environment and what probability of contaminating nearby crops does the Commission choose and monitor these storage sites?

What has been the (industrial and financial) result of MIEFA’s (Interministerial campaign to eradicate animal meal) actions in France?

 
  
 

(EN)

1. The Commission has not established such a plan. Member States are responsible for approving and maintaining the lists of establishments and facilities for the collection, storage and disposal of meat and bone meal and rendered fats. In the case of France, these establishments must respect legislation in relation to 'établissements classés pour la protection de l’environnement', and to this effect an assessment vis-à-vis public health is carried out before any new establishment is approved.

Question no 53 by Brian Crowley (H-0345/03)
 Subject: Ring-fencing company pensions for employees
 

With the on-going difficulties of securing company pensions for employees and having regard to the high number of liquidated company pension plans, has the Commission looked at the possibility of establishing European legislation aimed at ring-fencing company pensions for the pensioners in the event of bankruptcy or the liquidation of companies?

 
  
 

(EN) The Commission considers that the protection of occupational pension funds is of the utmost importance. This was of the main objective of the proposal for Directive on the activities of institutions for occupational retirement provisions that was adopted by the Commission in October 2000. This Directive has now been formally adopted by the Council on 13 May, further the second reading amendments voted by the Parliament on 12 March.

This Directive is based on a fundamental principle of legal separation between the institution and the sponsoring undertaking. In the absence of a guarantee or insolvency insurance, this is a key element to ensure the protection of the persons receiving retirement benefits. This would ensure that in the case of a bankruptcy of an undertaking paying contributions to a pension funds, the acquired pension rights of the members and beneficiaries are preserved. This directive, which will enter into force in tow years, also contains a number of other stringent prudential standards that aim at protecting members rights .

The Commission has therefore no intention to propose any other legislation in this field in the foreseeable future.

 

Question no 54 by John Walls Cushnahan (H-0347/03)
 Subject: SARS and the Special Olympics
 

Would the Commission make representations to the Irish authorities to ensure that they allow athletes from Asia who are symptom-free and have had no contact with the SARS disease, to participate in the Special Olympics due to be held in Ireland? Would the Commission express its concern to the Irish Government that the current ban is discriminatory because different standards are applied to business people travelling from Asia to Ireland?

 
  
 

(EN) The Commission has taken a proactive and leading role in responding to the outbreak of Severe Acute Respiratory Syndrome (SARS).

As recently stated by the Council on SARS (6 May 2003) this has been undertaken through the Community Network for the Epidemiological Surveillance and Control of Communicable Diseases in the Community working under the Decision 2119/98/EC, and it has provided a very effective basis for the rapid exchange of information within the EU and for a co-ordinated response, in which the EFTA and Candidate Countries have fully participated in collaboration with WHO.

The application of health protection measures, including those undertaken to prevent the spreading of SARS during mass gathering, falls to the authorities of the Member States.

Nevertheless, concerning the prevention of SARS during events such as the Special Olympics, according to the information now available, only persons with symptoms are contagious and can transmit the disease. A close contact is required for transmission. A ‘close contact’ means having cared for, lived with, or had direct contact with respiratory secretions or body fluid of a suspect or probable case of SARS.

As recently stated by WHO (15 May 2003: Guidance for Mass Gathering: hosting persons arriving from an area with recent local transmission on SARS), a person who is symptom free and has not been in close contact with a suspected SARS patient may freely be welcomed without any other measures. However, he or she should remain vigilant, and if symptoms develop within 10 days of arrival, contact the Medical Service of the hosting Organisation in the country.

 

Question no 55 by Karin Riis-Jørgensen (H-0348/03)
 Subject: Developments concerning tendering procedures
 

In a number of recent judgments, the Court of Justice has established a precedent which may have major practical implications for the purchase of goods, works and services by public authorities and public enterprises.

The Court of Justice has extended the ban on discrimination on grounds of nationality so far as to require a form of advance publication of the wish to conclude a contract even if the value of the purchase is below the generally applicable thresholds and the procedures under the EU's directives on tendering therefore do not apply. The Commission confirmed this practice in its written answer of 13 May 2003 (H-0273/03). However, the Court of Justice does not specify a new minimum value of public contracts for which tenders must be invited or which specific criteria must be met in inviting such tenders.

Clear rules are needed in order not to muddy the waters over public procurement (minimum values of contracts for which tenders must be invited, procedures relating to invitations to tender, etc.) Are these in the pipeline?

 
  
 

(EN) As the honourable Member is aware, the Commission has already given an answer to her question H-273/03 during Question Time of Parliament’s May 2003 Plenary session(1). In answer to the renewed question on whether the Commission intends to prepare detailed provisions applicable to contracts below the current thresholds, the Commission can only reiterate that to extend the detailed provisions of the Directives to all contracts irrespective of their value would be disproportionate and might consequently raise some criticisms of over-regulation and ensue excessive 'red tape'. In this context, the Commission has taken due notice of the fact that a number of Member States have already adopted national rules on how to ensure transparency and equal treatment for smaller contracts, for example by adapting the provisions of the Community Directives to procurements below the thresholds. According to the information available to the Commission, yet more Member States are currently envisaging the introduction of national measures for procurements below the current Community thresholds.

 
 

(1) Written answer 13.5.2003
