QUESTIONS TO THE COMMISSION
Question no 11 by Paul Rübig (H-0455/02)
 Subject: Liability rules for enterprises in the EU internal market
 

European enterprises face an ever increasing volume of liability rules in vastly different areas (environmental liability, product liability, etc.). The host of national and Community legal provisions under which an entrepreneur can be held liable for his business activities are a burden for European SMEs, in particular, because they are so numerous as to cause confusion and so complex in terms of what they regulate and in many instances, because of the financial consequences they involve, they are even a threat to a firm's existence.

Is the Commission thinking of including a schedule of the business liability rules which exist in the 15 Member States and in Community law in the Green Paper on Entrepreneurship decided on at the Barcelona summit?

 
  
 

At Lisbon, the Community set itself the clear goal of becoming the most competitive and dynamic knowledge-based economy in the world. To achieve this goal, the Community also has to have the will and the means.

Economic analysis and surveys seem to indicate a lack of dynamism in Europe and it is generally felt that there is scope for enhancing entrepreneurial activity, particularly if the Community is to exploit fully the opportunities arising in the knowledge economy. The Community must allow entrepreneurship to play its full part in delivering the shared objectives.

Hence the significance of the Green Paper on Entrepreneurship, which must look at the whole range of issues that has to be addressed.

The Barcelona Council (March 2002) has noted that the Commission will submit the Green Paper before the 2003 Spring European Council.

The approach that is envisaged is that a broad debate should be launched on the significance of entrepreneurship, on how to promote entrepreneurial drive and how to achieve the right balance between entrepreneurship policy and other policy considerations.

The Lisbon conclusions were based on the idea that economic performance, social cohesion and sustainable development mutually reinforce one-another.

In this context, it will certainly be necessary to face the issue of regulatory and other restrictions on entrepreneurial activity. It will be necessary to stimulate debate about the complementarity between the objectives of providing an encouraging environment for entrepreneurs, to enable them to create growth and viable jobs and the objectives of social cohesion and sustainable development (NB. as stated in Lisbon, economic performance and social cohesion do not oppose each other, but rather go hand in hand!).

In referring to these issues, it is important to bear in mind the parallel debates on Corporate Social Responsibility and Sustainable Development, and the fact that many businesses recognise the need to integrate ethical, social and environmental considerations into their strategy and daily operations. They do so as a way of better managing risks, improving their performance and ensuring their long-term sustainability.

The particular issues concerning environmental liability, product liability, etc raised by the Honourable Member, are certainly instances of a more general regulatory issue that arises from both Community and Member State legislation. There may indeed be reference in the Green Paper to such specific concerns, by way of focusing attention. However, it does not look likely at this stage that the Green Paper will want to highlight the question of regulations concerning liability, to the exclusion of other regulatory questions.

 

Question no 14 by Bernd Posselt (H-0409/02)
 Subject: Paris-Strasbourg-Munich-Vienna high-speed railway
 

Has the implementation of the trans-European networks come to a complete standstill as far as rail transport is concerned, and in particular what is the situation regarding the Paris-Strasbourg-Munich-Vienna high-speed railway, which is a priority in the context of enlargement? Is it certain that the route will pass through Strasbourg, the seat of Europe's Parliament?

 
  
 

The development of the trans-European rail network features in the political guidelines proposed by the White Paper on European transport policy. Progress updates are given on a regular basis in the report on the implementation of Community guidelines for the development of the trans-European network which were adopted in 1996.

As part of a review of the above-mentioned guidelines, the Commission proposed, last year, to include in the list of specific projects the East European combined transport/high speed train link (Stuttgart-Munich-Salzburg/Linz-Vienna), which will be carried out in phases until its completion in 2012.

Parliament adopted this proposal at first reading on 30 May.

In addition, since 1996, one of the specific projects of the trans-European network is the high-speed train east, between Paris and Strasbourg, which is due to enter into service around 2007. The Commission hopes that these two projects will be implemented in tandem.

 

Question no 15 by Richard Howitt (H-0427/02)
 Subject: Integrated European railway area
 

It is very much welcomed that the European Commission is committed to promoting in EU transport policy the concerns and access requirements for disabled persons in the field of transport.

In light of the recently published European Commission proposals on an integrated European railway area, the Commission proposes to encourage rail operators to develop a voluntary charter on service quality covering issues including those of concern to persons with reduced mobility.

What role will the Commission play in the development of a voluntary charter, how will disability organisations be involved and how will it complement the mandatory access provisions present in the EU directives on rail interoperability?

 
  
 

The Commission has clearly stated its desire to see the rail industry take steps in order to improve the situation of all passengers, including persons with reduced mobility, particularly in international transport.

It is for the rail industry alone to decide whether to involve passenger organisations and organisations specialising in the situation of persons with reduced mobility in this work. As far as the Commission is aware, this work is still in its initial stages.

As announced in the White Paper on European transport policy, the Commission is working on a regulation on the rights and obligations of passengers in international transport.

In addition, work on rail interoperability has recently started with regard to technical interoperability specifications for high speed links and is due to begin on conventional rail. Aspects of importance to persons of reduced mobility will be examined and discussed as part of this work.

In any case, the Commission is ensuring that all the work carried out is consistent and intends to involve all the organisations affected by these concerns, through individual consultations and more extensive public hearings.

 

Question no 16 by Robert J.E. Evans (H-0442/02)
 Subject: Aircraft passengers and deep vein thrombosis
 

Can the Commission inform me what action it is taking to ensure that airlines are protecting their passengers from the dangers of deep vein thrombosis?

In its reply to a Parliamentary question E-1330/01 Commissioner de Palacio stated that the Commission would continue to monitor all initiatives. Is the Commission satisfied that airlines are currently doing enough to alert passengers of the danger and to limit its effect?

What pressure is the Commission bringing to bear on airlines, both schedule and charter, to provide adequate legroom for passengers?

Are there any more statistics or findings available on this serious health risk than the Commission recognised in its answer to Parliamentary question E-0349/01(1)?

Will the Commission be undertaking studies on its own?

 
  
 

The Commission sent out a letter last year, encouraging airlines to inform their passengers on the possible risks of deep vein thrombosis (DVT) during long flights, and on precautionary measures to take, for example leg exercises during travel and it plans to contact the airline associations shortly, to find out which initiatives have been taken so far.

As for adequate legroom for passengers, it should be noted that the distance between seats in aircraft cabins is regulated for safety reasons, in particular to allow evacuations during emergencies. Medical opinion seems to indicate that the real problem concerns immobility. Both passengers from economy and first class can be vulnerable, even though more legroom is available in first class. The leg exercises, proposed by doctors, can be carried out in the legroom presently available in aircraft.

In order to find a clearer answer to the question of how far DVT is linked to air transport, the Commission will co-finance a study on DVT. This study will be carried out by experts, under the auspices of the World Health Organisation. The study aims to measure the strength of the link between air transport and DVT, and the importance of other, patient-related factors, like age, weight and medical condition. It will also examine the effects of cabin conditions, like low air pressure and levels of oxygen.

As the strength of this link is not known, it is not possible for the Member States or the World Health Organisation to collect statistics at this stage. The above mentioned epidemiological study will certainly facilitate the design and collection of statistics, once the results are known.

The Parliament will be kept duly informed of the results of this study.

Question no 17 by Mihail Papayannakis (H-0357/02)
 Subject: Exclusive rights for distribution of products in northern Cyprus
 

The Belgian company, Interbrew, recently signed an agreement with the Turkish company, Efes, for the exclusive right to distribute Interbrew products in Turkey and the northern part of Cyprus.

Since northern Cyprus is not an independent state or other recognised entity according to international law and UN resolutions, will the Commission say to what extent an agreement for the exclusive right to distribute products in such a geographical construct is a practice consistent with the rules governing the Community's trade policy, the EU's foreign policy in general and with international law?

 
  
 

The Commission is not informed of such an agreement between the Belgian company Interbrew and the Turkish company Efes.

While the northern part of Cyprus is obviously neither an independent state nor a recognised entity according to international law and United Nations resolutions, as the Honourable Member points out, as regards the possible inconsistency of such a practice with Union foreign policy and international law, private undertakings may choose the territorial scope of their activity as long as there is no national or international legislation that prohibits exports to a given country. In this case, there is no specific prohibition for Union undertakings to export their products to Turkey or Cyprus.

 

Question no 18 by Bart Staes (H-0359/02)
 Subject: Use of languages in receiving visitors to the European institutions in Brussels
 

In his answer to question E-0173/02, Commissioner Kinnock says that the Commission’s main buildings have receptionists, who speak a number of languages including, at the minimum, French or English. He does not answer the question as to whether the Commission intends to make knowledge at least of Dutch and French compulsory for staff whose job it is to welcome visitors, whether or not such staff are located at reception desks. Thus he disregards Article 4 of the Belgian Constitution, which lays down that Belgium comprises four language areas, including the bilingual Brussels Capital region.

Will the Commission comply with the Belgian Constitution and require all officials whose job it is to speak to visitors of whatever kind at the Commission’s premises in Brussels, no matter which, to have a command of Dutch and French, at the minimum?

 
  
 

The functional immunity enjoyed by international organisations as regards their host States was enshrined, as far as the Community language policy is concerned, in Article 290 of the Treaty establishing the European Community which states that ‘the rules governing the languages of the institutions of the Community shall, without prejudice to the provisions contained in the Rules of Procedure of the Court of Justice, be determined by the Council, acting unanimously.’ The internal provisions of Belgian law regarding the rules governing the languages of the Brussels Capital region do not therefore apply to the European institutions which have their headquarters in Brussels.

 

Question no 19 by Marialiese Flemming (H-0360/02)
 Subject: High-risk Chernobyl-type reactors
 

During a press conference, the President of Lithuania cast doubt on whether the second reactor unit at the Ignalina nuclear power plant would be shut down by 2009.

What steps is the Commission prepared to take to ensure that there are no delays in decommissioning the Ignalina power plant by 2009?

And what steps is it prepared to take generally to ensure that the shutting-down of nuclear power plants which according to AGENDA 2000 are high-risk and are non-upgradable, such as Ignalina in Lithuania, Kozloduj in Bulgaria and Bohunice in Slovakia, is not delayed?

 
  
 

Over the last months, the public debate in Lithuania on the early closure of the Ignalina Nuclear Power Plant (of an RBMK type similar to the reactors at the Chernobyl NPP, but with improved safety features) has been particularly intense. The Commission is encouraged that this debate will lead to Lithuania taking its decision in the near future. The Ignalina NPP is the only nuclear power plant with power reactors of the RBMK type in candidate countries. The Union expects Unit 2 to close by 2009, at the latest. On 14 May 2002, the Lithuanian Parliament passed a resolution, giving the Government a mandate to negotiate the terms for the closure of Unit 2 with the Member States of the Union. A clear commitment of Lithuania to close down Unit 2 by 2009 at the latest will be needed for the closure of the chapter Energy in the accession negotiations.

Within the framework of its regular dialogue with Lithuania, the Commission has constantly highlighted the expectation of the Union with regard to the closure date. Since 1999, the Commission has enhanced this dialogue through a joint Community-Lithuanian Working Group on Energy. Members of the Commission have persistently pursued the said closure date during their bilateral meetings with Lithuanian interlocutors.

Also since 1999, the Commission has committed Community funding from the PHARE financial instrument to support Lithuania, Bulgaria and Slovakia in their decommissioning efforts regarding the respective power reactors subject to early closure. With regard to Lithuania, in view of the burden of decommissioning that is not commensurate with the size and economic strength of the country, and acknowledging that decommissioning will last for decades the Commission furthermore proposed, earlier in 2002, to the Member States to continue its assistance as an act of Union solidarity with Lithuania also after accession.

In its information note of October 2001 to the Gent European Council meeting on a mid-term review of the implementation of the enlargement strategy, the Commission stated that the closure commitments regarding Ignalina, Bohunice V1 and certain units of Kozloduy must be respected and therefore duly included in the Accession Treaties.

With regard to Bulgaria, according to an Understanding reached in 1999, the Commission expects Bulgaria’s decision on the closure dates of Units 3 and 4 at the Kozloduy NPP by the end of this year, and that the firm date for final closure of units 3 and 4 will be no later than 2006. The Commission is already closely monitoring the preparation for final shut down of Units 1 and 2 by the end of this year, following the schedule that Bulgaria agreed with the Commission.

The Commission has no reason to assume that Lithuania, Slovakia or Bulgaria will not close the reactor units in time, once all closure dates will have been decided. The Commission is also engaged, together with the European Bank for Reconstruction and Development (EBRD) as the manager of the three International Decommissioning Support Funds through which the bulk of Community-funded support to decommissioning measures in the three respective candidate countries is delivered, in pursuing that the necessary decommissioning projects are designed and implemented without undue delay. The Commission both chairs the Assemblies of Contributors to these three Funds as well as represents the Community as the largest single Contributor.

Thus, the Commission is continually engaged in a variety of actions to ensure the timely closure of certain reactor types that the Honourable Member has summarised as “high-risk”.

Question no 21 by Glyn Ford (H-0366/02)
 Subject: North East Asian Development Bank
 

Concerning the answer to my question No 74 (H-0189/02)(1) with regard to the proposition of a North East Asian Development Bank, the amount of money from the Development Bank spent in North East Asia has been inadequate, with less than $170 million spent in 2001. Question no 22 by Sarah Ludford (H-0367/02)
 Subject: Planespotters
 

What assistance can the European Commission give in the pursuit of justice and clearing their names to the 14 planespotter tourists (2 Dutch and 12 British) unjustly convicted in Greece on espionage charges and sentenced for periods of up to three years in prison, against which they are appealing?

 
  
 

The European Convention on Human Rights (ECHR) lays down the standards required for a fair trial. These provisions are set out in Articles 5 (Right to liberty and security) and 6 (Right to a fair trial) and are developed in the case law of the European Court of Human Rights.

Any complaint that a conviction was “unjust” would have to be made to that court. However, before any action can be brought before the European Court of Human Rights, domestic remedies must first be exhausted.

Consequently, the reply to this question must be that the planespotter tourists must first of all appeal against their convictions in the Greek Court of Criminal Appeals. It is not clear from the question which aspect of their trial is considered “unjust” – this will be a matter for their lawyers.

If the appeal is unsuccessful, an application can be made to the European Court of Human Rights.

The facts of this case and in particular the Greek provisions on the crime of espionage fall outside the scope of Community law. Consequently, the Commission is not able to offer any assistance to the planespotters since there is no legal basis for an intervention on the part of the Commission. This remains entirely a matter falling within the ambit of the legal system of the Member State concerned. Additionally, the governments concerned may consider it appropriate to intervene at the diplomatic level.

 

Question no 23 by Liam Hyland (H-0370/02)
 Subject: Budget for Common Agricultural Policy
 

Is the Commission fully satisfied that it will be able to assure the budgetary commitments required for assuring the continuation of the European model of agriculture beyond Enlargement and post Agenda 2000; and will the Commission give a commitment to resisting any attempt to renationalise the CAP after 2006, attempts which would undermine the fundamental principles of the CAP and threaten its very survival?

 
  
 

The Commission has defined the preservation of the European agricultural model as one of the core elements of its Agenda 2000 reform proposal. This requires both a clearer recognition and a more determined defence, both internally and externally, of the multifunctional role of agriculture in European society: it needs to be stressed that agriculture not only plays an economic role but also makes a social and an environmental contribution.

To the above end, the Commission obtained from the budgetary authority a financial framework enabling the above objective to be met. It is clear from the first two years of implementation of the Agenda 2000 reform that attainment of the objective is feasible within that financial framework.

The Commission's recent proposal concerning the agricultural dimension of enlargement was based on the principle that the acquis communautaire should be applied. It is compatible with the existing financial perspective (which will, in any case, need to be adjusted in the wake of enlargement).

As regards the mid-term review shortly to be submitted to the Council and Parliament, the Commission will be guided by the same objectives. There is no question of the CAP being undermined.

From a longer-term perspective, the Commission will of course strive for the continuous improvement of the instruments of the CAP (entailing their simplification, as well as the further development of subsidiarity, as is already the case for the utilisation of the national envelopes for the COMs and the rural development measures), and it will, at the same time, continue to work for the preservation of the CAP's basic principles.

 

Question no 24 by Olivier Dupuis (H-0372/02)
 Subject: Georgia
 

At its fourth meeting, which took place in Tbilisi on 29 and 30 April 2002, the EU-Georgia Parliamentary Cooperation Committee adopted a final document in which (item 14) it ‘calls on the Georgian and EU authorities to fulfil, as soon as possible, the Partnership and Cooperation Agreement (PCA), in order to create the basis for starting negotiations for an Association Agreement’.

Can the Commission provide a comprehensive list of points of the Cooperation Agreement which have not yet been implemented and the reasons why this is case, together with a statement of strategy and the timetable prepared by the Commission so that the Agreement as a whole can be implemented as soon as possible?

Moreover, does the Commission not consider that the PCA, or even a possible Association Agreement, is not a suitable EU response for Georgia to be able to face the challenges arising out of the region’s extremely problematic situation? Does the Commission not consider that the issue of Georgia’s accession to the EU should no longer be evaded, and that therefore, without further delay, Georgia should be included in the list of candidate countries, on the understanding that its membership will depend on its ability to integrate the acquis communautaire into its national legislation?

 
  
 

The Partnership and Cooperation Agreement (PCA) with Georgia entered into force in July 1999. The Union supports Georgia's aspiration to integrate further into European models and structures. Since independence in 1991, the Union has provided technical assistance of some EUR 350 million to support Georgia’s transition. For example, the Union Food Security Programme covers about 65% of budget of Ministry of Agriculture, a sector accounting for 40% of Gross Domestic Product (GDP) and 60% of labour force.

However, the country faces very severe difficulties:

Since 1991, one million people have left the country;

GDP per capita has decreased by 70%;

60% of the population is poor;

Two unresolved internal conflicts have generated nearly 300 000 internally displaced persons;

Corruption (including vested interests in maintaining status quo on conflicts) is rampant;

Announced reforms in the electoral code, local self-government were not adopted following the Presidential elections in 2000;

Religious minorities are victims of aggressions which the authorities have difficulty in punishing.

All of this, as well as the lack of implementation of existing legislation, hamper progress and limit the impact of Union assistance. Commitments undertaken by Georgia in October 2000 to adopt a national programme for PCA implementation have not yet materialised. Respect for the rule of law in trade and business (customs, taxes) is a key concern. Foreign investments are almost non existent. The Union reviews all these issues during the regular annual meetings. Furthermore, we want to be stricter in ensuring Georgia fulfils all conditions linked to our assistance.

Under these circumstances, the Commission considers that it cannot envisage any further step at this stage other than to pursue full implementation of PCA. This depends mostly on political, social and economic reforms, as well as on resolution of two internal conflicts on which the lead must come from Georgia itself. The Georgian authorities who have not requested Union membership or candidate status are very well aware of the situation.

 

Question no 25 by Brian Crowley (H-0374/02)
 Subject: Fish processing industry and research
 

What new initiatives does the Commission consider can usefully be brought forward aimed at strengthening research in the fish processing industry with a particular emphasis on the island regions and creating new employment and training opportunities?

 
  
 

The thematic priority 5 (« Food Quality and Safety ») of the Union sixth framework research programme (FP6) aims at assuring consumer health and well being by providing safer, high-quality and health-promoting foods, including seafoods, relying on fully controlled and integrated production systems. This end-user driven approach will trigger research on the development of new, safer, fully traceable, seafood products and feeds. Question no 29 by Ole Krarup (H-0385/02)
 Subject: Denmark's JHA opt-out
 

Under the Edinburgh decision Denmark does not participate in supranational EU decisions in the area of justice and home affairs, and a number of parallel agreements have therefore been concluded with Denmark over the years, particularly in the field of police cooperation.

However, it was reported on 30 April that in future the Commission did not wish to enter into similar arrangements which could ensure coordination in the area of civil law, e.g. as regards the application of the insolvency regulation or the rules on decisions in custody cases, etc.

According to the newspaper ‘Politiken’, the Commission intended this alleged refusal to be a ‘pedagogical signal’ to the Danish government.

Can the Commission confirm or refute these rumours and indicate the objective grounds on which it would refuse, contrary to past practice, to conclude parallel agreements with Denmark in fields where the Danish government should wish to do so.

 
  
 

The measures relating to judicial cooperation in civil matters that the honourable Member has raised in his oral question are governed by Title IV of the Treaty establishing the European Community. However, the Protocol on the position of Denmark, annexed to the Treaty of Amsterdam, stipulates that Denmark shall not take part in the adoption of measures governed by Title IV of the Treaty. Furthermore, none of the provisions of Title IV and no measure adopted pursuant to that Title shall be binding upon or applicable in Denmark.

Nonetheless, in certain specific cases, the non-participation of Denmark in measures under Title IV of the EC Treaty risks jeopardising the homogeneity, coherence and legal certainty of Community rules and/or upsetting the balance between the mutual obligations and responsibilities arising from the commitments made by all the Member States, including Denmark, prior to the communitarisation of the areas in question.

Given these risks, the Commission believed that the Community should seek solutions legally compatible with primary Community legislation which will enable Denmark to ‘take part’ – on an exceptional basis and as a temporary solution – in certain instruments based on Title IV of the EC Treaty, by concluding agreements between the Community and Denmark.

With regard to legal cooperation in civil matters, the Commission perceives only two measures that meet these criteria:

- Council Regulation (EC) No 44/2001 of 22 December 2000 on jurisdiction and the enforcement of judgments in civil and commercial matters (‘Brussels I’)(1), which, following the Treaty of Amsterdam, communitarised the Brussels Convention of 27 September 1968. Denmark is, of course, party to the Brussels Convention;

- Council Regulation (EC) No 1348/2000 of 29 May 2000 on the service in the Member States of judicial and extrajudicial documents in civil or commercial matters, by which the Council communitarised a convention of 26 May 1997 concluded within the framework of Title VI of the Treaty on European Union (but which has never entered into force). The ‘Brussels I’ regulation explicitly refers to the provisions of the Regulation on the transmission of documents for its application.

This solution was devised by the Commission, observing the following principles:

In accordance with its Protocol, Denmark does not have a case-by-case opt-in facility as regards the measures based on Title IV of the EC Treaty which do not represent an extension of the Schengen acquis. The restrictive wording of Article 5 of the Protocol relating solely to the measures ‘to build upon the Schengen acquis’ does not allow, under any circumstances, its application to be extended to other measures under Title IV of the EC Treaty.

At any time, Denmark may, in accordance with its constitutional requirements, inform the other Member States that it no longer wishes to avail itself of all or part of this Protocol. In that event, Denmark will apply in full all relevant measures then in force that have been adopted within the framework of the Union (Article 7 of the Protocol).

Furthermore, in the Commission’s opinion, the long-term objective must be Denmark’s full participation in the measures based on Title IV of the EC within the institutional framework of the Community, once Denmark has fulfilled the necessary constitutional requirements to this end.

It follows that any mechanism enabling Denmark to participate in all or a significant number of the measures based on Title IV of the EC Treaty, such as the conclusion of agreements on the instruments mentioned by the honourable Member and adopted in accordance with Article 65 of the Treaty in the area of family law or insolvency, where this participation did not take place within the institutional framework of the Community, would be incompatible with the spirit of the Protocol on the position of Denmark. It would certainly make it less in Denmark’s interest to renounce its Protocol and the answer to the fundamental problem raised by the honourable Member must, ultimately, be that it should renounce the Protocol.

The Commission therefore considered, when adopting its decision of 30 April 2002, that the conclusion of parallel agreements with Denmark in the area of judicial cooperation in civil matters can only be envisaged in the two exceptional cases set out above.

The Commission considers that, within the framework of the Community method – which is a unique decision-making method adapted to the specific nature of the Union, a Union of States and peoples – the Commission alone must have the right of initiative, the right of decision-making being the joint prerogative of Parliament and the Council. This distribution of powers is intended to avoid the institution responsible for making a decision being responsible for the proposal as well.

The Commission points out that Parliament has already declared itself to be of this opinion in the resolutions containing Parliament’s proposals for the Intergovernmental Conference which drew up the Treaty of Nice(1) and again, recently, in the resolution on the division of competences between the European Union and the Member States (the Lamassoure report)(2).

The reason, which is still valid, why the founding fathers conferred an almost exclusive right of initiative on the Commission is the need to make an independent institution responsible for discerning the general interest of all the Member States and proposing suitable solutions for furthering it. Indeed, it is important for the decision-making and legislative process to be based on an initiative which is not geared towards the specific interests of one or more Member States and/or towards the interests of certain ‘stakeholders’. It is Parliament and the Council, however, who make the final decision.

The Commission would point out that granting Parliament a right of initiative would, in all probability, lead to a right of initiative being granted to the Council or the Member States. In an institutional system which is not based on the conventional national model in which there is a government formed by a parliamentary majority, this solution would be likely to cause great dispersion of the Union’s action. The results of the experience within the ‘third pillar’ of a right of initiative shared between the Commission and the Member States have not been conclusive.

Lastly, it should also be pointed out that Parliament – like the Council – may, at any time, request that the Commission submit any appropriate proposal on matters on which it considers that a Community act is required for the purpose of implementing the Treaty (Article 192 EC). Management of fishing effort and capacity in line with sustainable catching opportunities will have a positive impact on the fish and shellfish stocks. There is no estimate of the overall effect of a reduction of effort and capacity to sustainable levels, but the scientists (International Council for the Exploration of the Sea (ICES) and Scientific Technical and Economic Committee for Fisheries (STECF)) have assessed the effects of a reduction in fishing mortality on a large number of stocks. Public aid should work for and not against conservation. Given the current dramatic state of affairs in European fisheries, with which the Parliament agrees, this is the only way to deal effectively with fleet over-capacity which is at the root of the current problems.
