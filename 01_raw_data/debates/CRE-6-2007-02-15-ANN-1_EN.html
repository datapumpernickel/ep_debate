QUESTIONS TO THE COUNCIL (The Presidency-in-Office of the Council of the European Union bears sole responsibility for these answers)
Question no 9 by Richard Corbett (H-1070/06)
 Subject: Duty-free allowances
 

How does Council justify its decision to double the duty-free allowances for people returning from outside the EU? Does it not agree that such a doubling increases unfair competition with domestic retailers, undercuts government revenue in EU Member States, weakens the possibilities for national governments to use fiscal measures to discourage tobacco and alcohol consumption, and encourages purchases at airports and/or aircraft, which adds nothing to airline security but boosts a ludicrous and inefficient sales point that would not exist were it not for this undue tax break?

 
  
 

(DE) The following answer, which was drafted by the presidency and is binding on neither the Council nor its members, was presented in a non-oral form at the Council’s question time in the European Parliament’s plenary sitting in February 2007 in Strasbourg.

On 28 November 2006 the Council reached political agreement on the proposal for a directive on the exemption from valued added tax and excise duty of goods imported by persons travelling from third countries.

The Council decided to raise the current 175-euro monetary threshold, which was laid down in 1994, to 300 euros for people travelling over land, including on inland waterways, and 430 euros for air and sea passengers. In the view of the Council, it was appropriate to adjust the threshold in line with the evolution of monetary values. As a result of the planned removal of the specific maximum quantities for perfume, coffee and tea, these items will also fall beneath the threshold in future. If the threshold were not raised, travellers would be able to carry fewer duty-free items than before.

The amendment to the directive also gives the Member States greater flexibility and reduces the administrative burden involved in routine controls and, especially, calculating charges in the case of small quantities. In the interests of travellers, goods of relatively low value need no longer be declared.

As far as tobacco products and alcoholic drinks are concerned, I should point out that these are subject to quantitative restrictions and are thus unaffected by the increase in the threshold. On the contrary, in the interests of supporting their health policies, Member States are to apply lower maximum quantities for imports of tobacco products.

Question no 11 by Georgios Papastamkos(H-1076/06)
 Subject: Beyond the EU's period of reflection
 

The way out of the constitutional crisis should take the form of a global integration project which will 'politicise' and make visible to the public all the activities of the Union, from both an internal and external perspective. How can we talk of a cohesive, effective and competitive Union on a world scale without first determining the main thrust of its policy? Has a link been made between the constitutional crisis and the dialectical relationship between deepening and enlargement? Should the boundaries not be drawn to mark the limits of the Union's political and economic geography? Will the German Presidency put forward a proposal which will make European civil society the focal point of input-output legitimacy at international level?

 
  
 

(DE) This answer, which was drafted by the presidency and is binding neither on the Council nor on its members, was not given orally during Questions to the Council at the February 2007 plenary sitting of the European Parliament in Strasbourg.

In essence, the honourable Member’s question relates to two points, namely what he terms the ‘constitutional crisis’ and enlargement.

Further to the first point, the honourable Member is referred to the statement by the Heads of State and Government, on the occasion of their meeting in December 2006, to the effect that the Union’s approach is twin-track, in that it makes use of the best possible options afforded by the Treaties currently in force in order to achieve concrete objectives, while at the same time working to prepare the way for the continuation of the reform process. As announced in December, the Finnish Presidency handed over the outcomes of its consultations with the Member States on the constitutional treaty to the German Presidency as part of its preparations for the report to be presented in the first six months of 2007. The intention is that the report should highlight possible future developments. It will also serve as a basis for further decisions as to how the process of reforming the EU should be planned.

I can answer with greater brevity the second point of the honourable Member’s question, relating to enlargement. Firstly, in reaching, in December, their renewed consensus on enlargement, the Heads of State and Government stressed that the EU would honour the promises made to the countries already in the enlargement process. Secondly – and this is no less important – they stated that, as the enlargement of the European Union progresses, European integration can be successful only if the EU institutions continue to function efficiently and if EU policy continues to develop and be funded in a sustainable manner.

 Subject: Equal reductions on air and sea fares for non-Community residents of the Balearics
 

Royal Decree 1316/2001 regulates the discounts given to residents of the autonomous communities of the Canaries and the Balearic Islands on standard fares in air and sea transport (43% discount on the price of tickets, rising to 50% in 2007). The regional government of the Balearics is seeking to extend the right to claim this discount to immigrants resident in the Balearics. Achieving a genuine integration policy entails offering non-Community residents the same incentives as those granted to national or Community residents. This will make it possible for these residents to be effectively and genuinely integrated, in a context of solidarity and equality.

Does the Council believe that the European institutions should be involved in this issue, since it relates to integration policy seen from the point of view of human rights, democracy and the rule of law? Could the Council oblige or advise the Spanish Government to amend the above decree so as to extend equal rights to all residents, regardless of whether they are nationals of Member States or of non-Community countries?

 
  
 

(DE) This answer, which was drafted by the presidency and is binding neither on the Council nor on its members, was not given orally during Questions to the Council at the February 2007 plenary sitting of the European Parliament in Strasbourg.

Since this question does not fall within the Council’s remit, the Council has not discussed it.

 

Question no 13 by David Martin (H-1082/06)
 Subject: A European Code of Good Practice
 

In December 2003, the European Parliament approved a report drafted by Uma Aaltonen, based on a petition submitted by a British person with MS, Louise McVay. The report identified disparities and a general inadequacy in the level of treatment and care of people with MS across the EU. On average only 28% of people with MS have access to vital disease-modifying drugs, despite there being a great amount of evidence showing that high standards of treatment have a positive affect on the lives of people with MS and ultimately on the social security budgets of the Member States. The EP has called for a code of good practice on the rights and quality of life of people with MS, which would look at crucial issues around employment and job retention, a joint research agenda and meaningful participation of people with MS themselves. A draft code has been developed by the European Multiple Sclerosis Platform. What steps does the German Presidency intend to take towards the endorsement and implementation of such a code?

 
  
 

(DE) The following answer, which was drafted by the presidency and is binding on neither the Council nor its members, was presented in a non-oral form at the Council’s question time in the European Parliament’s plenary sitting in February 2007 in Strasbourg.

The Council thanks the Honourable Member for his interest in this matter.

The Council has not yet discussed a Code of Good Practice such as this. However, the Programme of Community Action in the Field of Public Health (2003-2008) offers the possibility of implementing measures and programmes to establish indicators on neurodegenerative brain diseases, impairments of neurological development and non-psychiatric brain diseases, including multiple sclerosis. The European Commission is supporting the European Multiple Sclerosis Platform’s ‘MS-Information Dividend’ project, which is due to start this year.

The Council has reached political agreement on the Second Programme of Community Action in the Field of Public Health, and the presidency hopes to reach agreement with the European Parliament at second reading. The programme provides for the possibility of furthering an exchange of knowledge and best practice, collection and analysis of health-related data, and distribution of information on health issues.

In addition, the Seventh Research Framework Programme opens up possibilities for health research in the field of neurodegenerative diseases.

The Commission has not so far presented a specific proposal on multiple sclerosis and the presidency has planned no special activities of the Council on this subject.

 

Question no 14 by Eugenijus Gentvilas (H-1087/06)
 Subject: Economic sanctions against Belarus
 

The Council has taken steps aimed at temporarily suspending access for Belarus to the Community's generalised system of preferences. In my view, the introduction of such economic sanctions contradicts the document entitled 'What the European Union could bring to Belarus', which was presented by Commissioner Ferrero-Waldner on 21 November 2006. The measures taken against Belarus by the EU thus lack coherence and are hard to foresee.

Did the Council take account of the following aspects when considering initiatives aimed at imposing economic sanctions on Belarus:

The economic damage suffered by EU Member States that maintain active trade relations with Belarus, i.e. primarily Lithuania, Poland and Latvia?

The possibility that Russia might decide not to raise the price of gas for Belarus or might lessen its threats, thereby gaining the support of the population and presenting EU policy as being hostile to or indeed designed to isolate Belarus?

 
  
 

(DE) This answer, which was drafted by the presidency and is binding neither on the Council nor on its members, was not given orally during Questions to the Council at the February 2007 plenary sitting of the European Parliament in Strasbourg.

On 21 December 2006, the Council adopted Regulation 1933/2006/EC temporarily withdrawing access to the generalised tariff preferences from the Republic of Belarus(1). It needs to be emphasised, however, that this measure is not conceived as a sanction, but rather as a suspension of preferences resulting from Belarus’ failure to comply with international labour standards, particularly the right to freedom of association.

The Council is aware of the potential economic consequences that might result, for certain Member States, from the withdrawal of access to the generalised tariff preferences and from the consequent return to the generally applicable tariffs. It has to be said, however, that such economic consequences would be limited, since most goods imported into the EU from Belarus have not hitherto enjoyed preferences under the generalised scheme. What is also at issue is the maintenance of the European Union’s credibility, of coherence in the application of its decisions and the upholding of its core values.

The withdrawal of access to the generalised tariff preferences does not, however, enter into effect immediately, but rather only six months following the decision being taken, and even then only if it has not become evident before that date that Belarus has remedied the deficits as found and a decision has therefore been taken that the conditions resulting in this Regulation no longer apply. This gives the authorities in Belarus a further opportunity to comply with the relevant international standards. The International Labour Organisation (ILO) will be continuing its examination of this matter at the 298th sitting of its Governing Body in March 2007 and at the 96th International Labour Conference in June 2007, and will be subjecting to particular scrutiny the latest changes to the law submitted by the Belarus Government. The Commission has undertaken to report to the Council on current developments in Belarus following the ILO governing body meeting in Geneva in March 2007.

As for the last part of the question, the withdrawal of the generalised tariff preferences has had no effect on Russia’s determination to increase gas prices for Belarus, and the Council has no evidence of any attempt on Russia’s part to depict the EU’s policy as hostile.

There is no contradiction between the withdrawal of access to the generalised tariff preferences and the non-paper ‘What the European Union could bring to Belarus’, according to which the EU is unable to normalise and deepen its relations with Belarus for as long as the authorities there continue to offend against democratic values and violate the fundamental rights of the country’s people.

 
 

(1)OJ L 405 of 30 December 2006, p.35.

 

Question no 15 by Marian Harkin (H-1089/06)
 Subject: Open Skies Agreement - bilateral agreements
 

Given the lack of progress on the Open Skies negotiations, what is the Council's position with regard to individual Member States negotiating their own bilateral air transport agreements?

 
  
 

(DE) This answer, which has been drawn up by the Presidency and is binding neither on the Council nor on its members, was not given orally at Council Question Time at the European Parliament's February 2007 part-session in Strasbourg.

The negotiations on the EU-US air transport agreement were wound up in November 2005. The EU subsequently waited for more than a year for the outcome of the US Government’s internal procedures as a result of which rules were to be drawn up on foreign holdings in US carriers.

At its meeting of 12 December 2006, however, the Council was informed that the US Department of Transportation had withdrawn the Notice of Proposed Rulemaking (NPRM) concerning extended scope for holdings in US carriers, which had been issued as long ago as November 2005. The Council expressed deep disappointment and its regret at that decision, and reaffirmed its commitment to the goal of securing a historic, first-stage EU-US comprehensive air transport agreement that would increase legal certainty and replace the existing bilateral agreements. To that end, and further to the proposal of the US, the Council requested the Commission to enter into urgent consultations with the US as soon as possible in order to seek elements that could be used to restore a proper balance of interests.

EU-US relations in this area are addressed only in the context of the EU-US air transport agreement referred to, and the possibility of concluding bilateral air transport agreements between EU Member States and the US is not being considered by the parties involved.

 

Question no 16 by Nicolaos Vakalis (H-1090/06)
 Subject: Environmental specifications for automobiles
 

The report adopted on 13 December 2006 by Parliament concerning the implementation of the Euro 5 regulation specifies the following nitrogen oxide (NOx) emission limits for automobiles and vans: 180 mg/km from 2009 and subsequently 80 mg/km from 2014, while in the United States the limit value is 44 mg/km to be implemented progressively from 2004 to 2007. The European automobile industry has recently succeeded in producing automobiles to US specifications.

When considering technical texts and specifications, should we not take account of what is being done by our competitors at international level and measure improvement in terms of not only our own performance to date but also what is being achieved at international level, particularly regarding innovation and environmental sustainability?

Regarding automobile specifications, does the Council not agree that we should take more decisive measures and follow the example of our competitors in adopting more stringent pollutant emission limits?

 
  
 

(DE) This answer, which was drafted by the presidency and is binding neither on the Council nor on its members, was not given orally during Questions to the Council at the February 2007 plenary sitting of the European Parliament in Strasbourg.

The honourable Member points out that, in the United States, more stringent NOx emission values currently apply than is the case in the European Union. Because of differences in the test procedures, however, the limit values are not directly comparable.

The ‘Euro 5’ and ‘Euro 6’ limit values recently adopted accomplish a drastic reduction in NOx emission in Europe. Compliance with the requirements in Europe – as also in the USA – is achieved by enforcing the removal of the most particulates possible from the exhausts of diesel private motor vehicles.

It is worthy of note that there has been, to date, only a small market for private diesel motor vehicles in the USA, and so the technology required to achieve the American limit value was made available only for a few diesel cars at the top of the medium range. Private motor cars of all classes equipped with the most modern diesel engines occupy a markedly larger segment of the European market than they do in the United States, and one of the effects of this is a reduction in the amount of CO2 emitted by vehicles in the EU. The problem of CO2 emissions will be addressed in the forthcoming Commission communication on the amendment of the Community’s strategy for reducing the emission of CO2 from motor vehicles, to which the Council will be giving close attention.

The EU is engaged in putting the case for further harmonisation of the requirements applicable to motor vehicles to the UN Economic Commission for Europe, which is based in Geneva.

 

Question no 17 by Ivo Belet (H-0003/07)
 Subject: Takeovers of football clubs by obscure investors - involvement of Europol
 

The Kazakh businessman and politician Rachat Aliyev wishes to invest in the Belgian football club Excelsior Moeskroen. Aliyev is a controversial individual: according to Human Rights Watch, he is corrupt and has committed abuses of power.

In view of the importance to society of professional football and in the interests of its image, it is absolutely undesirable for obscure financiers to gain influence over the policies of professional football clubs.

Does the Council agree that it is desirable for Europol to investigate this case and other similar ones in order to prevent dubious or dishonest investors from gaining control over professional sports clubs?

 
  
 

(DE) This answer, which was drafted by the presidency and is binding neither on the Council nor on its members, was not given orally during Questions to the Council at the February 2007 plenary sitting of the European Parliament in Strasbourg.

The honourable Member should note that a number of conditions have to be fulfilled in order for Europol to be able to act in connection with suspected criminal activity. Above all, the acts in question must be subject to Europol's mandate (Article 2(1) of the Europol Convention, q.v.). If they are not, then only the national police authorities are responsible. It is worthy of mention that Europol does not itself conduct criminal investigations, but instead supports the Member States, in cases of cross-border organised crime, by means of the sharing and analysis of information.

 

Question no 18 by Vytautas Landsbergis (H-0008/07)
 Subject: Do we not want an Energy Charter?
 

After Poland set two conditions on commencing negotiations for a new cooperation agreement between the EU and Russia – one relating to the Energy Charter (Russia’s refusal to ratify it) and the other to the embargo, viewed as politically motivated, on Polish food products (with threats to apply it to the EU as a whole) – we have become aware of efforts to talk about, focus on and promise future decisions on only one thing: meat. According to the press agency Interfax, the High Representative for the Common Foreign and Security Policy, Javier Solana, has stated that much progress has been made on removing the issue of energy from Poland’s demands. Is this a superficial attitude, or is it the conscious position of the Council to have greater concern for the feelings of Russia’s leaders than for Europe’s energy security interests?

 
  
 

(DE) This answer, which was drafted by the presidency and is binding neither on the Council nor on its members, was not given orally during Questions to the Council at the February 2007 plenary sitting of the European Parliament in Strasbourg.

The Council wishes to assure the honourable Member that security of supply represents one of the most important priorities of the Community’s energy policy, as is evident from the many approaches being employed, in accordance with this policy, in order to make the Community’s supplies more secure, including, among other examples, the diversification of sources of supply and transport routes, the diversification of energy sources, greater energy efficiency, an external energy policy, the optimisation of the functioning of the internal market in energy and measures aimed as promoting transparency.

The objective of guaranteeing the security of the Community’s supplies is also a key characteristic of the Community’s dealings with Russia concerning energy. The Council is persuaded that the Community’s interest in secure supplies is best served by a mutually beneficial and stable energy relationship with Russia – a relationship that is transparent and must function in a reliable manner.

The European Council of 23/24 March 2006 stated that improvement of security of supply involved ensuring common operational approaches to address crisis situations in a spirit of solidarity, taking subsidiarity into account. It ought also to be noted that the 2006 statement on security of supply by the G8 explicitly endorsed the principles of the energy charter.

The Council can assure the honourable Member that no effort was spared or is being spared to find a compromise as regards the incorporation of relevant references to the Energy Charter Treaty into the negotiation guidelines.

As regards the other parts of the question, the Council does not comment on statements to the press.

 

Question no 19 by Dimetrios Papadimoulis (H-0009/07)
 Subject: Entry visa requirement for citizens of 10 EU Member States
 

The USA continues to impose a selective entry visa requirement on the citizens of ten Member States of the European Union. Greece is the only country among the 15 old Member States whose citizens still require a visa to travel to the USA on business or as tourists. Despite the fact that new, more secure passports have been issued - identical throughout the European Union - the USA continues to maintain the visa system for one group of States only.

Moreover, as Parliament pointed out in its resolution (P6_TA(2006)0238), adopted on 1 June 2006, a visa requirement on citizens of ten Member States of the EU perpetuates inequality among EU citizens and implies a lack of trust in the European Union's own visa arrangements (recital K and paragraph 37).

What does the Council propose to do to promote Parliament's resolution P6_TA(2006)0238 and persuade the USA to rescind the visa requirement which it imposes on selected citizens of the Union only?

(EN) The scheme of generalised tariff preferences (GSP) is one of the EU's key instruments to respond to the needs of developing countries and to promote sustainable development and good governance.  Subject: Discrimination in the EU labour market
 

With reference to the Commission's failure to consider the case registered as EMPL/E/3 MC/ek D(2005) 29782 and closed under reference EMPL/E/3 MC/ek D(2006) 13375 on the grounds that there was no ongoing and general administrative practice contrary to Community law, which runs counter to the conclusions of the Solvit report 16481/05 of 26 October 2005, based on the judgment in cases ECJ C-66/85 (Lawrie-Blum, para. 17) and C-109/04 (Kranemann, para. 15), there has been a series of complaints from EU citizens who have suffered discriminatory treatment at the hands of the municipal authorities of Melun, in the Ile de France region, department of Seine et Marne, as a result of the deliberate infringement of Article 39 of the EC Treaty and of the provisions of Annex XII of the Treaty of Accession of the Czech Republic, Estonia, Cyprus, Latvia, Lithuania, Hungary, Malta Poland, Slovenia and Slovakia to the European Union, in particular Article 2(2) thereof.

Can the Commission provide a legal interpretation specifying how extensive breaches of Community law by Member States need to be before a particular case can be considered an ongoing and general practice and thus subject to official scrutiny by the Commission?

Will the Commission address the question of the probable infringement of the principle of the free movement of workers by the authorities of Melun, particularly in the light of the French Government's declared intention of partially opening up its labour market after 1 May 2006?

 
  
 

(FR) The honourable Member referred to a letter from a Polish national condemning a situation of which he was the victim. The complainant indicated that he had been working in France between December 2003 and June 2005 for an uninterrupted period of 18 months on the basis of three consecutive work experience contracts. When the last contract expired, he obtained another job in a French company. In his letter, the Polish national maintains that he is entitled to free access to the French labour market pursuant to Point 2.2 of Annex XII to the Treaty of Accession, which states that ‘Polish nationals legally working in a present Member State at the date of accession and admitted to the labour market of that Member State for an uninterrupted period of 12 months or longer will enjoy access to the labour market of that Member State’.

The French authorities refused him access to the labour market, however, and did not issue him with a work permit. The situation of this Polish national was passed on to SOLVIT(1), which was unable to resolve the case. The Polish national therefore approached the Commission in December 2005.

The Commission carried out a thorough analysis of the case. On the basis of information provided in December 2005, the Commission held that the condition referred to in Point 2 of Annex XII to the Accession Treaty had been met. The Commission gave notice of this position in the letter to which the honourable Member refers. The Polish national then forwarded supplementary information on the basis of which the Commission discovered that three work experience contracts had been concluded within the framework of a bilateral agreement between France and Poland. It would appear that, under the terms of that agreement, prior to beginning his work experience in France, the complainant was required to agree not to take a job other than that provided for under the conditions of his entry into France. In breach of that undertaking, he looked for another job in France on the basis of Annex XII of the Accession Treaty, In the light of this new information, the Commission concluded that the Polish national in question could not be deemed to have been admitted to the French labour market for an uninterrupted period of 12 months or longer pursuant to Annex XII to the Accession Treaty.

Under the discretionary powers invested in the Commission by the Treaty(2), the Commission decided that it had no obligation to follow up the case because it had not been demonstrated that there had been a general and ongoing administrative practice that was in breach of Community law.

The Commission nevertheless forwarded useful information to the Polish national in its last letter in case he wished to initiate proceedings at national level.

As regards the second phase of the transitional periods, France has decided to maintain national measures or those arising from bilateral agreements until the end of the five-year period after accession, that is to say, until the end of April 2009. This means in practice that the application of Community law as regards access to the French labour market for Polish nationals will be delayed by three extra years and that France has the right to apply national law when it comes to access to the labour market. In this context, France has also indicated that it plans to liberalise access to the labour market in some sectors for which work permit procedures would be simplified. According to the Accession Treaty, France must, however, comply with the standstill clause and principle of Community preference.

The Commission remains prepared to examine any further information that the honourable Member may have so as to establish whether there has been a violation of Community law.
