Questions to the Commission
Question no 27 by David W. Martin (H-0402/99)
 Subject: Wild­caught monkeys used for experiments
 

Can the Commission inform me of the progress of complaint number 97/4932 made by the British Union for the Abolition of Vivisection on the above subject? In particular, and with reference to oral question number H-0621/98(1), does the Commission still consider that the use of monkeys in the production of the vaccine and the use of monkeys for neurovirulence testing of the vaccine falls outside the scope of Directive 86/609/EEC on animal experimentation(2) and, if so in either case, why?

 
  
 

The complaint to which the Honourable Member refers is still under examination. Since the answer given to Question H-0621/98, the complainant and the Commission have exchanged several correspondences.

On the questions asked by the Honourable Member on the scope of Directive 86/609/EEC regarding the protection of animals used for experimental and other scientific purposes(3), as already stated in the reply to Question H-0621/98, the production of vaccines is considered to be outside of the scope of the directive, since the production when interpreted in the light of article 2(d) does not fulfil “experimental and other scientific purposes”. Moreover, the usual interpretation is that euthanasia is not considered as an experiment within the meaning of the directive.

On the contrary, the Commission is of the opinion that the use of monkeys for neurovirulence testing falls within the scope of the directive.

(2) OJ L 358, 18.12.1986, p. 1.
(3) Council Directive 86/609/EEC of 24 November 1986 on the approximation of laws, regulations and administrative provisions of the Member States regarding the protection of animals used for experimental and other scientific purposes, OJ L 358 of 18.12.1986, pp. 1-28.

 

Question no 28 by Caroline F. Jackson (H-0403/99)
 Subject: Task Force on safety of vitamins and minerals
 

Could the Commission confirm that its Scientific Committee has established a new task force to look at the safety of vitamins and minerals and also publish the membership of the task force and its detailed terms of reference?

 
  
 

The Honourable Member will be aware that the Commission is developing a proposal to harmonise legislation for food supplements containing vitamins and minerals and for the addition of vitamins and minerals to foods. Such a proposal will need to take account of both nutritional recommendations and upper safety limits for vitamins and minerals. The Scientific Committee on Food has already made recommendations on the nutritional requirements for these nutrients and has recently been requested to advise the Commission on upper safety limits for some 29 individual vitamin and minerals.

Details concerning the terms of reference and the composition of the Committee’s working group will be sent directly to the Honourable Member.

 

Question no 29 by Dana Rosemary Scallon (H-0404/99)
 Subject: Infrastructure in western Ireland
 

After 22 years of the operation of the European Regional Development Fund the infrastructure in western Ireland still lags very far behind that in which the majority of European Union citizens gain their livelihood.

What steps is the Commission taking to ensure that telecommunications, road, rail and air communications in western Ireland match the best in Europe?

 
  
 

Despite significant contributions from the European Regional Development Fund (ERDF) to infrastructure projects in Ireland the Commission is aware that there are still gaps in the endowment of same as compared to more developed regions in Europe. Most of Western Ireland (the Border, Midlands and Western Region) will continue to receive funding from the ERDF as a region with full Objective 1 status during the next programming period 2000-2006, whilst the remainder of Ireland will receive funding as an Objective 1 region in transition.

The Irish Government is for the moment preparing the National Development Plan which will form the basis for the negotiations with the Commission of the use of the Structural and Cohesion Fund in 2000-2006. No doubt infrastructure investments will be one of the major spending priorities under the next round of funding to further improve the relative position of Western Ireland.

 

Question no 30 by Jonas Sjöstedt (H-0410/99)
 Subject: Social dumping in the transport sector
 

There is a growing problem in Sweden with foreign haulage firms carrying out operations without respecting the applicable labour agreements in that sector, a problem which has been exacerbated by the deregulation of cabotage.

Can Sweden require hauliers from other Member States to comply fully with Swedish collective labour agreements when operating in Sweden?

 
  
 

The Honourable Member enquires whether hauliers from other Member States can be required to comply with Swedish collective labour agreements when operating in Sweden.

Swedish collective agreements apply, in principle, to employers and employees falling within their scope and do not cover, in principle, employees working for foreign employers, unless there is a posting of workers within the meaning of Directive 96/71/EC(1).

In other words, drivers of foreign vehicles operating in Sweden normally fall under the collective agreements of their home state where they concluded their employment contract. In reality, international transport operations mean that the driver of a commercial vehicle may at any time drive to or transit other Member States. It would be impracticable for such a driver automatically to come within the collective labour agreements of every Member State he transits or where he loads or unloads goods.

Transport operations between Member States are governed by Council Regulation (EEC) No 881/92(2).

Only those hauliers established in a Member State who are in possession of a Community licence in accordance with this regulation may perform international transport operations.

Holders of such a Community licence may also perform cabotage operations in other Member States on a temporary basis in accordance with Council Regulation (EEC) No 3118/93(3). This regulation lays down the conditions under which non-resident carriers may operate national road haulage services within a Member State. Article 6 of that regulation stipulates the areas in which the laws, regulations and administrative provisions in force in the host Member State also apply. These include for instance the relevant national rules on driving and rest time which do apply therefore also to drivers from other Member States engaged in cabotage operations. Sweden can thus require compliance by foreign hauliers with laws, regulations and provisions corresponding to article 6 of Council Regulation (EEC) No 3118/93.

 
 

(1) Directive 96/71/EC of the European Parliament and of the Council of 16 December 1996 concerning the posting of workers in the framework of the provision of services (Official Journal No L 18, 21.1.1997, p. 1)
(2) Council Regulation (EEC) No 881/92 of 26 March 1992 on access to the market in the carriage of goods by road within the Community to or from the territory of a Member State or passing across the territory of one or more Member States (Official Journal No L 95, 9.4.1992, p. 1)
(3) Council Regulation (EEC) No 3118/93 of 25 October 1993 laying down the conditions under which non-resident carriers may operate national road haulage services within a Member State (Official Journal No L 279, 12.11.1993, p. 1)

 

Question no 31 by Benedetto Della Vedova (H-0411/99)
 Subject: Obstacles to competition and state aid in the television sector
 

The Italian Minister, Mr Cardinale, caught in a blatant conflict of interests, has criticised the attempt by the outgoing Commissioner, Mr Van Miert, to ensure that the scope of the rules on competition and the free market includes the state television sector as well.

Can the Commission and, in particular, the Commissioner responsible for competition, Mr Monti, confirm the Commission's declared intention to intervene in order to remove obstacles to competition and put a stop to unjustified state aid in the television sector?

 
  
 

The Commission, on 3 February, enjoined the Italian, French and Spanish governments to produce the necessary information in order to assess whether the State aid granted to the respective public service broadcasters can be considered as aid pre-dating the signature of the Treaty or, in the case of Spain, the accession to the Community.

On the basis of the information provided, the Commission decided, on 20 July, to initiate the formal investigation procedure with regard to some ad hoc aid measures (capital increases, tax exemptions, etc.) granted to the French and Italian public broadcasters. It has not yet reached a conclusion as to whether the licence fee (and for Spain the State grants) are to be considered as “existing aid” in the sense of Article 88(1) of the EC Treaty.

The Commission, as guardian of the Treaty, has to ensure that the Treaty rules are not infringed. In this sense, it has an obligation to act and to take a decision on the public funding in favour of public service broadcasters – as recalled by the Court in the Spanish and French cases, where the Commission was condemned for failure to take a position within a reasonable time.

Any State aid granted to public broadcasters would have to be examined in accordance with the Community competition rules, taking also into account the Protocol on Public Service in Broadcasting, which entered into force on 1 May last.

As a matter of principle, State aid is generally forbidden by the EC Treaty, unless it can be found compatible under the exemptions provided for by Article 87, paragraphs 2 and 3, and, for companies entrusted with a public service mission, Article 86, paragraph 2. Accordingly, the Commission will first assess the nature of the aid granted to public broadcasters (existing or new) and, secondly, its compatibility under the mentioned Treaty rules.

 

Question no 32 by Marco Pannella (H-0412/99)
 Subject: Situation in Tunisia
 

The six-month suspended sentence passed on the Tunisian lawyer Mrs Radhia Nasraoui, the arrest of Mr Abderraouf Chammari, brother of the opposition leader Mr Khémais Chammari, and the arrests of Mr Khémais Ksila and numerous political and human rights activists, are clear evidence that the political situation in Tunisia is not only not improving (as might have been hoped given the economic situation), but is continuing to deteriorate. In addition, a growing number of economic analysts are calling attention to worsening corruption in the Tunisian economy, especially in relation to privatisation.

What concrete action has the Commission taken, in particular in the context of the association agreement which now links the EU and Tunisia, with a view to creating the conditions which would enable Tunisia finally to commit itself to introducing democracy and the rule of law?

What action has the Commission taken with a view to an end to the persecution of Mrs Radhia Nasraoui, Mr Abderraouf Chammari, Mr Khémais Ksila and the many others who are suffering at the hands of the legal system and the police?

 
  
 

Since the launch of the Euro-Mediterranean Partnership in Barcelona in 1995 and the implementation of the Association Agreement between the European Union and Tunisia in 1998, two principal instruments have been used with regard to relations with that country. On the one hand, political dialogue, and on the other, financial support for human rights programmes.

Political dialogue has been carried out both multilaterally amongst the 27 countries and bilaterally between the Union and Tunisia. To date it has dealt with all the issues of common interest and in particular those which concern the human rights situation. Both individual and general matters concerning respect for these rights were on the agenda of the first Association Council with Tunisia which took place on 14 July 1998.

In addition to these formal meetings during which the Union stated its commitment to respecting the undertakings made in the Barcelona Declaration and in the bilateral agreement, the Union’s Heads of Mission to Tunisia endeavour on each occasion to persuade the Tunisian Authorities of the validity of the Union’s point of view.

With regard to the financing of programmes, the Commission decided, within the framework of the MEDA democracy programme, and in conjunction with the Tunisian Authorities, to subsidise, in 1996, 1997 and 1998, six actions. They include training and awareness seminars dealing with “migration and human rights”, the “training of trainers in the field of human rights”, the “Arab human rights information, documentation and training network”, “socio-economic rights”, the “condition of women in the Maghreb” and “institutional support for NGOs”.

 

Question no 33 by Marco Cappato (H-0413/99)
 Subject: Political prisoners in Georgia
 

Reports by international organisations suggest that there are at least 100 political prisoners in Georgia, held in extremely disturbing circumstances. The police and legal authorities appear to have gravely violated these prisoners' basic civil and political rights (the right to a fair trial, the right to a defence lawyer, the right to publicisation, the right to an impartial court), as well as their human rights: detainees have been tortured and maltreated, and have been refused medicine, resulting in several deaths (Mr Kvirtiya, Mr Abramishvili and Mr Loladze), while the health of other prisoners (Mr Chanturia, Mr Dgitshonaia and Mr Goreleshvili) is critical.

Can the Commission supply information on the case of Mr Petre Gelbakhiani, at present imprisoned in inhuman conditions, tortured several times, and sentenced to death on 6 March 1999 by the Supreme Court, following legal proceedings and a trial which were highly dubious from the viewpoint of the prevailing internationally recognised standards (given also that at the time of the alleged offences the death penalty had already been abolished)? Can the Commission also supply more general information on the situation of political prisoners in Georgia, and state what action it has taken or intends to take on the matter?

 
  
 

Mr Gelbakhiani, together with a number of other prisoners, was condemned in March 1995 for murder and terrorist acts. Their treatment, and also the state of Georgian prisons, have been extensively criticised both inside and outside Georgia. The Georgian authorities have informed the Commission that the death sentences were commuted to 20 years’ imprisonment when the death penalty was abolished in November 1997. The circumstances under which Mr Gelbakhiani was arrested, tried and detained were condemned by the UN Human Rights Committee in April 1998.

Following its accession to the Council of Europe, and ratification of the European Convention on Human Rights, Georgia has now committed itself, inter alia, to ensure strict observance of the human rights of detainees and to reform the prison system. Moreover, the cases of persons convicted for their part in the political upheavals following Georgia’s independence should be reviewed within two years.

The Commission is carefully following implementation of Georgia’s commitments in the field of human rights through its Delegation in Tbilisi, and is supplying Georgia with assistance for this purpose. These issues will form part of the political dialogue with Georgia under the Partnership and Co-operation Agreement which entered into force on 1 July.

 

Question no 34 by Inger Schörling (H-0415/99)
 Subject: Protection of wild birds
 

The municipality of Varberg in Halland recently unveiled a general development plan for the area, which includes the possible development of the airstrip on Getterön. At present, the Getterön airstrip is only used by small propeller-driven aircraft and the actual runway is simply grassland. Under the plans, the landing strip would be asphalted to enable much bigger aircraft to use it. Getterön is a bird protection area and a nature reserve and is also listed under Natura 2000 pursuant to Directive 79/409/EEC(1) on the protection of wild birds. In addition, the EU has contributed SEK 4.5 million under the North Sea 2C project towards the conservation of the wetlands in and around Getterön.

Development of the airstrip would severely disturb the bird life in the area through the increase in noise and pollution and is, therefore, contrary to Sweden's commitments under the above Directive. What possibilities are open to the Commission to prevent the further development of the airstrip? What measures can the Commission or other EU bodies take, should the Commission find that the development of the airstrip is contrary to the Directive?

 
  
 

An area called “Getterön” has been classified as a Special Protection Area (SPA) by Sweden pursuant to Article 4 of Council Directive 79/409/EEC(2).

The protection regime applying to the Getterön SPA is defined in Article 6(2), (3) and (4) of Council Directive 92/43/EEC(3). Therefore, any plan or project not directly connected to the management of this site but likely to have a significant effect on it must be subject to an appropriate environmental assessment in view of the site’s conservation objectives.

Given that the Honourable Member has alleged that the development of the airstrip at Getterön will severely disturb the bird life in this area the Commission will open a complaint file on this subject and investigate the matter with the Swedish authorities. Should it be established that Sweden has not taken the necessary steps to respect its obligations under these directives with regard to the protection of Getterön, the Commission will pursue this matter in accordance with the provisions of Article 226 of the EC Treaty.

Question no 35 by Gerard Collins (H-0417/99)
 Subject: EU support for science in the school
 

What measures does the Commission consider it could legitimately introduce to encourage science education in schools throughout the EU?

 
  
 

The Commission acknowledges the importance of science education in the school curriculum. It is the key to preparing young people for a world in which science and technology are constantly evolving and it is an important factor in encouraging competitiveness and innovation in Europe.

The Commission can play an important role in encouraging science education, while fully recognising the responsibility of the Member States for the content of teaching and the organisation of education systems as set out in Article 149 of the EC Treaty. The Community mandate in the field of education is to “… promote co-operation between Member States thus contributing to the quality of education”. The SOCRATES programme has been developed to facilitate this co-operation. Although the SOCRATES programme does not have an action which is specifically targeted at science education in schools, it does support science education under different parts of the programme. For example under COMENIUS (school education) science education in schools is promoted in the following ways:

By making science a topic for co-operation in school partnerships

By supporting projects developing materials and teacher training courses for science teachers.

There is also scope in Erasmus (higher education) to promote science education generally. The Community intends to continue to promote science education in schools under the successor programme to SOCRATES, the decision in relation to which is currently going through the legislative process.

In a more general sense the Commission supports non-curricula-specific science activities under its annual European Science and Technology Week. Activities supported include international competitions, joint projects, and on-line Internet debates involving the active participation of school students and science teachers. Such activities stimulate school children’s interest in important topics such as the environment, energy, food supply, information technology and biotechnology.

 

Question no 36 by Liam Hyland (H-0419/99)
 Subject: Euro canal routes
 

All interested parties were invited by the Commission to give their views on its Communication, 'The Common Transport Policy - Sustainable Mobility: Perspectives for the Future' not later than 1 April 1999.

Can the Commission indicate what level of response there was to its Communication from interested parties in the different Member States and will it state what new initiatives could result from these responses and will it agree, in particular, to establishing a Euro network of canals as has been established for Euro-routes as part of an overall EU process aimed at maximising the potential of canals and waterways in Ireland and the EU as a whole?

 
  
 

On the basis of a Commission Communication to the Council, the European Parliament, the Economic and Social Committee and the Committee of the Regions on the “Common Transport Policy – Sustainable Mobility: Perspectives for the Future” (COM(1998)716 final of 1 December 1998), the Commission invited these institutions as well as all other interested parties to contribute their opinion on the further development of the Common Transport Policy.

The consultation process has taken place in the course of the first semester of 1999.

The Economic and Social Committee has adopted its opinion in July 1999. The Committee of the Regions is due to adopt an opinion in September 1999. The Commission hopes that the Parliament will also adopt an opinion in due time.

In total, some 20 other interested organisations have sent in contributions, of which:

4 representing the interests of mobility-impaired persons;

1 is an environmental interest group;

2 representing the interests of cyclists;

4 representing the maritime transport sector;

4 representing the rail transport sector;

1 representing the aerospace industries;

1 representing an automotive construction company;

and 3 Member States.

The contributions from the various consulted parties are currently being analysed. In the light of these contributions and in the light of any data and elements contributed by information systems and research projects financed under the 4th and 5th Framework Programmes for Research and Development, the Commission intends to carry out a global evaluation of the strategic dimension of the Common Transport Policy as a framework for sustainable mobility.

As far as a European network of canals is concerned, one objective within the context of the development of a Trans-European Network is to build a network of inland waterways which is coherent, interoperable and economically as well as environmentally rational. This should allow the inland waterway sector to properly develop its function as a low-cost, safe and environmentally-friendly transport mode. Instruments in place to implement this policy are Decision No. 1692/96/EC of the European Parliament and of the Council of 23 July 1996 on Community Guidelines for the development of the Trans-European Transport Network(1) and Council Regulation (EC) No. 2196/98 of 1 October 1998 concerning the granting of Community financial assistance for actions of an innovative nature to promote combined transport.(2)

Question no 37 by Pat the Cope Gallagher (H-0421/99)
 Subject: End of duty­free
 

Following the ending of duty-free on 30 June 1999, will the Commission outline how the EU will assist Irish airports, ports, airlines and shipping companies to overcome the loss of essential income and will the Commission further outline how it intends to safeguard the consumer from higher travel fares and guarantee employment?

 
  
 

In November 1998 the Commission issued a call to the Member States to make full use of the possibilities provided by existing Community instruments to alleviate any economic difficulties resulting from the abolition of duty-free sales between Member States.

It also confirmed this position in its communication of 17 February 1999 to the Council and the European Parliament concerning the employment aspects of the decision to abolish tax- and duty-free sales for intra-Community travellers.

Since the spring of 1999, neither the Council nor the European Parliament has expressed any desire to consider, together with the Commission, the approach set out in its February communication.

According to information in the Commission's possession, operators in the duty-free sales sector adjusted their commercial policies (notably with regard to prices) with effect from 1 July 1999, to cope with the abolition of such sales within the Community. The Commission has not been informed of any difficulties in this connection. Furthermore, it considers that this issue is a matter of commercial strategy; it is in this commercial context that the question of maintaining income arises.

Where fares are concerned, the Commission has not so far become aware of fare increases linked to the abolition of intra-Community duty-free sales. The Commission will keep a close watch on this question, in the interests of consumers. Finally, it wishes to state that, as a result of the technical co-operation established between the Commission, national tax authorities and operators, the application of normal Community rules on tax and duty by this sector has not given rise to any problems since 1 July 1999.

 

Question no 38 by Niall Andrews (H-0423/99)
 Subject: Commission communication on anti­personnel landmines
 

Will the Commission indicate when it is likely to submit its communication recommending enhancement of the EU's capacity to embrace an integrated and co-ordinated approach in the fight against anti-personnel landmines (APL), taking into account the situation in Kosovo and the fact that children are particularly vulnerable to landmines, and will the Commission strongly support the view that humanitarian mine clearance be made a standard part of peace agreements?

 
  
 

The Commission attaches great importance to the fight against anti-personnel landmines.

In line with its 1999 Work Programme, the Commission foresees to submit a draft Council regulation on Action against Anti-Personnel Landmines before the end of 1999. The regulation aims to improve the coherence and consistency of demining activities and their reinforced co-ordination at Commission, EU and international levels. The regulation will be accompanied by a Communication to the European Parliament and Council, summarising the mine actions of the EU to date and outlining future policies.

As regards developments in Kosovo, international co-operation and co-ordination on humanitarian demining worked very well. The UN Mine Action Service as lead actor was unanimously commended by donors and NGOs for its effective, swift and constructive performance. The Commission wants to draw the conclusions from this positive experience for the future, in view of further fostering international co-operation and co-ordination in this important field.

Mine awareness and prevention of accidents have been emphasised in the recent past. Children are in fact a core target group of this action. While results have been very good in Kosovo, it turned out that a rather high share of casualties was caused by unexploded ordnances. The Commission reorients its mine awareness activities to take account of this fact.

The Commission supports the integration of humanitarian mine clearance into peace agreements, provided anti-personnel landmines had been deployed in the specific context. However, the Commission is only one of the actors contributing to EU second pillar polities. It should also be recalled that there is the Ottawa Treaty which bans APL completely. The Commission works towards universal adherence to the Treaty and its full compliance.

 

Question no 39 by Brian Crowley (H-0425/99)
 Subject: Millennium bug
 

In view of the dangers posed by the millennium bug and possible inadequate preparation at national, EU and international level, will the Commission provide an up-to-date assessment of the switch-over plans in the EU and countries outside the EU?

 
  
 

The “Millennium Bug” issue continues to be an important concern for the Commission during 1999. The main focus is on cross-border and cross-sector issues.

As requested by the recent European Council in Cologne, a European Union High Level Working Party on the Millennium Date Change was established by the Commission in July 1999. It will meet monthly during the remainder of this year to address various aspects of the problem at Community level, including the co-ordination of contingency plans for the rollover period between countries.

Moreover, the Commission has convened a meeting of representatives of the public infrastructure providers from the Member States to examine the situation regarding cross-border dependencies in April 1999. A follow-up event to assess the current state of progress and planning will take place at the end of September 1999.

In order to encourage the co-ordination of activities in this domain at international level, the Commission also participates in the activities of the G8 Y2K Expert Group and the International Y2K Co-operation Centre, as well as carrying out liaison with various international sectoral organisations.

Nevertheless, it should be noted that the role of the Commission is essentially to facilitate the exchange of information. As the responsibility for national preparedness lies with each Member State, it would not be possible or appropriate for the Commission to evaluate the degree of preparedness at national level, nor to assess the adequacy of country contingency plans.

 

Question no 40 by (Jim) James Fitzsimons (H-0427/99)
 Subject: 'People carriers' which failed frontal and lateral impact tests
 

At the end of June 1999 the results of frontal and side impact crash tests which were carried out on eight 'people carrier' vehicles as part of the European New Car Assessment Programme were published. Only two 'people carriers' received a four-star rating for their performance.

Does the Commission share my concern about comments that many of the cars tested did not provide a satisfactory safety performance in frontal collisions and, if so, does the Commission believe that no new car should be allowed on the market if it has not passed verifiable frontal and lateral crash tests?

 
  
 

The Commission is aware of the results of the tests carried out as part of the European New Car Assessment Programme (EuroNCAP); moreover, it supported them and they received a financial contribution from it.

The tests were developed independently of, but parallel to, European Parliament and Council Directives 96/79/EC and 96/27/EC on the protection of the occupants of motor vehicles in the event of a frontal impact and a side impact, respectively.

The test specifications and biomechanical criteria laid down in the two directives, which determine the protection provided by vehicles, were developed within the European Enhanced-Safety Vehicle Committee (EEVC), an organisation which brings together the road safety research organisations of the major car-producing Member States.

It is acknowledged internationally that the two directives constitute the most advanced legislation in this field, in terms of both its scientific basis and the reproducibility and repeatability of the tests, partly as a result of the input provided by the European Parliament under the co-decision procedure.

Since 1 October 1998 new types of car may not be placed on the market unless they satisfy the requirements laid down by the Community legislation in this field, which is in itself a major step forward in passenger safety.

The tests carried out under EuroNCAP use precisely the same specifications as Directive 96/27/EC concerning side impact. Where frontal impact is concerned, Directive 96/79/EC is followed in its entirety, except with regard to the test speed: this is set at 56 kph in the European legislation and 64 kph in the EuroNCAP tests.

The results of the tests are indicative, and their main purpose is to provide consumers with independent information. Such information, while it cannot have the force of law, for obvious reasons, will nonetheless be assessed, in the same way as that from other sources, as part of the procedure for adjusting to technical progress which is provided for by the directives.

Indeed, this question has been the subject of in-depth discussion for some time now at all levels in the Council and was discussed at the last Fisheries Council on 10 June 1999.

On that occasion, the Council reiterated the importance of bilateral fisheries relations with Morocco in the context of our global political and economic relations with that country. It also confirmed the socio-economic dimension for certain fisheries-dependent regions of the Community of a continuing substantive fisheries relationship with Morocco beyond the expiry of the current agreement. Against this background it urged the Commission to undertake as soon and as far as possible with Morocco the joint identification and elaboration of a new, mutually beneficial co-operative mechanism in the fisheries sector.

Finally, the Commission was to report to the Council on the outcome of these talks and to make any appropriate proposals.

The Council understands that the Commission has been exploring, internally, on an on-going basis, possible wider avenues of co-operation beyond the present agreement with Morocco with a view to presenting a recommendation for a negotiating mandate to Council; it has also been making efforts to set up talks with Morocco. However, the recent death of the King of Morocco has affected the administration in Morocco and a meeting planned for mid September 1999 has been deferred.

The Council, of course, will act with all due speed when the Commission comes forward with a Recommendation to negotiate any future arrangements between the European Community and Morocco in the fisheries sector.

In accordance with its conclusions, the Council will also consider any other proposals which the Commission may deem appropriate in this context.

 

Question no 25 by Esko Olavi Seppänen (H-0455/99)
 Subject: Ex-Commissioner Bangemann's pension
 

Commissioner Bangemann has announced that he is moving to work for a Spanish company before the term of office of the Commission has even finished. What EU-approved document authorises a Commissioner to be paid a special temporary allowance for 3 years even if he or she accepts a post and a salary from another activity, and what does the Council intend to do to put an end to this iniquitous practice, both in general and in Commissioner Bangemann’s specific case?

 
  
 

Article 7 of Regulation 422/67/EEC, 5/67 Euratom of the Council of 25 July 1967 determining the emoluments of the President and members of the Commission and of the President, Judges, Advocates-General and Registrar of the Court of Justice(1) provides that for three years after having ceased to hold office, a former member of the Commission shall receive a monthly transitional allowance of 40 to 65% (according to the duration of his service) of the basic salary which he was receiving when he ceased to hold office. Entitlement to this allowance shall cease if the former member of the Commission is reappointed to office in one of the institutions of the Communities.

If, during this three-year period, the person concerned takes up any new gainful activity, the amount by which his gross monthly remuneration (i.e. before deduction of taxes) together with the transitional allowance exceeds the remuneration (before deduction of taxes) which he was receiving as an active member of the Commission shall be deducted from the allowance. In calculating the amount of remuneration received for the new activity, all forms of remuneration shall be included, except those representing reimbursement of expenses.

Pursuant to Article 13 of the above-mentioned regulation, the transitional allowance provided for in Article 7 and any pension to which the person concerned is entitled by virtue of Articles 8 and 10 of this regulation shall not be drawn concurrently by the same person. In such a case, only that provision which is the most favourable to the claimant shall be applied.

For the time being, the Council is not considering amending these provisions.

As regards Mr Bangemann, it is recalled that on 9 July 1999 the Council decided to refer this case to the Court of Justice of the European Communities pursuant to the last sentence of the third subparagraph of Article 213(2) of the Treaty establishing the European Community and the corresponding provisions of the ECSC and EAEC Treaties(2). Pursuant to this article, the members of the Commission are particularly obliged to behave with integrity and discretion as regards the acceptance, after they have ceased to hold office, of certain appointments of benefits. In the event of any breach of these obligations, the Court of Justice may, on application by the Council or the Commission, rule that the Member concerned be deprived of his right to a pension or other benefits in its stead.

The Council’s Application in that sense was entered in the Register of the Court of Justice on 3 August 1999 as case C-290/99.

 
 

(1) OJ L 187 of 8 August 1967, p. 1
(2) Council Decision 1999/494/EC, ECSC, Euratom of 9 July 1999 on the referral of the case of Mr Bangemann to the Court of Justice, OJ L 192 of 24 July 1999, p. 55

 

Question no 26 by Richard Howitt (H-0460/99)
 Subject: Human Rights in Peru
 

Following the 19 July Council demarche on the decision of the Peruvian government and its Congress to withdraw from the jurisdiction of the Inter-American Court of Human Rights, what further action will the Council take?

 
  
 

As a continuation to the Council demarche of 19 July relating to the Peruvian Government and its Congress’ withdrawal from the jurisdiction of the Inter-American Court of Human Rights the Council is following closely the situation of human rights in Peru and all further action by the Peruvian Government in this field.

On 19 July, as the Honourable Member may know, the EU has shown concern for the effects of this decision on the weakening of the regional system of human rights and stressed the fact that Peruvian citizens will be left without the opportunity, provided by Peru’s constitution, of recourse to an international court in case their basic rights are violated.

Among other points, the EU has also recalled paragraph 12 of the Rio Summit Declaration, signed on 29 June 1999 by the Heads of State of the European Union and Latin America and the Caribbean which emphasises the implementation of universal and regional human rights instruments and standards.

Peru’s Deputy acting Foreign Minister recognised before the Troika that this action weakens the regional supervision of human rights. Peru is apparently seeking a review of the Inter-American judicial system and, once that is completed, would consider again acceptance of the Court’s jurisdiction.

The EU position has nonetheless received positive comments in public from the President of the Inter-American Court, Peru’s independent media, the Conference of Bishops, various experts on international law including several ex-Ministers of Foreign Affairs, as well as Peru’s Ombudsman in his comprehensive report. On the other hand, although it is apparent that other countries in the hemisphere do not support Peru’s mode of action, they seem unwilling to enter into direct confrontation with Peru.
