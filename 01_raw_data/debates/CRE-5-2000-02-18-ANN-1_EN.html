Question no 13 by Dana Rosemary Scallon (H-0084/00)
 Subject: Use of bovine growth hormone (BST)
 

At present, there is a ban on the use of the bovine growth hormone (BST) within the European Union. However, there is a loophole regarding the importation of milk and milk products produced from cows given this hormone.

How soon can the proposed Authority deal with this issue?

Joint Answer

The Council has decided to provide a joint answer to the questions by Mr Seppänen, Mrs Izquierdo Rojo and Mrs Scallon, as all three concern food safety and the establishment of a European Food Authority.

1. In reply to the questions by Mr Seppänen and Mrs Izquierdo Rojo, the Council wishes to stress that, following the Commission's submission of its white paper on food safety, a wide-ranging debate has been taking place on the Commission's suggestions concerning a European Food Authority. The principle of such an authority has been warmly welcomed by the Member States. The Commission is preparing a timetable under which it is hoped that the Authority will be set up in 2002. Mr Seppänen will no doubt agree with me that it would at this stage be premature to speculate on a geographical location for this new Authority.

2. Following on from answer No 1 and in reply to Mrs Scallon, the Council considers that the European Food Authority, once set up, would certainly be competent to deal with matters related to the impact of growth hormones on milk and dairy products. On 17 December 1999, the Council adopted a decision to prohibit the marketing and administration of growth hormones for livestock, on the basis of scientific considerations related to animal health and welfare. As is stated in the final recital of that decision, it has been established, by the report of the Scientific Committee on Animal Health and Animal Welfare, that BST can adversely affect the health and welfare of animals.

Concerning the problems related to human health, the Commission, when it adopted the above decision, stressed that the Scientific Committee (Public Health) had, with respect to consumer risk assessment, drawn attention to the lack of available data, and that should scientific developments confirm the existence of the risk the Commission would submit suitable proposals to the Council with a view to ensuring appropriate risk management, which would also cover imported products.

In the present state of scientific knowledge, there is no justification for a ban on public health grounds on imported dairy products obtained from BST-treated cattle. Such a ban would not be compatible with the Community's international obligations.

 
 

Question no 14 by Nuala Ahern (H-0058/00)
 Subject: OSPAR agreement, discharge at sea of radioactive and other toxic wastes
 

Following the meeting of the parties to OSPAR held in Sintra, Portugal, in July 1998, what new initiatives does the Presidency intend to take to strengthen the OSPAR agreement on limiting the discharge at sea of radioactive and other toxic wastes?

 
  
 

The question of the Honourable Member apparently refers to the text of the proposed water framework Directive which is currently being discussed in its second reading.

The European Parliament would like to modify the text of the Council’s common position (OR) to emphasise the importance of radioactive waste by modifying the Council’s common position, i.e. to include the OSPAR commitments as an aim in Article 1 and as a binding objective in Article 4. The Commission supports the relevant European Parliament amendments.

The Council is currently discussing these proposals. It is the strong wish of the Presidency that the Institutions will find a way to reconcile their views in this contentious issue.

 

Question no 15 by Gerard Collins (H-0061/00)
 Subject: Information technology and regional policy
 

Presenting its programme to Parliament last month, the Portuguese Presidency spoke of its aim to launch an IT modernisation programme for Europe so as to allow the EU to complete in the global economy. Can the Presidency say what efforts it will make to put regional policy at the centre of this programme, in other words to begin by narrowing the gap in technology that currently exists within the UE? What concrete proposals will the Presidency be making to ensure that the peripheral regions of the Community are given priority in any plans to create a more democratic and widespread access to new technologies, thus compensating, in some measure, for their disadvantage?

 
  
 

Modern information technology makes an important contribution towards eliminating the disadvantages of peripherally located regions. The Fifth Framework Programme for Research, Technological Development and Demonstration of the European Community therefore puts a distinct emphasis on the development of the necessary technologies: Euro 3.6 billion have been allocated to its Specific Programme ‘User-friendly Information Society’, during the period 1998-2002. This programme is closely co-ordinated with activities supported by the Structural Funds.

The Portuguese Presidency of the Council has given full consideration to the regional dimension of the development of the innovation and knowledge society. The Special European Council of 23-24 March in Lisbon, ‘Employment, economic reforms and social cohesion – for a Europe of innovation and knowledge’ is called upon to define, at the highest level, a new strategy for growth, competitiveness and employment. Its key question is how to develop a society of innovation and knowledge with more and better employment perspectives and increased social cohesion as between the different regions of the European Union. The preparatory paper submitted by the Presidency contains the blueprint for an ambitious but realistic approach to the challenge of the innovation and knowledge society. By reinforcing RTD activities, enabling broad access to Internet, speeding up the construction of trans-European broadband telecommunication networks and ensuring the wide dissemination of the necessary information technology skills through education and training of the population, it aims to remove most of the remaining problems, particularly prevalent in the peripheral regions, with regard to access to innovative technology and information.

The Commission submitted its Communication ‘Europe, An Information Society for All’ on 17 December 1999; this will also be discussed at the Special European Council. This document calls for an ‘Europe’ initiative with the ambitious aim of bringing everyone in Europe – every citizen, every school, every company – online as quickly as possible. Both the Fifth Framework Programme and the Regional Funds are called upon are called upon to contribute to financing the efforts required.

The Presidency further intends to initiate a political debate on the Commission Communication ‘Towards a European Research Area’ at the informal meeting of Research Ministers on 6-7 March, with further, more formal discussions taking place at the Research Council on 15 June. The Commission’s paper, which sets the pattern for future thinking on the European Union’s RTD policy, suggests strengthening considerably the role of the regions in research and training in science and technology as well as creating centres of excellence and enabling the transfer of knowledge to the economic sector at regional and local level.

Question no 17 by Gorka Knörr Borràs (H-0072/00):
 Subject: Involvement of parliaments in the European Union in the intergovernmental conference
 

Past errors should serve to ensure that the principles of transparency and involvement become the bases for the method applied to the next reform of the Treaties. In addition to the national parliaments of the Member States, whose involvement is required because, in the final analysis, it is they who must ratify the decisions taken at the intergovernmental conference, there exist within the European Union political bodies below the state level which enjoy direct, and in some instances exclusive, competence in many fields within the Community sphere.

Does the Council not take the view that suitable involvement for parliaments operating below the state level in the process of reforming the Treaties would be a means of gaining wider acceptance for such reform amongst Europe’s citizens? Is it considering a proposal of any kind to increase the extent to which these parliaments are kept informed and consulted? Would it be prepared to examine specific proposals from the parliaments themselves?

 
  
 

The Council would like to remind the honourable Member that the process for amending the Treaties is laid down in Article 48 of the Treaty on European Union, which stipulates that the government of any Member State or the Commission may submit to the Council proposals for the amendment of the Treaties on which the Union is founded, that amendments to be made to the Treaties are adopted by common accord in a conference of representatives of the governments of the Member States, and that the amendments shall enter into force after being ratified by all the Member States in accordance with their respective constitutional requirements.

The Council is aware of the importance of the opinion of political bodies below state level in discussions on amendments to the Treaty, but it is bound to recall that as the law stands, these bodies can only play a full role through national governments, in accordance with the constitutional requirements of each Member State.

With regard to information, political bodies below state level will be kept informed of discussions in the conference by their respective governments, in accordance with the processes applying at national level. Bodies below state level in each Member State will in this way be able to benefit from information coming direct from the conference, which has taken certain measures to ensure that its work is as transparent as possible. Accordingly, all official documents from the conference, together with all documents from public or private bodies transmitted to it, will be made available to the public using the most rapid and efficient means of communication.

With regard to any proposals or suggestions emanating from political bodies below state level, it will be possible for these to be taken into account in the same way as proposals from other public or private bodies which are not directly party to the conference.

Finally, we hope that the Committee of the Regions’ debates and opinions on topics discussed by the conference will of course reflect the concerns and wishes of bodies below state level in the Member States in respect of general questions of concern to them.

 

Question no 18 by James (Jim) Fitzsimons (H-0073/00)
 Subject: Natural gas and the future of supplies after enlargement
 

Will the Council state to what extent it has considered the impact of enlargement on the future of supplies of natural gas and coal in the European Union and does it believe that the accession of new Member States will make a positive contribution to securing alternatives to nuclear energy or will it lead to an increased dependence on nuclear energy?

 
  
 

The Council considers that security of gas supply in the EU is of growing importance in view of the increasing EU dependence on energy imports. The priority of the Council attaches to this issue has been underlined in the Council conclusions of May 1996. Since then, the Commission has presented a Communication on EU Gas security of supply in November 1999, which the Council is examining with great attention under the Portuguese Presidency. The conclusions which will be presented to the Energy Council in May 2000, will take into account all aspects of security of supply, including the impact of enlargement.

As concerns the ongoing enlargement negotiations, specific attention is being given to nuclear issues. On 7 December 1998, the Council adopted conclusions on nuclear safety in the context of the enlargement of the European Union which give a clear message to the candidate countries. In particular, they emphasise the necessity of the earliest practicable closure of reactors which cannot be upgraded to international accepted levels of safety at a reasonable cost.

The Council is convinced that the accession of new Member States will contribute positively to the achievement of the objectives of energy policy: security of supply, competitiveness and protection of the environment. Furthermore, the Council recalls that the enhancement of the external dimension, including the enlargement process, is part of the strategy for integrating environmental aspects into energy policy that was submitted to the European Council in Helsinki in December 1999.

In this strategy, the Council sets a high priority on the promotion of new and renewable sources of energy, which will contribute to an increased use of environment-friendly renewable energy sources and thereby lead to diversification of energy sources.

 

Question no 19 by Niall Andrews (H-0075/00)
 Subject: Cuba
 

In answer to my earlier question on Cuba and the development of EU relations (H-1147/98),(1) the Council reconfirmed the strong desire of the European Union to act as Cuba’s partner with a view to the progressive and irreversible opening of the Cuban economy. It nevertheless considered that full cooperation with Cuba would depend on an improvement of the situation regarding human rights and fundamental freedoms. Will the Council now state what its current position is with regard to EU/Cuba relations?

 
  
 

Last November the Council made the sixth evaluation of the EU Common position on Cuba (adopted by the Council on 2 December 1996). The Council concluded that there have not been changes in the conditions in the country that would justify a modification of the EU’s approach towards Cuba. Furthermore, the Council considered that the Common Position offers enough flexibility to the Union and its Members to react if developments in Cuba were to give sufficient reasons for changing the strategy.

The EU objective towards Cuba remains, as stated in the Common Position, to encourage a process of transition to pluralistic democracy and respect for human rights and fundamental freedoms, as well as sustainable economic recovery and improvement in the living standards of the Cuban people. The Council has underlined that, while continuing to vigorously promote the fundamental principles of EU policy as stated above, result-oriented dialogue and humanitarian aid, not isolation, are EU priorities towards Cuba.

Question no 20 by Brian Crowley (H-0077/00)
 Subject: Social policy and older workers
 

Will the President-in-Office of the Council indicate to what extent it intends to ensure that older people who have reached retirement age and who so wish can continue to play an active role in society, including in the workplace, and will the Council actively support the right of older people to participate in training programmes including training in skills in new technologies?

 
  
 

One element in the Council decision on the employment policy guidelines for 2000 - approved in late 1999, and shortly to be adopted formally - specifically concerns improving senior citizens' chances of integration into the world of work and continuation in active life. This text expressly calls on the Member States to define and implement a policy for the prolongation of active life. The measures proposed include the preservation of working capacity, lifelong learning, and the introduction of flexible working formulas, to enable older workers to continue their professional life on a basis of active participation.

The guidelines for 2000 also stress the need to encourage a partnership approach, bringing together the Member States and the two sides of industry, with the aim of developing opportunities for lifelong learning, especially in the information technology and communications sectors and with particular stress on ease of access for older workers.

As you will certainly be aware, the implementation of the employment guidelines is a matter for the individual Member States. The Council, at all events, wishes to stress that it is fully committed to the notion that older workers have an active role to play on the labour market.

 

Question no 21 by Liam Hyland (H-0079/00)
 Subject: Employment in the services sector
 

The Commission has estimated that if Europe had the same level of employment in the service sector as the US, there would be more than 30 million additional jobs in Europe. How does the Council respond to this very positive projection for employment in the service sector and what initiatives does the Council consider need to be taken to translate this job potential into real jobs in Europe?

 
  
 

The decision on the employment policy guidelines for 2000 was approved by the Council in late 1999, and will be adopted formally within the next few weeks, following which it will be published in the Official Journal. This decision stresses the need for the Member States to take full advantage of all the opportunities offered them by the creation of new jobs. The Council especially emphasises the need for effective exploration of the whole range of employment possibilities created by the new technologies.

In this connection, the Member States will be expected to implement a reference framework targeted on maximisation of the job potential of the services sector, especially industry-related services. This is particularly relevant in relation to the information society and the environmental sector - an especially interesting source of new jobs whose potential should be vigorously explored (also with a view to developing job quality).

As you will certainly be aware, the implementation of the employment guidelines is a matter for the individual Member States. The Council wishes, at all events, to stress that it places great hopes in the services sector in the context of boosting employment levels.

The Council further wishes to stress that on 17 December 1999, jointly with the representatives of the Member State governments, it adopted a resolution on the potential for job creation and the social dimension of the information society. In this resolution, the Council called on the Member States to take maximum advantage of this sector's major job-creating potential as part of the fight against unemployment, which is, without any doubt, one of the greatest challenges facing the Union at the dawn of the third millennium.

 

Question no 22 by Mihail Papayannakis (H-0083/00)
 Subject: The 'Monti Package' on taxation
 

Will the Council say exactly what progress has been made on the 'Monti Package' on taxation which seeks on the one hand to curb tax evasion and on the other to pave the way for the harmonisation of taxation between the Member States?

Does the Council agree that there must be equal treatment for all the Member States on such a serious matter as the taxation of savings, and will it say what initiatives it is taking to achieve agreement on this controversial subject?

 
  
 

In recent month, the “tax package” has been the subject of heated debate in the Council and the various bodies concerned with this dossier. This applies both to technical meetings and high-level meetings. These debates have revolved around both technical issues and issues of principle. Whilst enormous progress has been made, especially with regard to the Code of Conduct on business taxation and the Directive on the taxation of interest and royalty payments, it was not possible to agree conclusions on the three elements of the “tax package” before the Helsinki European Council on 10 and 11 December 1999.

In view of this, the “tax package” dossier was presented to the European Council which, in its conclusions, requested the Council to establish a high-level working group with the remit of finding solutions to the present problems, in particular with regard to the Directive on the taxation of savings.

On 31 January 2000, by way of follow-up to the above-mentioned conclusions of the European Council, the Council set up a high-level group on taxation. This group is to present to the Council an overall report on possible solutions to outstanding problems as well as on the Code of Conduct on business taxation and the Directive on the taxation of interest and royalty payments. This group is due to meet for the first time in February 2000, and the Council will in turn present a report to the European Council by June 2000 at the latest.

Question no 24 by Olivier Dupuis (H-0093/00)
 Subject: Situation in Syria and association agreement
 

In the negotiations with a view to the conclusion of an association agreement with Syria – which includes human rights clauses – has the Council made the lifting of the state of emergency that has been in force in the country for 38 years and respect for the rights and guarantees enshrined in the suspended Syrian constitution a precondition? In the same context, what steps has the Council taken or does it intend to take to secure the release of the some 1700 prisoners of conscience still held in Syrian prisons? What measures has the Council taken or does it intend to take to persuade the Syrian authorities to abandon the practice of torture and improve conditions in Syrian prisons significantly. Furthermore, what steps has it taken or does it intend to take to ensure that the competent Syrian authorities legally recognise the Committee for the Defence of Democratic Freedoms and Human Rights in Syria (CDF)? Finally, has the Council made provision, in the draft association agreement, for instruments and arrangements that will allow continuous and independent monitoring of the human rights situation in Syria?

 
  
 

1. The Council is aware that the human rights situation in Syria is not acceptable, despite the progress made in recent years. The Syrian Government signed the Barcelona Declaration in 1995, in which it undertook to respect human rights and fundamental freedoms. More recently, in 1998 and 1999, Syria has freed hundreds political prisoners with long sentences, including Lebanese citizens. The Council is pleased that these prisoners have been freed, but remains concerned about the remaining prisoners of conscience, about reports of torture, the continuation of the state of emergency and the absence of freedom of expression. This means that on reaching personable age a person entitled to a pension can no longer transfer his or her capital to a different insurance company, since there is no question of a change of employment. The most important reason for this provision in the PSW is to prevent the flight of capital abroad.

Does the Commission agree that this has the effect of distorting competition since the pensioner can no longer choose, on reaching pensionable age, the company which offers the most favourable annuity for the capital accumulated?

Is this provision in contravention of the rules of competition on the Internal Market, in particular with regard to the free movement of services and capital? If so, what does the Commission intend to do?

 
  
 

The question of the Honourable MEP refers to a law that entered into force in the Netherlands on 1 January of this year. Additional time will be needed in order to enable the Commission to assess the impact of the law on Community provisions. At this stage, only the following preliminary remarks can be made.

Two questions are in fact being asked. Does this provision distort competition? Does it infringe Single Market rules with regard to the free movement of services and capital?

As regards competition, European rules only apply (except the state aid rules) to anti-competitive behaviour of undertakings – i.e. entities that exercise an economic activity. The prohibition at stake is laid down by the Dutch State in the Dutch pensions and savings funds act. It follows that, even though the prohibition may possibly distort competition, the European competition rules do not appear to apply. It is only in case the Dutch State, by adopting the prohibition, has been involved in anti-competitive agreements, decisions or concerted practices or undertakings, that Article 3, 10 and 81 of the treaty may apply.

From a single market perspective, if the law prohibits the transfer to both domestic and foreign providers, there would apparently be no discrimination on the basis of nationality. Infringement of the free movement of services principle would seem difficult to prove.

As the provision prohibits residents to move the capital accumulated from one country to another, one could argue that the application of the free movement of capital principle is hampered. Such restriction would have to be justified by one of the general exceptions of the EC Treaty applicable to the freedom of capital movements, and in particular, could possibly be based on Article 58 which allows Member States ‘to take all requisite measures to prevent infringement of national law and regulations, in particular in the field of taxation and prudential supervision of financial institutions’.

The matter will be examined further, the Honourable Member will be kept informed of any action the Commission could launch in this regard.

The question is about cross-border credit transfers in euros: the Honourable Member is surprised at the fact that, given the same initial amount, the recipient may receive an amount which differs from one Member State to another and sometimes even from one bank to another.

Granting and maintaining an exclusive right for the performance of certain services could be contrary to Community law, in particular the rules on the freedom of establishment of the free movement of services, as undertakings from other Member States are excluded from establishing themselves in the Member State concerned and providing the same services. According to the case law of the European Court of Justice, a monopoly can be justified by imperative requirements in the public interest or to the extent it is necessary for the necessary for the performance of a public service obligation in the sense of Article 86(2) (ex article 90) EC Treaty. No are they hindered or obliged to obtain or re-obtain ownership in private or privatised undertakings thus turning or returning them into public undertakings. However, these rules have to be distinguished from the separate issue discussed above, i.e. whether services of general economic interest can – possibly subject to some regulation - be ensured by undertakings competing with each other, no matter whether they are private, privatised or public undertakings.

Question no 112 by Richard Howitt (H-0133/00)
 Subject: Leader
 

Does the Commission agree that allowing only proposals involving population coverage less than

100 000 in the new Community initiative LEADER does not allow enough flexibility and scope for the rural areas, such as my constituency in the east of England, to benefit from past experience and success by targeting thematically over a wider area rather than concentrating all resources on a smaller area where only some would benefit because overall population has to be less than 100 000?

 
  
 

The Commission is indeed proposing in its draft guidelines for the new Community Initiative for rural development for the period 2000-2006, LEADER+, to target small-scale local rural areas, with a population which must, as a general rule, not be more than 100 000.

A similar maximum population ceiling was used under LEADER 1 and II. The Commission considers that it is essential in order to secure the local character of those rural areas selected for assistance and also for the encouragement of active community based partnerships, both features which have been central to the success of LEADER in identifying new and innovative approaches to the development of rural areas, which may serve as a model for other areas. Question no 113 by Konstantinos Alyssandrakis (H-0135/00)
 Subject: Questions and concerns raised by the issue of a Community directive on combating fraud
 

The content of the forthcoming new Community directive to combat the illicit squandering of Community funds and subsidies and, more specifically, the decision, currently under consideration, to grant immunity from all criminal or civil liability to businessmen, estate agents, lawyers and notaries who inform the official EU bodies of the illicit use of Community funds and subsidies raises serious questions and concerns.

Does the Commission not consider that a directive such as this, in the name of cracking down on fraud, will create a ‘special category of specialist-informers’ and that it will open up a Pandora’s box leading to mud-slinging in all the Member States by setting up a mechanism whereby anyone who receives the protection of immunity may make unsubstantiated accusations against anyone else?

 
  
 

The Commission interprets the Honourable Member’s question as referring to its proposed amendment to Directive 91/308/EEC on preventing the use of the financial system for the purposes of money laundering(1).

This proposal(2) is a response to a range of concerns far wider than the aspects raised by the Honourable Member.

As regards the specific question of the financial interests of the European Communities, the proposal extends the definition of criminal activity given in Article 1 of the directive to fraud, corruption or any other illegal activity damaging or likely to be damaging to these interests.

Extending the terms of the directive, as envisaged by the proposal, to professions and categories of business other than financial establishments involved in activities likely to be used for the purposes of money laundering, has a more general scope than the laundering of gains from fraud and other illegal activities harmful to the financial interests of the Communities.

When the financial interests of the Communities are involved, exchanges of specific information are envisaged by Article 12 of the proposal. They concern suspicious transactions and involve both the national authorities responsible for combating money laundering as well as the Commission. The Commission stresses the fact that only these national authorities have any responsibility in the matter, so as to comply with the obligation of confidentiality and the imitations upon the jurisdiction of the Commission.

Ultimately, the proposed amendment to the directive does not entail natural or legal persons informing the Commission of suspicions of fraud against the Community budget.

Question no 114 by Inger Schörling (H-0136/00)
 Subject: Marking of bovine animals
 

Two Swedish oxen are at risk of being put down since the Swedish National Board of Agriculture refused to grant an exemption for them from the EU Directive on ear tags for bovine animals. The oxen are used for events on a nature reserve where farming practices of yesteryear are demonstrated.

The National Board of Agriculture points out that the EU allows exemptions from the requirement to apply plastic ear tags to cattle only in respect of animals intended for use in cultural and sports events (bullfights).

Does the Commission consider that exemptions should be granted for animal-baiting, as in the case of bullfighting, but not for the use of oxen in cultural events on a museum farm?

Does the Commission not have a duty to ensure that EU rules do not give rise to absurd situations?

 
  
 

At the present time, Council Regulation (EC) No 820/97 establishing a system for the identification and registration of bovine animals and on the labelling of beef and veal and beef-based products does not afford the Commission the possibility of approving an identification system offering the same guarantees as those resulting from ear-tagging except for bulls intended for cultural and sports events. To this end, the Commission has adopted regulation (EC) No 2680/1999 of 17 December 1999 approving an identification system for bulls intended for cultural and sports events (See OJ L326, 18.12.99, p. 16).

Subsequent to establishing the elements of the system for the identification of bovine animals, the Commission has been made aware that the application of ear-tags in compliance with Community requirements may prove to be unsightly in some situations such as, for example, museum farms. So in the proposed Parliament and Council regulation establishing a system for the identification and registration of bovine animals and on the labelling of meat and veal and beef-based products, abrogating regulation (EC) No 820/97 (COM (1999) 487), the Commission no longer refers exclusively to bulls, but in general to bovine animals intended for cultural or sports events (Article 4(1), final sentence). It must however be stressed that, should the Parliament and the Council accept this wording, it will be up to the Swedish authorities, if they deem it necessary, to submit a request to the Commission with a view to the recognition of an alternative system offering equivalent guarantees for the type of situation described by the Honourable Member.
