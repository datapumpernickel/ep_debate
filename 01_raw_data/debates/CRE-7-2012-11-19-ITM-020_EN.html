Monday, 19 November 2012 - Strasbourg OJ edition

20. One-minute speeches (Rule 150)
Video of the speeches
PV
 

  President. − The next item is the one-minute speeches.

 
  
 

  Anna Záborská (PPE). – (SK) Mr President, Slovakia is preparing to issue a memorial coin representing Saints Constantine and Methodius, Christian missionaries who brought faith – and the first writing – to our ancestors 1 150 years ago. However, after intervention by the European institutions, the National Bank of Slovakia has decided to remove crosses and haloes from the artistic design. The attempt to remove everything Christian from public life has also been demonstrated in the attacks by Members of this Parliament on Commissioner-designate Tonio Borg for his personal values and attitudes as a practising Christian. Questioning Europe’s Christian heritage harms European integration, and forcing Christians out of public life tramples on the freedom of thought, conscience and religion guaranteed by the European Charter of Human Rights.

 
  
 

  Monika Flašíková Beňová (S&D). – (SK) Mr President, in the Member States of the European Union, recent years have been dominated by the crushing effects of economic and financial crisis, which have had particularly destructive social impacts.

Citizens constantly hear from politicians about savings measures, including in national budgets, whether in the context of state budgets or our common European budget. Earmarking financial resources to rescue the banking sector or financial institutions or to contribute to the European Financial Stability Facility helps indebted countries. However, citizens hear little or almost nothing about how their desperate social situation is to be tackled. Even today, in the Member States of the European Union, especially in most of those that joined the European Union in 2004, more and more people continue to fall below the poverty threshold. Wages in our countries are not comparable with wages in the developed economies, and therefore I think that we too – at European Parliament level, in the European Commission and the Council – should start to talk more about the fact that our fellow citizens are living in such a difficult social situation and should also seek solutions in this sensitive area.

 
  
 

  Tatjana Ždanoka (Verts/ALE). – Mr President, I would like to speak about what took place in Latvia on 1 November 2012 when the Central Election Commission decided to reject the application for a referendum aimed at solving the mass statelessness problem in Latvia. It was the first case in more than twenty years of Latvia’s history when a draft law supported by more than 10 000 signatures, certified by notaries, was not officially submitted to the next stage as a state-organised collection of signatures.

We regard this case as a threat to democracy in Latvia. The absence of official status for the Russian language and mass statelessness are a cornerstone problem for the Russian speakers in Latvia and I invite those colleagues who want to learn more about this issue to attend the hearings of the Intergroup for Traditional Minorities which take place this Thursday here in Parliament before the vote.

 
  
 

  Kinga Gál (PPE). – (HU) Mr President, ladies and gentlemen, I welcome the decision for the EU to use the sum from the Nobel Peace Prize to support children suffering as a result of war or other armed conflict. There has been an increase in child poverty and violence against children worldwide as a consequence of such conflicts, and we must fight them with all the means at our disposal.

Nevertheless, I would like to draw your attention to another phenomenon that is a serious problem here in the Union and which affects our young people day after day. We often hear the expression ‘lost generation’ owing to the large proportion of youth unemployment, so it is important to create as many governmental programmes as possible to improve the youth employment situation, and to establish forums where young people themselves can express their opinions to decision-makers. I am therefore pleased that this has finally started in Hungary, under the ‘Új nemzedék’ [‘New Generation’] programme. Every young person has a talent within them that is waiting to be discovered and nurtured, and through this they will be able to develop skills that could increase competitiveness in the labour market. I would like to contribute to this personally with a written statement on the nurturing of talent, which has started this November. I hope you will support it.

 
  
 

  María Irigoyen Pérez (S&D). – (ES) Mr President, Ms Reding, Europe has shown huge potential in terms of economic growth and social cohesion, but its mission is not purely economic: the protection of citizens and the right to housing, especially in the case of the more vulnerable groups, or in emergency situations, should be a priority for the European programme and in its legislation.

Thousands of families in Europe are losing their homes, especially in Spain, as a result of the grave economic and financial crisis. We frequently hear of citizens who take their own life rather than face eviction from their home. For this reason, we need to take urgent action and ensure that what is happening now does not recur in the future: lifelong financial hardship.

On the basis of the principle of effective judicial protection, the Court of Justice of the European Union ruled that the Spanish Land Register Law infringes European law. Despite pressure from its citizens, the Spanish Government has not reacted.

I would therefore request the European institutions to ensure that Spain complies with the rights guaranteed under Council Directive 93/13/EEC.

 
  
 

  François Alfonsi (Verts/ALE). – (FR) Mr President, the European Parliament must comment on the events that have taken place between France and Spain but which relate to European democracy as a whole.

Aurore Martin is a Basque political activist with French nationality, resident in France, against whom Spain issued a European arrest warrant for offences linked to her membership of the Batasuna movement, which is banned in Spain because of alleged links to ETA. As such, Aurore Martin’s activities have been deemed to be linked to a terrorist organisation and are subject to heavy penalties in Spain. By contrast, Batasuna is a party that is authorised in France, and the political activities in question conducted by Ms Aurore Martin are completely legal there. She was recently arrested by the French police and immediately handed over to the Spanish police.

How can we allow the European Union, whose aim is to improve the freedoms of European citizens, to sanction distortion of the European arrest warrant procedure and reduce political freedoms in Europe to the lowest common denominator? How can we allow Spain and France, by acting in this manner, to endanger the peace process currently under way in the Basque Country?

 
  
 

  Marina Yannakoudakis (ECR). – Mr President, last week the speaker of Uganda’s Parliament announced that the country’s anti-gay bill would be passed before the end of 2012. She claims that homosexuals pose a serious threat to Uganda’s children and has promised that the law will be passed as a Christmas gift.

This is a terrible present for the Ugandan people at a time when we should be celebrating peace and goodwill to all men and women. I have spoken to this House about the brutal way in which homosexuals are treated in Uganda. The law, if passed, will allow gays and lesbians to be sentenced to life in prison.

I believe that this is a violation of human rights, and the EU must respond immediately by suspending its aid to Uganda. Forty per cent of all EU aid to the country is channelled through the general budget support, which means that the money is paid directly to the Ugandan Government.

The British Prime Minister, David Cameron, has promised that EU aid will have more strings attached. The EU needs strings of its own to protect LGBT people in third countries who are under threat of oppression.

 
  
 

  Alda Sousa (GUE/NGL). – (PT) Mr President, a massacre is currently taking place in Gaza. In just six days, Israel has broken the ceasefire agreement, bombed 80 targets and launched over 1 200 air attacks. An Israeli missile struck a house in a residential area and killed 11 civilians, including four children and an 81-year-old woman.

An Israeli surgical missile strike hit the offices of a Palestinian newspaper to block reports of the massacre, injuring 10 journalists. Yesterday, an Israeli missile hit the home of the al-Dalu family, immediately exterminating nine Palestinians from four generations. In only six days, 81 people have been killed and 535 have been injured, including 520 civilians. They claim that it is an intelligent defensive war. War is never surgical, let alone intelligent.

The question is: what is this Europe, which has just received the Nobel Prize for Peace, going to do to end this barbarity?

 
  
 

  Martin Ehrenhauser (NI). – (DE) Mr President, allow me a moment to state a personal position. In April 2011, I introduced a statement alleging suspected misuse of tax funds of EUR 1.5 million by an Austrian MEP. In turn, this MEP accused me of hacking into his computer. The European Parliament subsequently waived my immunity on 23 October, but 10 days later the public prosecutor suspended the investigation. In other words, the MEP’s accusations have all come to nothing, the inquiry has been definitively closed, and I just wanted to mention this briefly.

 
  
 

  Petru Constantin Luhan (PPE). – Mr President, colorectal cancer is the most common cancer in Europe. Bearing in mind the guidelines for colorectal cancer screening from 2010, last week I hosted the conference which launched a White Paper setting the standards for the prevention, diagnosis, treatment and care of colorectal cancer across Europe.

With 450 000 new cases of this cancer being diagnosed every year in the EU, what struck me was the opportunity to reduce the number of new cases. Speakers confirmed the possibility of reducing new cases by 30 % by embracing a lifestyle of healthy food, exercise and not smoking. Screening can identify a further 30 %. Such initiatives could prevent about 270 000 new cases per year. I therefore ask the Commission to urge the implementation of a local primary health strategy, to embrace a healthy lifestyle, awareness campaigns and the early introduction of screening programmes across Europe so that we can meet our goals of healthy ageing by 2020.

 
  
 

  Françoise Castex (S&D). – (FR) Mr President, Commissioner, ladies and gentlemen, I too would like to draw your attention to the case of Aurore Martin, the French citizen arrested in Spain and handed over to the Spanish authorities who is serving a 12-year prison sentence. Her crime consists in having participated in Spain in meetings of the Batasuna party, a party that is illegal in Spain but legal in France where it even has some elected representatives. She is the subject of a European arrest warrant that has been enforced.

Although the establishment of the European arrest warrant in 2004 was greeted as the first step towards a European area of security and justice, today there are differences in its implementation that create inequalities between citizens of the European Union, and that is contrary to the very spirit of the framework decision establishing the European arrest warrant.

Moreover, where there are important political implications, as in the case of this Basque activist, shouldn’t the European arrest warrant include broader exceptions and not be issued automatically so as to provide a better guarantee of fundamental rights, including the freedom of expression, which has been prejudiced in the case of Aurore Martin?

 
  
 

  Jelko Kacin (ALDE). – (SL) Mr President, Slovenia has been hit by truly terrible floods. Fortunately there were no human fatalities in this unfortunate situation. There has been material damage on a huge scale, with thousands of residential buildings flooded out, many of them entirely destroyed. Landslides have carried away many sections of road, so the clean-up operation will be long and challenging. We have moved people, but the roads will take time.

The worst floods along the River Drava have shown that climate change is a reality to which we must adapt. This applies also to the dangers and problems of a chain of hydroelectric power stations. The wave of water threatened dams, so the flow had to be increased in Austria through additional emptying of storage dams. This unfortunately caused an overflowing of banks and flooding, major damage in Slovenia and additional threats below the dams.

We need better mechanisms for managing hydroelectric power stations in catastrophic weather, as well as proper cross-border material solidarity in dealing with the consequences and reimbursing damage. Without the European Union this is not possible, so for this reason we need the EU budget and solidarity mechanisms.

 
  
 

  Gerard Batten (EFD). – Mr President, I would like to comment on an article in today’s Daily Express by Leo McKinstry. He writes that the Tory Party should offer some kind of electoral deal to the UK Independence Party.

Now UKIP should never accept any such deal for the following reasons:

The Tories can never be trusted. They have betrayed Britain for over 40 years and they would betray anyone. If the Tories wanted to offer the British people a referendum on continued membership of the European Union, they could easily do so. They do not need to make any deal with UKIP.

If UKIP entered any such pact, it would destroy our credibility with the electorate. UKIP would end up in the same position as the Liberal Democrats, facing electoral annihilation, and the cause of Britain’s exit from the European Union would be lost forever.

UKIP must never compromise its campaign to restore Britain’s status as a sovereign, independent, self-governing democracy.

 
  
 

  Georgios Toussas (GUE/NGL). – (EL) Mr President, I requested the floor in order to speak on labour and social rights, but developments in the Middle East are extremely critical for the Palestinian people and for the peoples of the region in general. The Communist Party of Greece therefore wishes, from this podium, to express its condemnation of Israel’s military operations in the Gaza Strip, including the murder of 91 Palestinians, with 24 children and many women among the victims, and hundreds more injured. We demand an end to Israel’s murderous air raids in the Gaza Strip, and the withdrawal of Israeli occupation forces from the Palestinian territories. We support the struggle of the Palestinian people to create an independent, viable Palestinian state within the 1967 borders and with East Jerusalem as its capital. Ladies and gentlemen, the anti-popular policy of the EU monopolies and the governments of the Member States is aimed against the minimal labour and social rights remaining to the working class and the popular strata. It does away with the universality of the national general collective labour agreement, and now the minimum wages are set by law, by the governments. The results are mass unemployment, starvation wages and pensions, an increase in the pension age; destruction of state social security, health and welfare systems; poverty, misery and a living hell for the working class and the popular strata. Now is the time for a popular uprising. It is time to step up the fight against the monopolies, it is time to disengage from the European Union, time for unilateral debt cancellation for the debt-burdened countries. It is time for people’s power.

 
  
 

  Andreas Mölzer (NI). – (DE) Mr President, to my mind, the most important initiative of the EU’s neighbourhood policy is to maintain European aspirations, namely the accession hopes of the states of the Western Balkans – that cannot be said often enough. The recent reactions in the Croatian, but also the Serbian media to the acquittal of the Croatian general are proof that emotions, antagonistic emotions amongst the people of the Western Balkans, remain very strong and dangerous. The hope of accession, this European aspiration, is therefore especially meaningful for this region.

We must try to find answers to unresolved issues, over Kosovo for example, in such a way that they are acceptable to the peoples concerned, the states concerned. It will be especially important here not to overlook the historical sensibilities of the Serbs, the Serbian identity, if you will.

 
  
 

  Czesław Adam Siekierski (PPE). – (PL) Mr President, on 17 November 2012, the PSL, the Polish People’s Party, held its 11th Congress. The PSL is a party with an almost 120-year-old history. It arose in a context of opposition and the struggle for freedom and national independence, fighting to improve the living conditions of large numbers of Poles, mainly in the provinces and rural areas. The Polish people’s movement has always put human beings at the centre of its concerns: their living conditions, access to work and education, and equal rights for all.

Our movement was constantly involved in the struggle and campaign for independent Polish statehood. Poland achieved independence in 1918. In 1920, when the Red Army advanced on our country with the aim of conquering not only Poland but the whole of Europe, it was Poland that halted the dangerous westward march of the Soviet Army. The prime minister at the time was the famous populist activist Wincenty Witos, who was three times prime minister in the Polish Government. During the Second World War, the populist movement formed its own army, the Peasant Battalions, numbering over 160 000 troops. When Poland found itself, after the Second World War, in the Eastern bloc as a result of the Yalta agreements, the People’s Party fought for freedom and democracy under the leadership of Stanisław Mikołajczyk. Now, for the past five years, we have been active partners in a coalition government with the Civic Platform, and the newly elected authorities have announced that the PSL will continue to play an active part in the work of the European People’s Party and the Polish Parliament. Thank you very much

 
  
 

  Monika Smolková (S&D). – (SK) Mr President, in my speech, I want to express my full support for the right of every citizen of the European Union to submit a petition to the European Parliament.

The petition must be in the form of a complaint or request concerning questions that come under the remit of the European Union. This right should not be abused, as it has been in the case where a request has been made, in the framework of a petition, for revocation of Decree 1487 of 2007 of the National Council of the Slovak Republic concerning the inviolability of the Beneš Decrees. The petitioner has referred to a non-existent resolution, on the basis of which he has requested that the Slovak Republic’s membership of the European Union should be revoked. This petition has been formally admitted, but in reality it should have been found inadmissible, in view of the fact that it referred to a non-existent resolution. Since this did not happen, the petition has been discussed in committee, pointlessly occupying the committee, the Commission and the follow-up committee.

There will definitely be other attempts to abuse the right of petition in the future; therefore, the competent authorities must strengthen their approach to evaluating petitions, rather than the committee asking for the Commission’s point of view.

 
  
 

  Filiz Hakaeva Hyusmenova (ALDE). – (BG) Mr President, it is clear that euroscepticism in the United Kingdom is growing, with 65 % of the country’s population seeing the future of Europe in black hues. It is unacceptable and outrageous for me personally and for the Movement for Rights and Freedoms, the opposition party in Bulgaria, which I represent, for xenophobia and mutual hatred to be stirred up at the difficult time of a general economic crisis.

 
  
 

  Jacky Hénin (GUE/NGL). – (FR) Mr President, an industrial policy is essential for Member States and for the European Union so that we can meet the needs of our peoples. Without a rapid change in direction, without strong determination to provide European industry with the means of continuing to develop, to merely exist, we will rush headlong into disaster.

 
  
 

  László Tőkés (PPE). – (HU) Mr President, Commissioner, the Ceauşescu regime fell 23 years ago.   Cătălin Sorin Ivan (S&D). – (RO) Mr President, first of all, I would like to congratulate Lithuania for passing the democracy test, because a social democrat prime minister has been nominated by the President, who belongs to the group of peoples’ parties.   Emer Costello (S&D). – Mr President, I do not think there can be any argument in this House that unemployment, particularly youth unemployment, is probably the biggest challenge facing Europe at this time.

We talk of a lost generation and we have the figures to bear that out – 23 % across the European Union. In Ireland, 30 % of young people are unemployed, and I appreciate that the statistics are worse in other countries.

But behind every statistic is the tragedy of young people who cannot make a contribution to their society. They suffer loss of earnings, lack of career prospects and indeed a loss of dignity.

The situation in relation to long-term unemployment is of particularly concern and one of the findings of the Troika in Ireland was that there is a very worrying evolution from short-term unemployment to long-term unemployment.

I know that the Commission has proposals to introduce a youth guarantee in December 2012 and I very much welcome this proposal and I would ask the Commission to ensure that there is adequate funding for the youth guarantee. It is not a magic bullet, but we do need to take urgent and drastic action to address the issue of youth unemployment in Europe.

 
  
 

  Salvador Sedó i Alabart (PPE). – Mr President, one of the most pernicious effects of the current economic crisis has been its impact on poverty in our societies. In recent months, we have seen a worrying increase in social inequality in several EU Member States. The destruction of jobs has also led many families to face situations of financial despair.

Spain has had to agree to measures to stop evictions after several suicides. In this regard, unemployment is a key factor exposing people to the risk of poverty. Job destruction has increased the likelihood of social exclusion, pushing people to the edge of society and limiting their access to resources and opportunities.

Poverty in the EU is nowadays at an alarmingly high level: nearly one in seven people are at risk of poverty. The figures are even higher in some groups, such as children and elderly people. The most vulnerable people in our societies have borne much of the impact of the economic crisis. This situation is unacceptable. For that reason, I urge the European Commission to create a European fund to fight poverty and social exclusion. We need to find new ways to address poverty while continuing to develop policies to combat social exclusion.

 
  
 

  Claudiu Ciprian Tănăsescu (S&D). – (RO) Mr President, in February 2010, I told you about the difficulties that Romania’s Cantacuzino National Institute for Research and Development in Microbiology and Immunology was going through. At the time, the Institute’s manufacturing and marketing licence for products for injection had been withdrawn, including for many sera and vaccines of great importance to public health. Following pointless negotiations with the government of the time, and after establishing a programme of measures, the Cantacuzino Institute regained the right to work normally.

Unfortunately, my repeated appeals and the help offered fell on deaf ears at the Democratic Liberal Party (PDL) governments, and the situation of this institute, which is of strategic importance not just nationally but also for Europe, continued to deteriorate gradually, coming to the brink of bankruptcy. At this stage, it was inherited by the Social Liberal Union (USL) government, led by Victor Ponta, who despite all the problems managed to put together a strategy to save the Cantacuzino Institute in record time. Admittedly it is only a start, which is why I stand before you today, to reiterate my call for more active involvement by EU institutions in monitoring and supporting the work of these institutes of European strategic importance, for the good of all Europe’s citizens.

 
  
 

  Erik Bánki (PPE). – (HU) Mr President, ladies and gentlemen, on 5 May 2010, the European Parliament adopted, with a very large majority of 488 votes in favour, the multi-party motion for a regulation, tabled by Mr Áder and Mr Tőkés, which urged for a ban on cyanide-based mining technologies by the end of 2011. Unfortunately, to date, the European Commission has not done anything since Parliament’s decision to create legislation that would deal with this matter and ban this exceptionally dangerous method of mining in Europe.

Events in the recent past and anticipated steps in the future suggest that further problems will emerge within the European Union on this topic, since the environmental protection authority in Romania has already issued an operating licence for a cyanide mine, albeit a slightly smaller one, at Certeju de Sus, while at Kremnica in Slovakia preparations are also under way to re-open a mine. If we do not want these procedures to lead to initiatives to re-open the mine at Roşia Montană too, then the European Commission must do something.

 
  
 

  President. − That concludes this item. However, I gather – the later the evening, the more welcome the guests, as the saying goes – that we can fit in Mr Watson, who is still on the list, for one minute before we move on to the next agenda item.

 
  
 

  Graham Watson (ALDE). – Mr President, I rise to speak about the situation in Gibraltar, where the Spanish Government is causing huge delays for people going in and out of the territory, and to congratulate the mayor of La Linea and her council who have themselves protested against this needless disruption to the lives of Gibraltarians and others wishing to enter or leave the territory.

I know that the Partido Popular has many domestic problems and no doubt wishes to turn attention away by reviving this great ghost of Gibraltar, but it has no right to do so and I call on the European Union to intervene to bring sanity.

 
  
 

  Csaba Sándor Tabajdi (S&D). – (HU) Mr President, Commissioner Reding, ladies and gentlemen, I hereby express a cry for help from Hungarian citizens, who are in a state of complete insecurity. In three opinions now, the European Parliament has issued a warning to the Hungarian Government, which will not even in the future be capable of respecting fundamental human and social rights. Just one year ago, the Hungarian Government announced a review of entitlements to disability pensions. I would like to draw Ms Reding’s attention to the fact that they have turned support for those who were previously in receipt of disability or incapacity pensions or regular social allowances into grants that may be reduced or stopped. Yet again, therefore, the Hungarian Government has retroactively withdrawn entitlements from a group of Hungarian citizens who had acquired a right.

The Government previously praised its single-party Constitution, saying that it guaranteed the rights of disabled people. In the light of the current decision, this is not only a scandalously cynical attitude but also a crude violation of the fundamental principles of social Europe.
