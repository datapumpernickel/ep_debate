Question no 22 by Myrsini Zorba (H-0638/01)
 Subject: Restrictions on freedom of expression in Turkey
 

A draft law which the Turkish President Ahmet Sezer has provisionally refused to ratify is set to restrict freedom of expression and consolidate the oligopolistic structure of media ownership. The law provides that all Turkish internet sites must obtain authorisation from the local authorities and that daily copies of their content are to be submitted for censorship by the local public prosecutors.

The draft law has met with strong opposition from journalists and intellectuals both in Turkey and internationally.

How will the Council intervene to protect the right to freedom of expression in Turkey, an applicant country which has given definite commitments that it will resolve many internal problems relating to human rights?

 
  
 

Issues concerning freedom of expression in Turkey form an essential part of the priorities of the accession partnership for Turkey, the intensified political dialogue with Turkey and the evaluation of the progress that Turkey has made towards accession. The Council is therefore giving all the attention necessary in order to guarantee those priorities, and will obviously do so when the competent Turkish authorities assess the law referred to by the Honourable Member.

 

Question no 23 by Concepció Ferrer (H-0640/01)
 Subject: Control of diamond imports
 

At its last meeting on 11 and 12 June the General Affairs Council adopted the Council Regulation banning certain forms of technical assistance to Liberia, in order to encourage the Liberian Government to comply with its obligations in the peace process in Sierra Leone.

In addition to banning technical assistance relating to the delivery, manufacture, maintenance or use of weapons and related equipment, the Council included a ban on the import of diamonds.

Can the Council say what control measures will be established to ensure that these bans, especially the one on the import of diamonds, are carried out?

 
  
 

It is the task of the Commission and the Member States to ensure that Council Regulation (EC) No 1146/2001 of 11 June 2001 concerning certain restrictive measures in respect of Liberia is applied. Article 4 of that Regulation lays down that the Commission and the Member States shall immediately inform each other of the measures adopted under the said Regulation and shall supply each other with any other relevant information at their disposal concerning, in particular, violations, enforcement problems encountered, or judgments handed down by national courts. Moreover, under Article 6 of that Regulation, each Member State has to decide on the sanctions applicable in cases of infringement. Such sanctions must be effective, proportionate and dissuasive.

As to the specific question of the controls on imports of diamonds laid down by Council Regulation 1146/2001, the same applies here because those controls are carried out within the framework of existing customs provisions in Member States. In practice, the effectiveness of control measures can be considerably increased if the Member States of the EU agree bilaterally to introduce a specific programme of cooperation with the third country in question. Thus some Member States are cooperating closely with Angola and Sierra Leone under the Council regulations on imports of diamonds from those two countries (1)1.

The Council therefore asks the Honourable Member to put the question again to the authorities that are competent in matters concerning Liberia.

 
 

(1)1 Angola: Council Regulation (EC) No 1705/98 of 28 July 1998 (OJ L 215 of 1.8.98).
Sierra Leone: Council Regulation (EC) No 1745/00 of 3 August 2000 (OJ L 200 of 8.8.00).

Question no 25 by Francisca Sauquillo Pérez del Arco (H-0648/01)
 Subject: Review of the common position on relations with Cuba
 

Is the Council considering reviewing the EU’s common position on relations with Cuba?

Does it envisage including this issue as an item on the agenda for the Council of Foreign Ministers and, if so, when?

Joint Answer

As the Honourable Members are well aware, the Council, at its meeting of 25 June 2001, noted the ninth review of the common position of the European Union regarding Cuba.

The Council noted that, since the previous review in December 2000, there was no evidence of any significant fact which would indicate a change of policy on the part of the Cuban Government which would contribute to the achieving of the objectives of the common position. The Council therefore noted that the common position, the basis of the European Union’s policy on Cuba, remains valid. The Council will carry out a new review in six months’ time, as laid down in the common position.

The Council also reaffirmed that the objectives of the European Union with regard to Cuba are still to encourage a process of peaceful transition towards democratic pluralism and the respect of human rights and fundamental freedoms. Although the social and economic situation remains fragile, the Council found signs of an improvement in the population’s standard of living.

Moreover, the Council stated that it is essential that the European Union and the Cuban authorities should engage in a constructive and frank dialogue on all issues of common interest. The Council demonstrated the European Union’s willingness to resume such a dialogue. As Honourable Members will know, the dialogue was broken off by the Cuban authorities when they cancelled an EU Troika mission at Director General level, which had been planned for 27 and 28 April 2000.

In the light of the ninth review of the common position, the Council also considered that the presence in Cuba of greater numbers of actors from the European Union would be a more effective contribution towards achieving the objectives of the common position, because this would provide opportunities for engaging in a process of dialogue, the exchanging of experiences, and apprenticeship for the future. The Council therefore encouraged the European Commission to step up its efforts to cooperate with, in particular, the civilian and non-governmental organisations in Cuba.

Finally the Council stated that it was in favour of holding a constructive dialogue with Cuba on a future framework cooperation agreement based on respect for democratic principles, human rights and the rule of law.

On 10 July, the Belgian Minister for Foreign Affairs met the Cuban Foreign Minister in Brussels. Although the meeting was a bilateral one, Mr Michel informed Mr Pérez Roqué of the Council’s conclusions following the ninth review of the common position and said that the European Union was willing to resume the dialogue as soon as the Cuban authorities were ready for a constructive and frank exchange of views on all issues of common interest including issues concerning democratic principles, human rights and the rule of law.

Following this meeting, the Cuban authorities expressed a desire to continue discussions with the European Union. Therefore Mr Michel visited Havana on 23 and 24 August, in his capacity as President of the Council of the European Union, after having consulted the partners. Mr Michel led a fact-finding mission, and he was accompanied by representatives of the Commission, the Council Secretariat and Spain, which will be taking over the presidency. The aim of this mission was to assess the possibilities of engaging Cuba in an open and frank dialogue on all issues of common interest, in the light of the conclusions of the ninth review by the Council of the common position of the EU on Cuba. Mr Michel held meetings with President Fidel Castro, the Cuban Minister for Foreign Affairs, Felipe Pérez Roqué, and the Vice President of the Council of Ministers and Minister for the Economy, Mr Rodrigues. He also met four representatives of Cuban dissidents. This fact-finding mission resulted in agreement between the two parties on the need to resume dialogue, without any preconditions and as quickly as possible, to discuss all issues of common interest, including human rights.

Mr Michel will be reporting back to his fellow ministers at the informal (Gymnich) Council meeting on 7 and 8 September.

With regard to the next review of the common position, when the time comes, the Council will have to take into consideration the situation on the ground and any developments that may have taken place.

 
 

Question no 26 by Rodi Kratsa-Tsagaropoulou (H-0646/01)
 Subject: Fresh impetus for tourism policy
 

Following the ministerial conference on tourism held in Bruges on 1 and 2 July and in the run-up to the Commission communication 'Framing the future for European tourism', what practical form will the Council give to the possible application of the open coordination method to tourism policy? What role might the European Parliament play in shaping tourism policy? Would the Council agree with the establishment of a dialogue with representatives of the tourism and leisure industry as part of the process of defining a coherent strategy for tourism? Will the Council approve a multiannual action programme for tourism? How will it promote and strengthen social tourism programmes?

 
  
 

The European Ministerial Conference “Tourism for All”, which was held in Bruges on 1 and 2 July 2001, recognised that the working method adopted by the working parties set up to explore the priority areas listed in the conclusions adopted in June 1999 by the Internal Market Council had proved to be very effective. This working method consisted of bringing together experts from Member States and from the tourism industry and interested groups, and reaching agreement on priority areas for action.

This shows that the open coordination method, as defined in the conclusions of the Lisbon Summit, is an appropriate working tool for achieving closer cooperation between Member States in the field of tourism.

With regard to initiatives in the social tourism sector, we must remember that it is the Commission’s task to propose new initiatives in this sector. To this end, the Commission also maintains regular contact with the tourism sectors in all Member States.

On this point, the Council refers the Honourable Member to its conclusions of 21 June 1999, in which it invited the Commission and the Member States to cooperate closely, taking due account of the principle of subsidiarity and to consult experts in the tourism sector.

At the moment, owing to the lack of any consensus on this question, the Council is not in a position to adopt a multiannual programme of the "Philoxenia" type. It remains convinced, however, as indeed it has stated on more than one occasion, that the tourism sector plays an important role in the economy, and that better use should be made of its contribution to growth and employment, by means of measures aimed at improving quality and making the most of human resources, and by means of better information and better communications.

 

Question no 27 by Efstratios Korakas (H-0655/01)
 Subject: Surrender of Slobodan Milosevic to the International Criminal Tribunal in The Hague
 

On 28 June 2001 the former President of Yugoslavia, Slobodan Milosevic, was brought to The Hague. Mr Milosevic was handed over to the self-styled International Criminal Tribunal for the Former Yugoslavia despite the categorical opposition of Serbia's Upper and Lower Houses and Supreme Constitutional Court. The 'International Criminal Tribunal' has no international legal validity, but was set up at the instructions of the USA to carry out the New Order's political agenda. It was essentially founded by the very people who bombed Yugoslavia in breach of the most fundamental principles of international law, causing innumerable casualties and incalculable material and environmental damage. Mr Milosevic was handed over as part of a sordid financial deal following pressure on the country's present leaders. The Greek People has been - and still is - unanimous in expressing its revulsion at these developments. Typical expressions of this opposition are the letter signed by 83 Members of Parliament belonging to all parties (out of the 100 attending the summer session of the Greek Parliament) and the statements made by a large number of eminent public figures which appear in the press on a daily basis.

Will the Council say whether it condones practices of this kind which are in flagrant breach of the most fundamental principles of national sovereignty and international law?

 
  
 

The Council draws the attention of the Honourable Member to the fact that the European Council, meeting in Gothenburg on 15 and 16 June 2001, invited the authorities of the Federal Republic of Yugoslavia / Serbia in the region to continue to cooperate fully with the International Criminal Tribunal for the Former Yugoslavia. The Council emphasised the importance attached to the handing over of Mr Milosevic to The Hague, at the same time pointing out that this decision constituted an important step towards a rapprochement between the Federal Republic of Yugoslavia and Europe. On 25 June 2001, at its General Affairs meeting, it welcomed the adoption by the Government of the Federal Republic of Yugoslavia, on 22 June, of a decree on the subject of cooperation with the International Criminal Tribunal. It saw this as a positive step towards the total cooperation which should be given to the Tribunal.

As to the method by which Mr Milosevic was extradited, that is a matter that falls within the jurisdiction of the internal law of the Federation and its constituent states.

The Honourable Member’s objections to the handing over of Mr Milosevic to the International Criminal Tribunal in The Hague therefore go against the positions taken up by the Council, and the latter reminds the honourable Member that the International Criminal Tribunal is a body set up by the Security Council of the United Nations, and a body with which all the member countries of the United Nations, including the Federal Republic of Yugoslavia, are obliged to cooperate under Resolution 827 of 25 May 1993. In conclusion, the Council believes that by transferring Mr Milosevic to The Hague the Federal Republic of Yugoslavia has fulfilled its obligations as a member of the United Nations.

Question no 29 by Willy C.E.H. De Clercq (H-0657/01)
 Subject: Trade in raw cattle hides
 

For several months, European dealers in raw cattle hides have been forbidden to export their products to markets in Asia, because of the situation with regard to foot and mouth disease. Although exports of meat and other food products have now been resumed – from Belgium as well as elsewhere – Asian markets remain closed to European dealers in raw cattle hides.

This ban on exports is bringing heavy pressure to bear on the European market in raw cattle hides because Italy is the only country to which all the other European countries can sell them. Moreover, Italy is taking advantage of the situation by negotiating very low prices. What is more, countries outside Europe and America are steadily taking over as suppliers for the whole of the Asian market, so that firstly European dealers are losing all their Asian customers and secondly the market is being flooded with hides.

Will the Council take action to deal with this situation? If so, how, and can it do so soon, in view of the highly precarious situation facing European dealers in raw cattle hides? If not, why not?

 
  
 

As the Honourable Member has explained, because of the restrictive measures applied in the European Union since February 2001 following the foot-and-mouth disease epidemic in Great Britain (and to a lesser extent in France and the Netherlands), exports of animals and sensitive animal products to the Union’s trading partners were interrupted.

In the meantime, the Community measures have been lifted as far as France and the Netherlands are concerned, since it proved possible to eradicate the disease in those countries. This was not the case in Great Britain where, even now, the disease is not fully under control, and where the measures remain in force.

In most cases, trade with third countries has gradually resumed its normal course. However, some of the Union’s trading partners have not thought it advisable to restore normal trading relations with the Union since the measures were lifted. The Council reminds the Honourable Member that it is the Commission’s responsibility to negotiate with third countries to obtain the removal of any obstacles to trade resulting from sanitary and phytosanitary problems. In fact, the Commission is the Union’s spokesperson in Geneva at the Committee on Sanitary and Phytosanitary Measures set up by the 1994 Marrakech Agreements.

According to information that the Council has received, the Commission has maintained constant contact with the countries in question in recent months.

The Council therefore asks the Honourable Member to put his question on this matter directly to the Commission.

Question no 33 by Olivier Dupuis (H-0677/01)
 Subject: Death penalty
 

In its resolution of 5 July 2001(1) Parliament 'commits the Belgian Presidency of the Council to reintroducing and this time bringing to a vote in the United Nations General Assembly of September 2001 a resolution on the abolition of the death penalty and to taking all steps necessary to obtain its adoption'. Given that 125 UN member countries now support abolition to varying degrees – and not 110 as certain NGOs suggest – a resolution aimed at establishing a universal moratorium on executions as a first step towards the universal abolition of the death penalty would have every chance of being adopted by the UN General Assembly provided that the EU acts decisively and coherently, whilst at the same time demonstrating openness and flexibility towards third countries.

Has the Council Presidency already asked or will it ask, on behalf of the 15 Member States, that the introduction of a universal moratorium on executions be entered on the agenda for the forthcoming United Nations General Assembly? What steps has it already taken to ensure that the Union speaks with one voice and with the determination necessary for this initiative to succeed?

 
  
 

The Council reminds the Honourable Member of the content of the declaration on the abolition of the death penalty which was annexed to the Amsterdam Final Act. The European Union is opposed to the death penalty whatever the circumstances and has agreed to work towards its universal abolition. The EU is concerned by the fact that about 90 states retain the death penalty, although only a minority (30 countries) carry out executions each year.

In the context of its objective of universal abolition, in 1998 the European Union set out a series of guidelines to determine the circumstances in which it should take specific targeted action. In order to obtain the principal objective, which is the definitive abolition of the death penalty, the European Union attempts, in countries where the death penalty still exists, to limit its use progressively, and it insists that the penalty is carried out in a manner that observes certain minimum international standards. The European Union’s efforts to achieve the abolition of the death penalty are the expression of a deep conviction shared by all the Member States of the European Union. The abolition of the death penalty helps to improve respect for human rights.

Moreover, the Council reminds the Honourable Member of the European Union’s memorandum on the death penalty, issued in 2000 and included in the annual report on human rights in 2000, and of the policy guidelines of the European Union regarding third countries in relation to this subject.

By virtue of these guidelines, the European Union also raises the question of the death penalty within multilateral bodies. Thus in 1999, 2000 and 2001 the European Union succeeded in having a resolution against the death penalty adopted by the Human Rights Commission. That resolution contains, amongst other things, a request to those states that still use the death penalty to limit its use progressively before abolishing it completely and, while they continue to use it, to observe certain fundamental criteria.

At the 54th United Nations General Assembly in 1999, the European Union attempted, for the first time, to have a resolution on the death penalty adopted which incorporated the wording of the text adopted on the Union’s initiative by the Human Rights Commission. Despite gaining a large measure of support (72 countries cosponsored the motion), the European motion for a resolution came up against strong opposition from those countries in favour of retaining the death penalty, which were determined that the text include a series of amendments that would to some extent have rendered it meaningless. At the time, this led the European Union not to pursue its initiative within the General Assembly.

In 2000, after having considered the timeliness of a European initiative on the death penalty at the 55th United Nations General Assembly, the European Union found that the political and diplomatic climate did not yet make it possible to remove the obstacles that had arisen at the 54th United Nations General Assembly. In those circumstances, it appeared that to adopt a resolution which might have constituted a step backwards, in comparison with what had been achieved by the Human Rights Commission resolution, would be unthinkable. Therefore the European Union took the decision not to submit its resolution.

As far as the 56th United Nations General Assembly is concerned, no decision has yet been taken. The European Union’s experts are continuing to examine all the elements relating to a possible initiative against the death penalty. That assessment will take into account, in particular, the difficulties encountered during the first half of 2001 in the context of the negotiation process, in particular on HIV/AIDS, racism and the treatment of children, and the current events which are threatening to overshadow the work of the United Nations General Assembly.

Ideally such information should be submitted with original notifications, but this is not always possible, particularly where a general contamination problem is being investigated. Question no 82 by Paulo Casaca (H-0665/01)
 Subject: Protection of the rights of Community workers outwith their country of origin
 

The Portuguese press is carrying increasing numbers of stories of how the rights of Portuguese workers recruited to work in other Member States are being flagrantly violated in respect of either Portuguese or the other countries’ legislation.

The Commission would like to draw the attention of the Honourable Member to the fact that Community law – notably Regulations 1408/71 and 574/72(1) 1 - protects the social security rights of migrant workers by co-ordinating the different national social security schemes of Member States. One of the main principles of this co-ordination is to determine the social security legislation which is applicable to a migrant worker. As a rule, such a worker is subject to the social security legislation of the Member State where he or she is employed. Therefore, if a person works in Northern Ireland he or she is principally subject to the social security legislation of the United Kingdom. But the Honourable Member seems to refer to an important exception to this rule which is contained in Regulation 1408/71, namely the one relating to the posting of workers. A posted worker is normally employed by an employer in one Member State, but the employer sends him or her to another Member State to perform work there for a limited period of time. Under these circumstances the posted worker remains subject to the social security legislation of the sending Member State and contributions must continue to be paid to the social security scheme of this Member State. Nevertheless, the posted worker is entitled to health care in the Member State of employment on behalf of the Member State to which contributions are paid.

Although Community law determines which social security legislation is applicable, Member States remain free to determine both the conditions concerning the right or duty to be insured with their social security scheme (e.g. as concerns coverage for health care) and the conditions for entitlement to benefits provided that these do not discriminate between their own nationals and those of other Member States. It then remains for each Member State whose social security legislation has been determined to be applicable to ensure that its respective legislation, e.g. concerning the payment of social security contributions, is complied with. It should furthermore be stressed that the Commission can only ensure proper application of Community law in the field of social security by governments of Member States and does not act as inspector in the social field.

As regards minimum wages and holiday rights, which are working conditions, the rules of Directive 96/71/EC of the Parliament and of the Council of 16 December 1996 concerning the posting of workers in the framework of the provision of services(2) 1, must be observed if the agency is a temporary employment undertaking or placement agency hiring out workers to a user undertaking in Northern Ireland, provided that there is an employment relationship between, in this case, the Portuguese company and the posted workers.

In the situations covered by the Directive, the Member States are required to ensure that, whatever the law applicable to the employment relationship, the undertakings concerned guarantee posted workers certain terms and conditions of employment in force in the host country. These terms and conditions - considered mandatory rules - include in particular minimum rates of pay and minimum paid annual holidays. Where such mandatory rules are determined by the law of the host country, the Directive covers all sectors of activity. Where the terms and conditions of employment are laid down by collective agreements which have been declared universally applicable, the Directive applies to activities relating to construction.

Question no 92 by Karin Riis-Jørgensen (H-0682/01)
 Subject: Failure to implement properly parts of Directive 92/46/EEC in Denmark
 

Further to the reply to question H-0364/01(1) and question P-1794/01 to the Commission concerning failure to implement properly parts of Directive 92/46/EEC(2) in Denmark, can the Commission provide the following information:

Does it consider that it is acceptable under existing case law that Denmark has not yet introduced statutory health requirements regarding the production and marketing of raw milk for human consumption in accordance with those set out in paragraphs 1-3 of Article 4 of Directive 92/46/EEC?

Further, does the Commission consider it acceptable under existing case law that Denmark has not introduced statutory health requirements for the production and marketing of all milk-based products obtained from raw milk in accordance with Articles 6 and 7 of Directive 92/46/EEC?

 
  
 

Denmark does not have any legislation on the production and marketing of raw milk intended for human consumption or of products based on raw milk, since the latter are prohibited.

Under Article 249 (ex Article 189) of the EC Treaty, a directive is binding, as to the result to be achieved, upon each Member State to which it is addressed, but it leaves the national authorities to decide upon the form and methods.

As the Court of Justice has regularly confirmed, the transposition of a directive into national law does not necessarily require the provisions being transposed to be reproduced formally and word-for-word in a specific national provision. However, it is essential that the restrictive measures contained in a directive should be included in a national act in such a way as to create a legal context which will suffice to ensure that the measures are applied.

Consistent judgments by the Court also show that in cases where a directive lays down optional provisions or does not place a specific area entirely under regulation, the Member State has the right, within the limits of the general principles of the EC Treaty, either not to transpose the optional part of the directive or to retain or create national legislation to supplement the Community provisions.

In the case referred to by the Honourable Member, and with reference to the provisions of Council Directive 92/46/EEC of 16 June 1992, it is necessary to point out that Community legislation has not laid down any instructions regarding health, except in cases where a Member State wishes to permit the use of raw milk.

The result, as the Commission has already stated in its replies to Oral Question H-0364/01 during Question Time at the plenary session of Parliament in May 2001 and written question P-1794/01 put by the Honourable Member, is that the requirements laid down in Article 4 of Directive 92/46/EEC are only applicable in cases where a Member State authorises the placing on the market of milk intended for direct human consumption, whereas under Article 7(A)(9), each Member State has to lay down its own health rules for milk-based products which are not made from heat-treated milk or which have not been subjected to any heat treatment during their manufacturing process, and those rules must be sufficient to enable such products to meet the health criteria guaranteed for every finished product.

Question no 93 by David Robert Bowe (H-0686/01)

What powers does the Commission have to examine the circumstances in which EU-based manufacturers transfer their component manufacturing facilities to Central and Eastern European countries while maintaining final assembly of the goods in the EU, labelling the goods as having been manufactured in the EU?

 
  
 

Owing to the lack of consensus within Community industry, there are no harmonised rules at Community level on indication of the origin on products marketed in the Community, as this is a matter for the national legislation of the Member States. Where such national regulations exist, they generally refer to the non-preferential origin of products in accordance with the World Trade Organisation Agreement on rules of origin. Under the Community Customs Code(1), goods are deemed to originate in a country if they are wholly obtained or produced in that country or, where several countries are involved, if they underwent their last, 'substantial', processing or working in that country. In the very general case described by the Honourable Member, assembly operations in the Community of imported components would therefore have to be of a substantial nature for the finished product to be deemed to originate in the Community.

The Commission could not be called upon to intervene in this matter except where non-compliance with this rule for acquiring Community origin is likely to have an impact on the proper application of Community provisions. This is not the case where the impact is confined to the implementation of purely national provisions. In any event, European producers also have other instruments enabling them, on the one hand, to protect certain geographical indications and, on the other, to inform consumers about the origin of their products(2).
