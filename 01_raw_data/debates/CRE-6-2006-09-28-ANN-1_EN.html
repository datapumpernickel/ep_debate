Question no 1 by Marie Panayotopoulos-Cassiotou (H-0695/06)
 Subject: Youth unemployment
 

How will the action of Member States be coordinated under the Finnish Presidency that the commitments made by the Council progressively to eradicate youth unemployment and poverty are honoured?

 
  
 

(FI) This answer by the Presidency is not in itself binding on the Council or its members.

First it needs to be stressed that it is the responsibility of the Member States to develop and practise a policy to reduce youth unemployment and poverty. The Finnish Presidency will continue the processes that have already been established to compare policies in Member States whenever the Employment, Social Policy, Health and Consumer Affairs Council is sitting.

The importance of youth employment is repeatedly highlighted in the European Employment Strategy. The following objectives and benchmarks, which particularly concern the young, have been agreed under the Lisbon Strategy and within the framework of the European Employment Strategy:

Firstly, every young person will be offered a new start before reaching six months of unemployment. They will be offered education, retraining, job training, a job or some other means of promoting employment.

Secondly, at least 85% of 22-year-olds will have completed secondary education in the EU by the year 2010.

Additional guidance on how these objectives could and should be achieved is given to Member States in the guidelines for employment policies 2005-2008 adopted by the Council in 2005.

First of all, it should be realised that all Member States had already included young people in their national reform programmes for 2005-2008, which they presented in autumn 2005. Member States, however, were asked to propose their own priority objectives, and they have chosen different ways of dealing with youth employment issues. It should also be realised that the targets set at EU level have already been achieved in some Member States.

The Member States are at present updating their national reform programmes, which will probably be finalised at the end of October this year. The national reform programmes and the related measures taken to improve youth employment will be examined during the Finnish presidential term: in November by the Employment Committee and again in December by the Social Affairs Council. The findings will be submitted to the Commission, which will draft its annual report based on them.

The Member States are also currently preparing their national action programmes for the next phase of the European Social Fund (2007-2013). The links between the ESF and the policy framework – that is, the European Employment Strategy – will be strengthened so that the ESF can have a more effective role to play in achieving targets on employment under the Lisbon Strategy for Growth and Employment. It is intended to use the ESF to support efforts aimed at extending and enhancing investment in human capital, in particular by improving educational and training systems. One aim is to enhance employability among students by improving the way vocational training can be adapted to the needs of the job market. In this way, Member States can include in their programmes measures to boost the employability of young people, reduce the drop-out rate among students and support the transition from education to employment.

With regard to youth poverty, the mid-term evaluations of the EU’s Lisbon Strategy revealed that the young have benefited less than other age groups from policies to encourage inclusion and dynamic job market policies. The honourable Member can ask the Commission for further information on this.

The Council and Commission Joint Report on Social Protection and Social Inclusion 2006 stressed that action under the Open Method of Co-ordination needs to focus clearly on child and youth poverty. At the same time, the crucial importance of general education and vocational training was highlighted in aiming to break the intergenerational transmission of poverty.

During the Finnish Presidency, the Social Protection Committee will have the opportunity to continue the debate on this subject while the Joint Report on Social Protection and Social Inclusion 2007 is being prepared on the basis of national action programmes on social inclusion for the period 2006-2008.

 

Question no 2 by Manuel Medina Ortega (H-0698/06)
 Subject: Relations with South American regional organisations
 

Based on the latest political changes that have occurred in South America, how does the Council intend to strengthen relations with integration organisations in that part of the world?

 
  
 

(FI) This answer by the Presidency is not in itself binding on the Council or its members.

In connection with this, allow me to remind you of the important commitments to regional integration given at the Fourth EU-Latin America/Caribbean Summit in Vienna on 12 May 2006 and in the Council’s conclusions on the Commission communication on Latin America. The Council acknowledges that the European Parliament has done much to encourage regional integration in Latin America and to improve relations with organisations in the region.

Paragraph 32 of the Declaration of Vienna states: “Regional integration is a key element for stability, economic growth and investment and for increasing the importance of both regions on the world stage. We affirm the principles of co-operation and solidarity as a basis of our integration processes. In this context, we encourage and strongly support Latin American and Caribbean countries to proceed with their respective regional integration processes.”

Regional integration can do much to promote stability. Both are key goals of the strategic partnership between the EU and Latin America. The EU and its partner countries in Latin America have evolved comprehensive systems to promote both of these, because it requires continued and considerable effort to be able to sustain them, to implement them more effectively, to allow both sides to benefit from them, and to be able to extend them in the future. These systems go beyond commerce and cooperation. They include political dialogue aimed at trying to achieve common views, and joint action in multilateral issues in order to strengthen the EU’s capacity, and that of Latin America, to influence global questions of the greatest importance to both sides.

Historically, all integration processes have had their problems, and this is also true of the EU. I can justifiably say that at Vienna the EU was prominent in trying to resolve the various problems connected with the integration process in Central America, the Andean Community of Nations and Mercosur.

It is with particular satisfaction, then, that I can mention that at Vienna and since Vienna there is and has been adequate progress, so that now we can start the much-needed internal negotiations and take the necessary action to commence talks on Association Agreements between the EU, Central America, the Andean Community and Mercosur. The Association Agreements will complement the agreements that have already been made with Mexico and Chile and those now being negotiated with Mercosur. The European Parliament’s Committee on Foreign Affairs held a meeting to review the situation on 11 July.

At the same time we also discussed the possible effects of changes in the membership of the Andean Community and Mercosur due to Venezuela’s decision to go over to Mercosur. Both the current Presidency of the Andean Community, Bolivia, and its General Secretariat deserve credit for their skilful crisis management in recent weeks. Mercosur will be looking at the challenges associated with accepting a new member into its fold. This could also be discussed at the forthcoming meeting with Mercosur on political dialogue. The EU confirms that it is willing to recommence talks with Mercosur as soon as possible.

Finally, may I say that, as is stated in the Council’s conclusions adopted in February 2006, it is the EU’s aim to promote integration throughout the entire region.

 

Question no 3 by Brian Crowley (H-0703/06)
 Subject: Mobile phone charges
 

Can the Council make a definitive statement as to the progress that has been made this year in eliminating roaming charges from the bills of mobile phone users in Europe?

 
  
 

(FI) This answer drafted by the Presidency is not binding as such on the Council or its members.

The Council and Commission have been keeping a close watch for several years on the trend in roaming charges. The matter has recently become a focus of greater attention, especially since October 2005, when the Commission set up the first website aimed at providing more transparent information to users of roaming services and helping them to find the best solutions in the jungle of prices and deals.

As a result of the Commission’s activities, operators appear to some extent to have lowered their prices and made them more transparent. International roaming charges, however, continue to vary greatly, and customers are often still unable to understand how they are calculated. The softly-softly approach would seem to have proved insufficient, and the Council in its law-making capacity is ready to consider other ways to stamp out overcharging.

The Commission has submitted a proposal for a regulation that might help to put things right. The Council has only just begun to discuss the proposal, which was published on 12 July 2006. It will continue the discussion in cooperation with Parliament and aim specifically for a balanced solution, avoiding distortion of the market, needless disruption to operators’ business, and price rises in other sectors.

 

Question no 4 by Liam Aylward (H-0705/06)
 Subject: Climate change
 

Can the Council state what new initiatives are being considered by the Finnish Presidency of the EU to combat the growing problem of climate change?

 
  
 

(FI) This answer drawn up by the Presidency is not in itself binding on the Council or its members.

As the Honourable Member knows, the plans of the Finnish Presidency regarding climate change were presented when Minister Jan-Erik Enestam spoke to the Committee on the Environment, Public Health and Food Safety on 12 July 2006. In brief, these plans are as follows:

During the next few months, the EU will prepare for international negotiations to be conducted at the follow-up conference in Nairobi (6-17 November 2006). At the conference, the EU must again show that it is a strong leader in implementing the Montreal Action Plan in order to create a basis for a system for 2012 and subsequent years which is genuinely worldwide.

To this end, the Presidency is raising climate change issues at summits between the EU and third countries (China, India, the Republic of Korea, ASEM, Russia, Canada). Summits between the EU and China, the Republic of Korea and ASEM have already been held, and the ASEM statement on climate is already available. In order to prepare for the international negotiations, the Council and Parliament will have an opportunity to debate the new cost-benefit analysis of the emission reduction strategy which the European Council has requested from the Commission. The EU and the USA recently decided to launch a dialogue on climate change, clean technology and sustainable development. The first meeting concerning this subject will be held in Finland this autumn.

In addition, the EU is continuing its work to fulfil its commitments. At the proposal of the Commission, the Council and Parliament are again reviewing the European Union's emissions trading system and assessing the second stage of the European Climate Change Programme. The Second European Climate Change Programme, which was launched in October 2005, is investigating new cost-effective options for reducing greenhouse gas emissions in accordance with the principles of the EU's Lisbon Strategy. New working parties have been set up to consider carbon capture and sequestration, carbon dioxide emissions from light commercial vehicles, emissions from aviation and adjustment to the impact of climate change. The working party on aviation has concentrated on technical aspects concerning the extension of emissions trading to emissions from aviation. One working party has also assessed the implementation of the policies and measures of the First European Climate Change Programme in the Member States and their impact on reducing emissions.

Finally it may be mentioned that Finland has compensated for the emissions arising from journeys by those attending some of the meetings which it has organised during its Presidency or offers participants an easy way of adjusting their own emission levels on a voluntary basis.

Question no 6 by Gay Mitchell (H-0746/06)
 Subject: Iran
 

Will the Council please state the future course to be taken by High Representative Javier Solana in his negotiations with Iran over remaining nuclear issue?

 
  
 

(FI) This answer drawn up by the Presidency is not in itself binding on the Council or its members.

The Council has repeatedly expressed its commitment to a diplomatic solution which takes account of the international concerns that exist regarding Iran's nuclear programme but at the same time affirms Iran's right to use nuclear energy for peaceful purposes in accordance with the Nuclear Non-Proliferation Treaty. In this connection the Council welcomed the initiative by the EU's High Representative and the Foreign Ministers of France, Germany, the United Kingdom, China, the Russian Federation and the United States of America which the Secretary-General of the Council and High Representative communicated to Iran on 6 June 2006.

The Council has given its full support to the balanced approach contained in this initiative and encouraged Iran to opt for the positive course of action proposed to it. The proposal would pave the way for new relations with Iran based on mutual respect and greater political and economic cooperation, while assuaging the international community's concerns about the peaceful nature of Iran's nuclear programme.

The Council would recall that the Board of Governors of the IAEA and the UN Security Council have repeatedly called on Iran to halt all activities pertaining to uranium enrichment and reprocessing. On 31 July the UN Security Council adopted Resolution 1606 (2006), calling on Iran to halt all activities pertaining to uranium enrichment and reprocessing, which the IAEA should be allowed to inspect. If Iran did not comply with the resolution by 31 August the UN Security Council stated that it would take measures as provided for in Chapter VII, Article 41(1), of the UN Charter.

The Council has stated that if Iran decides to halt all its uranium enrichment activities and implements the Additional Protocol, negotiations can resume and the procedure in the Security Council be halted.

On 15 September 2006 the Council noted the report of 31 August 2006 by Dr ElBaradei, the Director General of the IAEA, stating that Iran had not acted as called for in UN Security Council Resolution 1696 and expressing the opinion that it would now be appropriate to consider follow-up measures to the resolution in question. The ministers were satisfied with the negotiations which the High Representative had held with Iran's Supreme National Security Council (SNSC) Secretary Ali Larijan with the aim of exploring the possibility of launching negotiations with Iran. They supported these efforts and stressed that it was important to bring about a solution quickly.

 

Question no 7 by Seán Ó Neachtain (H-0709/06)
 Subject: Broadband initiatives
 

Can the Council state what initiatives it is pursuing to promote the use of broadband to a greater extent in regional, peripheral and island regions of Europe?

 
  
 

(FI) This answer drawn up by the Presidency is not in itself binding on the Council or its members.

Widespread use of broadband has been on the EU's political agenda for many years and will undoubtedly remain on it in future. Broadband services bolster the attainment of the general objectives of the Lisbon Strategy, and all the development stages of the policies relating to the matter are to be considered in conjunction with the pursuit of the two objectives of the i2010 strategy (which in themselves are compatible).

The first objective is to create a Single European Information Space, promoting a diverse internal market based on open, competitive and electronic communication, messages and content.

The second objective is to create a European information society which promotes participation, in which priority is assigned to improving public services and the quality of life and, in connection with this, reducing the digital divide between the haves and have-nots with regard to access to broadband services.

As the Council stated in its conclusions of 1 December 2005 on the i2010 Strategy, the Member States, Community institutions and stakeholders are jointly responsible for this strategy. The Council called on Member States '… to promote the roll-out of advanced seamless networks through the rapid implementation of national strategies, aimed at increasing broadband coverage and multiplatform access, and stimulating take-up, making use, where appropriate, of EU structural funds, in line with the Commission Guidelines'(1).

All the national reform programmes which Member States submitted to the Commission last year after the launching of the Lisbon Strategy discussed issues relating to broadband cover and its introduction. Significant broadband programmes are mentioned in many of the national reform programmes (AT, IE, EE, FI, FR, HU, IT, LU, LT, PT, SI and ES). Although competition is regarded as the most important factor in stimulating the development of broadband communications, EU State aid rules permit Member States to provide State aid for broadband networks in regions where coverage is poor. This is permitted where State aid is used to promote the introduction of broadband and the aid is essential in order to correct a market disturbance or unreasonably high prices for broadband services.

The proposed strategic guidelines for the Community's cohesion policy also state that it is important that an appropriate broadband communications infrastructure should be available at a reasonable price throughout the EU. Accordingly, it has been made a special action guideline of the new cohesion policy programmes for 2007-2013 that the availability of an information and communications infrastructure and associated services should be guaranteed, particularly in peripheral and rural areas and the new Member States, if the market cannot maintain the necessary services at a reasonable price and to a sufficient extent.

At the ministerial meeting in Riga from 11-13 June 2006 at which information and communications technology for a participatory society was debated, a statement was adopted concerning an information society which promotes participation, stating that a large-scale EU strategy was a precondition for this. The EU ministers decided to concentrate, inter alia, on reducing the geographical imbalance in the distribution of broadband by facilitating reasonably priced access to information and communication networks and terminal equipment, content and services everywhere, particularly in remote and rural areas and areas which were in a weaker position, including small population centres, and by reducing substantially regional differences in Internet access, increasing broadband access in poorly covered areas and seeking to ensure that broadband services are available to at least 90% of the EU population by 2010.

The Riga statement called on the Commission to support the gathering of material, the creation of networks at all levels, including regional and local indicators, comparative assessments, exchanges of good practice and experiences gained from Europe and outside it. In addition, the Commission was asked to propose, as part of the 2007 i2010 strategy(2) and in accordance with the Riga statement, a common approach to the 2008 information society initiative to promote European participation.

Finally, the ministers encouraged future Presidencies to propose appropriate initiatives and actively continue to support the Commission in developing the 2008 initiative. On this basis, promotion of broadband services in rural areas will be considered at the annual i2010 information society conference to be held in Espoo, Finland on 27 and 28 September 2006.

 
 

(1) Commission staff working paper, 'Guidelines on criteria and modalities of implementation of Structural Funds in support of electronic communications' (SEC(2003) 895).
(2) In the longer term, Member States will develop lasting and coordinated measures in a high-level working group on the i2010 initiative and in its subgroup on an information society which promotes participation. This subgroup is particularly working on preparations for the 2008 European initiative. The subject will be debated in the high-level working group in April 2007.

How does the Council Presidency assess the chances for peace negotiations with the Chechnyan Government which was democratically elected in 1997 under OSCE supervision, a government which President Putin has forced underground, above all in the light of peace proposal made by its Foreign Minister, Ahmed Zakayev, who is living in exile in London?

Question no 9 by Frank Vanhecke (H-0714/06)
 Subject: Regularisation of illegal immigrants in Italy
 

In January-February 2005, the Zapatero Socialist government in Spain made it possible for some 800 000 illegal immigrants to regularise their status. Now the new left-wing Prodi II government is similarly regularising the status of people who are in Italy illegally.

Because of the free movement of persons, hundreds of thousands of people who have undergone this procedure will soon be able to cross Europe's internal without hindrance.

Austria's Minister for Home Affairs, Mrs Liese Prokop, has rightly pointed out the damaging impact of such decisions on other Member States and called for agreements between the Member States on uniform action against illegal immigrants, while in principle aliens policy remains a national matter.

What is the Council's view of this proposal?

 
  
 

(FI) This answer by the Presidency is not in itself binding on the Council or its members.

As the honourable Member says, policy on the legalisation of the status of illegal immigrants falls within the sphere of national competence, and so far the Council has not deliberated on the possibility of trying to reach consensus among the Member States on a coordinated approach to regularising the status of such immigrants.

A tangible step in this direction will be the entry into force of the Council Decision on the establishment of a mutual information procedure concerning measures taken by the Member States in the areas of asylum and immigration. The Commission will be responsible for establishing the procedure and managing it. In September 2006 the Council agreed on a general approach to the matter. Still to come is the consideration of the European Parliament’s opinion, after which the instrument can be adopted.

Next year the intention is to begin a study of the regularisation procedures applied by the Member States and their impact on the EU as a whole. The study will form the basis for a future joint debate on legalisation. It will be on this occasion as well that the Member States will also discuss whether there is a need for common EU legislation on the matter.

Question no 11 by Philip Bushill-Matthews (H-0730/06)
 Subject: Immigration in Spain
 

Is the Council concerned about the increasing number of African immigrants arriving illegally in the Canary Islands? Given that those immigrants who cannot be repatriated within 40 days are allowed to stay in Spain, is the Council concerned that they may be free to move elsewhere in the EU? What action does the Council consider is appropriate to address this issue?

 
  
 

(FI) This answer drawn up by the Presidency is not in itself binding on the Council or its members.

The comprehensive approach to immigration adopted last December deals with priority measures relating to Africa and the Mediterranean region with the aim of reducing migration pressures in those regions. The purpose of the measures is to reinforce cooperation and interaction between the Member States and to increase dialogue and cooperation with African States and the whole Mediterranean region.

The Council is following the progress of the implementation of the comprehensive approach. In addition, the European Council has urged the Commission to report on the progress achieved by the end of 2006.

The Honourable Members' attention is drawn to the practical measures and initiatives listed in the joint answer given by the Council to Oral Questions H-0440/06, H-0455/06, H-0460/06, H-0473/06 and H-0478/06.

A European and African ministerial meeting, organised by Morocco with the active support of Spain and France, was held in Rabat on 10 and 11 July 2006 to debate immigration and development. At the meeting an action plan was adopted dealing in particular with controlling illegal immigration into the EU from Central and West Africa and the repatriation of illegal immigrants. All the States which took part in the meeting stressed their commitment to implementing the action plan as a matter of urgency.

In the meantime, the Commission urges the Council to resume discussion and adopt the Commission proposal for modernising the VAT treatment of postal services. Question no 70 by Hans-Peter Mayer (H-0731/06)
 Subject: Requirement for EU citizens to pay high import duties on cars imported into Portugal
 

Any EU citizen whose car is registered in a Member State other than Portugal and who remains on Portuguese territory with his car for more than 180 days per year (e.g. because he wishes to spend the winter there) is required to go through an import procedure and pay an import duty (see in particular Legislative Decree no. 40/1993). The only exceptions to this requirement are for lorries, camping cars, motor cycles and commercial vehicles (all of which are defined by very specific characteristics, e.g. number of seats).

A tax exemption for EU citizens wishing to become ordinarily resident in Portugal is possible only under very strict conditions (see Legislative Decree no. 264/1993).

The method for calculating the amount of duty generally mean that the sum payable for importing the car is disproportionately high compared to its purchase price or current market value.

Is the national legislation of the Portuguese Republic, which requires EU citizens remaining in Portugal for more than 180 days with their car to import it and pay the high import duties, compatible with EU law?

 
  
 

(EN) First of all, it must be noted that car registration taxes, such as Imposto automóvel (hereinafter "IA") levied in accordance with Legislative Decrees no. 40/1993 and no. 264/1993 in Portugal, are manifestly of a fiscal nature and they are charged not by reason of a vehicle crossing the border of the Member State applying it, but because of other operative events, which includes the first-time registration of the vehicle in that State.(1) They must, therefore, be regarded as part of a general system of internal dues on goods and not as import duties within the meaning of Articles 23 and 25 of the EC Treaty. Consequently, Portugal does not act in breach of Community law by merely maintaining such a tax, provided there is no discrimination against imported vehicles prohibited by Article 90 of the EC Treaty. The Commission does not have any information to prove such discrimination.

As to the requirement to register a vehicle in Portugal and pay a tax thereof when a person has spent more than 180 days in this country, it should be noted that Council Directive 83/182/EEC on tax exemptions for temporary imported motor vehicles provides for a quantitative requirement of six months in any 12 months, during which the Member States must exempt from such taxes private vehicles registered in another Member State. When a person spends more time in a particular Member State a general rule is that the Member State concerned can levy the tax at issue. Portuguese Legislative Decree no 264/93 by establishing a quantitative criterion of 180 days for that purpose in certain cases may fall short even of five days. The Commission intends to contact Portuguese authorities on the issue.

As to the possibility provided by the Portuguese legislation to obtain under very strict conditions the tax exemption from car registration tax in the cases when EU citizens wish to become residents in Portugal, it must be recalled that there is no harmonization at EU level in the area of car registration taxes, therefore, Member States do not have any obligation to exempt from car registration taxes vehicles permanently imported to their territories.(2) Nevertheless, Portugal, on its own initiative, has chosen to exempt from IA personal vehicles imported on the occasion of an owner moving his or her residence to Portugal. The Commission, of course, welcomes this initiative but has no competence to require Portugal to modify the conditions, which were set down for that purpose.

With regard to high tax rates, it must be reiterated that since there is no harmonization in the area of car taxation at EU level, Member States may levy such taxes and determine their level as they see fit. The Court has ruled in Commission vs Denmark that Article 90 of the EC Treaty does not serve to censure the excessiveness of taxation levels and that, therefore, Member States are at liberty to decide on tax rates, even if they might appear to be excessively high.(3) Thus, Portugal by imposing high tax rates on car registration does not breach Community law, provided that they do not give rise to cross-border obstacles to trade between Member States and they respect the non discrimination principle established in the EC Treaty.

 
 

(1) Judgment of the Court of 17 June 2003 in Case C-383/01 De Danske Bilimportører v Skatteministeriet, Told- og Skattestyrelsen. ECR 2003, p. I-06065, point 34.
(2) Unfortunately, according to the Court, taxes on car registration fall outside the scope of Directive 83/183/EEC, meaning that the exemption provided therein does not apply in cases of IA. See judgements of 15 July 2004 in a Case C-365/02 Marie Lindfors, points 22 and 23 and in a Case C-387/01 Harald Weigel and Ingrid Weigel v Finanzlandesdirektion fur Vorarlberg, point 45.
(3) Judgment of the Court of 11 December 1990 in Case C-47/88 Commission of the European Communities v Kingdom of Denmark, ECR 1990, p. I-04509, point 10.
