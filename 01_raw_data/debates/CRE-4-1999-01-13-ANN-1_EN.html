1. Questions to the Council

 

Question No 12 by Richard Howitt (H−1200/98) Subject: Progress on the new Structural Fund regulations

Since the publication of the draft regulations for Structural Fund Reform on 18 March 1998, there has been much progress in the negotiations between the European Commission, the European Parliament and the Council, not least the completion of the first reading in Parliament. As it will prove difficult for all those involved to focus on the areas of importance without an official instrument from which to work, does the Council intend to issue an official updated version of the draft regulations in good time before the second reading takes place, one which will reflect the changes which have occurred since negotiations began?

 Answer

The question of the European Parliament"s participation in the legislative process for Agenda 2000, including Structural Fund Reform, has already been the subject of correspondence between the Council and the European Parliament. I would remind you of the assurances given by the individual Presidents of the Council to the President of the European Parliament regarding the implementation of the legislative process for Agenda 2000. The presidents of the Council have always stressed the importance of effective coordination and of a regular exchange of information concerning discussions in both institutions.

In view of this, and having regard to the procedures laid down in the treaties, the presidency began informal dialogue with the European Parliament, during which the European Parliament was informed of the progress of discussions in the Council. This informal dialogue will continue under the German Presidency.

Question No 19 by James Nicholson (H−1218/98) Subject: Vienna Summit conclusion on Northern Ireland

Is the Council satisfied that the Commission has taken due regard of the conclusions of the Cardiff Summit in June 1998, reaffirmed in the conclusions of the Vienna Summit in December 1998, in which the Council noted Parliament's request to the Council and Commission to consider as a matter of urgency how the Belfast Agreement might be supported in practical terms and invited the Commission to submit appropriate proposals?

 Answer

As the Council pointed out in a written answer to a question put by the honourable Member in the framework of Question Time at the December session, the Council is satisfied that the Belfast Agreement was supported in practical terms by the European Community even many years before it came into existence, since this agreement largely results from the efforts of all those involved in the peace process in Ireland during the years up to 10 April 1998. The European Community, as the people of Ireland and the authorities on both sides of the border acknowledge, among many others brought an important contribution to this peace process, both through the PEACE Community initiative and through the Community contribution to the International Fund for Ireland.

Shortly after the Belfast Agreement was signed, the Commission proposed in the preliminary draft budget for 1999 to increase the overall financial framework originally foreseen for PEACE by 100 million euros which the Council accepted in both its readings of the draft budget.

The European Council meeting in Vienna in December 1998 recognised the importance of maintaining the momentum created by the historic agreement reached in Belfast on 10 April and of seizing the opportunities created by that agreement. It reiterated its determination, which is fully shared by Parliament and the Commission, that the Union should continue to play an active part in promoting lasting peace and prosperity in Northern Ireland and reaffirms the conclusions of the Cardiff European Council in that regard.

Question No 20 by Maj Theorin (H−1220/98) Subject: Children in war situations

Children are particularly vulnerable in war situations. An annex to the Geneva Convention stipulates that they must be afforded special protection. Children caught up in wars and armed conflicts must be treated with special respect and protected against all forms of cruelty and other dangers.

Often, the majority of a war's victims are civilians. The EU Member States are cooperating with armed forces in peace‐keeping missions and in a number of conflicts. Military personnel come into contact with the civilian population and also have a duty to protect them.

Military personnel must be made aware of the formal rights of the civilian population and of how their actions affect civilians. A positive encounter with an adult could be a turning point for a child that has been badly treated.

Since more and more conflicts are causing suffering amongst civilians, a large part of the training given to military personnel should focus on the needs of the civilian population. How, in the Council's view, can we best provide support for civilians and in particular children who are victims of wars?

 Answer

Within the Council, promoting the rights of children has a high priority. The Austrian Presidency stressed the importance of this issue in its speech to the 53rd Conference of the United Nations General Assembly, and called for a worldwide coalition to promote the rights of children and to fight against the exploitation of children. The position of the EU is based on the principle that children must be able to live in peace, freedom, prosperity and security, free from abuse, violence and exploitation.

The EU is deeply troubled by the situation of civilian populations – especially that of children – in war‐torn areas and in areas affected by armed conflict. For this reason the EU has actively supported the work of Mr Otunnu, the UN special envoy for children caught up in armed conflict, as well as that of his predecessor, Mrs Machel. It is of particular concern to the presidency and to Member States that progress is made in the drafting of an optional protocol concerning the use of children in armed conflict. As soon as a text has been agreed, the EU will call on all states to accede to the protocol.

The EU further welcomes the fact that, pursuant to Article 18 of the Statute of the International Criminal Court, the conscription or recruitment of children under 15 years of age to serve with the armed forces, or their enlistment to take an active part in hostilities in international or internal conflicts, are to be regarded as war crimes, over which the Court will have jurisdiction. This will help to protect children from direct participation in hostile confrontations and thereby reduce the danger to their lives that results from acts of violence.

The EU regularly brings up the issue of the rights of children during meetings held with third countries in the context of political dialogue. It calls on the parties involved directly or indirectly in conflicts to comply with international standards and to make every conceivable effort to protect children from acts of violence.

The Council has not looked into the question of the training of those people serving in the armed forces and involved in peace‐keeping missions. It is, as always, the responsibility of the Member States to see to it that their nationals who serve in peace‐ keeping forces work towards implementing the goals stated at the beginning.

Question No 21 by Ioannis Theonas (H−1223/98) Subject: Barbaric bombardment of Iraqi people and flagrant violation of international law

The wide‐ranging military action by the USA and the UK against Iraq in the form of the bombing of that country on 16 December – bypassing all UN procedures – is a violation of every concept of international legality and a flagrantly undemocratic act of provocation, replacing the international institutions with force of arms and international terrorism. This barbaric act is a severe blow to the already humiliated Iraqi people and yet again provides Saddam Hussein and his regime with a form of justification.

Was the Council informed of the USA and UK military action? Does not the participation of the UK – especially in the event that the Council was not previously informed – constitute a violation of the Community treaties, Community law and the Charter of Human Rights, the 50th anniversary of which fell on the very eve of the bombing raids? Does it intend to condemn the USA and the UK for this barbaric attack on the Iraqi people and put the matter before the international bodies, taking all the measures necessary to put an immediate end to military intervention in Iraq and avoid any repetition of measures which allow a particular party to act as world policeman, spurning the procedures available to achieve a peaceful settlement of international disputes?

 Answer

On a number of occasions the EU has called on the Iraqi authorities to ensure full cooperation with the representatives of UNSCOM and the IAEA, in accordance with the relevant resolutions of the UNSCR. The last of these was in December 1998, when the Heads of State and Government met in Vienna under the auspices of the European Council.

In reply to the question posed by the honourable Member in respect of the raids carried out against targets on Iraqi territory, the Council has never been asked to take joint action to tackle this situation. The Council is therefore not in a position to provide an answer to this question.

However, the Council would like to stress that the raids on Iraq were motivated by the persistent refusal of the Iraqi authorities to comply with the obligations arising from the memorandum of understanding between the Secretary‐General of the United Nations and the Government of Iraq of 23 February 1998 and the settlement reached on 14 November 1998, as well as the relevant resolutions of the UNSCR.

Finally, the Council once again expresses its commitment to compliance with the resolutions of the United Nations, with international treaties and with the universal principles on which the Charter of the United Nations was founded.

Question No 22 by Nikitas Kaklamanis (H−1224/98) Subject: Lack of transparency in Council proceedings and decisions

The meetings of the Council of Ministers of the Union are held behind closed doors, while no official record is kept of their proceedings. There is a lack of transparency in this situation which makes the Council the only legislative body in the world (together with the National People's Congress of the People's Republic of China) which legislates in secret.

Will the Council state the reasons for this lack of transparency and what measures it will take to ensure that European citizens have access to the decision‐making procedure of this key institution?

 Answer

The Council does not share the view of the honourable Member that there is a lack of transparency in Council proceedings. As proof of this I should like to mention the publication of the second report on the implementation of Council Decision 93/731/EC on public access to Council documents, including the minutes of Council meetings, which provide information on all points discussed during Council meetings as well as all the texts adopted. This report shows that the percentage of documents transmitted rose from 58.7 % in the period 1994‐1995 to 78.3 % in the period 1996‐1997, despite the fact that the number of requested documents increased twelve‐fold during the same period.

I would like to take this opportunity to point out to the honourable Member that many Council debates on general political issues are broadcast publicly.

With regard to the transparency of Community proceedings and decisions, the increasingly important role played by the European Parliament should not be underestimated. In the wake of the revision of the treaties – most recently in Amsterdam – the European Parliament"s rule‐making powers have been widened considerably, and this has been done on the basis of procedures such as the cooperation, assent and codecision procedures.

This role of co‐legislator also means that the European Parliament will have greater involvement – side by side with the Council – in the task of making the debates and, above all, the decisions reached by the institutions, more comprehensible to the general public.

The Council has confidence in the mechanism it has introduced. This mechanism will be developed further in order to enable satisfactory transparency to be achieved in the decision‐making process.

Progress made so far reflects the experiences that the Council and the General Secretariat have gathered through the practical implementation of this new policy of openness, and demonstrates – as the Council established in its conclusions of 29 June 1998 – the effectiveness of Council Decision 93/731/EC in terms of increased transparency of the EU and greater closeness to the citizen.

Further efforts will be needed to enable the Council to put the finishing touches to the instruments it has established. To this end, in its conclusions of 29 July 1998, it declared its intention to grant the widest possible access to documents relating to the acts that it, as legislator, has adopted, and to reduce recourse to Article 4(2) of Council Decision 93/731/EC to a minimum.

Question No 23 by Bernd Posselt (H−1229/98) Subject: Immigration into the EU

What scope does the German Presidency see for reducing immigration into the European Union and at the same time establishing a system using fixed quotas for sharing the burden of accommodating asylum applicants and refugees from civil wars?

 Answer

The fight against illegal immigration is of great importance to the Council. However, a clear distinction must be made between illegal and legal immigration. In mid−1998, the Austrian Presidency submitted a strategy paper on immigration and asylum policy, and in the course of initial deliberations in the Council, in which emphasis was placed on the need to draw up a coherent plan to combat illegal immigration and in particular to step up the fight against trade in human beings, the advantages of a well‐ordered and legal immigration programme were underlined. Despite the efforts of the last few years, the presidency sees further need for action in the fight against illegal immigration. Within the recently established high‐level asylum and immigration group, it will push ahead with work to elaborate a joint, integrated programme to encompass all pillars, in respect of the as yet unestablished countries of origin and transit countries of asylum seekers and immigrants, which aims to stem the flow of immigrants out of these countries.

Furthermore, the presidency intends to pursue the discussion already begun under the Austrian Presidency on conducting information campaigns in the countries of origin and transit countries, concerning the legal prerequisites for the influx of third country nationals into Member States. The aim is to reach an agreement on the content and form of such campaigns by the end of the presidency.

It is also the case that speedy and effective concerted measures against illegal immigration can be introduced only if timely information is available on early signs of illegal immigration flows and of trends towards the spread of immigration. A substantial proportion of the flow of immigrants reaches the EU via South‐East and Eastern Europe. The Austrian Presidency proposed an early warning system for illegal immigration to ensure a speedier reaction to events and the immediate introduction of counter measures. The German Presidency takes the view that the countries of Central and Eastern Europe should be included in this system.

The presidency will also continue the work introduced by the Austrian Presidency in the CIREFI to speed up the exchange of information between Member States, and it will extend this process to include the applicant countries. The aim is to achieve this within two years.

With regard to returning illegal immigrants to their country of origin, the presidency will continue the process begun under the Austrian Presidency to elaborate a joint draft readmission agreement with third countries. In this connection, the inclusion of readmission clauses in European Community and Member State agreements, and the use of standardised EU travel documents will be discussed further.

The presidency also plans to deal with selected transit problems when formulating repatriation measures. Efforts could be made to introduce a joint measure to provide support from the competent authorities in the transit countries for the officials responsible for repatriation in the Member States.

The issue of burden sharing raised in the second part of the question, which will be discussed within the framework of the Commission proposals for temporary protection and solidarity in receiving and accommodating refugees from civil wars, is of the utmost importance to the Council. It is doubtful, however, whether fixed quotas are consistent with the requirement of the European Parliament, as put forward in its opinion, that any distribution process presupposes the consent of those involved.

The presidency will support a solution which expresses the idea of European solidarity. Intensive discussions will thus be held in respect of distribution criteria.

Question No 24 by Christine Oddy (H−1231/98) Subject: Puerto Morazan – Hurricane Mitch

Would the Council advise how much construction help, in the form of road and bridge repair teams and equipment, it has sent to Nicaragua and Honduras?

 Answer

The Council has twice examined the effects of Hurricane Mitch and the possibilities of organising construction aid.

Member States have kept each other informed of the emergency aid they have supplied, and the Commission has helped to coordinate emergency aid on the spot. Be that as it may, road and bridge repair has not been part of any specific coordination plan. Question No 28 by Olivier Dupuis (H−1248/98) Subject: Kosovo

The fact that Albanian Kosovans are not directly involved in the Holbrooke‐Milosevic negotiations and, even more so, the recent rise in the perpetration by the Yugoslav and Serbian authorities of criminal acts against both the Serbian and Albanian populations of Kosovo, and of threats against the OSCE observers, point to a grim outlook for the next few months and even weeks in Kosovo. In this context, does the Council not deem it essential, and a matter of urgency, to put the question of the indictment of Slobodan Milosevic before the tribunal on crimes committed in former Yugoslavia on the agenda of the UN Security Council, via the Member States which are represented on it, to step up cooperation between the Member States and the tribunal regarding the collection of evidence about the intrigues of the Serb and Yugoslav authorities in Kosovo, to grant official recognition to the Kosovo Liberation Army as quickly as possible, and to set up a European Union radio station to broadcast around the clock in both Serb and Albanian throughout the territory of the Federal Republic of Yugoslavia?

 Answer

The Council shares the disappointment of the honourable Member at the worsening situation in Kosovo. It utterly condemns those responsible for the latest incidents there. It has asked the Belgrade administration to investigate these incidents immediately and has called upon all those involved to cooperate unreservedly with the International Criminal Tribunal for the former Yugoslavia in its investigation of human rights abuses within its jurisdiction.

Responsibility for the origins of the crisis in Kosovo lies first and foremost with Belgrade. The Albanian Kosovans in general and the Kosovo Liberation Army in particular are partly responsible for the fact that it has so far been impossible to get substantive negotiations off the ground which could bring about a peaceful solution such as that sought by the international community, based therefore not on independence but on far‐reaching autonomy for Kosovo within the Federal Republic of Jugoslavia.

The procedure put forward by the honourable Member (indictment of Milosevic, official recognition of the KLA which, despite the cease‐fire it has itself declared, has not abandoned its goal of achieving independence through force) would hardly be conducive to bringing about the rapprochement of both sides that is necessary to ensure a peaceful settlement. On the contrary, it would encourage extremism, an escalation of armed conflict and the spread of instability throughout the whole of the region.

The Albanian Kosovans must seize the opportunity afforded by the current fragile cease‐fire to reach an interim agreement which would enable them to determine their own affairs to a far greater extent. In tandem with the USA and others, the EU is endeavouring to put together a unified Albanian Kosovan negotiating team, in which all political persuasions are represented, in order to ensure that the Kosovans play an active role in the negotiation process. The current practice of informal contact with the political wing of the KLA, and contact with Mr Rugova as the only elected leader of the Albanian Kosovans, go some way towards achieving this goal. It is a goal which must be achieved if the alleged willingness on the part of the Yugoslavs to grant autonomy to Kosovo is to be seriously measured against what is happening on the ground.

In addition, the Council agrees unreservedly with the view that is implicit in the honourable Member"s question that independent, objective information on the ground is vitally important for Kosovo in supporting the interim agreement and would make it easier for the political leadership to accept and back the agreement. The EU special envoy is continuing his efforts to put in place an information strategy, and Member States have been asked to suggest to their media that they broadcast more extensively to Serbia and that they strengthen their contacts with representatives of the independent Serbian media. The Council would like to remind you that, in 1998, ECU 10 million were made available in support of the independent media in the former Yugoslavia, of which ECU 6 million were earmarked for the Federal Republic of Yugoslavia. In the context of the EU"s future information strategy, consideration will be given to setting up a radio station under the OSCE and to a special media centre for the Kosovo verification mission.

Question No 40 by Gerard Collins (H−1206/98) Subject: Research into the provision of public transport on un‐economic routes

Many parts of rural Ireland are suffering from severe demographic and infrastructure problems. An analysis of the most recent census figures for parts of West Limerick and North Cork reveals large numbers of elderly people living alone and lacking transport services.

Can the Commission indicate what research it has undertaken, or intends to undertake, into ways of improving public transport on un‐economic routes.

 Answer

The honourable Member will be aware that much public transport is socially desirable but financially unprofitable, so the challenges include getting the best value for money from public funding; raising the quality and attractiveness of services; and organising services in innovative ways which reduce their costs and make it easier to provide transport in areas where demand is low for structural reasons. The Commission has undertaken, or is supporting, research on all three points.

The ISOTOPE project completed in 1997 examined the effectiveness and efficiency of different organisational structures in urban public transport and concluded that the best approach is through a measure of ‘controlled competition' – without the loss of service integration that would come from a totally free market. The Commission is considering how to take best advantage of these recommendations.

Meanwhile, the QUATTRO project has studied how to enhance quality in public transport management and contracts and the MOTIF project is analysing the match between ‘the market' and the broad scope of possible public transport solutions.

As an alternative to strictly scheduled but under‐utilised services, demand‐responsive transport is one way to offer lower‐cost services in areas of low patronage. The SAMPO and SAMPLUS projects, which were cofinanced by the Telematics Applications Programme, have, therefore, looked into the potential of telematics services for information, for reservation and for flexible vehicle planning and management in order to promote maximum ‘accessibility' (in the broadest sense) of rural transport.

In addition, better use may be made of car‐sharing and of public sector vehicles such as postbuses and ‘people carrier' taxis in rural areas. The VIRGIL project is, consequently, preparing an extensive inventory and analysis of various practices in rural transport all over Europe. It will also provide recommendations for further research in consultation with participants in public transport services and experts and there should be further follow up through the ‘Sustainable Mobility and Intermodality' key action in the Fifth Framework Research and Development Programme.

Question No 41 by Robert Sturdy (H−1226/98) Subject: Trans‐European road network

A number of people have been killed because of congestion on the A14 near Cambridge, England, a trans‐European network road. It is 20 years since the road was upgraded. What action can the Commission take to persuade the British Government to bring forward urgent improvements to this road?

 Answer

The guidelines allow the Community to give financial support to projects that have the purpose of improving transport conditions – in particular safety and reliability.

Whilst the A14 is included in the network guidelines, the honourable Member will be aware that under the TENs Financial Regulation, the Community can only cofinance feasibility studies or infrastructure investments on the basis of specific requests from the Member State concerned.

Consequently, the Commission can only take further steps when the UK authorities have submitted such a request and the honourable Member will be interested to know that the UK has included a multimodal study of the Cambridge‐Huntingdon section of the A14 in the list of projects for which it seeks TENs support in 1999.

The Commission is, therefore, now giving active attention to that list and will make a recommendation after the normal procedures are concluded.

Meanwhile, the Commission understands from its enquiries that the UK Highways Agency has identified a number of safety‐related improvements for the Huntingdon‐Cambridge section of the route, which will be implemented during 1999.

The EU and the WEU are currently analysing these initial experiences with a view to improving cooperation between them in the future.

Question No 50 by Alexandros Alavanos (H−1175/98) Subject: Greek court judgment and Community directives on recognition of diplomas

The Greek Supreme Court has ruled against recognition of diplomas of students who begin their studies in Greece with a two‐year programme at ‘Free Study Centres' and continue at partner universities in a Member State of the European Union from which they receive an academic qualification., In the event that the diplomas in question are recognised by the Member State awarding the academic qualification as giving access to professions which are regulated by law, will the Commission clarify whether the above Greek court decision is in keeping with Community directives on the recognition of diplomas?

 Answer

The question asked by the honourable Member is linked to the problem of franchised or transnational training programmes. It relates to training programmes provided in a Member State, in this case Greece, by an institution, university, private or other training body in another Member State. The diploma awarded on completion of the programme is not a Greek diploma, but a diploma of the Member State where the training body is based.

This phenomenon of transnational training is a relatively recent one and is evolving very rapidly. It is a matter of reconciling the principles of freedom of movement, freedom to provide services and encouragement of cooperation between the educational institutions of the different Member States, with the need to maintain a high level of quality in higher education.

In the light of Community directives, it can be considered that, if a diploma which is issued at the end of a period of franchised training conforms to the definition of the diploma in the directive, especially as regards the level of training, the holder of the diploma could invoke the provisions of these directives in order to obtain recognition. It is vital that diplomas marking the successful conclusion of the franchised training programmes are rigorously controlled by the competent authorities in the country of origin, so that the principle of mutual trust which inspires the recognition mechanisms within the Community is not called into question.

The Commission draws the attention of the honourable Member to the fact that Greece has not yet incorporated into national law Directive 89/48/EEC on a general system for the recognition of higher‐education diplomas. There still remain obstacles to the recognition of diplomas in Greece, not only in respect of franchised training programmes. The Commission has referred this to the Court of Justice on the basis of Article 171 of the EC Treaty.

As far as the ruling of the Greek Supreme Court is concerned, the Commission is unable to give an opinion until it has made a careful examination of this judgment.

Question No 51 by Graham Watson (H−1179/98) Subject: EU fire safety recommendations

Is the Commission satisfied with the conclusions of the study it commissioned Ceten‐Apave to undertake on compliance and non‐compliance with the 1986 EU fire safety recommendations and does it intend to bring forward proposals for an EEC fire safety directive?

 Answer

The Commission has instigated a number of measures in this area, including: the Council Recommendation of 22 December 1986 on fire safety in existing hotels (Recommendation 86/666/EEC)(1) , the Council Directive on construction products (Directive 89/106/EEC)(2) and the provisions on fire detection and fire fighting laid down in the Council Directive concerning minimum safety and health requirements at the workplace (Directive 89/654/EEC)(3) .

 Answer

The Commission is making every effort possible to avoid any US sanctions from entering into force. Question No 78 by John Cushnahan (H−1247/98) Subject: Riding helmet standards

Has the Working Group 5 on standards met to discuss the safety issues relating to equestrian helmets, with particular reference to document N326, as requested by Parliament's Petitions Committee?

 Answer

Given that helmets for equestrian activities are items of personal protective equipment covered by Directive 89/686/EEC, the Commission instructed the CEN to draw up a harmonised standard indicating compliance with the basic health and safety requirements laid down in that Directive.

The resulting standard – EN 1384, 'Helmets for equestrian activities' – was adopted in September 1996 and its reference was published in the Official Journal of the European Communities of 14 June 1997.
