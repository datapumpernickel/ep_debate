Question no 9 by Niall Andrews (H-0087/04)
 Subject: Ethiopia and hunger
 

Will the President-in-office of the Council indicate how the Council considers the international community should respond to renewed concerns that millions of people could soon be suffering again from severe hunger in Ethiopia, despite the fact that the harvest in 2003 is likely to be 60% up on the 2002 harvest, and does it believe that the crisis could be mitigated if Ethiopia had more resources to build the stores, roads and other infrastructure needed to conserve surplus food?

 
  
 

(EN) Over the last 20 years, the EU has been working on strategies to improve food security in Ethiopia and has been among the major donors in providing food and humanitarian aid. In this context, the EU has advocated the replacement of external aid by internal food security mechanisms. The Government of Ethiopia has designed a five-year food security programme, the Coalition for Food Security (CFS). This initiative is aimed at addressing the root causes of food insecurity, moving from emergency interventions to a long-term security policy. The EU commends the political will of the Government of Ethiopia to work together with stakeholders through the CFS in addressing the root causes of food insecurity. The EU remains a committed partner in development and will seek to provide assistance, with sufficient flexibility, to enable the government to translate its vision into action.

In order to avoid funding problems, it is crucial that the international community focus on a consistent support to initiatives within the framework of the the CFS, as opposed to promoting a number of scattered initiatives, a view underlined by Martti Ahtisaari, UN Special Envoy for Humanitarian Affairs in the Horn of Africa. Experience has shown that a higher harvest does not imply that food security will be achieved all over Ethiopia. Some food insecure areas still depend on aid, even in surplus years. This is a matter of distribution and of purchasing power. It is crucial to provide incentives to farmers to produce. The EU is committed to address the fundamental problems through support to the Coalition in its long-term food security policy.

At the CFS meeting last December, the EU strongly encouraged the Government of Ethiopia to fully integrate the CFS objectives in its Sustainable Development and Poverty Reduction Programme (Ethiopia's poverty reduction strategy) to avoid efficiency and financial losses due to programme duplication and the risk of missing linkages between food security policy, economic growth and poverty reduction.

EU assistance to Ethiopia from the European Development Fund takes into account this long-term vision. The National Indicative Programme provides for an “A” allocation (allocations to cover programmes and projects) of € 384.2 million and a “B” allocation (allocations to cover unforeseen needs and contributions to internationally agreed debt initiatives) of € 153.7 million. The sectors of concentration are transport (rehabilitation and upgrading of the road network) (€ 211 m), food security (€ 54 m) and macro-economic support (€ 96 m).

 

Question no 10 by James (Jim) Fitzsimons (H-0088/04)
 Subject: Scrapping toxic ships and new legislation
 

The safe disposal of obsolete ships is an international problem. The fate of four of the 13 naval vessels from the US National Defence Reserve Fleet docked in Hartlepool rests on a UK High Court decision. The remaining nine ships, still moored in the James River, feature on the US Maritime Administration’s (MARAD) list of ‘40 Worst NDRF Scrap Ships’. Problems include corrosion, on-board fuel, asbestos, and PCBs. An audit by the Department of Transportation Office of the Inspector General reports that ‘Environmental dangers associated with MARAD's old, deteriorating ships are very real and increasing daily. These vessels are literally rotting and disintegrating as they await disposal. Some vessels have deteriorated to a point where a hammer can penetrate their hulls…’.

In the light of the problems related to the need for the safe scrapping of obsolete vessels, what new regulatory initiatives does the Council consider are needed to ensure that EU waters are not used as toxic highways for transporting ‘ghost ships’ for scrappage, and does the Council consider that the Basel Convention banning the export of toxic waste from industrialised countries to developing countries should be amended?

 
  
 

(EN) As the Honourable Parliamentarian will be aware, there is no Community competence as far as shipping and this particular issue is concerned.

Nevertheless, decommissioning of ships was raised at the meeting of the Environment Councils on 27 October and 22 December last year. There, some Member States urged the EU to take appropriate economic and environmental measures and to clarify certain legal aspects of the issue in particular in relation to the Basel Convention.

The Presidency - the Irish Minister for Maritime Transport Dermot Ahern - discussed this issue and the concerns raised at a recent meeting with Commissioner de Palacio, during which he invited the Commission to come forward with appropriate proposals.

So far, the Council has not received a proposal from the Commission. Whenever the Commission does make a proposal, the Council will, of course, welcome the views of the European Parliament.

As the Honourable Member will be aware, the EU co-operates closely with relevant international organisations such as the International Maritime Organisation (IMO), the International Labour Organisation (ILO) and the Basel Convention in this area. In the IMO, EU Member States have already worked actively together on a related issue - supporting a proposal for the creation of ‘Particularly Sensitive Sea Areas.’ This proposal aims at creating mandatory monitoring and reporting on routed maritime traffic, ensuring the utmost extra care is taken by vessels navigating through particularly sensitive sea areas. Furthermore, the IMO's Marine Environment Protection Committee (MEPC) recommended at its 49th session in July last year, that the Secretariat of the Basel Convention make all relevant information available of this issue.

 

Question no 11 by María Izquierdo Rojo (H-0094/04)
 Subject: Promoting women in rural development
 

What measures will be adopted under the Irish Presidency to promote women in rural development?

 
  
 

(EN) Over the past few years, the Council has been taking a special interest in the promotion of women in rural development. In the reply to Oral Question No H-0415/03, put by the Honourable Rodi Kratsa-Tsagaropoulou on the interim review of the common agricultural policy and the situation of women in rural areas, it was pointed out that the Agriculture Council had approved conclusions on the valuable contribution of women to rural development, which were based on the Spanish Presidency's memorandum and the results of the Conference on Gender Mainstreaming, organised jointly by the Presidency and the European Commission in Santander (Spain) in June 2002. It was further indicated that the Greek Presidency had launched a debate on employment in rural areas, on the basis of a working document initially examined by the Special Committee on Agriculture in June 2003. That debate, which broached the issue of the promotion of female employment in rural areas, was followed by the adoption, during the Italian Presidency, at the Agriculture and Fisheries Council meeting in July 2003, of Council conclusions stating that:

– "Maintaining employment in rural areas depends on economic activity and, in large part, both on having young people set up there and on creating conditions conducive to the presence of women on the land and, hence, on measures to support those two groups of workers.",

and that the Council:

– "calls on the Commission to follow up the Agriculture Council conclusions of 27 May 2002 on incorporating the gender perspective, in order to consolidate the economic and social cohesion processes under way, by means of increasing involvement of women in rural development activities;".

The Irish Presidency, in turn, also attaches great importance to the promotion of women in the countryside. When speaking before the Committee on Agriculture and Rural Development on 26 and 27 January 2004, the Irish Minister responsible for rural development, Mr O'Cuiv, emphasised that it was important for rural policy to make investments in the rural economy in order to generate the employment and living conditions necessary to maintain the rural population, especially young people and women. He further highlighted the need to recognise and reward the vital role played by women in rural communities.

The interest shown is borne out by the conference on the improvement of conditions and quality of life in rural Europe, to be organised by the Irish Presidency in Westport on 31 May and 1 June 2004, at which gender equality in the countryside will be specifically addressed.

I would also remind you that the Council's preparatory bodies are currently examining the proposed Directive implementing the principle of equal treatment between women and men in the access to and supply of goods and services. Adoption of this Directive will mark a major step towards equal opportunities for women in general and, hence, for rural women too.

Lastly, the Irish Presidency of the Council will certainly have consideration given to any relevant legislative measures which the Commission may place before it during this six-month term.

Question no 13 by Olle Schmidt (H-0098/04)
 Subject: Freedom of movement in the EU and risk of misuse of welfare systems
 

1 May 2004 will be a historic day on which western and eastern Europe reunite on peaceful terms. At the same time as welcoming that event, account should be taken of developments that may arise in connection with freedom of movement. In the light of the different levels of prosperity within the Union following enlargement, it is a possibility that individuals will try to move to another Member State to avail themselves of the new country's social security system. The Court of Justice has ruled that the minimum amount of work which may be defined as employment is ten hours per week but that the remuneration need not cover the worker's own upkeep. At the present time, ten hours a week of employment gives entitlement to a residence permit, which opens the door to the Member States' welfare systems. By changing the definition of employee/self-employed person so that the work should cover the individual's upkeep, the discriminatory transitional rules would be avoided.

Does the Council intend to take the initiative to redefine the terms employee/self-employed person in order to prevent the misuse of Member States' welfare systems and avoid discriminatory transitional rules for the new Member States?

 
  
 

(EN) As the Honourable Member is certainly aware, the acquis on the freedom of movement for persons will apply as from the date of accession, subject to specific transitional arrangements as set out in the Act of Accession.

Under these arrangements, by way of derogation from Articles 1 to 6 of Regulation (EEC)N° 1612/68 on freedom of movement for workers within the Community and until the end of a two- year period following the date of accession, the present Member States will apply national measures, or those resulting from bilateral agreements, regulating access to their labour markets by nationals from acceding countries (other than Cyprus and Malta). Depending on how liberal these national measures are, they may or may not result in full access to labour markets.

The current Member States may continue to apply such measures until the end of a five-year period following the date of accession, subject to notification to the Commission by those Member States concerned no later than at the end of the two-year period following the date of accession.

This transitional arrangement may be prolonged for a further two years in those current Member States where there would be serious disturbances of the labour market or a threat of such disruption.

In addition, Austria and Germany may apply flanking national measures to address serious disturbances, or the threat thereof, in specific sensitive service sectors on their labour markets, which could arise in certain regions from the cross-border provision of services.

Moreover, safeguards may be applied by Member States up to the end of the seventh year, allowing for Articles 1 to 6 of Regulation (EEC) N°1612/68 to be wholly or partially suspended, in order to restore to normal the situation in the region or occupation affected.

During the transitional period, it will be up to the present Member States to determine the conditions for access to their labour markets and, therefore, to their social protection systems.

In addition, it is to be noted that, during the transitional period, acceding countries' nationals legally working in one of the present Member States at the date of accession will enjoy access to the labour market of that Member State on condition that they have been admitted to this labour market for an uninterrupted period of 12 months or longer. These nationals will not, however, enjoy access to the labour markets of other Member States applying national measures.

Finally to answer to the concrete question raised, the Council recalls to the Honourable Parliamentarian that it would only act in the context of a proposal presented by the Commission and that no particular proposal of measures of the type suggested by the Honourable Parliamentarian has been presented at that stage. But, the Council also underlines that the functioning of the specific transitional arrangements provided by the Act of Accession will be reviewed in the future, on the basis of a report from the Commission.

Question no 17 by Proinsias De Rossa (H-0109/04)
 Subject: Debt cancellation
 

The European Parliament passed a resolution calling for a human development approach to developing country debt (P5_TA(2002)0209(1) ). European debt campaigns have proposed that the Barcelona Commitment on debt be amended to include a human development approach to debt sustainability. This would mean that the first call on poor country government resources would be expenditures needed to meet the Millennium Development Goals.

Will the Irish Presidency place the question of linking debt cancellation to the Millennium Development Goals on the agenda of the April GAERC meeting which is due to discuss progress on the commitments made by the EU on the Millennium Development Goals?

 
  
 

(EN) One of the most significant issues undermining long term poverty reduction is the huge level of debt owed by the poorest nations.

As stated in the Monterrey Consensus, agreed in March 2002 by the Heads of State and Government of the UN Members States, the EU recognises that sustainable debt financing is an important element for mobilising resources for public and private investment and that external debt relief can play a key role in liberating resources that can be redirected towards activities consistent with attaining sustainable growth and development.

Achieving the Millennium Development Goals is a key objective for the European Union and the wider international community. The commitments made by EU member states at the Monterrey Conference reflect the Union’s leadership role in international efforts to achieve the MDGs. The importance of achieving the MDGs was reinforced by the European Union by the agreement of Council Conclusions following the Orientation Debate in January 2004. Progress in this regard will be reviewed at the April 2004 GAERC.

The HIPC initiative has already provided significant levels of debt relief for many poor countries. The EU objective remains to ensure that within this initiative the eligible countries can reach and maintain a sustainable level of debt in the long term, thus reinforcing their efforts to reduce poverty and promote growth. The EU has provided more than half (around 60%) of the financing of the HIPC initiative. In addition, all Member States have announced their intention to go beyond the HIPC targets.

We have to recognise that the HIPC initiative has had mixed effects in macro-economic terms, positive in terms of stock reduction, unclear on interest flows. However, the whole concept of a Poverty Reduction Strategy was born with the HIPC. This initiative was also a catalyst for a better and wide-ranging donor co-ordination.

I would also like to point out that, apart from the relief of multilateral debt through the HIPC Trust Fund and other initiatives, the EU Member States have provided significant relief of bilateral debt owed by developing countries.

The EU objective remains to ensure that, within the HIPC initiative, the eligible countries can reach and maintain a sustainable level of debt in the long term, thus reinforcing their efforts to reduce poverty and promote growth. The EU is also examining ways of ensuring long term debt sustainability for countries after they exit the HIPC process and for all other low income countries.

The EU is convinced that the responsibility for long-term debt sustainability is shared between HIPC and the donor/creditor countries.

The GAERC on 27 April will consider a second Commission progress report on commitments agreed by the EU at the 2002 Barcelona European Council and announced at Monterrey. One of these commitments concerns long term debt sustainability in the context of the HIPC. The GAERC discussion will enable Ministers to review the current situation in relation to debt relief. The Council has mandated the Commission to undertake a comprehensive monitoring exercise in relation to the eight EU commitments made at Monterrey. In the view of the Presidency, any effort to amend the commitments at this stage of the process would only affect the credibility of the exercise. It would also risk opening up all of the commitments for review. This might have implications for the credibility of the EU’s commitment to following up on the Monterrey consensus.

Question no 19 by Gerard Collins (H-0111/04)

(EN) In the aftermath of the events of 11 September 2001 the Council agreed on a comprehensive action plan on terrorism covering all the sectors of activities of the European Union. With regard to organised crime, the position is that this is an area, which has been, and continues to be, a priority for the Council. In particular, the 2000 new Millenium Strategy on the Prevention and Control of Organised Crime and the 2003 Mid-Term Review on its implementation provide a detailed framework for the development and improvement of international co-operation in the sphere.

During the Irish Presidency, the Council has been active in ensuring that organised crime can be tackled as effectively as possible throughout the Union. In that regard I might mention that at the JHA Council on 19 February 2004 arrangements were put in place to review the effectiveness of existing instruments and measures to combat organised crime originating from the Western Balkans. In addition, the Presidency has introduced proposals to promote the development of co-operation between the public and private sectors for the purpose of combating organised crime.

Question no 21 by Brian Crowley (H-0115/04)

In this framework, in support to Union competitiveness, growth and employment, a key priority of the proposals is to make of the Union a performing knowledge-based economy, as foreseen in the Lisbon agenda. For this purpose, alongside education and innovation, the Union research policy must be reinforced, concentrating the efforts on 5 axes corresponding to 5 major structural issues in this field: stimulating the creativity and excellence of European research by supporting individual teams in competition at European level; strengthening research capacities by supporting infrastructures of European interest and a reinforced support for human resources; fostering public/private partnerships in fields of both public and industrial relevance; creating “poles of excellence” through networking and joint projects at laboratory level; and improving the co-ordination of national and regional research programmes and policies.

During the current planned period 2000-2006, Structural Funds funding for Research and Technological development, including R&TD infrastructures and equipment as well as regional innovation strategies, innovation clusters and technology transfer projects, has been estimated at nearly 10 billion € for Objective 1 regions and over 2 billion € in Objective 2 regions.

For the future and in a converging evolution, in conformity of the orientations of the 3rd Cohesion Report “A new partnership for Cohesion: Convergence, competitiveness and cooperation”, it is proposed to refocus those Structural Funds towards the Lisbon and Gothenburg priorities, including research and development as well as innovation, for all European regions, as well as for territorial cooperation programmes.

As for agriculture, the Commission’s proposals take full account of the financial framework for market and direct aid support for the period 2007-2013 for the enlarged Union, fixed by the Heads of State and Government at the Brussels European Council of October 2002.

All these proposals take place in the context of a proposed general increase of the Union budget, allowing to respond to the needs of a Union of 25 and more Member States in a “globalised” world.

 Subject: Fourth CSF and Greek Objective 1 regions
 

According to the Commission's third report on economic and social cohesion, the increase in Greek GDP mean that four regions will exceed 75% of the average Community GDP: Attica (78.1%), Central Greece (104.2%), Western Macedonia (75.4%) and the Southern Aegean (83.9%), and they will now no longer be Objective 1 regions. These are: Central Macedonia (73.6%), the Peloponnese (70.2%) and Crete (70.7%). However, many of these regions fall foul of an unfortunate statistical phenomenon: even though the per capita income remains relatively low, the fall in the Community average owing to enlargement will artificially push them past the 75% mark.

Does the Commission intend to take this aspect into account and, if so, what initiatives does it intend to take? Does it intend to make fresh proposals for specific support measures for these regions before the final decisions are taken so that resources from the Fourth CSF are correctly but equitably, distributed?

 
  
 

(EN) In the Third cohesion report – adopted on 18 February 2004 – the Commission sets out proposals for the reform of cohesion policy after 2006.
