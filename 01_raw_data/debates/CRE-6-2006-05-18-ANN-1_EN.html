QUESTIONS TO THE COUNCIL (The Presidency-in-Office of the Council of the European Union bears sole responsibility for these answers)
Question no 11 by Marie Panayotopoulos-Cassiotou (H-0401/06)
 Subject: Application of a reduced VAT rate to products and services essential to the wellbeing of children
 

Given that 15% of European citizens are threatened with poverty (Eurostat statistics), could the Council give the reasons for babies’ nappies not being included among the ‘pharmaceutical and medical products of a kind normally used for health care’ that benefit from a reduced VAT rate, along the lines of sanitary protection for adults? Is this not an inconsistency which should be remedied, given that babies’ nappies and adults’ sanitary protection serve the same purpose of providing protection against urinary incontinence?

In so far as this would not affect the operation of the single market, does the Council envisage including in Annex H to the 6th VAT Directive those products that are absolutely necessary for the wellbeing of children (e.g. clothes, shoes, pacifiers, bottles, bottle-warmers, pushchairs, etc.), thus helping to reduce the outgoings of families with children, particularly those facing financial difficulty or poverty?

 
  
 

(DE) Having come to an agreement following almost three years of deliberations, the Council, in February 2006, adopted a directive on reduced rates of VAT. The Council did not believe that it served any purpose to amend Annex H of the sixth directive, specifically by including in it the products to which the honourable Member refers. The Directive applies only to labour-intensive services of the kind listed in Annex K of the Sixth VAT Directive and which, being rendered locally, cannot result in distortions of competition.

It ought also to be pointed out that the Commission had not proposed the inclusion in Annex H of the products to which the honourable Member refers, and that Parliament made no amendments other than that relating to children’s clothing and children’s shoes.

 

Question no 12 by Andreas Mölzer (H-0101/06)
 Subject: Budget savings through effective combating of fraud
 

For the 12th year in succession, the European Court of Auditors has refused to sign off the EU budget, because it was able formally to account for the proper use of only nine percent of expenditure. A business manager involved in such wrongdoings would soon end up in prison. It is specifically in the payment of EU subsidies – which are administered by the relevant authorities in the Member States – that irregularities repeatedly arise. Rather than constantly looking for new sources of funding – in the form of EU taxes, for example – and raising expenditure, it is high time that the fight against fraud was stepped up and money wasting was finally brought under control.

What is the Council’s position on this matter and what ways of improving the situation are being considered?

 
  
 

(DE) The Council shares the concern that the honourable Member has expressed at the Court of Auditors’ inability, for what is now the eleventh year in succession, to issue a Statement of Assurance (termed a DAS) in respect of the transactions to which much of the EU’s annual accounts refers. The Council has regularly and repeatedly addressed this lamentable state of affairs in its discharge recommendations, in particular in its recommendation in respect of the Budget years 2002 and 2003(1). When it came to the financial year 2004, though, the Court of Auditors was able to establish that progress had been made in developing the supervisory and control systems associated with the pre-accession strategy and with parts of the common agricultural policy in which the Integrated Administration and Control System was being properly used.

The Council has examined the Commission communication on a timetable for the creation of an integrated internal control framework, which would make a positive statement of assurance more likely.

The Economic and Financial Affairs Council, in the conclusions that it adopted in relation to this communication on 8 November 2005(2), confirmed that a ‘positive DAS’ was an ambitious medium-term goal, and went on to make a number of recommendations relating to such subjects as simplification, control systems, measures to be taken by the Commission, the role of the Member States, declarations and decentralise management of EU funds and audit issues. A number of these aspects are also reflected in the InterInstitutional Agreement between Parliament, the Council and the Commission, which was signed this morning.

Where the combating of fraud is concerned, the Council attaches the greatest importance to the protection of the European Union’s financial interests and shares the honourable Member’s view that it is in the interests of the EU to take effective action to make the EU’s expenditure more effective and make better use of the resources allocated to all programmes.

On 8 November 2005, the Council also adopted conclusions on the special report No 1/2005(3) of the Court of Auditors on the management of the European Anti-Fraud Office (OLAF)(4), in which it strongly urged both OLAF and the Member States to step up their cooperation in order to achieve better coordination of their anti-fraud activities and improve the exchange of information.

In this connection, the Commission is contemplating submitting an amended proposal relating to Regulation No 1073/1999, and the Council will carefully examine it.

Finally, the Council wishes to point out that the President of the Court of Auditors, when presenting the Court’s annual report for 2004 to Parliament on 14 November 2005, stated that the Court’s qualified opinion was to be interpreted neither to mean that all the transactions to which the payments budget for 2004 referred were defective, nor as an indication that fraud had been committed.

In line with its 8 November 2005 conclusions, the Council will continue to endeavour to improve sound financial management in all areas of the EU.

Question no 13 by Erna Hennicot-Schoepges (H-0143/06)
 Subject: Games of chance
 

During the debates in preparation for the ‘services’ directive we were told that an impact study on the volume and transfers of money in connection with games of chance was not available.

Does the Council intend to have such a study carried out?

Can the Council tell me what measures are being implemented to combat money laundering in this sector?

What amounts, in terms of tax, are paid annually to the budgets of the Member States by gaming companies?

 
  
 

(DE) Directive 2005/60/EC of the European Parliament and of the Council of 26 October 2005 on the prevention of the use of the financial system for the purpose of money laundering and terrorist financing complies with new international standards in containing specific and detailed provisions on the determination of the identities of customers and beneficial owners in relation to business relationships and transactions covered by the directive.

The directive applies, inter alia – as indeed already did the so-called ‘Second Money Laundering Directive’ of 2001 – to casinos and provides that the identity of all visitors to casinos is to be established when they purchase or sell gambling chips with a value of EUR 2 000 or more. Casinos subject to state supervision can also comply with this requirement by checking the identity of patrons as soon as they enter the casino. The directive also applies to those activities in which those institutions and persons subject to it engage over the Internet. The function of these rules, which were drafted in accordance with the international standards of the Financial Action Task Force on Money Laundering (FATF), is to effectively combat the use of games of chance for money laundering purposes in the European Union.

The Council is not in possession of statistics showing what precise amounts of tax are paid annually by gaming enterprises.

 

Question no 14 by Seán Ó Neachtain (H-0335/06)
 Subject: Money laundering
 

Can the European Council make a comprehensive statement as to the effectiveness of existing EU legislation to combat money laundering within the European Union ?

 
  
 

(DE) The legal framework in the European Union provides comprehensive protection against money laundering and terrorist financing. With the Second Directive on money laundering the European Union took a major step towards ensuring harmonised implementation of the international standards of the Financial Action Task Force on Money Laundering (FATF).

On the basis of new developments during the revision of the FATF standards the decision was made within the European Union to take account of these as soon as possible, and to revise the 2001 Second Directive on money laundering: Directive 2005/60/EC of the European Parliament and of the Council of 26 October 2005 on the prevention of the use of the financial system for the purpose of money laundering and terrorist financing came into force on 15 December 2005 and repealed Directives 91/308/EEC and 2001/97/EC.

Pursuant to Article 45 of Directive 2005/60/EC Member States must bring into force the laws, regulations and administrative provisions necessary to implement the Directive by 15 December 2007. Until such national measures are in place it is impossible to judge the Directive's effectiveness. Pursuant to Article 42 of the Directive, meanwhile, the Commission is required to submit a report to the European Parliament and the Council by 15 December 2009, and at least at three-yearly intervals thereafter, on the implementation of the Directive.

 

Question no 15 by Hubert Pirker (H-0367/06)
 Subject: Combating tax fraud
 

The Austrian EU Presidency announced that it would give a prominent place in its work programme to combating VAT fraud. What concrete initiatives have been taken on this in the past few months? What concrete measures are planned in this area between now and the end of the Presidency?

 
  
 

(DE) The Council has a continuing interest in combating VAT fraud. The work it does in relation to Value Added Tax is intended not only to simplify the obligations on market participants but also to safeguard the collection of tax by state treasuries and combat the evasion and avoidance of tax.

This includes an increasing number of Council decisions under Article 27 of the Sixth Value Added Tax Directive and the Council’s work on the proposal to amend the Sixth Value Added Tax Directive with certain measures to simplify the collection of VAT and to combat tax evasion and avoidance.

As the honourable Member has emphasised, the combating of VAT fraud has an important place in the working programme that the Austrian Presidency has presented together with the future Finnish Presidency.

The Austrian Presidency organised a high-level meeting in Vienna, on 6 and 7 February of this year, the primary subject of which was the issue of VAT fraud.

It informed the Council, at its 14 February meeting, of the results of this meeting. It was on this occasion that the Commission announced its intention of reporting to the Council before the end of June concerning the work already carried out on the matters discussed at this meeting.

It is also worthy of note that the Commission wishes, pursuant to its legislative and working programme for 2006, to present to Parliament and to the Council a communication on the strategy whereby tax evasion may be more effectively combated.

 

Question no 16 by Danutė Budreikaitė (H-0275/06)
 Subject: Introduction of the euro in Lithuania
 

In May 2006 the European Union will be required to take a decision on the requests for introduction of the euro submitted by Lithuania and Slovenia. This will be the first genuine enlargement of the euro zone, which will at the same time see the introduction of euro banknotes and coins. According to various political speculations, some new Member States do not have the necessary financial prosperity to form part of the euro zone. The Director of the European Central Bank has stated that it is not enough to meet the Maastricht criteria at the time of assessment, but also in a longer-term perspective.

Among the eleven countries which joined the euro zone on 1 January 1999, no fewer than six have failed, at one time or another, to meet the price stability criteria. In Ireland, for example, inflation was higher than authorised between November 1998 and June 2005.

Inflation in Lithuania may not exceed the Maastricht criteria by more than 0.1% despite the fact that it was lower than the limit set by the criteria for six years.

In assessing the situation in the new countries applying to join the euro zone, will consideration be given to the experience of euro zone countries as regards exceeding authorised inflation rates and infringement by certain euro zone countries of the Stability and Growth Pact requirements? Does the Council not consider that account should be taken of external factors, such as the rise in energy prices and the fact that it is actually impossible to have rapid economic growth and price cuts simultaneously? Would it not also be appropriate to improve the Maastricht criteria themselves in order to avoid an unintended fall in commodity prices in Finland and the Netherlands, which have a lower rate of inflation?

 
  
 

(DE) At least once every two years, or at the request of a Member State with a derogation, the Commission and the European Central Bank are obliged, under Article 122(2) of the Treaty, to report to the Council on the progress made by the Member States in fulfilling their obligations regarding the achievement of economic and monetary union, and on whether or not a high degree of sustainable convergence has been achieved.

The Commission can also, if it thinks fit, submit suitable proposals for legal acts to suspend the derogation for a Member State and also to determine the exchange rate at which that country’s currency shall be replaced by the euro.

The criteria by which sustainable convergence is to be evaluated are laid down in the Treaty and in a protocol thereto. Under the Treaty, the Commission and the European Central Bank assess the degree to which the individual Member States meet these criteria and communicate the results of their assessment to the Council.

In accordance with the Treaty, the Council reaches a final decision on the suspension of individual derogations after consulting Parliament and following discussion among the Heads of State or Government. This is done on the basis of a proposal from the Commission.

Slovenia and Lithuania have formally asked the Commission and the ECB to draft the relevant reports and submit them to the Council. The Council looks forward with interest to receiving these reports and will examine them, and the proposals for legislation appended to them, with the utmost care. The European Parliament will also, in the manner provided for by the Treaty, be able to express an opinion on the legislation.

 

Question no 17 by Eugenijus Gentvilas (H-0316/06)
 Subject: Introduction of the euro in new EU Member States
 

The requests made by Slovenia and Lithuania for assessments to be made of those countries’ chances of adopting the euro raise new questions on the future of the euro zone. Before the introduction of the euro, certain parameters (e.g. inflation) were calculated on the basis of all EU countries, because none of them had adopted the euro at that stage. It would now be appropriate to calculate inflation only on the basis of parameters of the countries in the euro zone. To calculate average inflation on the basis of countries that have not even made a commitment to fulfil the Maastricht criteria on inflation is today illogical and hinders enlargement of the euro zone. Some countries have artificially reduced inflation by lowering duty on alcohol and petroleum products, making it more difficult for countries that reject state control of their economies to join the euro zone.

What steps does the Council intend to take to ensure that enlargement of the euro zone is not held back by artificial measures? Are there plans to amend the Maastricht criteria (which are entirely technical in nature) to ensure that the requirements laid down for countries aspiring to join the euro zone are supported by economic logic? Do not the current criteria make the EU a bureaucratic anachronism in which neither political will nor economic principles have any influence?

 
  
 

(DE) The Council will apply the procedure under Article 122(2) of the Treaty in order to evaluate whether the Member States with a derogation comply with the conditions required for the introduction of the euro. This procedure has been used before, when the derogation for Greece was lifted in 2000.

Under the procedure, the Commission and the European Central Bank report to the Council on whether the Member States in question have achieved a high degree of sustainable convergence. If a Member State has succeeded in doing so, the Commission can propose that the derogation for this Member State be abrogated and that it should adopt the euro as its currency. The Council can act only in response to a proposal to this effect from the Commission.

The Council is highly confident that the Commission and the European Central Bank will discharge the obligations placed on them by the Treaty and will apply the convergence criteria listed in it. There is no intention that these criteria should be amended.

 

Question no 18 by Richard Seeber (H-0365/06)
 Subject: Extending the euro area
 

On 16 May 2006 the Commission and the European Central Bank presented their convergence reports on Slovenia and Lithuania.

What is your judgment of the assessment by the Commission and by the European Central Bank? What is the next stage in the procedure?

 
  
 

(DE) The Council will consider the convergence reports from the Commission and the European Central Bank when it meets on 7 June. The Council is unable to state a position on the reports before this discussion has taken place.

Article 122(2) of the European Communities Treaty explicitly lays down the procedure in accordance with which a decision is reached as to whether the derogation applicable to a Member State should be abrogated so that it may be enabled to adopt the euro; it is this procedure that will, of course be used. A decision will be reached on each and every proposal from the Commission following consultation with the European Parliament and debate in the Council composed of the Heads of State or Government.

It is envisaged that such a debate should take place in the margin of the meeting of the European Council of 15/16 June in order that the proper procedures may be completed by the formal adoption of the relevant Council decisions on 11 July.

 

Question no 19 by Liam Aylward (H-0331/06)
 Subject: Single euro payments area
 

Within the European Union, it can take up to five days for a euro payment to be made from one Member State to another. This is an unacceptable delay at a time when the single European currency is supposed to be growing from strength to strength.

Can the European Council therefore make a statement as to the progress that has been made to introduce a single euro payments Area within the different territories of the European Union?

 
  
 

(DE) I should like to refer the honourable Member to my answer to Question H-1121/05.

In December 2005 the Commission presented a proposal for a directive of the European Parliament and the Council on payment services in the internal market; the proposal sets deadlines for both national and cross-border payment transactions.

Providers of payment services are to be required to credit payments in euros within the European Union to the payee's account essentially within one day. To allow the banks time to adjust their infrastructure to the new requirements, this deadline may be extended to a maximum of three days until 1 January 2010 – subject to an agreement to that effect.

The negotiations on this proposal for a directive within a Council working party have commenced under Austrian chairmanship, and are making rapid progress. In addition, there are close contacts with the European Parliament, and in particular with the rapporteur of the committee responsible (ECON), so that an agreement can be reached with the European Parliament and this directive can be adopted as quickly as possible under the codecision procedure.

 

Question no 20 by Manuel Medina Ortega (H-0297/06)
 Subject: Financial perspective and funds earmarked for immigration
 

Does the Council believe that the funds earmarked for immigration in the 2007-2013 financial perspective will be enough to tackle the exponential rise in immigration into the European Union as a whole?

 
  
 

(DE) As is clearly stated in the explanatory note, the object of the proposals for the establishment of a fund under the programme for solidarity and the management of migratory flows for the period from 2007 to 2013 (COM(2005) 123 final) is to ‘address the issue of a fair share of responsibilities between Member States as concerns the financial burden arising from the introduction of an integrated management of the Union’s external borders and from the implementation of common policies on asylum and immigration.’

The proposals with more specific reference to immigration, in particular those for the establishment of a fund for integration and a fund for repatriation, are aimed at guaranteeing economic support for the Member States in implementing measures in these areas and for Community initiatives towards solidarity and cooperation.

The sums allocated to each fund have been arrived at following negotiations on the budget within the framework of the Financial Perspective, in which the European Parliament played a decisive part.

 

Question no 21 by Chris Davies (H-0303/06)
 Subject: Declarations of national assurance
 

At their meeting of 8 November 2005, EU finance ministers refused to adopt the Parliament's proposal P6_TA(2005)0092(1) to require ministers to personally assume responsibility for how EU money is spent in their countries by making Declarations of National Assurance.

On 15 November 2005, the European Court of Auditors refused for the 11th consecutive time to issue a positive Declaration of Assurance as to the legality and regularity of EU financial transactions.

Will the Council now reconsider the principle that government ministers should take personal responsibility for the spending of EU funds in their own Member States?

 
  
 

(DE) The Council considers it very important that there should be further improvement in financial management, and that controls should work correctly and effectively, so that, in the medium term, a positive statement of assurance (DAS) can be given for all transactions underlying the EU’s accounts.

With regard to the financial year 2004, the Council regrets that payments relating to CAP expenditure, which is not covered by the integrated administration and control system (IACS), to structural measures and to internal and external policy areas are still materially affected by errors. However, the Council notes with satisfaction the progress found in that financial year in areas where supervisory and control systems are implemented and allow for appropriate risk management, that is, revenue, commitments, administrative expenditure and pre-accession strategy. The Council is pleased to note that, with regard to CAP expenditure, the Court was able to conclude that the IACS, if properly used, is an effective system for limiting the risk of irregular payments.

The Council commented on the question of declarations at national level in its conclusions of 8 November 2005. As the honourable Member is aware, the President of the Council, Mr Finz, raised the question of national declarations when presenting the Council’s discharge recommendation at Parliament’s Committee on Budgetary Control on 20 March 2006. He stated that with regard to compatibility with administrative structures in the Member States, particularly those which are federally organised, this would entail considerable difficulties, pointing out how important it is that, in keeping with the EC Treaty, the Commission's ultimate responsibility for Community funds be maintained. He further pointed out that, in the Council’s view, the introduction of such statements could lead to an increased administrative burden and additional costs for Member States. With regard to the aim of attaining a positive DAS, then, the Council proposes an approach based on improving existing declarations at operational level with greater Member State involvement.

(EN) The competition rules of the EC Treaty establish the framework for ensuring undistorted competition in the common market. These rules ensure that media undertakings do not engage in anti-competitive behaviour or abuse a dominant position in what is a rapidly evolving media environment. These rules also ensure that competition between undertakings - irrespective of their private or public ownership - is not distorted through State aid which is incompatible with the Common Market.
