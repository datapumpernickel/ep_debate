QUESTIONS TO THE COUNCIL (The Presidency-in-Office of the Council of the European Union bears sole responsibility for these answers
Question no 1 by Manuel Medina Ortega (H-0872/07)
 Subject: Criminal penalties to combat ship-source pollution
 

Given the ruling of the Court of Justice of 23 October annulling the Council Framework Decision to strengthen the criminal-law framework for the enforcement of the law against ship-source pollution, what steps does the Council think should be adopted to fill the resulting legal lacuna?

 
  
 

This answer was drafted by the Presidency and is not binding on the Council or the Member States as such.

I would point out that the laws promulgated in the Member States to comply with the annulled Framework Decision to which the honourable Member refers, are not automatically rendered invalid by the annulment. The decision as to the consequences of a declaration of non-validity of a Community act for the national acts transposing it is an issue of national law (see Judgment of the Court of Justice of 20 October 1975, Case 23/75). That said, the Commission must now decide whether to submit a proposal to the Council in order to include in the Directive in force an obligation for Member States to provide for penal sanctions to ensure that Community standards are respected as regards ship-source pollution.

 

Question no 2 by Georgios Papastamkos (H-0874/07)
 Subject: European educational area
 

How successful has the Portuguese Presidency been so far in promoting a European educational area and what are its priorities up to the end of the programme period?

 
  
 

This answer was drafted by the Presidency and is not binding on the Council or the Member States as such.

As far as the European Qualifications Framework is concerned, the Portuguese Presidency has collaborated closely with the EP’s rapporteur to draft a text that is acceptable to all parties and which, notwithstanding certain procedural formalities, should receive official approval at the beginning of next year. It is hoped that this ambitious new instrument will prove useful as a link between different countries’ qualifications systems, acting as a translation tool making qualifications more comprehensible for Member States, employers and individuals, and thus enabling each citizen to move more easily from one country to another to work or study.

The second area concerns talks between Parliament and the Council with a view to reaching a consensus on the proposal for setting up the EIT (European Institute of Technology). If everything continues to run as smoothly and constructively as it has to date, it is likely that an agreement can be reached quickly at second reading at the beginning of 2008. Although they are not directly linked to the creation of the European Higher Education Area, higher education establishments will undoubtedly play a key role in the EIT, and it is therefore hoped that they will make a significant contribution to creating that Area.

In addition to these two dossiers, the Portuguese Presidency’s work in the area of higher education has focused on three issues. Firstly, on the conference held in Lisbon on 4 and 5 October 2007 to mark the 20th anniversary of the Erasmus Programme. One question arising from that conference – how to widen the Programme’s social dimension – was discussed by Ministers at the Council meeting on 15 and 16 November 2007. Secondly, the proposal to extend its ‘sister’ programme, the Erasmus Mundus Programme (and the Council is looking forward to hearing Parliament’s opinion on that subject), was also discussed by the Ministers at the 15 November meeting. Lastly, the third issue is that of finding the best way to respond to the modernisation agenda for European universities, on which the Council approved a Resolution at the same November meeting. All these issues can be regarded as making a contribution towards creating the European Higher Education Area.

 

Question no 3 by Claude Moraes (H-0877/07)
 Subject: EU cooperation on asthma
 

Asthma is the major chronic childhood illness in Europe, costing the European Community more than three billion euros each year. According to the charity Asthma UK, 5.2 million people in the UK are currently receiving treatment for asthma, 1.1 million of whom are children, and there is a person with asthma in one in five households in the UK.

The EU is currently funding a major research project (Gabriel) involving over 150 scientists from 14 European countries in order to identify key factors in the development of asthma. Findings so far suggest that asthma is caused by a combination of genetic and environmental effects, and that increasing urbanisation may be contributing to the rise in the numbers affected.

What measures are Member States taking to complement the research with practical measures to combat asthma?

 
  
 

This answer was drafted by the Presidency and is not binding on the Council or the Member States as such.

The Council has already examined several of the fundamental factors in the development of asthma. In terms of public health measures, in its Recommendation of 2 December 2002 on the prevention of smoking and on initiatives to improve tobacco control (OJ L 22 25.01.2003, p. 31.), the Council points out that given the health risks (e.g. asthma) linked to passive smoking, Member States should try to protect smokers and non-smokers from exposure to environmental tobacco smoke.

The Council recommends that Member States should implement legislation and/or other effective measures that provide protection from exposure to environmental tobacco smoke in enclosed public places and public transport. Several Member States have already effectively banned smoking in public places chiefly to protect children and pregnant women from exposure to tobacco smoke.

As for air quality, on 25 June 2007 the Council approved its Common Position on the proposal for a directive on ambient air quality and cleaner air for Europe. One of the proposal’s objectives for ambient air quality is the protection of human health, which includes targeting emissions of pollutants that aggravate asthma attacks. As well as covering the main air pollutants; special attention is given in this proposal to fine dust (also referred to as particulates) and to tropospheric ozone, as they pose a major risk to human health. This proposal will set rules for fine particulates present in the air, known as PM 2.5, which penetrate deep into human lungs.

As regards ensuring that asthma sufferers have access to health services and medical care, it should be borne in mind that organising the provision of health services and medical care, and in particular ensuring that consultation, diagnostic and treatment services are available for patients, is the sole responsibility of the Member States.

 

Question no 4 by Sarah Ludford (H-0882/07)
 Subject: Implementation of international human rights instruments by Member States
 

Has the Council established a formal common position whereby Member States should sign and ratify the UN Convention on Enforced Disappearance, the UN Convention on Corruption, Protocol No 13 to the European Convention on Human Rights (on abolition of the death penalty in all circumstances) and the European Convention against Trafficking?

What mechanisms exist in the Council to ensure that Member States implement these and other important international human rights instruments?

 
  
 

This answer was drafted by the Presidency and is not binding on the Council or the Member States as such.

The Council of the European Union always tries to ensure that Member States deposit their respective instruments of ratification at the same time. However, Member States are not obliged to do so.

The Council can inform the honourable Member that 17 Member States have so far signed the UN Convention for the Protection of All Persons from Enforced Disappearance.

The UN Convention on Corruption has been signed by 25 Member States and ratified by 17.

As regards Protocol No 13 of the European Convention on Human Rights, it has already been ratified by 22 EU Member States and signed by 5 more.

Lastly, it is of note that the European Convention against Trafficking in Human Beings has already been signed by 23 Member States and ratified by 6.

Question no 7 by Olle Schmidt (H-0887/07)
 Subject: Criminalisation of drug khat within the EU
 

Misuse of the drug khat is an increasingly common occurrence in Europe. Some 60 tonnes of khat are smuggled into my home town of Malmö each year across the Öresund bridge alone. The people affected often belong to traditionally vulnerable groups, such as refugees and those living in socially deprived areas. Young people and children are affected most. The efforts of the customs service to stop imports of khat, therefore, not only form part of the fight against drug abuse but also perform an important function in combating exclusion and social stigmatisation in society.

Unfortunately, the EU does not speak with one voice on the subject of khat. In the UK and the Netherlands, the drug has yet to be classified as such. The outcome of this situation is that those countries currently operate as transit destinations from which khat - which is perishable and not storage tolerant - can be forwarded and smuggled into countries where it is illegal.

Does the Portuguese Presidency intend to advocate the total criminalisation of khat throughout the EU and, therefore, a more restrictive drugs policy?

 
  
 

This answer was drafted by the Presidency and is not binding on the Council or the Member States as such.

Full criminalisation of khat has not been discussed in the Council bodies dealing with such matters. Council Framework Decision 2004/757/JHA laying down minimum provisions on the constituent elements of criminal acts and penalties in the field of illicit drug trafficking, covers drugs that are illegal under the terms of the 1961 Single Convention on Narcotic Drugs, amended by the 1971 Protocol, the 1972 Vienna Convention on Psychotropic Substances, as well as substances subject to control pursuant to Joint Action 97/396/JHA. Khat is not covered by any of those instruments and no initiatives have been taken to include it.

Question no 9 by Dimitrios Papadimoulis (H-0890/07)
 Subject: Implementation by Turkey of the EC-Turkey Association Agreement and the Additional Protocol
 

In paragraph 12 of its recent resolution on EU-Turkey relations (P6_TA(2007)0472, 24 October 2007), Parliament ' urges the new Turkish Government to fully implement the provisions stemming from the EC-Turkey Association Agreement and its Additional Protocol; recalls that the non-fulfilment of Turkey's commitments as referred to in the Accession Partnership will continue seriously to affect the process of negotiations;'.

In what way is the Council exerting pressure on Turkey fully to implement the EC-Turkey Association Agreement and the Additional Protocol? Has Turkey made any commitment to the Council concerning the timetable for signing the Additional Protocol?

 
  
 

(PT) This answer was drafted by the Presidency and is not binding on the Council or the Member States as such.

In December last year, the Council noted that Turkey had not fulfilled its obligation of full non-discriminatory implementation of the Additional Protocol to the Association Agreement. The Member States, therefore, meeting at the Intergovernmental Conference, decided not to start negotiations on the chapters covering areas of action related to Turkey’s restrictions on the Republic of Cyprus until the Commission had confirmed that Turkey had fulfilled the commitments which it entered into under the Additional Protocol. The chapters are as follows: Chapter 1: Free Movement of Goods; Chapter 3: Right of Establishment and Freedom to Provide Services; Chapter 9: Financial Services; Chapter 11: Agriculture and Rural Development; Chapter 13: Fisheries; Chapter 14: Transport Policy; Chapter 29: Customs Union; Chapter 30: External Relations. Moreover, the Council agreed that Member States meeting at the Intergovernmental Conference would not provisionally close any chapter until the Commission had confirmed that Turkey had fully implemented the commitments which it assumed under the Additional Protocol.

The Council also agreed it would monitor and analyse the progress made on questions covered by the declaration of 21 September 2005 and invited the Commission to include information on this matter in its next annual reports, namely in 2007, 2008 and 2009, if relevant. In its 2007 report on Turkey the Commission noted that, since the Council Decision of December of the previous year, Turkey had made no progress whatsoever towards full non-discriminatory implementation of the Additional Protocol. The Council made a detailed analysis and assessment of the report at its meeting on Monday 10 December. In its conclusions, the Council noted with regret that Turkey had not fulfilled its obligation of full non-discriminatory implementation of the Additional Protocol to the Association Agreement and added that it would continue to follow up and review progress on those issues. However, I can reiterate how important it is for Turkey to deal with this issue as quickly as possible, as it is clearly affecting the progress of accession negotiations.

 

Question no 10 by Bernd Posselt (H-0891/07)
 Subject: Situation in Angola
 

What is the Council’s view of the situation in Angola, which has been afflicted by war and civil war for so long? What has it done in 2007 to support this important partner of the European Union?

 
  
 

This answer was drafted by the Presidency and is not binding on the Council or the Member States as such.

The Council has been following the situation in Angola closely. The general framework governing relations between the EU and Angola is still the Cotonou Agreement and entails the political dialogue established pursuant to Article 8 of that Agreement and development cooperation under the European Development Fund. The dialogue provided for under Article 8 was launched in December 2003 marking the commitment, although it did lose some steam in subsequent years; the EU is now making endeavours to revitalise and consolidate this important instrument (for the record, the last talks were held in October 2007).

Relations between the EU and Angola have generally developed slowly in key cooperation areas established under the aegis of the Cotonou Agreement as regards, specifically, democratisation, development of civil society, respect for human rights, good governance and combating corruption. Hence there has been only a slight improvement in overall relations since 13 October 2003, when the issue was last dealt with in the Council.

While Angola has specific interests in the Democratic Republic of the Congo, it continues to be an important partner as regards stabilisation of the Great Lakes region and has contributed to the EU’s Police Mission in the DRC (‘EUPOL Kinshasa’). Angola seconded 13 Angolan police officers over the electoral period (July 2006 to March 2007) to help the Congolese police guarantee safe elections. That was the first time the Angolan police force had assumed an international commitment of that kind. Moreover, the EUSR for the Great Lakes is in regular contact with the Angolan authorities.

Question no 14 by Colm Burke (H-0896/07)
 Subject: ESDP Mission to Chad, CAR and Darfur Border
 

Can the Council indicate what Member States – apart from Ireland, France, Belgium, and Poland – will be contributing troops to the ESDP mission to the Chad, Darfur and Central African Republic border? Can the Council and EU Member States mobilise sufficient political, financial, logistical and technical support for troops forming part of this mission?

Does the Council realise the importance of not having our troops ill-equipped like the beleaguered African Union Mission in Sudan (AMIS)? Does the Council agree that if we are putting our troops in a volatile environment we must give them full means to protect themselves?

What were the Council’s impressions of the talks on Darfur in Tripoli at the end of October? What moves has the Council been making to convince the many and fragmented rebel groups to come to the table to negotiate? Does the Council agree that, owing to the lack of participation of some of the main rebel groups in Darfur, the talks were something of a failure? Has the Council urged the Sudanese Government and rebel groups to cooperate fully with an independent investigation into the attack in Haskanita and to bring all those responsible to justice?

What has the Council been doing in relation to the Sudanese Government’s continued refusal to cooperate with the International Criminal Court?

 
  
 

This answer was drafted by the Presidency and is not binding on the Council or the Member States as such.

Preparations continue for the ESDP mission to Eastern Chad and the northeast of the Central African Republic, and this includes planning for the composition of the force. Thus far Belgium, Greece, Spain, France, Ireland, the Netherlands, Austria, Poland, Romania, Finland and Sweden have pledged contributions to the force. The force will receive the appropriate equipment and support. EUFOR/CHAD/CAR will operate within the scope of Chapter VII, following unanimous approval of UN Security Council Resolution 1778, with a suitable mandate to match the situation on the ground.

The Council has been concerned about the peace negotiations in Darfur in recent months. In this regard, the June, July and October 2007 conclusions from the (External Relations) Council meetings clearly set out the policy line followed in many bilateral and multilateral contacts, including those by SG/HR Javier Solana and the EU Special Representative (EUSR) in Sudan. That line includes strongly urging all parties to play a constructive part in the negotiations and to reach an agreement to put an immediate end to the violence. Moreover, the Council has highlighted the need for an independent investigation into the Haskanita attack as well as cooperation with the International Criminal Court. The Council also stressed that it was willing to consider new measures, namely within the framework of the United Nations, against any party obstructing implementation of the UN aid package and the deployment of UNAMID as well as measures guaranteeing humanitarian supplies and the protection of civilians.

With regard to the peace negotiations in Darfur, the Council firmly supports joint AU/UN mediation headed by the AU and UN special envoys. The difficult start to the negotiations in Sirte last October posed a special challenge. At present, it is still not clear whether, when, in what form and under what body the talks can resume. Despite the difficulties, we are aiming for an ongoing process and in this respect much is being done to make the peace negotiations genuinely comprehensive with wide-ranging participation. We are clearly involved in a difficult and complex process, namely with regard to consolidating the position of different rebel groups and ensuring they participate in future talks. With this objective in mind, the EUSR in Sudan, Ambassador Brylle, remains in close contact with the main stakeholders in this process.

 

Question no 15 by Jim Higgins (H-0898/07)
 Subject: Palestine
 

Could the Council provide an up-to-date review of the current Palestinian-Israeli situation and the EU’s role in this context?

 
  
 

This answer was drafted by the Presidency and is not binding on the Council or the Member States as such.

Israel and the Palestinians have been engaged in a political dialogue since last summer, starting with several meetings between Prime Minister Olmert and President Abbas. Question no 90 by Hans-Peter Martin (H-0957/07)
 Subject: Cost of the pensions of contract agents in the European Union agencies
 

The European Union agencies currently employ several hundred contract agents, who are entitled to pensions funded from the EU budget.

Has the Commission had any thoughts, and/or made any calculations, concerning future expenditure from the EU budget on pensions for contract agents in the Community Agencies?

If so, what form have these thoughts and/or calculations taken? Does the Commission foresee any problem with the cost of contract agents' pensions?

 
  
 

Contract agents taken on by executive agencies and regulatory agencies pay the same contribution to the pension scheme as officials and temporary staff (1/3 of the amount necessary for actuarial balance, which is currently 10.25% of basic salary) and they acquire the same pension rights (1.9% of their final basic salary per year up to a maximum of 70%).

The situation of contract agents is therefore no different from that of officials and temporary staff, and falls into two categories:

like officials and temporary staff, contract agents leaving service without having acquired the right to a retirement pension in the European institutions scheme (less than ten years’ service and under 63 years of age) are obliged to transfer the actuarial equivalent of their retirement pension rights, updated to the actual date of transfer, to another pension scheme (Article 109(1) of the CEOS and Annex VIII, Article 11(1) of the Staff Regulations);

contract agents leaving service with at least ten years’ service or after the age of 63 have the right to a pension under the same conditions as officials (Article 109(1) of the CEOS and Article 77 of the Staff Regulations).

The funds necessary for the payment of pensions are provided for in the budget (Title 30, Chapter 1, Article 13). The 2008 preliminary draft budget provides a breakdown of the calculations, including the estimated number of pensioners of the agencies(1).

 
 

(1) see COM(2007) 300, pp. 313 ff.
