Questions to the Council
Question no 18 by Bernd Posselt (H-0572/00)
 Subject: EU-Morocco
 

What is the current stage reached in negotiations between the EU and Morocco, and what prospects does the Council see for cooperation with this important EU neighbour?

 
  
 

Relations between the European Union and Morocco are based on the Euro-Mediterranean Association Agreement, signed on 26 February 1999, which came into force on 1 March 2000, and on the Barcelona Process which establishes the framework for relations between the EU and the countries of the southern Mediterranean. This agreement, the same type as those proposed to other Mediterranean partners under the Barcelona Process, should make it possible to strengthen the already privileged links that exist between the European Union and Morocco by establishing relations based on reciprocity, codevelopment and dialogue.

This agreement forms part of an overall cooperation strategy intended to establish, in a spirit of partnership, an area of political stability and economic prosperity throughout the entire Euro-Mediterranean region.

Respect for democratic principles and human rights forms an important part of the agreement. The bodies created under the agreement, the Association Council of ministers and the Association Committee of civil servants, meet once a year, providing a forum for dialogue on all the aspects covered by the agreement, including the political aspects.

The agreement provides for the development of trade with a view to implementing a Euro-Mediterranean free exchange area by 2010. To this end, it contributes to cooperation in economical and financial, human, social and cultural fields. The implementation of this agreement is broadly supported by the European Union under a financial cooperation arrangement. This has been in place since 1996, particularly through the MEDA regulation. Between 1996 and 1999, MEDA allocated EUR 581 million to Morocco. Between 1996 and 1998 the MEDA-Democracy programme allocated financial support of around EUR 2.4 million to projects in the field of democracy and human rights. In addition, since 1996, the EIB has granted Morocco EUR 392 million in loans to contribute mainly to funding major infrastructure projects.

In the area of agriculture, the agreement includes a periodic review clause providing for negotiations, as of this year, on the conditions for the gradual liberalisation of agricultural trade. These negotiations will cover a number of sensitive products, as shown recently by the dispute arising after the quota for Moroccan tomato exports allowed into the Community market duty free was exceeded.

Furthermore, the Commission will be looking into appropriate proposals to open negotiations with Morocco as soon as possible in order to pursue mutually advantageous cooperation in the fisheries sector. These new arrangements will have to be obtained in the spirit of partnership.

On 11 October 1999, the Council adopted an Action Plan for Morocco, as the country of origin of many asylum seekers and immigrants, with a view to investigating solutions to the deep-seated causes of emigration from this country as part of an overall approach. Two investigative teams have already visited Morocco in order to establish an ongoing dialogue with the relevant authorities regarding emigration. The Council meeting on 29 May 2000 welcomed the progress achieved in implementing this Action Plan.

The Council considers that the various instruments set in place in this way, the establishment of regular political dialogue under the Association Agreement, and the prospects for future cooperation justify looking forward with confidence to deepening relations between the European Union and this extremely important partner.

 

Question no 21 by Bárbara Dührkop Dührkop (H-0580/00)
 Subject: Future of Community programmes for decentralised cooperation with Latin America
 

Is the Council aware of plans to suspend (on either a temporary or a permanent basis) the Community programmes for decentralised cooperation with Latin America (ALFA, ALURE, AL-INVEST, URB-AL etc.)? What view does the Council take of this possibility?

 
  
 

We should like to remind the Honourable Member that the ALFA, ALURE, AL-INVEST and URB-AL programmes were adopted by the Commission after consulting the opinion of the LAADC Committee. Consequently, only the Commission has the authority to suspend these programmes, temporarily or permanently. The Council is nevertheless in a position to point out that it has received no notification from the Commission, either officially or informally, of its intention to suspend these programmes.

 

Question no 24 by Luisa Morgantini (H-0591/00)
 Subject: Displacement by violence in Colombia
 

In Colombia over 2 million people (including more than 300 000 in 1999) have been displaced by violence.

Apart from providing humanitarian aid (which will become increasingly necessary as each day goes by, unless solutions are found to the problems which cause people to become displaced), what action is the Council taking in order to help Colombia tackle the causes of displacement, in particular the atrocities committed by paramilitary groups?

 
  
 

In 1999, EUR 6.5 million of aid intended for people who have become displaced as a result of violence in Colombia was approved.

The European Union is closely monitoring events in Colombia, both at political and diplomatic level and through contacts with civil society and the NGOs involved with the situation in that country.

While the European Union supports the efforts made by the Colombian Government to move the peace process forwards, as it has confirmed on a number of occasions, it has insisted on the commitment which the Colombian Government must provide in order to improve the human rights situation in Colombia.

The European Union has also appealed to the commitment of all parties to respect human rights and fundamental freedoms and to seek a negotiated solution to the conflict as the only possible basis for a sustainable peace to be established in Colombia.

The European Union has strenuously condemned the serious human rights violations which are still being committed, and also the acts of terrorism and infringements of international law, of which all the armed groups are guilty.

On several occasions, the European Union has assured the democratically elected authorities in Colombia of its willingness to support – as far as it is able and by the most appropriate means – all efforts to achieve a peace agreement based on respect for human rights and fundamental freedoms.

 

Questions to the Commission
Question no 34 by Astrid Thors (H-0594/00)
 Subject: Humanitarian aid for Liberia
 

Is the Commission aware that the situation in Liberia has deteriorated as a result of the reduction in humanitarian aid and the fact that the USA has precipitated a state of emergency in that country, which affects its weakest members? Is the Commission prepared, in this situation, to support non-governmental organisations such as UNICEF despite the misgivings about the country's role in the trade in diamonds from Sierra Leone?

 
  
 

Liberia continues to suffer from the effects of the seven years of civil war (1989-96). GDP per capita remains at around US$ 200, or roughly one third of the pre-war level. Government capacity is very poor, and there is still hardly any electricity.

The United States have significantly reduced their aid programme to Liberia compared to pre-war levels. As of today, the Community is probably the most important donor in the country. The Commission re-opened an office in the capital Monrovia in 1999. Since the end of the civil conflict in Liberia in 1996, the Community has gradually phased out its humanitarian emergency assistance programmes and switched towards multi-sector rehabilitation programmes. A first programme of EUR 25 m has been implemented from 1994 to 1998. A second programme of EUR 27 m is presently under implementation. It concentrates on providing vital services to the population such as water and drug supplies, resettlement of returnees, rehabilitation of basic rural infrastructures. These programmes are widely seen as having played an important role in stabilising Liberia after the civil war.

The Commission is fully committed to continue providing essential services to the Liberian population. Wherever this brings operational advantages this will be done in cooperation with non-governmental organisations and/or United Nations organisations.

 

Question no 38 by Juan de Dios Izquierdo Collado (H-0568/00)
 Subject: Supply of water to Zaragoza
 

On 4 February 2000 the Commission forwarded to the Spanish authorities an unfavourable assessment of the project to supply water of a high standard to Zaragoza and the Ebrus corridor. The Spanish authorities subsequently submitted further information to the Commission regarding the value of the project.

In a letter of 23 May, which was signed by Mr Barnier and addressed to the Zaragoza city council, it was stated that the Commission was considering this new information.

Could the Commission envisage financing part of this project out of the Cohesion Fund during the 2000-2006 period? Has the Spanish Government submitted a request in respect of any other project (also to be financed out of the Cohesion Fund between 2000 and 2006) to restore the condition of the Ebrus river?

 
  
 

The Commission confirms that it notified the Spanish authorities, on 4 February 2000, that the project to supply water of a high standard to Zaragoza and the Ebrus corridor was not the type of project that could be part-financed from the Cohesion Fund. This decision was taken following extensive consultation between the Commission’s departments, the Spanish authorities and the EIB.

The Commission is, however, continuing to look into this matter, particularly in the light of the additional information provided by the Spanish authorities. At this stage it is not possible to anticipate the outcome of their investigations, since the project, which is related to a number of current and future schemes for water resources in the Ebrus Basin, raises complex questions which warrant a detailed investigation, and the Commission is undertaking to carry out this investigation in partnership with the Member State as soon as possible.

The Commission can, moreover, assure the Honourable Members that it has not yet received any other proposals from the Spanish authorities regarding other Cohesion Fund projects for the period 2000-2006.

 

Question no 39 by Samuli Pohjamo (H-0574/00)
 Subject: Support for work of NGOs
 

In the EU Member States there are numerous NGOs working for the development of the areas bordering the EU. These associations carry out really valuable grass-roots work in towns, villages and the countryside.

Fund-raising for these associations is carried out on a voluntary basis, often through neighbourly mutual-aid events. The support of society has been quite essential to these associations which work hard year in, year out. In some Member States national development aid to neighbouring regions has been of help, but this represents only a small amount. Some form of action would be in the general interest of the whole EU.

In the areas neighbouring the EU what is needed is in many cases to give a boost to fundamental activities, such as creating an entrepreneurial culture, familiarisation with harvesting and storage procedures for agricultural products, etc. In these areas working on one’s own initiative has to be relearned.

The opportunities offered by existing EU programmes such as TACIS and INTERREG too often relate to projects which are too ambitious and are beyond the means and capabilities of people in these border areas.

Has the Commission any plans to help NGOs and associations in their own-initiative activities in a more effective way in the EU’s neighbouring areas? What concrete form would this take?

 
  
 

The Commission is aware of the role of non-governmental organisations in the development of areas bordering the European Union, particularly on the Finland-Russia border. It will continue to encourage their participation in European Union programmes in two forms:

- Interreg does not stipulate any maximum or minimum scale for the selection of a project, or any restrictions on the participation of a partner. Any non-governmental organisation may therefore take part in an Interreg programme.

- Even though TACIS does support a number of large-scale projects, it must be emphasised that – just like the Interreg programme – it does have the opportunity to support smaller projects.

In this connection, the Commission would like to mention the existence, for example, of the LIEN (Link Inter-European NGOs) programme which calls for the participation of non-governmental organisations in small-scale projects (maximum EUR 200 000) intended to boost the social aspects of civil society in the countries concerned.

Moreover, under the TACIS CBC (Cross Border Cooperation) programme, the Commission has always encouraged NGOs to participate in the Small Project Facility in order to improve the economic and social situation of border regions.

 

Question no 40 by Gary Titley (H-0584/00)
 Subject: Contracts with local agents in the administration of European Regional Funds
 

In the administration of European Regional Funds, local agents are contracted to deliver services such as small business advice. Are the contracts concluded between the Commission or its representatives and such local agents open to the public for inspection? If not, why not?

 
  
 

In response to the question posed by the Honourable Member regarding the administration of the ERDF, the Commission or its representatives do not enter into contracts with local agents to provide services such as small business advice.

However, it is possible for programmes cofinanced by the Structural Funds to include measures to provide such services. In such cases, the authorities of the Member State responsible for implementing the programme in question may opt to use local agents with whom, consequently, they sign contracts. The signing of such contracts is therefore subject to national rules on public tenders, and Member States are obliged to comply with Community legislation.

 

Question no 46 by Manuel Pérez Álvarez (H-0550/00)
 Subject: North Sea oil rig
 

The media have been focusing attention, and could hardly have been expected to do otherwise, on the oil rig that broke adrift in the North Sea with 77 people on board.

Leaving aside the alarm caused by the danger to the lives of the persons concerned, which would be sufficient reason in itself to take every form of action deemed necessary, what steps have been or will be taken to ensure that the risks of all kinds posed by such an incident, be it to life at sea, the environment, shipping, and so forth, will never occur again?

 
  
 

The Commission is deeply concerned about all types of marine accidents which endanger human life at sea and the environment. Sea search and rescue operations and response in case of pollution incidents fall however within the responsibility of the Member States.

In particular, concerning the response to accidental marine pollution, the Community plays a central coordinating role, being a party to the most relevant regional agreements (Bonn Agreement, Helsinki Convention, Barcelona Convention) which facilitate mutual assistance whenever a pollution incident threatens coasts. In this context, the Commission has been informed of the Norwegian oil rig accident which did not result in any loss of life or pollution.

In the field of maritime safety the Commission adopted on 21 March 2000 a communication on the safety of seaborne oil trade(1), which envisages different measures to improve maritime safety.

In the field of response to marine pollution the Commission has proposed a decision to strengthen the Community framework for cooperation in the field of marine pollution. This proposal is now under examination by the Council after the second reading of the Parliament.

Question no 47 by Phillip Whitehead (H-0587/00)
 Subject: Separation distances for the cultivation of GM and non-GM crops
 

Does the Commission intend, under new proposals deriving from Directive 90/220/EEC(1) to approve appropriate distances to separate the cultivation of GM crops from ordinary crops intended for organic food production?

 
  
 

The common position on the Commission proposal to revise Directive 90/220/EEC does not specifically provide for separation or isolation distances between genetically modified and conventional crops, including those intended for organic food production.

However, both the existing Directive 90/220/EEC and the common position require that a comprehensive assessment is conducted, on a case-by-case basis, to identify and evaluate potential adverse effects of the GMO that may arise from its deliberate release into the environment. This would, as a matter of course, include assessment of the risk of pollen transfer from GM crops to any type of conventional crop, including crops intended for organic food production, and consequences for human health and the environment.

Measures to limit pollen transfer from genetically modified to conventional crops, where a requirement for risk management measures is identified from the risk assessment, may include separation or isolation distances as part of the final consent. Authorisations are, in any case, only granted on the basis that there is no reason to believe that the release will have an adverse effect on human health or the environment under the conditions of consent.

The Commission is confident that the provisions of the common position will provide the necessary safeguards for human health and the environment with respect to authorisation of GMOs.

Question no 48 by Roy Perry (H-0526/00)
 Subject: Digital radio
 

In the light of the recent hearing on digital radio, is the Commission confident that it is doing enough to ensure that the roll out of digital radio services across Member States is maximising benefits to the European manufacturing industry, broadcasters and consumers?

 
  
 

In an answer to a previous question(1)1 the Commission welcomed the discussions which took place during the hearing on radio, held on 24 May 2000, which confirmed the support from the public that radio enjoys. The meeting also helped to indicate, on the basis of the opinion of the experts who participated in the hearing, that in their view there is a possible market failure which may manifest itself in the period 2010-2015. It is precisely this timescale which makes the approach of "technological neutrality" adopted by the Commission, most appropriate, given the rate at which technologies develop. It has, however, to be borne in mind that local radio lacks a trans-national -European- dimension, which as a result renders the notion of circulation of radio content void of any meaning; nonetheless the Commission is very vigilant on the respect from national authorities of the principle of non-discrimination and freedom of establishment in delivering radio licences to Community operators. Moreover, on the basis of recent studies, radio is one of the most dynamic and flourishing sectors in the audiovisual.

The Commission is interested in a rapid take up of digital services in Europe, including radio, as demonstrated by the recently launched eEurope initiative. The Commission, however, cannot interfere with the preferences of consumers and considers that digital radio can best be administered by existing legislation. The speed of introduction of new radio services only depends on the added value of those services. It should, therefore, be left to the market, broadcasters, consumers and industry to decide which services are to be chosen. Maximising the benefits of digital radio will require a balance in the interest of consumers, broadcasters and the manufacturing industry.

Question no 50 by Mary Elizabeth Banotti (H-0530/00)
 Subject: Taxation of EU yachts in Rhodes, Greece
 

Local government in Rhodes, Greece, has imposed a tax over and above the normal harbour rates on all foreign flagged yachts, including those registered in the EU, for berthing in Rhodes harbour. It is a tax based on vessel length and is not imposed on Greek flagged vessels.

Can the Commission look into this unfair situation and take the necessary measures to end such distinction between Greek and other EU flagged vessels?

 
  
 

The Commission does not have full information, and it has not for the moment been possible to completely verify the information at its disposal. Greece will shortly be asked to submit its observations.

Subject to the above reservations, this tax has all the appearance of a charge having equivalent effect to a customs duty on imports, in contravention of Articles 23 and 25 (ex Articles 9 and 12) of the EC Treaty.

The chargeable event appears to constitute the entry into Greek waters of a pleasure boat more than 7 metres in length, which does not reside permanently in Greece. This tax appears, therefore, to represent a pecuniary charge imposed unilaterally on Community goods (in this instance, pleasure boats) by virtue of crossing a frontier. If that is the case, it meets the definition of a charge having equivalent effect to a customs duty on imports.

It also does not appear to be covered by one of the three exceptions which permit a charge not to be characterised as a charge having equivalent effect (namely charges forming part of a general system of internal dues; consideration for a service actually determined and individually rendered to the person liable for payment of the charge, of an amount proportionate to the said service; charges imposed by reason of checks carried out in order to fulfil obligations under Community law).

As there are doubts concerning the compatibility of the taxes on entry examined with Community law, the Commission will be contacting Greece in order to clarify the situation. If necessary, it will investigate the matter under the procedure provided for in Article 226 (ex Article 169) of the EC Treaty.

 

Question no 51 by John Walls Cushnahan (H-0532/00)
 Subject: EU-China WTO Agreement
 

Is the Commission aware that China has ordered state-run television, newspapers, and billboard owners to stop running Sprite commercials featuring the young pop-star, Sherry Chang Huei-mei, who sang at the inauguration of President Chen Shui-bain? Is the Commission also aware that China has recently warned Hong Kong and PRC business organisations not to trade with Taiwanese firms whom the PRC deems to be pro-independence? In view of the fact that we have recently signed an agreement with China on their admission to the WTO, how will the Commission respond to the use of this type of political blackmail, which is contrary to the principles of free trade?

 
  
 

The Commission is aware of the rumours concerning the alleged ban by the Chinese authorities of a Sprite commercial, made by the Coca-Cola Company, featuring the Taiwanese pop star Sherry Chang Huei-mei. The Commission is also aware of the alleged warning to PRC and Hong Kong companies about trading with certain Taiwanese firms. However, it is not clear whether the alleged ban and the alleged warning were carried out by the order, or with the knowledge, of the central Chinese Government.

It is difficult in instances of this kind to obtain clear evidence of exactly what has taken place. Recent information, however, seems to indicate that new commercials featuring Sherry Chang Huei-mei will encounter no problems. Furthermore, the Chinese authorities have issued statements to the effect that they refrained from interfering in Hong Kong's internal affairs. The Hong Kong Government has also issued a statement stating that investors and businessmen operating in Hong Kong are free to chose their business partners.

However, the Commission shares the concerns expressed by the Honourable Member. From the moment China enters the World Trade Organisation (WTO) and the terms of accession become legally binding, the Commission will be paying close attention to the implementation and enforcement of those commitments by the Chinese authorities. Other WTO members will do likewise.

In addition, the operation of a new, multilateral mechanism to review annually China's adherence to WTO obligations is currently under discussion as part of the protocol of accession. The Commission itself will also focus efforts on assisting China, through targeted aid programmes, in adapting to the demands of full participation in the world trading system.

 

Question no 52 by Carlos Carnero González (H-0533/00)
 Subject: Employment agreement signed between the Madrid Autonomous Community and the trade union organisations Comisiones Obreras and UGT
 

Last year, the government of the Madrid Autonomous Community, with the support of all the parliamentary groups represented in the Regional Assembly and MEPs including myself, signed an agreement on employment with the Comisiones Obreras and UGT trade union organisations, with the goal of encouraging the creation of stable jobs and finding employment for particularly disadvantaged groups such as the physically and mentally handicapped, by establishing specific clauses to this effect in contracts between the Autonomous Community’s administration and companies putting in for public tenders. The agreement was warmly welcomed by the public, but was contested, however, by certain employers’ organisations, which asked for the matter to be considered by the Commission.

Has the Commission formulated an opinion on the subject? Has it discussed the issue with the Spanish authorities? Does it believe that the agreement in question is in keeping with the European social guidelines on employment?

 
  
 

After several ad hoc meetings with the Spanish authorities, the Commission sent a letter on 12 April 2000 to the Spanish Government in order to inform it of its views on Decree No 213/1998 of 17 December, by means of which the Madrid Autonomous Community intended to implement the framework agreement in order to support the stability and quality of employment, and offered it an opportunity to present its comments on these views. This decree appears to infringe Community directives coordinating procedures for the award of public procurement contracts, insofar as it obliges the public authorities concerned to introduce the creation or preservation of sustainable jobs by the tendering firms as a criterion for the award of such contracts. The fact is, however, that in the context of completing the internal market, the directives stipulate, firstly, that observance of the principles of non-discrimination and equal treatment, among others, must be guaranteed and, secondly, that public procurement contracts must be justified on the basis of the lowest acceptable offer. Under such circumstances, a contract may not be awarded on the basis of a firm’s structure but only on the basis of the details of the tender submitted.

In view of the priority given to employment in Community policy, the Member States have undertaken to implement measures to promote employment or to pay special attention to disadvantaged persons or groups of people (the disabled, ethnic minorities, etc) within the labour market. Both sides of industry have been encouraged to participate actively – with the support of the national authorities – in implementing the guidelines for employment adopted by the Council for the year 2000, within the framework established by and in accordance with Community law.

 

Question no 53 by Sebastiano (Nello) Musumeci (H-0534/00)

Does the fact that a trade union can conduct proceedings on behalf of an employee who is a member of that organisation satisfy the requirements for implementation of a directive or must the employee be directly entitled as an individual to bring a case before the relevant courts?

 
  
 

The question of whether or not Denmark has correctly implemented the Working Time Directive is currently being examined by the Commission.

On a more general level, it should be noted that the European Court of Justice has addressed the question raised by the Honourable Member.

The Court has stated that

"Where the directive is intended to establish legal rights for individuals, the persons concerned must be able to ascertain the full extent of their rights and, where appropriate, rely on them before the national courts.(1)”1

Furthermore, the Court has held that Member States

“Must ensure in particular that infringements of Community law are penalised under conditions, both procedural and substantive, which are analogous to those applicable to infringements of national law of a similar nature and importance and which, in any event, make the penalty effective, proportionate and dissuasive.”(2)2

These paragraphs clearly show that Member States must provide individuals with procedural means to enforce their rights. However, the precise nature of those means is a matter for national law provided they fulfil the requirement of being effective, proportionate and dissuasive.
