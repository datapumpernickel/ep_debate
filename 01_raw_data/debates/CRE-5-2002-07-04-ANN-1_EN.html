Question no 26 by Richard Howitt (H-0426/02)
 Subject: Recent violent attacks on peaceful striking banana workers in Ecuador
 

What actions does the Council envisage taking in the light of recent extremely serious violent attacks on peaceful striking banana workers on the Los Alamos plantations in Ecuador? Will priority be placed on rapid and effective action given that these plantations are producing for Noboa, the fourth biggest banana company in the world, and the fact that Ecuador produces 40% of the world's banana exports? Does the Council support the process of unionisation for banana workers, currently in its vulnerable early stages, as a potential for allowing employees to reclaim their rights and reverse inhumane conditions in a country where wages and conditions are some of the worst in the Latin American banana industry? Does the Council believe that the Danish company (Expomarte) which owns the plantation in Rio Culebra should be held accountable with regard to threats made to workers striking there?

 
  
 

The Council follows closely the situation in Ecuador and is aware of the events referred to by the Honourable Member. The Council took note with regret of the violence that took place in the Los Álamos plantation on May 16 in the framework of the strike declared by the banana workers since May 6. The Council was deeply grieved by the shooting of ten striking workers by a large number of hooded, armed men. The Council was also appalled at the insults, looting, and other violent actions perpetrated by the assailants of the Los Álamos plantation group, where workers living on the plantations were sleeping.

The EU in its conversations with Ecuador on all occasions and, in particular in the framework of its political dialogue with the Andean Community calls for the respect and promotion of all human rights and fundamental freedoms. On the occasion of the EU - Andean Community summit that took place last 18 May in Madrid, the Heads of State and Government analysed the achievements made in the strengthening of democracy in the Andean region through the development and application of the Inter-American Democratic Charter. In this context they agreed on the necessity of preserving full institutional and democratic normality and the rule of law. They also stressed the importance of good governance and policies directed towards promoting fundamental rights and freedoms and eradicating poverty.

In this framework, the Council considers that the local judicial institutions and the national authorities should solve all violations of human rights and fundamental freedom issues according to the existing mechanisms.

 

Question no 27 by Antonios Trakatellis (H-0429/02)
 Subject: Tackling the issue of illegal immigration - repatriation agreements between the EU and third countries and management of external borders
 

Illegal immigration has assumed huge proportions in many countries of the European Union (the Prime Minister of Greece referred to 260 000 in 2001) and serious concerns are emerging as a result of the increase in illegal immigration, which often has dramatic consequences both for the illegal immigrants themselves and for political and economic stability in the partner countries, their societies and the European Union itself.

In view of the above, does the Council intend, on the basis of the provisions of Article 29 of the EU Treaty and of Article 63 of the EC Treaty, to take an integrated approach to this issue by seeking to enhance cooperation in the fields of foreign policy, economic development and the management of refugee flows, concluding repatriation agreements with all the partner countries and especially with those with a particularly high level of refugee flows? Which Member States have taken initiatives to conclude repatriation agreements with countries participating in the Barcelona Process, and what specific measures will the Council take to improve the management of external borders and to combat illegal immigration networks and human trafficking?

 
  
 

The European Union is currently giving the issue of combating illegal immigration and the most suitable measures for preventing and overcoming this very alarming phenomenon higher priority than ever before. The matter was recently discussed in great detail in the bodies of the Council, and subsequently the Union drew up and adopted a great many instruments in this area. It was on the agenda of the Seville European Council.

In November, the Commission presented a communication regarding a common policy to combat illegal immigration. Based on this, an overall plan was drawn up for combating illegal immigration and human trafficking. The plan was adopted by the Council at the meeting on justice and home affairs on 28 February 2002. The overall plan deals with a number of actions in various areas, which are to be discussed in the Council, as well as with a timetable for adopting and implementing the actions. It concerns areas such as visa policy, exchange and analysis of information, ‘pre-border’ measures, measures in respect of border controls in the broad sense of the term and readmission and repatriation policy, as well as Europol and sanctions.

In addition, reference may be made to two conclusions adopted by, respectively, the General Affairs Council on 15-16 April 2002 and by the Council on Justice and Home Affairs on 25-26 April 2002. The first conclusion concerns illegal immigration and the second illegal immigration by sea. The purpose of the conclusions is first and foremost to draw attention to the fact that a number of concrete and operational measures must be passed as quickly as possible so that illegal immigration – in particular illegal immigration by sea – can be combated effectively. Among other things, this is to be done by strengthening border controls. However, these conclusions also have a broader aim: namely that they are to contribute to immigration being one of the elements permanently included in the basis of the EU’s foreign policy – and, in particular, in the EU’s contacts with the countries from which the illegal immigrants come or through which they travel.

The main idea behind this integrated strategy is that the combating of illegal immigration must be seen in a broader context than that of the special instruments in the area of justice and home affairs. Taking this approach, we should not restrict ourselves simply to considering the adoption of measures for the prevention and combating of the phenomenon. We must also tackle the issue of the common development of, and economic assistance to, the home countries and transit countries that are affected by the major migration flows. This new strategy will also mean that the support that the third countries in question receive will be highly dependent on whether they are willing to cooperate effectively with the European Union on the prevention and control of illegal immigration.

This integrated strategy is an extension of the policy laid down in conjunction with the conclusions of the European Council in Tampere in October 1999. The wish in Tampere was to lay the foundations for a coherent and balanced strategy for immigration into the EU which, among other things, was to promote development in the third countries from which the immigrants originate and to strengthen the prevention and control of illegal immigration. It must also be expected that this strategy will be developed further in connection with the new instruments that are being devised at present, for example in the area of integrated management of external borders. This issue was dealt with by the Council at the meeting on justice and home affairs on 13-14 June 2002 as part of the preparations for the European Council in Seville on 21-22 June 2002, at which illegal immigration was one of the priority issues.

With regard to the practical measures to be agreed, the EU has always regarded entering into repatriation agreements with the illegal immigrants’ home countries and transit countries as one of the most appropriate and effective instruments for combating illegal immigration. The importance of such an instrument is recognised both in the aforementioned overall plan and in the two conclusions, and it is stressed that it will be appropriate to enter into new repatriation agreements.

The Commission has received mandates from the Council to negotiate six draft repatriation agreements between the European Community and Morocco, Sri Lanka, Russia, Pakistan, Hong Kong and Macau. These negotiations are currently in progress. In addition, the Council has just given the Commission a new mandate to negotiate a repatriation agreement with Ukraine.

The repatriation agreement with Hong Kong, which has already been initialled, is expected to be entered into officially in the next few months. The negotiations with Macau and Sri Lanka are very far advanced, and a final agreement is expected be entered into with these countries very soon.

Finally, reference is made to the conclusions agreed by the Council at the meeting on justice and home affairs on 25-26 April concerning the criteria to be established when a decision is to be made on the third countries with which new repatriation agreements are to be negotiated. In these conclusions, the Council calls upon the Commission to submit draft negotiation mandates with a view to entering into repatriation agreements with China, Turkey, Algeria and Albania. It is possible that other third countries will be selected later on the basis of these criteria.

The European Council in Seville on 21-22 June 2002 called upon the Council and the Commission to give the measures in the overall plan for combating illegal immigration the very highest priority within the frameworks of their respective powers. In particular, the European Council stressed that more rapid progress should be made on entering into the repatriation agreements that are in the process of being negotiated and that new mandates should be approved with a view to negotiating repatriation agreements with the countries already selected by the Council.

Question no 29 by Pedro Marset Campos (H-0443/02)
 Subject: Situation in Colombia
 

The new situation, created by President Uribe’s victory and his declared appeal to the UN and international organisations to promote the peace process, requires a clearly defined European Union position.

Does the Council not agree that it should revive the EU’s reasonable and positive approach of encouraging a negotiated and peaceful solution to the violent conflict that is destroying Colombia, through mediation between the government and the guerrilla groups?

 
  
 

I would like to be permitted to give a joint answer to the two Honourable Members’ questions concerning Colombia.

The Council has repeatedly reported on the EU’s position on Colombia here in Parliament. It will therefore be known that the Council attaches enormous importance to the situation in the country and is following developments closely.

The Council has always taken a constant, clear and firm attitude to Colombia. We have done this in the conviction that there is no future for Colombia without a negotiated solution. The Council has supported President Pastrana and his untiring and resolute work to achieve peace in Colombia, work which it has been possible to observe over the years. Unfortunately, it has not been possible to observe the same desire for peace among the guerrilla groups. In addition, some of the Member States have followed the negotiation process between FARC and ELN as members of the ‘group of friendly nations’ and the ‘group of facilitator countries’ respectively.

The Council understood and showed its respect for the decision that President Pastrana felt forced to take on 20 February 2002, partly in order to bring an end to the process that was commenced in 1998 of dialogue, negotiation and the signing of agreements with the Colombian Revolutionary Armed Forces – People’s Army (FARC-EP), and secondly in order to abolish the demilitarised zone.

Following the presidential elections on 26 May 2002 the Council noted with satisfaction the developments in the electoral process in Colombia, which showed an entire people’s desire to live in peace and freedom. However, the Council regretted that part of the civil population was not able to exercise its democratic right to vote freely due to poor security conditions which were the result of repeated terrorist attacks on the country’s democratic institutions, their representatives and the population as a whole.

The Council wished Colombia’s elected president, Alvaro Uribe, good luck and expressed its conviction that he would be fully able to fulfil the wishes for peace and prosperity for the whole population on the firm basis of a state governed by law and observing fully both human rights and fundamental freedoms.

Moreover, the Council repeated that it is working wholeheartedly to find a negotiated solution in Colombia and that it will follow the Colombian people and its government both in their efforts for justice, social progress and reconciliation for all Colombians and in their fight against terrorism, the avoidance of justice and infringements of human rights and international humanitarian law.

The Council has also expressed on various occasions the fact that it appreciates the work that the UN and the group of facilitator countries has carried out and which has made it possible to recreate the necessary confidence in the attempt to find a negotiated solution to the conflict. The EU has also expressed the conviction that the international community has played a very constructive and useful role in the negotiations. In addition, the EU has confirmed its desire to continue to support the international’s community’s participation in future negotiations.

The Council has also repeated that it rejects and condemns kidnapping, extortion and other crimes committed by the armed groups in Colombia and it has several times urgently appealed for the observance of international humanitarian law and protection of the civil population.

This is the background to the fact that, as part of the fight against terrorism, the Council has placed the Colombian Revolutionary Armed Forces (FARC) and the paramilitary forces in Colombia (UAC) on the EU’s list of terrorist organisations. The Council had decided previously not to reissue or renew visas for members of the armed groups and to withdraw their residence permits.

Question no 31 by Chris Davies (H-0451/02)
 Subject: World Summit on Sustainable Development in Johannesburg
 

In finalising the EU position for the World Summit on Sustainable Development in Johannesburg in September, what EU actions and measures in the environmental field will the Council present to the international representatives at the Summit? How will these actions demonstrate the EU’s commitment to the implementation of Articles 2 and 6 of the Treaty of Amsterdam on sustainable development within the EU?

Since one major strand of the WSSD sustainable development agenda is a Global Water Initiative, based on the promotion of the Integrated River Basin Management principles which underpin the Water Framework Directive (WFD), what commitments will the EU Council make in these discussions, and how will it present the EU’s position on the Spanish National Hydrological Plan - a 'water transfer/dam' law - which clearly violates the WFD and the principles of the Global Water Initiative?

 
  
 

The Council does not have the competence to decide whether the National Hydrological Plan is compatible with specific Community legislation.

As far as the Community is concerned, Article 6 of the EC Treaty states that environmental protection requirements must be integrated into the definition and implementation of the Community policies and activities. The National Hydrological Plan is a plan for national water resources submitted to the Commission by the Spanish Government. It is therefore the Commission which, as guardian of the treaties, must investigate whether the National Hydrological Plan gives cause for questions in connection with specific Community legislation, and it is the Commission which, in that case, must take the necessary measures.

The EU attaches huge importance to a positive result’s being achieved at the World Summit on Sustainable Development in Johannesburg, which is a follow-up to the WTO’s fourth ministerial conference in Doha and the Conference on Financing for Development in Monterrey. These two conferences must be regarded as essential milestones in the pursuit of internationally agreed objectives and as further commitments in this respect. The EU wishes to build on the positive results of these two conferences and intends fully to play its part so that far-reaching, practical and meaningful results are achieved in the negotiations in Johannesburg, in line with the EU’s internal and international obligations in respect of sustainable development.

So that the Honourable Member might obtain a more comprehensive overview of the preparations for the Summit in Johannesburg and of the EU’s intentions it is suggested that he look at the Council’s conclusions of 30 May 2002 and of 4 and 17 June 2002 concerning the external dimension of the EU’s strategy for sustainable development and concerning preparations for the Summit.

Very intense preparatory negotiations are being conducted both at international level and within the EU. The European Council in Seville supported the Council’s conclusions regarding the World Summit on Sustainable Development, namely the conclusions of 4 March, 30 May and 4 and 17 June, and also the general positions adopted by the European Union in this connection. It stressed that the European Union has undertaken to work towards a successful World Summit on Sustainable Development and remains committed to playing an important role in the preparations for the Summit so that a global agreement can be achieved based on the positive results in Monterrey and Doha.

The European Council emphasised the European Union’s desire for clear and practical political commitments to be entered into in Johannesburg with a precise timetable, the implementation of which is to be based on real partnership.

 

Question no 32 by Elizabeth Lynne (H-0460/02)
 Subject: Tensions between India and Pakistan
 

In view of the recent deterioration in Indo-Pakistani relations and the threat of war, does the Council agree that it is all the more urgent to find a solution to the dispute over Kashmir?

As the EU is not directly involved in the dispute, does the Council agree that the EU could act as a facilitator in talks about the region?

 
  
 

The Council agrees that the recent deterioration in Indo-Pakistani relations is extremely worrying, and that it is urgent to find a solution to the disputes between the two countries, including on Kashmir.

Over the past weeks, the EU has stepped up its diplomatic efforts to defuse the crisis. Visits to the region, including by Commissioner Patten, UK Foreign Minister Straw, [and, most recently by HR Solana,] as well as regular telephone contacts by other leading EU politicians with the key actors are taking place. The EU is also seeking close coordination with other international players, including the US, Russia, and Japan in order to reinforce the concerted efforts of the international community. The EU has repeatedly urged India and Pakistan in public statements to de-escalate the present level of tensions, to resume their dialogue and to solve peacefully, on the basis of the Simla agreement and of the Lahore declaration, the various issues of contention between them, including Kashmir.

The Council does not, however, believe that the EU could act as a facilitator as long as India continues to reject outright the principle of third party involvement, be it by the EU, the US, the UN or others. Any facilitator can only play a role if he is accepted by both sides. But the EU will continue to encourage and to support any negotiated solution between the two countries, and will convey privately the message that it stands ready to facilitate talks if both sides so wish.

The European Council, meeting in Seville on 21 and 22 June 2002, discussed the tensions between India and Pakistan, and subsequently adopted an EU Declaration. In this Declaration the European Council, inter alia, welcomed the recent de-escalatory steps taken by Pakistan, called on Pakistan to take further concrete action in accordance with its international obligations and encouraged India to respond with further de-escalatory steps. The EC noted the importance of free, fair and inclusive elections this autumn in Jammu and Kashmir. The European Council called on India and Pakistan to establish an effective monitoring system, in a form acceptable to both, to stop infiltration. Lastly, the European Council called on both parties to adhere to the NPT and to sign and ratify the CTBT.

 

Question no 33 by Ioannis Souladakis (H-0464/02)
 Subject: Reduction in subsidies for agricultural products
 

Recently, the gradual reduction in CAP agricultural subsidies has been confirmed by accounting data from the preparation of the Community Budget (see Early Warning System No 3/2002 and 4/2002) showing reduced amounts in subcategory 1a and 1b, which are mainly restricted to agricultural products from Mediterranean regions, such as wine, oil, tobacco, mutton and lamb etc.

In the Council’s view, what are the justifications for reductions in these subsidies?

Has the Council considered the possibility that these reductions will not apply to the EU's total agricultural production, but will continue to focus on categories of agricultural products from the southern agricultural areas of the European Union, thus affecting mainly and exclusively the agricultural production of Mediterranean products?

Has the Council provided safety valves to avoid selective reductions in these subsidies?

 
  
 

1. The budgetary authority is preparing the agricultural budget based on the agricultural guideline and the financial estimates prepared for each financial year in accordance with the rules of the common agricultural policy.

When the Commission prepares its estimates of grant usage it also draws up an expenditure profile for each individual agricultural financial year. The estimates of monthly payments from the EAGGF’s Guarantee Section are shown by this profile, which is generally based on the Member States’ grant usage in the three preceding years.

According to Early Warning System 4/2002, the Member States’ agricultural expenditure in a number of sectors – including agricultural products from the Mediterranean region – in the period up to 28 February 2002 were below budget. However, the Commission estimates that the differences both in the sectors that have had underusage and in the chapter on rural development will be evened out by the end of the financial year. However, this does not apply to the differences for sheepmeat and beef and veal, which will be below the grants budgeted.(1)

Account is also to be taken of the fact that, according to the expenditure profile, the expected level of usage up to February is just 36% in the sector of fruit and vegetables and in the wine sector, and just 18% in the chapter on rural development. The departures mentioned in Early Warning System 4/2002 are thus very insignificant at this point in the financial year.

2. The gradual reduction of the direct subsidy to certain sectors forms part of the measures that the Council approved in July 1999 in connection with the reform of the common agricultural policy in Agenda 2000.

As far as the future legal provisions for the sectors mentioned by the Honourable Member are concerned, the Council is awaiting a report on the mid-term review of the common agricultural policy which the Commission promised to submit when the reform was approved. The Council will then look at the legislative proposal presented by the Commission with a view to adapting the provisions in respect of a number of sectors.

 
 

(1) The latest Early Warning System 6/2002 (expenditure by Member States up to 30 April 2002), which the Commission presented at the Agrifin Group’s meeting in the Council on 2 April, shows that these sectors are reporting sustained underusage, particularly for olive oil €-27m ; fruit and vegetables €-141.8m; wine €-277.1m; sheepmeat €-124.8m; rural development €-99.7m.

 

Question no 34 by Efstratios Korakas (H-0465/02)
 Subject: Refusal to recognise the Communist Party of Russia
 

The Russian authorities, and in particular the Ministry of Justice, refuse to recognise any longer the Communists and Workers of Russia Party (KTS), although under the new law on political parties they ought to do so. This is a party which has been in existence for over ten years and participates in general elections for the Duma; millions of Russian citizens have expressed their support for it in these elections. The authorities justify their refusal with the argument that the KTS's goal is a radical shake-up of society’s structure, with common ownership of the means of production, thereby ensuring universal unfettered development and well-being for all members of society.

Does the Council condemn moves of this kind which obstruct free expression by citizens and the unimpeded functioning of political parties, does it intend to call for an end to actions and discrimination of this kind in a country with which the EU is linked in many ways, and does it consider that attempted justifications of this kind are, at the very least, detrimental to the memory and the struggles of the people for national and social liberation?

 
  
 

Russia recently passed a new act on political parties, the primary purpose of which is reduce the number of small parties and single-issue parties. According to the information that the Council has, the Russian Ministry of Justice has refused to recognise the Russian Communist Workers’ Party – Revolutionary Party of Communists (RKRP-RPK) for the following reasons: 1) The name of the party contains the word ‘revolutionary’. This has been interpreted as indicating that the party may possibly use violent methods in order to achieve its aims. 2) The party’s statutes contain no provisions stating that its leader is to be elected by secret ballot. Both these points infringe the law on political parties.

Based on the information available, the Council has not called into question the decision of the Russian Justice Ministry. On the other hand, within the frameworks of the political dialogue with Russia, the Council will continue to emphasise that it is important to maintain democratic principles, including freedom of expression for citizens and allowing political parties to carry on their activities unhindered, with a view to further strengthening links between the EU and Russia. Both parties emphasised recently at the summit between the EU and Russia in Moscow that they intend to utilise the partnership and cooperation agreement, the EU’s joint strategy in respect of Russia and Russia’s medium-term strategy for developing links with the EU in order to strengthen a society based on respect for democratic principles and human rights.

 

Question no 35 by Carmen Cerdeira Morterero (H-0468/02)
 Subject: Unaccompanied foreign minors in the European Union
 

The number of minors from third countries who are unaccompanied and without documentation is increasing daily in a very disturbing way. The situation developing in the Spanish cities of Ceuta and Melilla, among others, serves as an example. The fact is that minors are exposed to considerable risk, and are highly vulnerable. At the same time the local authorities are unable to cope with the gravity of the situation, for lack of the technical and budgetary resources.

Will the Council be dealing with this issue at its forthcoming meeting in Seville, together with the other proposals the Commission has put forward and that have been pending for the past two years? Will it be adopting measures to ensure these young people are properly treated and/or returned to their families? Does the Council consider that its Resolution of 26 June 1997 on unaccompanied minors who are nationals of third countries continues to apply? Does it have any plans to support and cooperate with the authorities of the cities in which this situation is developing, so as to deal with it properly? Does it have plans for any extraordinary budgetary measures that will make it possible to deal with this serious situation?

 
  
 

Based on the mandate in Article 63 of the EC Treaty, the Council is continuing, on the basis of the Commission’s proposal, with its work on measures in respect of asylum, refugees and displaced persons, as well as immigration policy.

A number of the future acts that are currently being discussed deal with measures in respect of unaccompanied minors.

In line with this, on 20 June 2001 the Council adopted a Directive on minimum standards of temporary protection in the event of a mass influx of displaced persons. According to Article 16 of this directive, concrete measures are to be taken to protect unaccompanied minors. Similarly, at the meeting held on 25-26 April 2002, the Council reached general agreement on a proposal for a directive laying down minimum standards for the reception of asylum seekers in the Member States. This directive takes into account a great many of the amendments by the European Parliament and will be formally adopted by the Council at one of its future meetings. A number of measures to protect unaccompanied minors are to be taken under Article 16 of this directive.

At its meeting in Seville on 21-22 June 2002, the European Council discussed the problems surrounding asylum and immigration in the European Union and declared that it is firmly resolved to accelerate implementation of all the aspects of the programme adopted in Tampere. It emphasised that it is necessary to develop a common EU policy on asylum and immigration, which are two separate but closely related matters.

Question no 84 by Efstratios Korakas (H-0521/02)
 Subject: 24 000 people die of hunger every day across the world
 

The recent statistics presented at the UN world food summit are horrifying. Across the world, 800 million people are starving and over 24 000 of them are dying every day of starvation. These figures represent the officially recorded victims of hunger, while the average American consumes annually as much food as 300 people in Africa. However, the facade of the 'prosperous society' conceals starving people and beggars searching through rubbish in order to appease their hunger in the metropolises of developed countries. This is the barbaric world created by the 'new order' and globalisation.

In view of the fact that the situation in the EU itself is no better, what measures does the Commission intend to take to combat global hunger and to rid the metropolises of its Member States of the sight of wretched people begging or sifting through rubbish?

 
  
 

The Commission is fully aware of the problem of poverty, starvation, and food insecurity at the global level, particularly in developing countries.

The Union has put the reduction – and the eradication – of poverty as its overarching objective for aid to these countries. It has also identified rural development and food security as one of the six priority areas for the implementation of its assistance. This implies that all the Union’s aid instruments and policies are geared towards reducing food insecurity and combating poverty in all developing countries.

The Commission believes that the best way to bring about food security is to pursue a broad based policy for sustainable development and poverty reduction at the national level. Promoting broad based growth and poverty reduction will in fact address the root causes of the food security problem by bringing the issues of food availability, access to food, responses to food shortages and nutritional problems to the centre of poverty reduction strategies. In addition, the political dimensions of food insecurity need to be tackled head on by giving greater attention to promoting good governance, preventing conflict and building peace.

Within the Union, it has also to be recalled that following the inclusion under Article 137 of the EC Treaty of the fight against exclusion among the social policy provisions, the Lisbon European Council agreed on the need to take steps to make a decisive impact on the eradication of poverty by 2010. To this end, it was also agreed that Member States’ policies for combating social exclusion should be based on an open method of co-ordination combining common objectives, National Action Plans and a programme presented by the Commission to encourage co-operation in this field.

All Member States subsequently committed themselves at the Nice Summit to developing their policy priorities in fighting poverty and social exclusion on the basis of four commonly agreed objectives: to facilitate participation in employment and access by all to resources, rights, goods and services; to prevent the risks of exclusion; to help the most vulnerable; and to mobilise all relevant bodies.

All Member States submitted in June 2001 National Action Plans for Social Inclusion covering the period mid-2001 to mid-2003 on the basis of which the Joint Council/Commission Inclusion Report was prepared and endorsed by the Laeken European Council. The process is an ongoing one and new National Action Plans will be prepared during 2003. Tackling aspects of poverty such as homelessness and begging cited in the question from the Honourable Member should form part of the National Action Plans prepared by the Member States.

Question no 86 by Hans Karlsson (H-0523/02)
 Subject: The UMTS/3G network
 

The extension of the UMTS/3G network is currently being implemented. In order for the project to be profitable, an extension needs to take place to some extent in parallel throughout the whole EU internal market, otherwise operators doing business in several countries will lose the advantages which the global UMTS standard has to offer. One precondition for operators on the market to risk investment in the infrastructure which an extension of UMTS/3G requires is that there should be a common strategy at EU level for the implementation of the regulations currently in force, e.g. the Access Directive. The EU currently lacks such a strategy, which means that the Member States are moving at a very different rate and operators hesitate to make any further progress. A report of the current situation has been requested, but that is not enough. Is the Commission planning to take any concrete measures to encourage and facilitate the Member States’ extension of the UMTS/3G network in the course of this implementation?

 
  
 

When preparing in the past for the roll-out of third generation mobile communications, action by the Commission was aimed at ensuring that all Member States put into place licensing modalities which would allow for interested market players to apply for licences sufficiently early to be able to start commercial services as of 2002, if wishing to do so. This effort has resulted in 61 3G licences issued in all Member States as of today.

All issued 3G licences contain conditions on specific coverage and roll-out obligations which were decided by national regulators.

Within the constraints of the licence conditions the Commission believes it is now up to the market players - and not for administrations - to decide when they intend to roll-out physical networks and when they want to offer commercial services.

The Commission adopted on 12 June 2002 a Communication(1) to the European Parliament, Council, the Economic and Social Committee and the Committee of Regions “Towards the full roll-out of third generation mobile communications” a copy of which is being sent to the Honourable Member.

In this Communication, the Commission analyses the current state of play of the roll-out of 3G and notes that the deployment of physical networks is presently high on the agenda of those operators who opt for an early start of commercial 3G services. The Communication notes that operators are actually already engaged in implementing the physical networks.

The Communication identifies as an issue of concern difficulties encountered by market players in finding base station sites and in getting base stations authorised: the Communication contains a detailed analysis and proposes actions to facilitate the roll-out of physical networks. Other measures outlined in the Communication regard notably the promotion of the development of applications and content for 3G. Efforts in this sense are, in the view of the Commission, key if 3G is to become a success.

Heads of States and Government have, at the Seville European Council (21-22 June 2002), recognised the importance of this issue and the Commission is committed to follow development in this area according to its conclusions.

Concerning the Access Directive mentioned in the question by the Honourable Member, Member States are starting the transposition process which has to be finalised by July 2003. The new framework foresees obligations of co-ordination before measures are taken at national level. These mechanisms have been proposed and adopted within the new regulatory package precisely to ensure a harmonised approach across the Union. The Commission intends to use these mechanisms even before July 2003 where needed, including if necessary for mobile communications such as 3G.
