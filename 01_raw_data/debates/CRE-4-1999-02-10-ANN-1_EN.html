1. Questions to the Council

 

Question No 18 by Brian Crowley (H−0032/99) Subject: Sustaining European cities

Following the holding of an EU‐supported international congress on energy and sustainability for European cities of the future (17 to 19 January 1999), will the Council outline the ways in which the conclusions of this congress may be used for the benefit of EU citizens generally, and does it intend to propose new policies as a result of the work of the congress and, if so, what are these policies and how, specifically, will citizens in Cork in Ireland benefit from them?

 Answer

The Conference referred to by the honourable Member on energy and sustainability for European cities of the future was organised by the Commission between 17 and 19 January 1999 in Athens.

It is the first in a series of information awareness events initiated by the Commission following the adoption, in December last year, of the 5th Research Framework Programme of the EC. As such, it aims at enhancing knowledge of policy makers and research actors in selected areas and is not intended to give rise to new policies.

The honourable Member will be aware that the specific programme ‘Energy, environment and sustainable development' adopted under the 5th Framework Programme contains a key action ‘City of tomorrow and cultural heritage' which provides for rational management of resources, including energy efficiency and conservation, for new technologies of the built environment, for new strategies for transport systems and for the protection and conservation of cultural heritage in an urban context.

Question No 48 by Konstantinos Hatzidakis (H−0010/99) Subject: Fourth EU‐Cyprus financial protocol

One of the objectives of the fourth EU‐Cyprus financial protocol is to support initiatives aimed at a settlement of the political problem in Cyprus by funding programmes which will benefit both communities. Unfortunately, however, the intransigence so far displayed by the Turkish Cypriots and the terms they have laid down (a freeze on the application for EU membership and recognition of the self‐styled ‘state') have not made it possible to use the appropriations concerned.  Answer

The Commission is well aware of the delays in using the appropriations under the fourth financial protocol with Cyprus, and regrets the lack of support for initiatives aimed at settling the conflict in Cyprus and for the implementation of bicommunal projects making it possible to bring together the two Cypriot communities.

Question No 49 by Niall Andrews (H−0041/99) Subject: Charter of Fundamental Rights

The German Presidency has indicated its wish to establish an EU Charter of Fundamental Rights.

How does the Commission envisage the objectives, application, scope and enforcement of such a Charter?

 Answer

For the past 20 years, the European Commission has regarded the definition of the values which are at the very heart of European integration as a priority issue. It therefore warmly welcomes the initiative taken by the German Presidency of the Council which seeks to establish a European Charter of Fundamental Rights.

The Commission considered the option of a European Charter as long ago as 1979, when doubts were expressed as to the democratic legitimacy of the Community legal order on the grounds of the lack of explicit references to human rights and democracy. At that time, the Commission presented two options: the establishment of a separate Charter or accession to the European Convention on Human Rights and Fundamental Freedoms. The second option was selected, on the grounds that accession would pose fewer problems and that it could be achieved over a shorter period of time.

That attempt came to naught because the Communication, presented by the Commission to the Council in November 1990, in which it sought a negotiating mandate defining the procedures for such accession, was not approved by the Council. In April 1994, the Council sought the opinion of the Court of Justice. In March 1996, the Court ruled on the compatibility of accession to that Convention with the Treaty establishing the European Community, finding that the Community did not have the power to accede to that Convention.

Given the aforegoing, the Commission takes the view that the German Presidency's wish to establish a European Charter of Fundamental Rights is very interesting because it bears in mind not only past experience but also future prospects, in that it takes account of

‐ the implementation of the Treaty of Amsterdam, which enshrines respect for human rights and fundamental freedoms among the principles on which the European Union is founded and refers explicitly to the European Convention on that subject, and

‐ the Vienna Strategy for Europe, set out in the Conclusions of the Presidency of the Vienna European Council (11‐12 December 1998), which includes firmer European action in the field of human rights among the ‘four issues of primary concern to European citizens where rapid and efficient action is necessary'.

The Commission intends to cooperate fully with a view to facilitating progress on this initiative in its various stages of more detailed consideration of the definition of the objectives and, in due course, of its implementation.

Question No 50 by Anna Karamanou (H−0046/99) Subject: Pollution of the River Nestos by radioactive waste from Bulgaria

Those responsible for the implementation of the PHARE programme in Bulgaria estimate that there is a risk that twelve million cubic metres of radioactive waste from mining activity in the now disused uranium mines in the Eleznitsa region some 90 kilometres from the Greek‐Bulgarian border may leak into the bed of the River Nestos and reach the Greek coast. Although mining ceased some eight years ago, the small dams of the artificial lakes containing the radioactive waste could collapse in the event of a natural disaster, polluting the water of the river and contaminating the groundwater in the surrounding region and eventually reaching the Mediterranean. Will the Commission say how it intends to intervene to avert the threat of pollution of the region and protect the groundwater and the ecological balance of the natural environment of this EU region?

 Answer

The Commission has been tackling the problems with former uranium mines in all the Central and Eastern European countries concerned for several years now, using multinational PHARE funding and, more specifically in Bulgaria, as part of the national PHARE programmes. The overall objective is to create a database on the subject and promote remediation projects.

In this context, a study on the measures which need to be taken to remediate the Eleznitsa site, in the south of Bulgaria, has just been completed. The Eleznitsa site consisted of a mine and a processing site, which are no longer used. The conclusions of the study have been the subject of an in‐depth debate between the Bulgarian and Greek authorities, in particular within the joint planning and monitoring committee, in the context of Bulgaria‐Greece cross‐border cooperation programmes (PHARE CBC‐Interreg programmes). These conclusions recommend that work should be carried out to limit the risk, in the event of a natural disaster, of the dam holding back the mine tailings bursting, and releasing them into the Mesta (Nestos) basin.

At its last meeting, which was held on 11 and 12 January 1999 in Sofia, the joint planning and monitoring committee proposed funding this project in 1999; the costs are estimated at around EUR 12 million.

In accordance with the committee proposal, the Bulgarian authorities have started preparing a project which will be examined as part of the 1999 PHARE cross‐border cooperation financing proposal. Subject to a favourable decision from the Commission on the funding, the Bulgarian authorities think that work would be able to start as early as the year 2000.

Question No 51 by Maj Theorin (H−0062/99) Subject: Environmental brigades

The end of the Cold War has profoundly changed the security situation in the world. Serious new threats to our security have arisen in the form of environmental dangers, streams of refugees, ethnic conflicts, terrorism and international crime.

Does the Commission intend to conduct a study of environmental threats which have implications for security in Europe and to develop plans for European protection teams from the military sector to be deployed in environmental emergencies?

 Answer

The Commission has no competence in military affairs. For this reason it has not developed plans for the use of military forces in an environmental crisis situation nor does it plan to carry out a study on such an eventuality.

The Commission would look favourably upon suggestions leading to increased cooperation with member states or international organisations which are competent in this area.

In the case of an emergency and as part of the Petersberg tasks, military units of WEU member states, acting under the authority of the WEU, could be employed for humanitarian and rescue tasks. Naturally such an arrangement would require a WEU Council decision.

Finally a series of expert information contacts between the Commission and NATO have been initiated recently. One of the subjects to be initially covered is reaction to civil emergencies.

Question No 52 by Marialiese Flemming (H−0068/99) Subject: Possible Euratom loans for two Ukrainian nuclear power stations

The European Bank for Reconstruction and Development is currently processing an application for loans to the Ukrainian power undertaking Energoatom for the completion of two nuclear power stations, at Khmelnitsky (No 2 reactor) and Rovno (No 4 reactor) – K2/R4 for short.

What stage has the procedure reached?

How does the Commission assess the safety of K2/R4, and should not the granting of EU funds to the Ukraine be dependent on the same nuclear safety standards as apply to the countries applying for EU membership?

On what criteria will these loans be granted or refused?

Does the Commission agree that these funds could be better spent in improving the economic and social situation in the Ukraine?

Is the Ukraine receiving aid to renovate and upgrade its fossil fuel power stations, with environmental and emission considerations in mind? If not, would it not be wise to give it such aid?

 Answer

The Memorandum of Understanding (MOU) agreed between the G7 countries, the EC and Ukraine (Ottawa, 20.12.1995) develops a comprehensive programme to support the decision of Ukraine to close Chernobyl by the year 2000. The MOU addresses the restructuring of the power sector, an energy investment programme, nuclear safety and social issues related to the closure of Chernobyl. The K24R project is part of this wide action plan intimately linked to the closure of the Chernobyl nuclear power plant. The challenge with this project is therefore to close dangerous reactors and to replace them by two new reactors upgraded to a safety level equivalent to that in the Community.

It is also worthwhile to recall here the major effort the Community is undertaking to rebuild the sarcophagus around the destroyed unit 4.

1. The application for a Euratom loan for the completion and upgrading of the two reactors at Khmelnitsky and Rovno is under consideration by the Commission since 1994. At present all the necessary studies and consultations concerning nuclear safety, environmental impact, economic and financial aspects have practically been completed, so that the decision can be taken in the near future (April). This will be done in parallel with the EBRD which also has to decide on a loan for the same project.

2. On the basis of relevant assessments made in the framework of the loan application, in particular by a Member States Expert Group, the Commission considers that the safety upgrading programme as envisaged for these reactors will contribute to achieve the overall objective of a safety level in accordance with international standards.

As far as applicant countries are concerned, the Commission applies the same principle to any project for which a Euratom loan application is received.

3. The criteria to be applied by the Commission when deciding on a Euratom loan are defined in the Council Decision 94/179/Euratom of 21 March 1994. For a project to be eligible, it must have received all the necessary authorisation by the competent national authority, of Ukraine in this case, and a favourable opinion from the Commission in economic and technical terms.

4. The Commission considers that the completion of K2R4 complements other efforts undertaken by the international community to improve the restructuring of the energy sector and, hence, more generally the economic and social situation of Ukraine. Through the closure of Chernobyl, this project will also increase nuclear safety in the country and in the region.

5. There is a substantial support already provided to Ukraine for the modernisation of its fossil power plants as part of the comprehensive plan agreed in the Memorandum of Understanding signed between the G7 countries and the Commission and Ukraine. This includes loans of the World Bank, the EBRD and bilateral programmes (USD 1.1 billion under implementation, USD 1 billion under preparation). The Community has supported this effort through technical assistance under the TACIS programme (EUR 70 million of grant money).

6. The Commission will supply shortly a detailed file to the EP in order to keep it fully informed on this project.

Question No 53 by Joan Vallvé (H−0069/99) Subject: The PHARE‐CBC Programme

Under Article B7‐502 of the 1999 budget relating to the PHARE‐CBC Programme, two‐thirds of the appropriation involved will be applied at the external borders of the EU and one‐third at the other borders of the applicant countries.

The need to make the external borders of the EU more watertight is essential if the integration of the CEECs is to be facilitated.

When a programme for cross‐border cooperation under the PHARE programmes is next implemented, will the same proportions apply?

 Answer

The new regulation concerning the PHARE cross‐border cooperation (CBC) programme, adopted by the Commission on 18 December 1998, aims at consolidating and updating the PHARE CBC programme initiated in 1994, and seeks to take better account of the enhanced pre‐accession strategy. It will bring PHARE CBC closer to Interreg, notably through:

‐ the set up of joint programming structures involving partners from both sides of the border,

‐ the elaboration of joint programming documents, and

‐ the extension of the geographical scope of the programme: besides regions bordering the Community, borders between applicant states will also become eligible to the programme, and will thus also be given the possibility to prepare themselves for their future involvement in Interreg; furthermore, this allows also the inclusion of Romania in the programme.

The new regulation is thus well in line with Parliament's budgetary comment, which provides that 2/3 of the budgetary appropriation will be allocated to borders between applicant states and the Community and 1/3 to the other borders of the applicant countries.

Question No 54 by Elisabeth Schroedter (H−0101/99) Subject: New PHARE‐CBC regulation

In early January 1999, on a proposal from the Commission, the Council adopted a new PHARE‐CBC regulation which contains far‐reaching changes to the underlying objective of this instrument.

Why is the Commission using this line for measures which could be financed with other PHARE money, and why was Parliament not consulted in the process of amending the regulation?

 Answer

A programme for cross‐border cooperation between border regions in the countries of Central and Eastern Europe and adjacent regions of the Community was established in 1994 in the framework of PHARE, on the basis of Commission Regulation No 1628/94 of 4 July 1994.

Although this programme produced a number of positive results, further improvements became necessary. Therefore, the Commission adopted a new regulation on 18 December 1998 (Commission Regulation (EC) No 2760/98 of 18 December 1998), with the aim of consolidating and updating the programme, of taking better into account the enhanced pre‐accession strategy, and of bring PHARE CBC closer to the Structural Funds, in particular Interreg.

The most important changes in this regulation concern the following:

‐ the geographical scope of the programme has been extended, in order to also comprise, besides the borders with the Community, the borders between applicant countries themselves; this will allow these new borders to prepare themselves for their future involvement in Interreg, and Romania, as the only applicant state without a common border with the Community to become also part of the programme.

‐ joint programming institutions will be established and joint programming documents will be produced, involving partners from both sides of the border, on equal footing;

‐ local actors will become better involved in the programme, notably through the establishment of small project funds.

These changes will allow more genuine cross‐border cooperation, and will make the programme more balanced and effective.

Question No 61 by Mary Banotti (H−0043/99) Subject: Ireland and CITES

Until Ireland has ratified the CITES Treaty on international trade in wildlife, the EU cannot fully implement its provisions, since Ireland is a potential loophole as an easy point of entry into the EU for illegal trade in CITES species.

What measures does the Commission propose to take in order to ensure full EU implementation of this Treaty?

Will the Commission press the Irish Government for a specific timetable for its ratification of the CITES Treaty?

 Answer

The question of Commission action against Ireland for non‐ratification of CITES (Convention on International Trade in Endangered Species) does not arise for a number of reasons. In the first place, the Commission has no power or basis to compel Ireland to ratify CITES. Indeed, the Community is not itself a party to the convention. In the second place, notwithstanding the fact that, formally speaking, the Community is not a party to CITES, the Community has adopted binding regulations which mirror the CITES controls on trade in endangered species. The current regulation is Council Regulation (EC) No 338/97 on the protection of species of wild fauna and flora by regulating trade therein(1) . The effect of these regulations is that, since January 1984, Ireland has been bound by requirements equivalent to or stronger than those of CITES. The regulations therefore ensure that Ireland's non‐ratification of CITES does not open up a potential loophole to trade in endangered species in the Community.

Question No 62 by Hugh McMahon (H−0048/99) Subject: Nuclear reactor shutdown at Hunterston, Scotland, in December 1998

Has the Commission been informed by the Nuclear Industry Inspectorate about the failure of the electricity supply and the shutdown of the reactor at the Hunterston nuclear power station in the west of Scotland that occurred shortly after Christmas last year?

Can the Commission assure the local residents in the west of Scotland that the highest possible standards of safety are being applied at the Hunterston site, and will it give a further assurance that the nuclear industry will be encouraged to employ sufficient numbers of experienced staff capable of reacting promptly when such emergencies occur?

 Answer

The Commission has not received official information related to the event at Hunterston from organisations in the United Kingdom. Nevertheless, inquiries have shown that the event (loss of power supplies at Hunterston power station) has been rated provisionally as level 2 ('incident') on the seven‐level International Nuclear Event Scale (INES) established at the International Atomic Energy Agency (IAEA). The event was given a level 2 rating on the grounds that some safety systems did not perform their intended functions, but this did not have any on‐site or off‐site impact. There was therefore no obligation to inform the Commission.

The British Safety Authorities are responsible for licensing of nuclear power installations and the respect of safety practices existing in the United Kingdom, including the availability of required human resources and expertise. The Euratom Treaty does not give the Community responsibility for the safe operation of nuclear power plants.

Question No 64 by Laura González Álvarez (H−0073/99) Subject: EU measures to prevent accidents involving toxic waste

The disastrous spillage of toxic waste at the Boliden mine in the Doñana reserve last year was followed in January of this year by another accident involving waste in Spain, a mere 50 kilometres from Huelva, when a rupture in a pond of acidic water caused large quantities of toxic acid to spill into the sea near the River Tinto.

Given that the impact of these spillages on the environment could have been avoided, had tighter safety measures been in place, does the Commission intend to close the legal loopholes and propose new Community legislation establishing technical standards to govern the storage of such waste? Does it intend to set up services offering technical advice on the sites, storage methods and management techniques required for toxic waste deposits?

 Answer

As far as the accident in the Aznalcóllar mine, worked by the company Boliden, is concerned, the Commission refers the honourable Member to its answer to question H−0056/99, tabled by Paul Lannoye.

Question No 66 by Jens‐Peter Bonde (H−0079/99) Subject: Risk assessment of chemicals

When does the Commission expect all chemicals to be risk assessed?

 Answer

'Existing' chemicals are chemicals which were on the Community market before 18 September 1981. Data provided by European Industry shows that there are approximately 2, 500 ‘existing' chemicals on the Community market in quantities greater than 1, 000 tonnes, representing some 90 % of the chemicals traded. These chemicals are prioritised for an assessment of their risks to human health and the environment, with the most dangerous ones being assessed first. Industry, Member States and the Commission are currently working to achieve a target of assessing 1000 of these chemicals by the year 2005. A successful outcome is, of course, dependent on the Commission having the necessary staffing resources to undertake this work.

All ‘new' chemicals are assessed for their risks to human health and the environment before they are placed on the Community market. Since 1993 some 600 chemicals have been assessed.

The Commission has commenced stock‐taking of the main Community legislation on industrial chemicals to see whether improvement is necessary.

 Answer

The Commission is aware of the inquiry by the investigating magistrate at Cherbourg. It awaits the outcome of that inquiry with interest but has no role to play at this stage.

Regarding the incidence of leukaemia in the region of Cap de la Hague, the available scientific evidence does not lead the Commission to conclude that there is a relationship between krypton−85 discharges or other wastes originating from Cap de la Hague.

It has no basis, therefore, for undertaking an investigation into these matters.

Question No 68 by Anita Pollack (H−0089/99) Subject: Greening EU policies

What progress has the Commission made in implementing the Cardiff Council idea that concern for the environment should be a major consideration in all decisions relating to the Structural Funds, overseas aid and trade, implementation of single market, and agriculture?

 Answer

As the follow‐up to the Commission Communication on a strategy for integration put to the Cardiff European Council in May 1998 calling for environmental appraisals for all key policy proposals, the Commission is currently taking stock of progress in this field. The Commission is invited by the Vienna European Council to submit a progress report in time for the Cologne European Council in June 1999. In this context the Commission is currently reviewing its internal measures on integrating environment issues in other policies which were adopted in July 1997.

In relation to progress on integration in the particular policy areas mentioned by the honourable Member of Parliament, the following can be mentioned.

In relation to agriculture and structural funds, the Parliament and the Council are currently discussing the proposals included in the Agenda 2000 package. In preparing these proposals, the Commission ensured that the environmental dimension was integrated into the ‘regulatory provisions' and this interface was explained in the ‘explanatory memorandum' of these proposals. In January this year the Commission presented a major Communication on the relationship between agriculture and environment ‘Directions towards sustainable agriculture'(12) . The purpose of this Communication is to elaborate on the approach taken by the Commission to the integration of environment in agriculture in its Agenda 2000 proposals. The Communication shows how environmental considerations have become a major concern for the common agricultural policy.

In relation to the structural funds on 3 February the Commission has adopted a working document setting out indicative guidance for programmes for the period 2000‐06, which addresses the integration of environment into the programmes.

Concerning the implementation of the Internal Market, the Commission will present in the near future a Communication on the Internal Market and the Environment.
