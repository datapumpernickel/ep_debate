Verbatim report of proceedings
Monday, 26 September 2011 - Strasbourg OJ edition

20. One-minute speeches (Rule 150)
Video of the speeches
PV
 

  President. − The next item is the one-minute speeches in accordance with Rule 150 of the Rules of Procedure.

 
  
 

  Rareş-Lucian Niculescu (PPE). – (RO) Mr President, I would like, just as other colleagues will also probably want to do this evening, to express my disappointment that the pull of populism made two European states block Romania’s accession to the Schengen area last week. Romania and Bulgaria were given a promise on the day they joined the European Union that, if they met the necessary conditions, they would be admitted to Schengen, but this promise has been broken.

I hope that both states will reverse this decision as soon as they can, in the nearest possible future, thereby demonstrating their impartiality. Romania and Bulgaria have fulfilled all the technical criteria for joining the Schengen area, and our citizens are entitled to enjoy the freedom of movement fully.

 
  
 

  Alexander Mirsky (S&D). - (LV) Mr President, there is a political expression: ‘Power at any cost’. Such methods were used by Hitler, Mussolini, Pol Pot and Saddam Hussein. In Latvia a political movement has appeared that promised to do one thing before the elections, but after winning, switched from the right [side] to the left and abandoned the promises it had made to the electorate. The name of this glorious political association is Saskaņas centrs. In everyday life those people who have changed their orientation are called gays. In Latvia we now have political gays going under the name of Saskaņas centrs, and they agree to anything for the sake of power. Thank you for your attention.

 
  
 

  Andrea Zanoni (ALDE). – (IT) Mr President, the hunting season opens in many European countries in September, and people begin to shoot millions of wild animals in the name of an anachronistic activity which can no longer be justified.

Despite the protection laid down by European laws, in certain countries, such as Italy, some local authorities allow exceptional hunting, namely the hunting of protected birds such as chaffinches or meadow pipits, just to pander to the hunters’ lobby and secure their votes. The Veneto and Lombardy regions have recently approved exceptional hunting again, demonstrating contempt and indifference, not least towards the European Court of Justice and its very recent judgments on this subject.

Europe needs stricter rules. In order to protect wild animals we have to put an end to this barbarian medieval practice. We need to put an immediate stop to these illegal rules in these regions.

 
  
 

  Marina Yannakoudakis (ECR). - Mr President, climate change, pollution and CO2 levels are a matter of constant debate in this Chamber. Today, I am going to take it one step further and talk about great rivers, which all Member States have, and in particular the river in my constituency of London, which is the River Thames.

The phrase ‘EU targets’ usually fills me with fear and trepidation. As is becoming more evident, common targets do not always work in a Union made up of individual members. However, I am pleased to note that in my constituency of London the water quality in the River Thames is well on its way to meeting the goal set by the European Water Framework Directive.

The British Government has made cleaning our rivers a priority, with a £110 million grant allocated to that objective. I hope other Member States are taking similar measures to reverse water pollution and thus to safeguard wildlife and human health against contaminated water supplies.

Whilst the EU can come up with goals and targets for our environment, it is vital that Member States follow the UK example and take control of their own environmental projects.

 
  
 

  Michail Tremopoulos (Verts/ALE). − (EL) Mr President, the image of a country such as Greece, which has failed spectacularly to achieve its fiscal targets, obviously raises problems. However, light needs to be shed on the underlying factors. Deep recession as a result of across-the-board austerity measures was forecast from the outset but the forecast was not taken into proper account. The troika insisted on obtaining a commitment on unfeasible fiscal targets and the Greek government passively accepted everything and, of course, failed to deliver.

At the same time, however, a clientelist network of protection and obscurity has been maintained. In vain have we proposed simple and effective measures to verify assets obtained through corruption and tax evasion. A recent law on buildings erected without planning permission wipes out fines totalling over EUR 10 billion. It has taken months of laborious effort to verify the origin of funds for Greek deposits abroad, and not just to tax interest, which is what the Greek government was trying to do. There is a growing credibility vacuum which risks being construed as unavoidable, even though it is not.

 
  
 

  João Ferreira (GUE/NGL). – (PT) Mr President, this year, in Portugal, more than 3 000 firms closed their doors, investment and industrial output fell to the levels of 15 years ago, and agricultural output declined to a level not seen for 16 years. The programme of aggression, impoverishment and extortion conceived by the International Monetary Fund, the European Commission and the European Central Bank is clearly compounding and aggravating this scenario. The recession is upon us. People in general are seeing their incomes fall as the cost of living rises, and the attack on strategic state-owned or partly state-owned companies is being prepared. Unemployment is spreading and with it poverty, while genuine social dramas are becoming ever more frequent. Is not the disaster we are witnessing in Greece enough for them to reverse course?Hence we wish to hail the struggle of Portuguese workers and the Portuguese people, of which the demonstrations by the CGTP Intersindical this coming 1 October will be a vivid expression. Certainly many thousands will be participating to say ‘no’ to injustices and blanket impoverishment and to give voice to their refusal to allow the recipes of this troika to despoil their present and rob them of their future. Their cry will find resonance in the struggles of workers and peoples throughout Europe. The troika really should start to listen to them.

 
  
 

  Nikolaos Salavrakos (EFD). − (EL) Mr President, I am happy to inform the House that last week Greece paid coupons of EUR 769 million on bonds that mature in 2037 and 2040 respectively. It proved once again that, in the face of stubborn specialists and non-specialists, with and without competence, Greece is discharging its financial obligations, while the people have suffered a massive squeeze on their income, to the point at which they cannot withstand any more.

I should like to take this opportunity to thank all our partners who have stood by Greece. I refer to Ms Merkel, who has given her full assurance that, so far, Greece is paying over par for the loans given to us. In response to the solidarity and honesty of the German people, I would ask them to read carefully the article in the Sunday edition of Die Welt setting out all the information on the forced occupation loan which increases Germany’s debt to Greece by 70 billion …

(The President cut off the speaker)

 
  
 

  Franz Obermayr (NI). – (DE) Mr President, in the Belgian city of Antwerp, a Sharia court is currently being established. The initiative is coming from the radical organisation Islam for Belgium. The aim is to create a parallel legal system. The court is intended, in particular, to provide mediation in family law disputes. In doing so, self-appointed judges will apply Sharia law instead of Belgian family law.

For women that means suppression, as women and men do not have the same rights under Sharia law. Criminal law is also to be handled. Legal experts fear that this will prevent the Belgian authorities from prosecuting so-called cultural crimes such as honour killings and forced marriages.

Sharia courts reinforce parallel societies that have nothing in common with our understanding of human rights. If we allow Muslims to found their own Sharia courts, it will be the end of a Europe of freedom and the rule of law.

 
  
 

  Georgios Koumoutsakos (PPE). − (EL) Mr President, at this critical time for the European economy, the least one would expect from the European Union would be for it to speak in a coordinated manner. Instead, we have a storm of uncoordinated and often contradictory statements, interviews and opinions.

Everyone is talking: Council, Commission, Commissioners, ministers, deputy ministers, the Central Bank, associates, advisors, advisors’ advisors, assistants’ assistants; it is a real ‘European Babel’. This makes the markets nervous, reinforces uncertainty, increases distrust and erodes effectiveness. In Greece’s particular case, it is receiving simultaneous messages of solidarity and punishment, of support and threat, of optimism and catastrophe. This perpetual swinging back and forth is doing a great deal of harm. It is further disorienting an entire nation, an entire society, which is already at the end of its tether. We need to put an end to this European Babel and we need to do so now, today, not tomorrow. It would be good for Greece, good for Europe and good for Europe’s status, credibility and effectiveness.

 
  
 

  Phil Prendergast (S&D). - Mr President, at the last plenary session I raised the issue of TalkTalk’s plan to close its call centre in Waterford with the loss of up to 575 jobs at just 30 days’ notice. Following this, the company wrote to me with a thinly-veiled threat of legal action. They copied their letter to the EU Commissioner for Employment, Social Affairs and Inclusion, László Andor, whom I had asked to verify the company’s compliance with EU employment legislation. This amounts to serious interference with my parliamentary duty to raise matters of public concern and with my right to do so as a Member of the European Parliament in this House without fear of legal reprisal.

Mr President, I would request that you write to the company asserting the right of Members of the European Parliament to defend their constituents’ interests.

 
  
 

  Ramon Tremosa i Balcells (ALDE). - Mr President, on 19 October the Commission will present a map of the priorities of the TEN-T core network. We need more than ever a strong mandate from the Commission in favour of efficiency in the investment policy-making process. We expect strong leadership from the Commission to overcome pressures coming from some inefficient Member States. The TEN-T core network has to be compulsory for Member States. It has to harmonise gauge width and the length of the trains. We need more than ever common European technical standards.

We also expect from the Commission a clear priorities map based on a cost/benefit analysis. The core network should maximise the macro-economic benefits in terms of economic growth and job creation.

It is time to connect the main European ports and industrial regions with the main European markets. This is especially important in the case of the Mediterranean nations and regions. The risk of economic divergence between north and south must be avoided.

 
  
 

  François Alfonsi (Verts/ALE). – (FR) Mr President, ladies and gentlemen, the changing situation in the Basque Country leads me once again to alert Parliament to the worrying behaviour for European democracy of one of the Member States, namely Spain.

In May, it required a last-minute appeal decision, passed by only 1 vote, by Spain’s constitutional court to allow the Basque independence movement Bildu’s list of candidates to stand in the local elections. The fact is, those candidates received 25% of the vote. The attempt to ban them was therefore an attempt to undermine the democratic expression of the Basque people, which is wholly unacceptable and even inconceivable at the very heart of the European Union.

Then, in July, I personally attended a hearing in the trial of Mr Otegi, the leader of the left-wing Basque independence movement, and several of his friends. In reality, they were being prosecuted for thought crimes; the court has just handed them the maximum sentence of 10 years’ imprisonment, a sentence that is wholly disproportionate, deliberately excessive and which has shocked all of democratic society, both in the Basque Country and in Madrid.

The European Parliament must voice its disapproval of the Spanish Government’s policy towards the Basque Country, at the very time when the political developments are such that a peace process of the Northern Ireland kind could result in a long-term political solution that benefits the Basques, the citizens of Spain themselves and all the citizens of Europe.

 
  
  

IN THE CHAIR: DIANA WALLIS
Vice-President

 
  
 

  Søren Bo Søndergaard (GUE/NGL). – (DA) Madam President, as the President mentioned at the opening of the sitting, Troy Davis was executed in the United States last Thursday. Just before his execution, he lifted his head from the trolley to which he was strapped and said to those listening: I am innocent! At the same time, he called for his case to be investigated further and for the truth to come out.

We have to say that murder is always abhorrent, even when it is committed by a state. What is even worse for us is when it happens in a country with which, traditionally, we have good relations. The European Parliament has repeatedly expressed its opposition to the death penalty. The President would therefore have my backing if he were to approach the US authorities on behalf of this House and, while making reference to this specific case, reiterate our opposition to the latest executions in the United States.

 
  
 

  Dimitar Stoyanov (NI). – (BG) Madam President, on Saturday 19-year-old Angel Petrov was deliberately knocked down by a car and killed on the spot. The man detained for his murder is an associate of the gypsy boss – and proven criminal – Kiril Rashkov. The following evening, an angry mob gathered outside Kiril Rashkov’s house and set it on fire.

These are some of the extremely ugly scenes which have occurred in my country. Kiril Rashkov was caught unawares by the arrival of democracy, while serving a 30-year sentence for a wide variety of crimes. At the moment, he is becoming a symbol representing two things: firstly, a symbol of vast wealth acquired illegally and, secondly, a symbol of special treatment due to his ethnic origin.

For 20 years Bulgarian governments, including the present administration, have made the judicial system and courts apply double standards. This escalates tension and creates Bulgarian citizens’ desire for justice. If this state of affairs does not change, I, for one, do not want the population to take justice into their own hands, but this is what will happen.

 
  
 

  Iosif Matula (PPE). – (RO) Madam President, last week the European Union abandoned, I hope only for a short time, one of the basic principles on which it is founded: equality among its members.

The JHA Council left the accession of Romania and Bulgaria to the Schengen area in abeyance, even though both states have met all the technical criteria, according to the experts. I feel that the refusal by a Dutch minister to keep a promise made by the EU through the accession treaties has gone too far. This has happened just when I think that the Dutch public has confidence in Romania, as Dutch investors are top of the league in my country, with subscribed capital amounting to EUR 5.37 billion, held in nearly 4 000 majority-controlled companies.

Introducing additional Schengen accession criteria into the debate is extremely unfair and discredits EU integration, especially when the rules adopted by common consent are changed during the game.

 
  
 

  Claudiu Ciprian Tănăsescu (S&D). – (RO) Madam President, Romania’s failure to join Schengen, although it can be explained by Romania’s representatives’ inability to present this case convincingly, has, unfortunately, a negative impact on the whole Romanian population, not only on those in government who have demonstrated, once again, that they are not up to the task they have been given.

Unfortunately, this point was overlooked when the vote was held on Romania’s accession to the Schengen area, with the states which openly opposed this move failing to take into account the far-reaching implications which a vote against Romania could have.

I hope that, in future, this hostile and purely simplistic attitude will be replaced by one reflecting the real spirit of the European Union, whereby we must not punish a Member State and its whole population just because they have foolish leaders.

 
  
 

  Kristiina Ojuland (ALDE). - Madam President, today two young Tibetan monks set themselves on fire in Kirti monastery to protest against the repressive policies of the Republic of China in the area. This year alone there have now been at least four occurrences of self-immolation in Kirti. Such a radical form of protest illustrates the despair of the Tibetans, who are seeking freedom of religion as well as meaningful autonomy in the historic area of Tibet. Continuous desperate acts of protest also demonstrate that Tibetans determinedly resist the ongoing violations of human rights and cultural genocide that are being carried out in Tibet.

Therefore, the European Union, and its Member States too, should lend their support and address the human rights of Tibetans more rigorously with the People’s Republic of China, but also with Nepal, as only recently concerns have been raised over the possible repatriation of Tibetan refugees.

 
  
 

  Oriol Junqueras Vies (Verts/ALE). - (ES) Madam President, the euro crisis is becoming alarmingly more acute and the steps taken so far have not succeeded in easing the pressure on the single currency. It is crucial that the European Union show clear common sense in the decisions ahead.

In this context, the decision as to which projects are to be given priority status in the revised central Trans-European Transport Network (TEN-T) will reveal to what extent we are on the right road out of the crisis.

The Mediterranean Corridor is a vital infrastructure for the future of the Union, and excluding it from European transport priorities would be a clear indication that institutional actions in the EU are lacking consistency. Nobody would understand these decisions being based on anything other than viability, efficiency and productivity, which are the criteria endorsing the Mediterranean Corridor as a priority.

Only decisions that are wise, reasoned and economically sound will enable us to reassert confidence in the future of the Union and the euro.

 
  
 

  Paul Murphy (GUE/NGL). - Madam President, after my visit to Kazakhstan on behalf of the European United Left Group, the government launched a slanderous campaign against me. That included the fabrication of a statement by the Chair of the Central Asia Delegation, and now an official communication to the EU from Kazakhstan suggesting that I broke Kazakhstan criminal law by creating social discord and declaring that ‘the Republic of Kazakhstan has the right to declare the abovementioned officials’ – referring to myself and my political adviser – ‘as persona non grata’.

Why have they taken this approach? Because I supported the heroic oil workers who have been on strike for four months now, fighting for decent wages and conditions, fighting for the right to an independent and free trade union, workers who have faced violent repression from company thugs, from State forces. Two people have now been murdered – a trade unionist and the daughter of a trade unionist. Natalia Sokolova, the lawyer of the workers, has been jailed for six years for stirring up social conflict. That was again reinforced today when a farcical appeal took place and her prison sentence was confirmed.

I call on international trade unionists and activists to step up the campaign against the company, against the government, to support these workers.

 
  
 

  Csaba Sógor (PPE). – (HU) Madam President, the new Law on Education adopted this year in Romania defines the University of Medicine and Pharmacy of Târgu Mureş as – amongst others – a multicultural higher education institution, where half of the student body is already made up of Hungarian nationals. While the law which was passed stipulates the establishment of a separate Hungarian faculty, the university’s ethnic-majority management is unwilling to comply. In an institution officially recognised as multicultural, and where half the students are Hungarian, the management are doing everything in their power to prevent them from studying medicine in their own language.

This is, essentially, the typical multicultural and minority-friendly policy that aptly reflects the attitudes taken towards the issue in certain Member States. The review of compliance with the Copenhagen criteria has brought into focus the treatment of minorities by states wishing to accede to the EU. However, it is also necessary to guarantee accountability for compliance with the Copenhagen criteria even after accession. Interethnic relations are tense in Europe, particularly in the Eastern regions; ignoring this would be a huge mistake, which could have considerable adverse implications in the future.

 
  
 

  Katarína Neveďalová (S&D). - (SK) Madam President, we are now discussing what the next budget period in the seven years starting from 2014 will be like. I personally believe that we should be talking more about our young people, our future generation, in this period.

When discussing the budget, we should surely talk about programmes and programme support over the next seven years, and not just mobility programmes and education programmes such as the Erasmus programme, but also other programmes that are important for young people. The main one is the Youth in Action programme, which helps young people to get an informal education, experience and perhaps a different view of the world from the one they would get in school.

In my opinion, we should therefore definitely support this programme in the coming period, as well as all of its components, just as they are. I also particularly support the non-profit and non-governmental youth organisations which are the prime movers in this, helping young people to gain experience and providing a space for them in which they can develop themselves and acquire their own outlook on the future. It is therefore important for us to support this.

 
  
 

  Marian-Jean Marinescu (PPE). – (RO) Madam President, the accession of Romania and Bulgaria to the Schengen area was postponed last week until an unspecified date. Two Member States, the Netherlands and Finland, opposed this measure, without giving any reasons in line with EU legislation. These Member States have not only blocked access to the Schengen area, they have also prevented both states from meeting the obligations arising from the Treaty.

I think it is unfair and unproductive to use reasons linked to domestic policy when making decisions at EU level. The European Parliament has already given a vote of consent on this matter. I think that it needs to come back and express its opinion very clearly, once again, particularly concerning compliance with EU regulations and respect among Member States.

We must give a reminder about a good behaviour rule: keeping one’s word when given. I believe that the European Council meeting in October needs to prove that the European Union still exists. The decision allowing both Member States to join Schengen must be adopted.

 
  
 

  Ioan Enciu (S&D). – (RO) Madam President, the current proposal for an energy efficiency directive presents measures for achieving a 20% reduction in energy consumption by 2020 in a number of ways, including by improving the technologies used and reducing energy systems’ losses.

The Romanian Government has come up with an original solution for achieving this compulsory objective much earlier and without any investments. Instead of proposing measures to eliminate a more than 40% heat loss from Romania’s centralised heating system, the Boc government has cut the heating subsidy for low-income families. This coming winter, a million families will no longer be able to pay their heating bills and will have to stop using heating and live with the cold. This means that, nationally, the 20% reduction target will be achieved quickly.

I appeal to the European Commission to take note of this and adopt measures relating to this decision which violates fundamental human rights to a decent life.

 
  
 

  Czesław Adam Siekierski (PPE). – (PL) Madam President, during the current part-session we are facing a very important vote on the six-pack, five regulations and one directive, which aim to strengthen economic governance in Europe in the light of the adverse experience of the economic crisis. This strengthening is to be achieved primarily by a fundamental reform of the Stability and Growth Pact, which has been found to be ineffective in preventing crises, as has been brutally exposed by the recent turmoil in the euro area.

After the initial green light given to the legislative package by the Ecofin Council at its meeting in Wrocław, the fate of the six-pack lies in Parliament’s hands. Only if it is adopted quickly will it be able to calm the recent instability in the financial markets and prevent another recession, something which is looking ever more likely. However, we cannot allow a similar situation to the one we had before our recess, when disputes within the Council prevented the rapid adoption of the package. New regulations providing for budgetary discipline in the Member States and strengthening of the EU’s economic stability are essential, given the constant economic troubles worldwide.

 
  
 

  Anna Záborská (PPE). - (SK) Madam President, it seems to me that many EU politicians have already found a cure to the problems currently afflicting the Member States. This miracle cure comprises a ‘United States of Europe’. All that is required, apparently, is to relieve Member States of their budgetary sovereignty, unify tax rates and create a completely new tax. Billions will be poured into a stabilisation mechanism from the coffers of eurozone Member States. A brand new official will be placed in charge – the European Minister of Finance. It will not be a United States but a United Purses of Europe.

Every one of the previously mentioned decisions is bad. We have already been through this in Europe and we know how it ended. Assets without an owner, people without motivation and the economy, which we want to save, sinking to the bottom. If we really care about building up a European identity, about genuine solidarity between nations, and about peace and prosperity, we need to discuss a far broader range of topics than mere financial issues. We need to define the values on which the whole enterprise will be based. At the moment it is built on sand.

 
  
 

  Agustín Díaz de Mera García Consuegra (PPE). - (ES) Madam President, on 29 June 2011 a group of Taliban terrorists murdered Antonio Planas in Kabul, a Spanish national like myself, aged 49, married and the father of a young girl.

Until a few days ago, certain Spanish nationals who were victims of terrorist attacks outside of the European Union territory could not have access to protection and compensation mechanisms. On 23 June 2011, however, the legislation was amended to benefit terrorist victims and their relatives, irrespective of the country where the attack took place.

The threat is not limited to a given territory and it must be prevented at national, EU and international level, strengthening cooperation with third countries.

The defence of terrorist victims and their rights should be a priority policy in the Union, and the institutions should encourage their public recognition. Thus, we welcome the actions proposed by the Commission, although more concrete instruments to help the victims of terror are still needed.

The family of Antonio Planas needs our help, and we urge the institutions concerned to provide it.

 
  
 

  President. − That concludes the item.
