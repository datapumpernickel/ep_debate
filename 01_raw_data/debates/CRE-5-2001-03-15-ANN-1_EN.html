Questions to the Council
Question no 1 by Alexandros Alavanos (H-0133/01)
 Subject: Increase in the European Union budget
 

The second report on economic and social cohesion in the EU points out the wide disparities in development between the Member States, the regions and the countries which will be joining the EU in the next few years. In order to reduce these disparities, or to prevent the gap from widening at least, a much greater amount of funding than that provided by the EU budget will be required. At the same time, the cost of dealing with the BSE crisis has raised spending on agriculture to its maximum permissible level, prohibiting any other action and necessitating cutbacks in other areas of agricultural expenditure.

Since it is becoming increasingly clear that the EU's budget is unable to meet the needs of enlargement or cover current EU policies, will the Council consider increasing the EU's own resources? Have the Member States raised the question of increasing own resources or revising the financial perspective? How will the Council satisfy these urgent financial needs?

 
  
 

The Council calls upon the honourable Member to study the answer already given at Question Time at the last part-session in February. This answer was sent to him in writing and must be regarded as the answer that the Council is still giving to this question. A copy of it will again be sent to him.

 

Question no 11 by Mihail Papayannakis (H-0161/01)
 Subject: Statements by the Presidency on behalf of the EU
 

One of the countries which has been countersigning statements by the Presidency on behalf of the EU within the framework of the common foreign policy since the beginning of December 2000 is Turkey. So far, this country has countersigned all the statements in which the associated countries have been involved. However, in the statement of 2 February 2001, (5697/01 - Press release 36), on the case of Mr Felix Kulof, Turkey's signature is missing. On the basis of what mechanism do the associated countries sign statements by the Presidency? Do they have the right to choose which statements they sign, and how is this choice accepted? How credible is the entire system of participation if those concerned are able to choose which statements they sign and what they condemn (for instance, human rights issues), depending on the practices within their own countries?

 
  
 

In line with the conclusions of the European Council of Helsinki in December, Turkey - like the Central and Eastern European countries associated with the European Union, the associated countries Cyprus and Malta, and the European Free Trade Associates (EFTA) countries, Iceland, Liechtenstein and Norway, members of the European Economic Area - is invited to align itself with CFSP declarations made by the Presidency on behalf of the European Union.

One cannot expect countries - even candidates for EU accession - to associate themselves automatically with EU declarations on foreign policy issues on which they have not been consulted and in the drafting of which they have played no part. On the other hand, as part of the process by which they approach accession the EU welcomes it when they feel able to associate themselves with EU positions in CFSP and indeed encourages them to do so. But it must be for these countries to decide freely whether they wish to do so.

In 2000, the associates were invited to associate themselves with 131 CFSP declarations. In 95 cases, all countries concerned aligned themselves; in 36 cases, one or more countries wished not to do so, in 18 cases of which Turkey was one of them. In 2001, the Associated countries have been invited to align themselves with 16 declarations. Turkey has aligned itself with 15 of these declarations.

 

Question no 12 by Ursula Schleicher (H-0162/01)
 Subject: Suspension of gas supplies from Russia
 

The Russian government has ordered a stop to gas supplies from Georgia. The European Union is currently heavily dependent on Russian gas. Does the Council agree that Russia has shown that it is not a reliable supplier of energy to the EU?

 
  
 

The Council is not aware of the commercial modalities of the gas supply agreements between Georgia and Russia.

However, the Council is concerned about the interruption of gas supplies from Russia to Georgia and its effect on the economic, social and humanitarian situation in Georgia. Interruptions of this kind, however short, do not only affect the people directly involved but, of course, in a wider perspective, also have an effect on Russia’s reputation as a gas supplier.

The interest of the Council in increasing the security of energy supplies is demonstrated by several items on the agenda of a forthcoming Council meeting.

Firstly, the Council is currently undertaking an in-depth examination of the Commission's Green Paper: "Towards a European strategy for the security of supply", which underlines our growing dependence on external energy sources and notably on Russia for gas. Within the context of enlargement, this dependence will increase further due to the historic links between Eastern Europe and the former Soviet Union. One of the responses to dependence will be increased diversification of energy sources.

Moreover, on the occasion of the 6th EU-Russia summit since the entry into force (1.12.1997) of the EU-Russian Partnership and Cooperation Agreement (PCA), held in Paris on 30 October 2000, it was agreed to institute an energy dialogue on a regular basis to enable progress to be made in the definition and arrangements for an EU-Russian Energy Partnership. Since then, exploratory contacts at high level have been taking place, taking into account the interest of the EU in safeguarding the continuation of reliable energy supplies from Russia. On the same occasion, Russia also confirmed its commitment to proceed to the ratification of the Energy Charter Treaty in the near future. The Community is currently participating in negotiations on a Transit Protocol to the Energy Charter Treaty, which should also contribute to increasing the security of supply, especially as regards the legal framework.

Finally, the Action Plan on the Northern Dimension, adopted at the European Council of Feira, 19­20 June 2000, comprises, within a series of priority actions, measures to ensure (legal) security of investment and of supply from i.a. Northwest Russia. Implementation of this Action Plan will be reviewed at the June European Council in Göteborg.

 

Question no 13 by Camilo Nogueira Román (H-0164/01)
 Subject: The civilian population in Chechnya
 

What action is the Council taking in support of the civilian population in Chechnya, given that credible sources state that it is now the victim of a tragic wave of unexplained disappearances, illegal arrests and assassinations attributable to the Russian military, while the legal authorities of the Russian Federation are failing in their duty to protect the most basic human rights?

 
  
 

The Council remains concerned at the difficult situation of the civilian population in Chechnya. In all political dialogue meetings with Russia, as well as in the OSCE and Council of Europe, the EU expresses its serious concerns at reports on violations of human rights and humanitarian law, in particular forced disappearances, summary executions, torture and arbitrary detentions. The EU is keeping the pressure on the Russian authorities to bring human rights violations before the court. The Council regularly expresses its disappointment that only very few complaints received by Mr Kalamanov have been followed by criminal proceedings so far.

Furthermore, the Council keeps stressing to Russia that the return of the OSCE Assistance Group to Chechnya is important and that it should be able to fulfil its full mandate. Foreign Minister Ivanov recently expressed Russia's agreement to the return of the Assistance Group. Only a few technical questions remain to be resolved. The Council will watch closely to see if Ivanov's statement is followed by concrete steps.

In view of the Commission for human rights in Geneva (19-27 March), the EU is to bring the human rights situation in Chechnya before the Commission, considering that the recommendations from last year's Commission have not been fully implemented and that the human rights situation remains unsatisfactory.

As long as the situation in Chechnya does not substantially improve, the Council will insist on discussing this point at all political dialogue meetings and it will explain that the lack of progress on Chechnya remains an obstacle to developing the full scope of the envisaged EU-Russia partnership. Upcoming opportunities to express the Council's concern are the Cooperation Council with Russia on 10 April and the EU-Russia Summit on 17 May.

 

Question no 14 by Gerard Collins (H-0168/01)
 Subject: Campaign against poverty in the world
 

Central to its programme for effective development cooperation, the Swedish Presidency is committed to the campaign against poverty. Will the Council make a statement on its efforts in this area, including its position on the elimination of Third World debt?

 
  
 

The Council as a whole is deeply committed to the fight against poverty. We believe that we possess the knowledge and resources to make substantial progress towards the international development goal of halving the proportion of people living in extreme poverty by the year 2015. You will remember that this goal was confirmed by 149 Heads of State and Government at the UN Millennium Summit last autumn.

In its joint declaration with the Commission on 10 November 2000, the Council stressed that the main objective of Community development policy must be to reduce and, eventually, to eradicate poverty. Poverty is a multidimensional phenomenon. Experience shows that poverty is not just a matter of lack of material resources – it is also a lack of access to power. To be poor means not being able to make one's voice heard. It is to feel insecure. To be poor is to have very little chance of influencing one's own life. This implies that poverty includes much more than the conventional dimensions of income or consumption. Poverty also includes risk, vulnerability, voicelessness and powerlessness. The fight against poverty must take all these dimensions into consideration. The perception of the poor themselves must be the starting point.

Bringing the different dimensions into our understanding of poverty inevitably leads us to see its context specificity.

Accordingly, in their declaration, the Council and the Commission went on to discuss ways of combating poverty, highlighting the importance of measures to improve:

Access to adequate food supplies, health and education, natural resources and drinking water, land, employment and credit facilities, information and communication technologies, political involvement, services and infrastructure. The Council also emphasised the need for consolidating the democratic process, respect for human rights, peace and the prevention of conflict, the development of social policies and the integration of social and environmental concerns in macro-economic reform programmes.

Another important dimension of development is the gravity of the communicable diseases, especially HIV/AIDS. In view of the global dimension of the problem and its impact on poverty, targeting this problem must be considered an absolute necessity.

Furthermore, the declaration stressed the integration of developing countries, in particular the least developed among them, into the world economy. In this respect, the Council recognised that economic growth was essential, although not sufficient in itself to ensure poverty reduction.

The Council also supported the Commission’s view that Community activities should be concentrated in a limited number of areas selected on the basis of their contribution towards reducing poverty and for which Community action provides added value. These are trade, regional integration and cooperation, support to macroeconomic framework, transport, food security and institutional capacity building.

As a follow-up to the new development policy, the Commission has, on several occasions, stated that it will, early 2001, deliver a communication on how to strengthen its capacity to address the objective of poverty reduction in the EC development cooperation. The Swedish Presidency is of firm belief that the Commission will live up to its commitment in this area. Poverty cannot be handled as a sector or separate part of development cooperation. To implement the development policy, the interdisciplinary and poverty focused approach to development partnerships must be strengthened. There is also a need for improved coherence among different policy areas focusing on poverty alleviation. Increasing competence and capacity of the Commission to fully embrace the poverty reduction objective is a means to achieve this.

As regards the elimination of third world debt, which is obviously relevant to the fight against poverty, the Council would refer to its answer to oral question H-0108/01 from Mr Brian Crowley.

 

Question no 15 by Niall Andrews (H-0170/01)
 Subject: Afghanistan
 

There is growing alarm at the situation in Afghanistan. People are dying in the camps, and the majority are children. The UN has warned of an imminent devastating crisis after two years of drought, war and lack of investment. Aid workers consider that UN sanctions against Afghanistan have disrupted the distribution of relief supplies. Will the Council therefore lead a move to end those sanctions?

 
  
 

Sanctions against the Taliban - and not against Afghanistan - have been adopted by the UN Security Council following the Taliban's refusal to extradite Usama bin Laden, who is abusing his host country as a basis to launch acts of international terrorism and as a convenient cover to set up a network of training camps for radical terrorist groups. The relevant UNSC Resolution 1267 (1999) further accuses the Taliban of, inter alia, massive violations of human rights and of international humanitarian law. The sanctions adopted concern a flight ban for aircraft under Taliban control and a freezing of Taliban assets.

As a result of the Taliban's persistent refusal to comply with the pertinent UNSC Resolutions, the UNSC adopted, in December 2000, its Resolution 1333 (2000), which oblige all UN Member States to adopt a new set of sanctions against the Taliban regime. These sanctions demand in particular: the prohibition of direct or indirect supply, sale and transfer of arms and related military material; the prohibition of the direct or indirect supply, sale and transfer of technical advise, assistance or training related to military activities of Taliban forces; the withdrawal of military experts and advisors from the Taliban regime; the closure of Taliban and of Ariana airlines offices; the freezing of assets of Usama bin Laden an individuals or entities associated with him; the prohibition of the sale, supply or transfer of the chemical acetic anhydride (necessary for the production of Heroin).

The Council, while fully sharing the honourable MEP's preoccupations concerning the disastrous humanitarian situation in Afghanistan, does not believe that these sanctions have or will disrupt the distribution of relief supplies. On the contrary, these targeted sanctions are aimed against a regime which, due to its ruthless pursuit of the civil war and of its massive violations of human rights and the international humanitarian law, is primarily responsible for the disastrous situation in Afghanistan. The severe drought has contributed to a further deterioration of the humanitarian situation. The Council expects that the sanctions will contribute to reduce outside interference and to promote a negotiated settlement of the Afghan conflict. The Council further expects that they will lead to a reduction in the production of Afghan drugs, thus reducing the revenue necessary for the Taliban to pursue and to finance the war.

In the meantime, the EU will continue to provide humanitarian aid to internally displaced Afghans and to Afghan refugees in neighbouring countries, whenever the difficult conditions on the ground allow.

 

Question no 16 by Brian Crowley (H-0172/01)
 Subject: Clostridium novyi bacterium and heroin deaths
 

Deaths caused in Ireland, Scotland and England by contaminated heroin are being linked to the toxin producing strain of Clostridium novyi type A which is a very virulent organism. This 'bad batch' of heroin may still be available. Health Boards in Ireland and Scotland collaborated in the investigations, as did Centres for Disease Control and Prevention in the US. Will the Council state what lessons it has learned from this case, and does it agree that the tragic deaths arising from such heroin use should strengthen further the EU’s resolve to fight drugs?

 
  
 

The Council informs the honourable Member that the matter referred to in the question has not been discussed in the framework of the Council.

A programme of Community action on the prevention of drug dependence within the framework action in the field of public health (1996-2000)1 was recently extended until 31 December 2001. The objective of this programme is to help in combating drug dependence, in particular by encouraging cooperation between Member States, supporting their action and promoting coordination between their policies and programmes, while ensuring consistency and complementarity with other relevant community programmes. It also provides for cooperation with non-member countries and relevant international organisations. The actions of the programme centre on the collection of data, research and evaluation as well as on information, education and training.

The programme will be replaced by the programme of Community action in the field of public health, presently being examined by Council.

Finally as the honourable Member knows the European Union attaches great attention to the fight against drugs. Thus, the EU Action Plan on Drugs (2000 - 2004) was drawn up and endorsed by the European Council at its recent June meeting in Feira. Collection, analysis and dissemination of objective, reliable and comparable data on the drugs phenomenon with support of EMCDDA and Europol, as well as drug prevention, demand reduction and the reduction of the adverse consequences of drug use are among the priority activities listed in the Action Plan.

 

Question no 17 by James (Jim) Fitzsimons (H-0174/01)
 Subject: Clean energy
 

Will the Council set out its views on the establishment of new initiatives aimed at facilitating a switch from the use of fossil fuels to greater use of clean energy sources, and does it consider that the recent severe winter storms across Europe confirm the need for such initiatives?

 
  
 

The honourable Member will certainly remember that in his recent speech delivered to the Committee on Industry, External Trade, Research and Energy of the European Parliament (23.1.2001), the President of the Council has indicated the high priority it attaches to initiatives contributing to a greater use of non-fossil energy sources. As the Council had already the opportunity to do in its answer given to the question raised by Mary Honeyball during Question Time last February, it recalls the ongoing work in the following areas:

Following last December's Council (Industry/Energy) agreement on a political orientation on the proposed Directive for the promotion of electricity from renewable energy sources in the internal market, the Council will, within the coming weeks, formally adopt its common position on this text, which should confirm the commitment of Member States to a indicative Community target of electricity from renewable energy sources of 22.1% by the year 2010. This Directive will represent a significant progress in favour of electricity from renewable energy sources, all the more needed given the difficulties faced by the Climate change negotiations.

The Council (Industry/Energy) has welcomed with much interest the presentation of the Commission's Green Paper: "Towards a European strategy for the security of energy supply" at its December meeting. This paper, launched to raise an overall debate, proposes a strategy for dealing with the increasing dependence of the Union with regard to energy supply over a 20-year period. Within this strategy, as proposed by the Commission the enhancement of diversification of energy supplies and sources is an important part, and includes the increased efforts in the research of (alternative) new and clean energy sources (ies).

A comprehensive European sustainable development strategy will be prepared with a view to its examination by the European Council (Göteborg) in June 2001. To that effect, the Council, on the basis of a report from the Commission, will be examining the ongoing work on the strengthening of the strategy for the integration of environmental aspects and sustainable development into the energy sector (policy), as adopted at the European Council in Helsinki (1999). This examination will also include consideration of the Commission's Action Plan to improve energy efficiency as well as the proposal for a 6th Community Action Plan on sustainable development. It is our intention to draw some conclusions at the May Energy/Industry Council, in order to bring this work forward. Moreover, we expect that a number of actions will be launched under the Action Plan on energy efficiency on the basis of the priorities identified by the Council at its meeting on 5 December last.

Furthermore the Council recalls that among existing instruments for the promotion of cleaner energy, (the ALTENER and CARNOT subprogrammes of the Energy), the framework programme on Energy, i.e. ALTENER, SAVE, ETAP and CARNOT, is of specific (are particularly) importance (t).

Last but not least, as regards the relation between clean energy and physical disruptions, the Council considers that the progress towards the achievement of a fully operational internal energy market, as stated in the Lisbon European Council conclusions, will contribute to diminishing the influence of external factors on security of supply.

 

Question no 18 by Pat the Cope Gallagher (H-0176/01)
 Subject: Childcare institutions in Romania
 

Will the Swedish Council Presidency outline what priority it attaches to securing a comprehensive reform of Romania’s childcare institutions and what emphasis it is placing on this issue in the context of the overall enlargement process?

 
  
 

The Council has already had the opportunity, in October last year, to assure the honourable Member that it attaches the highest priority to an improvement in the situation in Romania's childcare institutions. This commitment will continue under the Swedish and future Presidencies.

The Commission's 2000 regular report on Romania's progress towards accession concluded that, "after some delay, adequate budgetary provisions have been made for institutionalised children and a strategy for a comprehensive reform of child care policies has been launched". However, the report identified a number of shortcomings in this area and noted that "the key issue for the Romanian authorities will be to ensure that this strategy is successfully implemented; so as to allow an improvement in the actual living conditions".

The European Union will continue to take every opportunity to emphasise to the Romanian authorities the importance of tackling this issue. The subject will, in particular, be raised at the EU-Romania Association Council on 19 March 2001.

 

Question no 19 by Liam Hyland (H-0178/01)
 Subject: Identifying and supporting new growth areas in farming
 

Given the ongoing crisis affecting the farming sector and the knock-on effect on related service sectors in the Community, will the Council outline its strategy for guaranteeing a real future for farming, for young farmers and family farms, and will it give an undertaking that, as part of that strategy, new areas with potential for agricultural growth will be identified and supported?

 
  
 

Having regard to the evolution of the social and economic situation of the farming sector in the European Union, the Council endeavors to improve the common agricultural policy while taking into account the increasingly competitive international environment, the rapid evolution of science and technology in a society more and more concerned about environmental protection, food quality and sustainable development.

The ongoing BSE- and Foot-and-Mouth disease crisis affecting the farming sector and the knock-on effect which this has on related services sectors in the Community will inevitably lead to new approaches for the fulfilment of the objectives of the CAP being considered in particular in identifying and promoting new growth areas in farming, including the encouragement of consumer and environmentally friendly production as well as the development of a more diversified rural economy.

As already stated in the written reply given to the honourable Member question H-112/01 in February 2001 concerning measures to halt the reduction in agricultural employment, the Council will devote all its attention to any Commission proposals furthering sustainable development of European agriculture. In this way, it may better guarantee a real future for farming, for young farmers and family farms while defining a better framework for rural development. Furthermore, the Council hopes that the national authorities - which can already use for this purpose the facilities of the Regulation 1257/99 - will implement those measures while elaborating programmes and projects ensuring the cofinancing of such opportunities.

 

Question no 20 by Bart Staes (H-0181/01)
 Subject: Operating costs in the fisheries sector
 

Increased operating costs, especially fuel costs, are resulting in problems for the European fisheries sector. Accordingly, some Member States of the EU are intervening in order to reduce fuel costs for shipowners. Such divergent national and/or regional support measures result in a distortion of competition. A coordinated European approach is therefore required.

In line with the principle of subsidiarity, it is for local, regional and national authorities to decide on the pattern of public transport services they believe their area needs; to decide in what ways these services need to be integrated with each other; to decide on the division of roles and responsibilities between authorities and public transport operators; and to select the operator or operators that will fulfil their public service requirements.

The Community has neither the capacity nor the competence to take over these tasks.

However these tasks, like other actions in the field of services of general interest, need to be carried out in a way that takes into account the requirements of the rules of the Treaty, notably those relating to state aids and to the award of exclusive rights.

In general, these rules come into force only where a particular measure has a potential impact on trade between Member States.

In the past, public transport in the different Member States was organised on purely national or even local lines. Operators were active only within a single Member State; there were no international operators of local and regional public transport.

During the last decade, this situation has completely changed. The majority of Member States, including Denmark, have chosen to open at least part of their public transport sector to competition. International as well as national operators have taken advantage of this opportunity to enter new markets – again, Denmark is an example.

As a result, the organisation of public transport is no longer a matter that has purely local effects. In this emerging single market, abuses of Treaty rules on state aids or the award of exclusive rights can now have a direct effect on trade between Member States.

This is already the case as Community law stands today. However, the full implications of these Treaty rules in the public transport sector are not clear, and authorities and operators are experiencing legal uncertainty. The Commission has therefore made a proposal for a regulation on public service requirements in public transport. The aim of this proposal is to remove this legal uncertainty. Moreover, the proposal aims to ensure, through a system of controlled competition, that public authorities in the Member States have explicit powers to deliver quality and integration in public transport and to protect existing employees in the sector.

The evidence shows that competition, properly regulated, is an effective way to promote these objectives. It is certainly true that public companies often offer services of a high quality, and the Commission’s objective is not to promote privatisation. However it is appropriate for these companies, like any others, to show from time to time that they are still the best for the job of delivering services in their area. Such a demonstration is in the interest of passengers and of the Community at large.

A quarry for building materials situated at the edge of the urban area (at a distance of 300 metres) of one of the largest and most historic municipalities in Greece, that of Ioannina, has recently resumed operations. Apart from the fact that the quarry has turned the commune of Marmara (one of the oldest in the region which is mentioned in the Golden Bull of the Byzantine Emperor Andronikos II Palaeologos of 1318) into a wasteland, it is also destroying and levelling the hill on which the monastery of Aghia Paraskevi, dating from 1778, is situated. Even more serious is the fact that the quarry is situated in an area earmarked for reafforestation. Local inhabitants have to live with the daily nightmare of very powerful explosions which cause seismic shocks which could make their houses collapse, and their health is threatened by clouds of dust from the explosions which settles on the area, the woodlands and the pastures every day.

What measures does the Commission intend to take to preserve the monuments of the historical and cultural heritage of the region and protect the life and health of inhabitants and the environment in general?

 
  
 

The Commission is not in possession of any information regarding the reopening of the quarry condemned by the honourable Member. In order to identify the Community legislation applicable to the subject and to be able to conduct an investigation, a description of the type of quarry is necessary, together with the date it first went into operation and the text on the basis of which it has been authorised to resume operations.

 

Question no 112 by Miet Smet (H-0243/01)
 Subject: Aid for training measures
 

In Belgium, the social partners in certain sectors have agreed to pay a proportion of the total payroll into funds which finance training measures. The resources involved are not State funds, but the State has declared them compulsory by Royal Decree. The resources are therefore used to cofinance measures which also receive support from the ESF. Does Recital 6 of Commission Regulation (EC) 68/2001(1) mean that, henceforth, the Commission will regard such contributions as State aid? If that were to be the case, does the Commission realise that the sectoral social partners involved will be inclined to use such resources solely for their own company training schemes or for purposes other than training and not for training schemes which involve more than one company or are not directly applicable?

 
  
 

As sectoral training funds in Belgium (as well as in some other Member States) are made compulsory by way of national legislation they must legally be regarded as State resources within the meaning of Article 87 (1) EC Treaty and constitute State aid if the other conditions of this Article are met. As a consequence they are in principle notifiable to the Commission for advance approval pursuant to Article 88 (3) EC Treaty.

In respect to training aid, two important exceptions to the notification obligation should be pointed out:

Aid which complies with the 'de minimis' rule (when the amount of aid involved does not exceed the ceiling of EUR 100,000 over a three-year period) is not notifiable.

Aid which falls within the applicable levels specified in the Exemption Regulation on Training Aid (Regulation 68/2001) is no longer notifiable provided all relevant conditions are complied with. This should significantly reduce the incidence of notifications required relating to Belgian sectoral training funds: only all individual projects which involve expenditure in excess of EUR 1 million has to be notified to the Commission.

In the Commission’s view, training usually has positive external effects for society as a whole since it increases the pool of skilled workers from which other firms may draw, improves the competitiveness of Community industry and plays an important role in the employment strategy. In view of the fact that enterprises in the Community generally under-invest in the training of their workers, State aid might help to correct this market imperfection and therefore can be considered (under certain conditions) to be compatible with the common market and therefore exempted from prior notification.

The Commission is therefore of the opinion that Regulation 68/2001 is an important step forward since it considerably simplifies the procedural obligations of Member States who want to grant training aid. Regarding the use of sectoral training funds, the Regulation has clarified the existing situation and thereby provided a transparent framework for sectoral training funds.

In recently approved Objective 3 Single Programming Documents in Belgium, it is clearly stated that sectoral training funds (which fall within the applicable levels specified in the Exemption Regulation on Training Aid - Regulation 68/2001) continue to be used as cofinancing for ESF projects.

 
 

(1) OJ L 10, 13.1.2001, p. 20.

 

Question no 113 by Bárbara Dührkop Dührkop (H-0247/01)
 Subject: Communication of information on air accidents and their causes in Spain
 

Under Article 9 of Directive 94/56/EC(1), Member States must forward to the Commission reports of air accidents occurring on their territory and recommendations resulting from investigations into them.

How many such reports and recommendations has the Spanish Government submitted to the Commission since March 1998? Will the Commission give details thereof?

 
  
 

To date, the Commission has not received any inquiry reports from the Spanish government on air accidents occurring on its territory.

The Commission has officially requested the Spanish authorities to provide it with a list of accidents and incidents that should be the subject of an inquiry under Directive 94/56/EC, which have occurred since the entry into force of Royal Decree 389/1998 of 13 March 1998 transposing Directive 94/56/EC into Spanish law.

The Commission has also requested that it be sent copies of the inquiry reports and recommendations which have been published since that date.

 
 

(1) OJ L 319, 12.12.1994, p.14.
