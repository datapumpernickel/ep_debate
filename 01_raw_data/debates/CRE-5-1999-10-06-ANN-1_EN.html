Questions to the Council
Question no 18 by Richard Howitt (H-0515/99)
 Subject: NGOs and the European employment strategy
 

With reference to the recently published draft Employment Guidelines, does the Council accept that NGOs should be recognised as partners in the European employment strategy? What specific proposals will the President table at the EU Summit in Helsinki on 8-9 December?

 
  
 

Article 128 of the EC Treaty states that the Council will listen to, inter alia, the Economic and Social Committee and the Committee of the Regions before taking decisions on employment strategy in Europe, which includes, for example, approving guidelines on employment. In this way NGOs also have the opportunity to prepare guidelines on employment. The Council believes it is important, however, that all players at all levels should contribute to the success of the strategy in their own countries. It is clear that NGOs involved in issues of employment, including those that focus on, for example, promoting employment opportunities for the disabled or issues of equality, can have an impact at both regional and local level. All must play their part in the implementation of guidelines on employment.

I would like, furthermore, to draw the honourable Member’s attention to the fact that, although employers and unions are not NGOs in the conventional sense, they must be regarded from the start as being able to exert an important influence on employment strategy in Europe.

The Standing Committee on Employment, which was originally set up in 1970, was reorganised to make its work more effective, by increasing the influence employers and unions have in particular(1). Its next meeting is planned for November 11, i.e., the day before the Labour/Social Affairs Council meets on 12 November, at which the Ministers will examine the Commission’s employment package for 1999 – 2000 in detail.

In addition, the Committee on Employment and Social Affairs will also listen to employers and unions before it drafts its own opinion on a proposal for guidelines on employment in the year 2000. The Committee on Employment, to be set up on the basis of Article 130 of the EC Treaty, will replace the present Committee on Employment and Social Affairs. A new committee will also listen to employers and unions on matters of employment, on the basis of Article 130.

As for preparations for the European Council in Helsinki, the European Council will, in general, reconfirm in its conclusions its support for the political agreement on guidelines that it should achieve in its joint sitting (labour and social affairs / Ecofin) on 19 November 1999.

A jointly drafted report on employment will be presented on 10 – 11 December 1999 to a meeting of the European Council.

 
 

(1) Council Decision 1999/207/EC of 9 March 1999 on the reform of the Standing Committee on Employment and the abrogation of Decision No 70/532/EEC

Question no 20 by Niall Andrews (H-0527/99)
 Subject: East Timor
 

Will the Council state what action it proposes to take to comply with the terms of the resolution adopted by Parliament on East Timor at its plenary session of 13-17 September 1999?

 
  
 

The Council shares the concern shown in the resolution of the European Council on the situation in East Timor, and especially the atrocities that have occurred in the region since the results of the referendum were made known. Ministers have debated the issue repeatedly in recent weeks, and the Union has been very glad to see the formation of INTERFET, and has thanked the countries that have become members of it.

The Council declared in its adopted resolution of 13 September that it wishes to see the East Timorese nation achieve freely chosen independence. The Member States expect to be able to recognise East Timor when the independence process has been decided on. As the honourable Member knows, the Council agreed, in the same context, to an export ban on weapons, ammunition, and military supplies to Indonesia, and a ban on delivering such supplies as might be used for purposes of internal oppression or terrorism. Both bans are to last four years. In addition, it agreed to cease bilateral military cooperation with Indonesia, also for a period of four years. The Council also supported the call for a meeting of the UN Commission on Human Rights and, after that, the Union in a meeting moved for a resolution that would propose sending an international team to investigate alleged violations of international humanitarian law. The Commission has informed the Council of the existence of two batches of humanitarian aid, the first being two million euros, and the second, three million euros.

The resolution adopted by the European Parliament in September raised two essential questions: the acceptance of a common position on economic aid and the reconstruction of civil society and the reservation of a separate East Timor appropriation in the budget for the year 2000. The Council is prepared to investigate all the instruments at its disposal to promote the objectives of the Union in East Timor, and the possibility of joint action is not at all excluded.

 

Question no 21 by Liam Hyland (H-0530/99)
 Subject: The family farm
 

Will the President-in-Office outline the current thinking in relation to the importance of the family farm and what new initiatives, if any, the Council would support to maintain and promote the family farm?

 
  
 

The Council has long been attentive to the issues of family farms. It is well aware of the need to preserve and promote family farming. An indication of this is the current application of the legislation in force on improving the effectiveness of agricultural structures.

It has primarily come to my attention that family farms are one of the basic aspects of the multiform European agricultural model. This model plays a central role in the reforms of the common agricultural policy. The Council has firmly decided to continue developing this model and thus safeguard the future of family farms, as it emphasised recently in its conclusions on the millennium round of negotiations. This requires action to be started to strengthen the special qualities of the model, both in the European Union and outside it.

I would also like to remind the honourable Member that the policy on the development of rural areas, which the Council recently decided on in connection with the Agenda 2000 talks, will, in the future, form the Second Pillar of the CAP. This policy, as laid down in Council Regulation No 1257/99, will strengthen the legislation that exists for this sector and the measures included in it. These measures are there to endeavour to ensure the viability of family farms, encourage young farmers to embark on the profession, promote their training, facilitate recommencement of farming, and generally strengthen the social/professional networks in the rural areas.

The Council considers that this new policy of supporting rural areas offers farming families a chance to develop, taking account of the problems of preserving and developing family farms, and, at the same time, is an attempt to rid rural residents of the most common problems that they face.

This being the case, the Council believes that the decisions taken on rural development were an appropriate response to the problems of family farm viability and dynamics.

 

Question no 22 by Mihail Papayannakis (H-0532/99)
 Subject: Ban on Greek schools operating in Albania
 

The Albanian Ministry of Education has decided to ban Greek schools from operating in Himarë and Përmet despite declarations by the Albanian Government concerning the rights of the Greek minority. This action constitutes a violation of the constitutional right of the Greek community to be educated in their mother tongue, a right that has been endorsed by the International Court of The Hague.

Given that in 1993 the Albanian Government adopted a constitutional law on human rights, Article 26 of which refers to the rights of minorities, given that the Council has stated that the EU promotes moves to find a solution to the problems facing ethnic groups on the basis of the principles of the state of law and the international obligations of the parties concerned and given finally that acts of this kind expose Albania to criticism from the international community and do nothing to further friendly relations and cooperation between Albania and Greece and Albania and the EU, will the Council say whether it intends to intervene to persuade the Albanian authorities to respect all the principles, democratic laws and human rights which constitute the basis for institutional relations between Albania and the EU and enable all members of the Greek minority to enjoy the fundamental right to be taught in and learn their mother tongue?

 
  
 

Respect for democratic principles, human rights and the rights of minorities are important conditions to be fulfilled by the countries of the Western Balkans region when it comes to the development of bilateral relations with the European Union. As a country eligible for a Stabilisation and Association Agreement, Albania’s performance in these areas is of particular importance to the EU.

The Council reviews compliance by the countries of the region with such conditions on a regular basis. The next review is currently scheduled for November 1999.

According to the Council’s information, no Greek school has been closed in the villages of Himarë and Përmet. The only schools operating in these two villages are public schools in which Albanian is the compulsory language. However, 30 Greek families have called upon the Albanian Ministry of Education to set up a separate class in Greek in the public school of these villages. The Presidency is aware of the fact that this matter has also been taken up by the Greek organisation defending the interests of the Greek minority in Albania, Omonia.

Our information is, on the other side that the Albanian Government is trying to do its best to solve the problem. All of us should have, also in this case, some consideration for the very difficult situation it faces at all levels, in particular the economic level, with the lowest rate of GDP per inhabitant, or the internal security level, with major problems notably in the South of the country where the problems raised by the Honourable Parliamentarian are of peculiar acuteness.

Our information is that the Albanian Government is currently looking for a practical and legal solution to the problem of setting up Greek classes in a public school.

Question no 24 by Olle Schmidt (H-0536/99)
 Subject: Principle of public access to official documents in Sweden
 

In connection with negotiations for Swedish membership of the European Union, the Swedish Government proclaimed that Swedish policy was that the principle of public access to official documents and freedom to provide information would continue to apply if Sweden joined. Accordingly, a Swedish declaration to that effect was added to the act of accession.

The Swedish Agency for Administrative Development has now conducted a survey among Swedish government agencies. The survey has not yet been fully analysed but preliminary reports indicate that government agencies in other Member States have tried to persuade Swedish agencies not to make public documents which would normally be in the public domain under the principle of freedom of access. In its reply to the survey, the National Food Administration, for example, states that other countries' embassies have contacted the agency and complained about the fact that Sweden has freedom of access to official documents and the nuisance it causes. What does the Council intend to do in response to this situation?

 
  
 

The Council has repeatedly made it known publicly that it considers the principle of transparency to be important, and that it will strive to ensure it plays a role in its day-to-day work.

However, the Council is not familiar with the circumstances of the case the honourable Member refers to. Under Article 255 of the EC Treaty, the Council must, in the course of the two years from the entry into force of the Treaty of Amsterdam, reconfirm the common principles and restrictions that relate to the right recorded in the Treaty to have access to European Parliament, Council and Commission documents. Although the forthcoming act on transparency does not directly concern the facts of the case the honourable member mentions in his question, it will nonetheless serve to clarify the position regarding public accessibility to documents in the Union. The Council cannot, however, act before the Commission has produced a proposal for an act on transparency. Let us hope this happens as soon as possible.

 

Question no 25 by Nigel Paul Farage (H-0539/99)
 Subject: Potential UK share in ECB equity
 

Will the Council please clarify what the United Kingdom's potential share of the ECB's equity would be, both in percentage terms and the exact figure involved?

 
  
 

According to Article 28 of the ECB Statute, the overall capital of the ECB amounts to EUR 5 billion. Applying the formula for Member States’ subscriptions set out in Article 29.1 of the ESCB Statute, the share of the Bank of England in the ECB’s capital is 14.6811 per cent.

Article 48 of the ESCB Statute provides that non-participating Member States should pay up only a minimal percentage of their capital subscriptions as a contribution to the operational costs of the ECB, if the ECB General Council so decides. In accordance with this Article, non-euro area central banks were each required to pay up 5% of their subscribed capital share.

In the UK’s case, this amounted to EURO (36,702,750), offset against proceeds received from the liquidation of the EMI.

 

Question no 26 by Ioannis Theonas (H-0540/99)
 Subject: Risk of loss of human life from unexploded bombs scattered throughout Yugoslavia
 

A WHO investigation has found that 150 people have been killed or maimed by unexploded bombs scattered throughout Yugoslavia and, in particular, Kosovo, following the 78-day NATO bombing campaign which used 1,100 cluster bombs containing 220,000 bomblets. Explosive devices are also to be found scattered around neighbouring regions, as well as in the sea, which have also caused death and mutilation.

What measures will the Council take to deal immediately with the problem of the bombs and mines left behind by the murderous bombing campaign so that we are not left to mourn other deaths and injuries, particularly in Yugoslavia and the Adriatic?

 
  
 

The honourable Member will certainly know that the matter has already been discussed in the Council. The Council took a decision on 9 November 1998, based on Article J.4(2) of the Treaty on European Union, on practical arrangements between the European Union and the Western European Union for assistance in mine clearance in the Croatia region. The Council receives a report every month on how the aid action is progressing.

The European Union is involved in solving this serious problem. It has already granted five million euros to humanitarian mine clearance operations in Kosovo, and is also considering additional funding. It is worth remembering, however, that the problem does not concern the European Union exclusively. Other agencies also, such as NATO and the United Nations, are committed to solving the problems of unexploded bombs and other explosives in the Kosovo and Adriatic regions.

The European Union, in its own work, is focusing for the present on plans to reconstruct Kosovo and restore conditions there to their former state. The Council still believes, however, that it is a precondition of any action the European Union takes in the Yugoslavian region that substantial improvements have been made in the political situation there.

 

Question no 27 by Efstratios Korakas (H-0544/99)
 Subject: Failure to alert citizens to the dangers of cultivating genetically modified organisms
 

While EU consumers are becoming increasingly cautious about, and opposed to, genetically modified products, the European Union has so far failed to take any measures to ban them: in adopting this approach it is putting the interests of the multinationals before those of the consumer.

Will the Council say what measures it intends to take to satisfy the demands of European consumers that public health be protected, that consumers be fully informed with a clear indication of the use of genetically modified organisms in products, that imports of banned products and the companies importing them or using the products be subject to prosecution and finally, that the introduction of modified products in the food chain be prohibited?

 
  
 

The Council is fully aware of the importance of the honourable Member’s question and debated the issue in its sitting of 24 – 25 June 1999, at which it achieved consensus on a common position concerning an amendment to European Parliament and Council Directive, by which Directive 90/220/EEC on the deliberate release into the environment of genetically modified organisms would be amended.

The following matters have to be taken into consideration. Firstly, before anything is done to instigate procedures for introducing permits into the market, there has to be an assessment, made on the basis of common principles, of the harm caused to the environment and people’s health. The criteria to be followed have to be strengthened and broadened with reference to the directive in force. Secondly, clear and unambiguous labelling will be compulsory at all stages of the produce coming onto the market. Thirdly, further information on genetic modifications is available, furthermore, from the Community register, which is maintained within the framework of the exchange of information between the Commission and the Member States. Fourthly, demands on labelling are to be supplemented with legal effect measures on the traceability of GMOs, so that the competent authorities can monitor the appearance of GMOs at each stage while they are on the market. As imports are also included in the process of introducing goods onto the market, the legal effect measures must also cover imports of GMOs. It is primarily the task of the Member States to impose relevant penalties if these regulations are not obeyed.

With these legal effect measures, particularly those concerning labelling, we can supplement the legal effect measures under Regulation (EC) No 258/97 concerning novel foods and novel food ingredients, which is already the situation, for example, with relation to certain strains of maize and soya.

Question no 36 by Catherine Taylor (H-0524/99)
 Subject: Late payments on youth programmes
 

What strategies will the Commission be putting in place to tackle the problem of late payments on youth programmes and, more particularly, the Socrates programme?

 
  
 

The Commission is perfectly aware of the problems that delays in payment can pose for project promoters, and especially organisations for young people.

The Commission realises that it is very important for promoters to obtain at least part of the subsidy intended for them as soon as possible and has set up a procedure for payment in two stages. The first payment, forming an advance, usually represents 80% of the subsidy amount, and this is paid in the days following signature of the contract, and always within the six-week time limit to which the Commission is committed for payments to third parties.

The second payment thus forms the balance due, which does not usually exceed 20% of the total subsidy. The aim of rapid payment of the balance is more difficult to fulfil, because settlement cannot be completed until the Commission has gone through the financial accounts accompanying the promoter's activity report. In this respect there is a need to ensure that speed of payment does not contravene the Commission's legitimate control requirements. The need for proper financial management, the number of projects and the lack of resources may thus cause delays in paying the balance due.

To sum up, the Commission has to satisfy two demands at once: taking account of citizens' interests, and ensuring that payment procedures are properly carried out.

However, we do need to simplify the selection and contracting procedures, to enable promoters to draw as soon as possible on the funds that they have been granted.

The Commission is aware that some procedures are unduly cumbersome, and its proposals for the second generation of the Socrates II, Leonardo da Vinci II and Youth Programmes did recommend a substantial simplification of the procedures concerned which, unfortunately, the Member States did not accept. Parliament has supported the Commission proposal, but so far without success. Indeed, this problem is a point of disagreement between Parliament and the Commission on the one side, and the Council and the Conciliation Committee for the Socrates Programme on the other.

The Commission hopes that when the programmes are finally adopted this mutual concern will be taken into account.

 

Question no 37 by Roy Perry (H-0525/99)
 Subject: Sport in Europe
 

The Commission will be submitting a report on sport to the Helsinki European Council in December 1999. Will the Commission ensure that the report will address the ramifications of increasingly dominant commercial control over sport, in the light, in particular, of such developments as the proposal for a European Super League and the take-over of football clubs by media operators? Will the Commission also ensure that the Parliament is formally consulted on the report before it is presented at the Helsinki Council?

 
  
 

The Commission is aware of the problems that the Honourable Member has raised. The penetration of sport by major business interests does certainly pose a threat to the very nature of European sport.

For this reason, when the Commission is writing its report to the European Council it will take due account of the conclusions of the first European Union conference on sport (the 'Sports Assizes'), which point out that:

Χ sports competitions cannot be regarded merely as a way of attracting an audience or selling advertising,

Χ the integrity and autonomy of sport must be preserved,

Χ there must be no confusion between the role of broadcasters and that of the sports movement,

Χ the media must not either directly or indirectly influence the organisation or scheduling of sports competitions,

Χ the acquisition of sports clubs by broadcasters, which arouses serious doubts - if not outright opposition - should be avoided.

The Commission hopes that this approach will reassure the Honourable Member.

In preparing the report we have borne the Amsterdam declaration in mind, because the Commission has carried out an extensive process of consultation, culminating in the sports conference.

The report will be the subject of a Commission Communication to the Parliament and Council, and will be passed on to the Helsinki European Council. The Commission will be presenting it to Parliament beforehand.

 

Question no 38 by Christopher Heaton-Harris (H-0538/99)
 Subject: European Audiovisual Observatory
 

Can the Commissioner provide evidence that the European Audiovisual Observatory represents a good use of Community funds?

How often does the Commission itself use the Observatory and for what particular tasks?

Finally, how does the Commission envisage the future management and function of the European Audiovisual Observatory?

 
  
 

The Observatory is a public institution with the tasks to collect economic and legal information from various international sources and to assemble them in an organised and comprehensive way. I believe that these tasks are effectively fulfilled and that the return on the Commission’s participation in terms of support for the audio-visual industry of the Community, notably the SMEs, is positive.

Consultation of the Observatory publications, notably the ‘Yearbook’ and the legal newsletter ‘Iris’ is common practice in the Commission. In case the Community participation in the Observatory be discontinued, the Commission would be obliged to collect on a commercial basis the same information from several different sources. This procedure would prove time consuming and very expensive.

Observatory’s advice has also been sought on several occasions, in the context of on-going contracts; in particular, in the fields of employment, film-financing schemes, international trade statistics, statistical methodologies, market trends in the Community and other areas.

For the future, the activity carried out with the Observatory will increase, notably in relation to two specific areas: the implementation of a statistical information structure under EUROSTAT, and the preparation of GATS 2000 negotiations.

The active participation of the Commission in the Observatory will allow it to optimise the complementarity of the tasks carried out by EUROSTAT and the Observatory itself. Concerning the forthcoming GATS 2000 negotiations, the Commission wishes to stress that it is planning to make full use of the small but professional and flexible structure of the Observatory in order to collect economic and juridical information on the trade partners as well as specific aspects of the Community industry.

 

Question no 39 by Konstantinos Alyssandrakis (H-0542/99)
 Subject: The Acropolis Museum
 

Following the emergence of a broad popular movement to save the cultural heritage of Greece and Europe, and in particular the Acropolis in Athens, one of the most important historical monuments of mankind, a procedure was launched to build an Acropolis museum to house the masterpieces of art and world culture which are found on the Acropolis and in the museums of Europe. The results of the international competition held many years ago which led to the beginning of construction work on the museum are now being questioned by many experts as being outdated; the site chosen is also deemed unsuitable owing to a shortage of space and difficult access for visitors. The government’s decision not to have a complete fire safety system installed - owing to the presence of museum security guards - raises further questions about how this museum, which will house works of art of inestimable value and receive millions of visitors, will function.

Will the Commission say what initiatives and measures it intends to take to ensure that the museum that is eventually built is worthy of the exhibits it houses and provides full protection for these exhibits and complete safety for visitors?

 
  
 

The Commission recognises the great interest and benefit that the construction of the new Acropolis museum has in European cultural heritage terms. However, the Commission cannot intervene in this matter as, according to Article 151 (ex. Article 128) of the EC Treaty, the protection of cultural heritage is the exclusive responsibility of the national competent authorities.

The Commission is not aware of all the facts/problems surrounding the construction of the new Acropolis Museum. In the context of the regular contacts with the Hellenic competent authorities, the Commission will raise the matter and request further information on the subject in question.

Within the remit of its competences, the Commission would certainly be willing to assist the national competent authorities in this matter should the latter make a request to that effect.

 

Question no 45 by Riitta Myller (H-0520/99)
 Subject: Tacis Regulation currently being drafted; coordinating the TACIS-CBC
 

Developing good relations with Russia is one of the priorities of the European Union’s foreign and security policy. Our aim is to strengthen cooperation between the Union and Russia, which will also promote the attainment of the objectives of the Nordic-dimension policy.
