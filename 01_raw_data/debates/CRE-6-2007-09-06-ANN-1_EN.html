Question no 27 by Rodi Kratsa-Tsagaropoulou (H-0580/07)
 Subject: Development of sustainable and responsible tourism in popular tourist destinations
 

The Portuguese Presidency has set its first priority as 'solidarity between equals' and is highlighting the importance of developing the culture and tourism industry. Popular tourist regions in the EU are being blighted by phenomena, such as violence, alcoholism, and incidents, such as those involving British tourists at Laganas on the island of Zakynthos, which blacken the image of European tourists, undermine living conditions of the local population and bring the tourist destinations in question into disrepute.

Given the Presidency's orientation towards a sustainable, high-quality tourism based on solidarity, will the Presidency say whether it intends to promote cooperation between Member States in order to address such situations and exchange best practices? Does it believe that new measures must be taken aimed at boosting European tourism, while at the same time safeguarding the quality of life of the local population and the good name of tourist destinations? Does it intend to examine whether it is worth promoting codes of conduct which are binding for tourist undertakings active in Europe?

 
  
 

This answer, which has been drawn up by the Presidency and is not binding on the Council or the Member States, was not delivered orally during Question Time to the Council at the 2007 September I part-session of the European Parliament in Strasbourg.

The Presidency confirms that, in its statement entitled ‘A stronger Union for a better world’, the top priority is ‘solidarity between equals’, which in this context means values such as respect for human dignity and citizens’ rights, freedom, democracy, equality and the rule of law.

With regard to tourism, it should be remembered that the EU’s responsibilities in this area, as laid down in the Treaty, are minimal. The current legislation is basically aimed at protecting the consumer (for example, passengers’ rights).

Further to the Council’s Conclusions on tourism of 18 April 2005 and according to the timetable for the 18 months from 1 January 2007 – prepared in conjunction with the previous German Presidency and the subsequent Slovenian Presidency – the Portuguese Presidency will very closely monitor developments in the Commission’s initiatives on the European tourism agenda, all the more so as it regards tourism as a sector with great potential for growth and the sustained creation of employment.

Moreover, in order to highlight the need to integrate tourism within our common agenda, we will host the 6th European Tourism Forum on 26 and 27 October 2007.

The Presidency hopes that the aspects mentioned in the honourable Member’s question may also be tackled during future discussions and is counting on appropriate measures being taken by the competent bodies to prevent the harmful effects on tourism described in the honourable Member’s question.

Question no 38 by Eoin Ryan (H-0552/07)
 Subject: New measures to regulate soccer players' agents in Europe
 

Can the European Commission outline a specific timeframe for the introduction of a new EU legislative measure which will have the effect of regulating how soccer players’ agents can do their work in Europe?

 
  
 

Repeated calls have been made on the EU to regulate the activity of players' agents. This is often linked to reports of bad practices in the activities of some agents which have allegedly resulted in instances of corruption, money laundering and exploitation of under age players. However, it is a complex issue, since agents are subject to differing regulations in different Member States. Some Member States have introduced specific legislation on players' agents while in others the applicable law is the general law regarding employment agencies, but with references to players' agents. Moreover, some international federations have introduced their own regulations.

The Commission adopted on 11 July 2007 a White Paper on Sport(1). The Action Plan attached to the White Paper specifically mentions that it is the Commission's intention to "carry out an impact assessment to provide a clear overview of the activities of players’ agents in the EU and an evaluation of whether action at EU level is necessary, which will also analyse the different possible options."

For the realisation of the abovementioned impact assessment, which conditions any potential EU measure in this field, the Commission considers that an external study is necessary. Concerning the specific time frame for launching such a study, the Commission will inform the Honourable Member in the autumn 2007.

 
 

(1) COM(2007) 391 final.

Question no 40 by Stavros Arnaoutakis (H-0579/07)
 Subject: 'Youth in Action programme 2007-2013'
 

One of the objectives of the Commission's 'Youth in Action programme 2007-2013' is to provide operating grants to non-governmental youth organisations active at European level which seek to increase the participation of young people in public life and society and develop cooperation activities (Action 4.1). However, there are many youth organisations competing to receive these grants which amount to a maximum of only EUR 35 000 annually. Many youth NGOs which are eligible do not receive any significant support.

Does the Commission intend to increase the available resources for these operating grants? How will it ensure that the organisations which receive the grants meet the necessary criteria? How does it intend to support youth NGOs in the regions which really need them?

 
  
 

The ceiling of the grant awarded to youth organisations is currently fixed at € 35 000 due to the constant increase in the number of applications submitted in recent years and with a view to providing support to an optimal number of beneficiaries.

The Commission is currently exploring the possibility of increasing the ceiling for grants in the framework of the Call for proposals for the year 2008.

This measure would need to be accompanied by an increase in the resources assigned to the action of the Programme supporting youth NGOs, while taking into account the need to ensure a balance in the funding available among the different actions of the Programme.

Youth organisations established in any area or region of a country participating in the Programme can be financed as long as they comply with the aforementioned criteria.

 

Question no 48 by Athanasios Pafilis (H-0587/07)
 Subject: Standardisation of retirement age for men and women
 

A few days ago, the Commission decided to initiate proceedings against Greece before the European Court of Justice in an attempt to standardise the retirement age for men and women working in the public sector which is currently 65 and 60 years respectively.

Does the Commission not agree that the retirement arrangements for public sector workers form part of social security? Does it not agree that the difference in the retirement age of men and women constitutes positive discrimination in favour of women, justified by their disadvantaged and unequal position in economic and social life, particularly in employment? Does the Commission believe that the harmonisation of the public sector retirement age for men and women should be achieved by increasing the retirement age of women to 65 years or rather by reducing the retirement age for men to 60?

 
  
 

The Honourable Member puts several questions regarding proceedings against Greece before the European Court of Justice. The infringement procedure in question concerns the Greek Code for Military and Civil Pensions, especially the rules defining retirement age which are more favourable for women than for men. The rules vary depending on the circumstances but are in any event more favourable for women. For instance, widows, divorced or single women with unmarried children are allowed to go on pension after 15 or 17 years of service. The same rule does not apply to men.

As regards the question as to whether the Greek Military and Civil Pension scheme forms part of statutory social security, it should be noted that in a series of judgments, the European Court of Justice confirmed that, as a general rule, a pension paid by an employer to a former employee by reason of the employment relationship between them constitutes pay within the meaning of Article 141 of the EC Treaty. It does therefore not form part of statutory social security pensions. More in particular, the Court has also noted that pensions paid by the State to former civil servants who have worked in the public sector constitute pay within the meaning of Article 141 of the EC Treaty (C-7/93 Beune; C-366/99 Griesmar; C-351/00 Niemi; C-4/02, Schönheit). It has held in Case Griesmar that civil servants must be regarded as constituting a "particular category of workers". As a consequence, the Court has considered that the French pension scheme for civil servants therefore does not form part of statutory social security schemes but have to be considered as “pay” within the meaning of Article 141 of the EC Treaty. This approach has been confirmed also in regard to the German and the Finnish civil servants' pension schemes.

Pension schemes falling within the scope of Article 141 of the EC Treaty must respect the principle of equal treatment. This means that different conditions governing access to 'pay' (including pension) for men and women performing the same or comparable work infringe the principle of equal pay. Therefore, different retirement ages are to be considered as discrimination on grounds of sex. Provisions on different retirement ages are thus contrary to Article 141 of the EC Treaty.

The Commission takes the view that the Greek Code for Military and Civil Pensions provides for pensions which have to be considered as pay in the sense of Article 141 of the EC Treaty. They are paid to a “particular category of workers” by reason of the employment relationship.

Therefore these pensions do not form part of social security schemes.

As regards the second question on whether the differences in retirement age could be considered as “positive action” in favour of women, the Commission considers that different retirement ages cannot be considered as positive action. “Positive Action” within the meaning of Article 141 (4) of the EC Treaty are measures "providing for specific advantages in order to make it easier for the under-represented sex to pursue a vocational activity or to prevent or compensate for disadvantages in professional careers."

With regard to this particular aspect, in Case C-366/99 Griesmar (at point 65) the Court held that "(…) the measure … does not appear to be of a nature such as to offset the disadvantages to which the careers of female civil servants are exposed by helping those women in their professional life. On the contrary, that measure is limited to granting female civil servants who are mothers a service credit at the date of their retirement, without providing a remedy for the problems which they may encounter in the course of their professional career."

The same argument applies here: granting women in special circumstances an earlier retirement does not help them to pursue their professional careers but only helps them to receive more favourable conditions for an early retirement. Therefore, it cannot be considered as a measure in the context of “positive action”.

Finally, in response to the last question on how should the harmonisation of retirement age be achieved, in the Smith-judgment (C-408/92, point 22) the Court held that Community law precludes national equalizing measures which raise "the retirement age for women to that for men in relation to periods of service completed between 17 May 1990, the date of that judgment (i.e. case Barber, C-262/88) and the date on which those measures come into force. On the other hand, as regards periods of service completed after the latter date, Article 119 (now Article 141 of the EC Treaty) does not prevent an employer from taking that step. As regards periods of service prior to 17 May 1990, Community law imposed no obligation which would justify retroactive reduction of the advantages which women enjoyed."

Therefore, Community law does not allow for retrospectively raising the retirement age for women in relation to periods of service completed between 17 May 1990 and the date of entry into force of the measures by which equality is achieved in the scheme in question. However, as regards periods in the future, the Member State is free to decide how to achieve equalisation of retirement age or of other eligibility conditions to take up pensions, the objective being to achieve equal gender treatment in all respects.

 

Question no 49 by Glenis Willmott (H-0588/07)
 Subject: Fire sprinklers and their implications for safety in the workplace
 

Can the Commission say whether it has considered the various effects that the installation of fire sprinklers would have for the safety or workers throughout Europe if they were to be installed in all places of work? If not, will the Commission consider conducting research into this?

 
  
 

(FR) The relevant Community provisions on fire fighting are set out in Framework Directive 89/391/EEC(1), and more specifically in Directive 89/654/EEC(2) concerning the minimum safety and health requirements for the workplace.

These Directives do not lay down any requirement for fire sprinklers to be installed in all workplaces. Nevertheless, given that they are based on a preventive approach the Directives seek to protect the health and safety of workers from fire by requiring workplaces to be equipped with ‘appropriate fire-fighting equipment’ in accordance with the specific characteristics of each workplace. In addition, employers must ‘be in possession of an assessment of the risks’ covered by the Framework Directive and ‘take the necessary measures’ for fire fighting.

The Commission emphasises that fire sprinklers are not the most appropriate means of fighting fires in every case. In fact, their use is not recommended for fighting certain types of fire, for example electrical fires, fires involving gas or flammable liquids and certain fires of chemical origin. As a consequence, the Commission does not intend to conduct research into the installation of fire sprinklers in all places of work.

Moreover, rules on the installation of fire sprinklers are also laid down in the provisions of Directive 95/16/EC(3) concerning health and safety problems when lifts are installed. In this respect, Article 2(3) of the Directive stipulates that ‘Member States shall take all necessary measures to ensure that shafts intended for lifts do not contain any piping or wiring or fittings other than that necessary for the operation and safety of the lift’.

In addition, the automatic triggering of a sprinkler system that is not correctly installed could compromise the safe use of lifts equipped with such a system and pose an additional risk to people trapped in a lift as a result of a fire if wiring or other electrical components are installed in the lift shaft.

 
 

(1) Council Directive 89/391/EEC of 12 June 1989 on the introduction of measures to encourage improvements in the safety and health of workers at work, OJ L 183 of 29.6.1989.
(2) Council Directive 89/654/EEC of 30 November 1989 concerning the minimum safety and health requirements for the workplace (first individual directive within the meaning of Article 16(1) of Directive 89/391/EEC), OJ L 393 of 30.12.1989.
(3) European Parliament and Council Directive 95/16/CE of 29 June 1995 on the approximation of the laws of the Member States relating to lifts, OJ L 213 of 7.9.1995.

 

Question no 50 by Bart Staes (H-0519/07)
 Subject: Protection of workers' rights versus migration control
 

The proposal for a directive to penalise employers who employ illegally resident workers (COM(2007)0249 final) links the enforcement of labour rights to migration control. As a result, workers whose rights have been violated will not take any steps against their employers for fear of deportation. Yet under international law such workers also enjoy fundamental labour rights. It is open to question whether policy on irregular employment will benefit from the fragmentation of enforcement policy on the basis of employees' legal status. Moreover, it remains to be seen how effective the directive will be in view of the large scale on which false documents are already being used on the clandestine employment market.

Can the Commission clarify its position on this and indicate what importance it attaches to protecting employees, bearing in mind that the main purpose of this directive is to control migration?

 
  
 

The purpose of the Commission’s proposal for a Directive providing for sanctions against employers of illegally staying third country nationals(1) is indeed to fight against illegal immigration, by reducing the employment pull factor.

Illegally staying third country nationals can be subject to exploitation, sometimes serious, at the workplace. The proposal does not affect third country nationals’ fundamental rights as workers. The Commission has stated that Member States should also apply effective sanctions for breaches of legislation in particular on health and safety at the workplace which often occur in the context of this type of illegal employment(2). At the same time, the general principle is that an illegally staying third country national should be issued with a return decision.

As regards the wider problem of irregular employment mentioned by the Honourable Member, the Commission will in autumn 2007 present a Communication on undeclared work.

Concerning forged documents, the Commission considers that common guidelines should be developed on minimum security standards, in particular with respect to issue procedures, for documents including residence permits(3). Regarding the latter, work is currently being carried out to enhance the security features of the uniform format for residence permits for third-country nationals, laid down in Council Regulation (EC) no 1030/2002(4), by adding biometric identifiers. It is clearly unreasonable to expect employers to detect forged documents, which is a reason why the Commission proposes that employers who are a business or a legal person should notify a competent national authority when they recruit a third-country national.

 
 

(1) COM(2007)0249 final
(2) COM(2006)0402 final, at paragraph 38
(3) COM(2006)0402 final, at paragraph 31
(4) OJ L 157, 15.6.2002

Question no 52 by Bernd Posselt (H-0531/07)

Question no 53 by Zbigniew Krzysztof Kuźmiuk (H-0535/07)
 Subject: Mobilisation of financial assistance for apple growers
 

On 12 June 2007, the Council adopted a regulation on the reform of the fruit and vegetable sector and decided to grant additional payments of EUR 230 a hectare to strawberry and raspberry producers in the new Member States. Unfortunately, these arrangements do not apply to producers of other soft fruits (such as cherries and currants) or, most importantly, to apple growers, which is of particular significance to Poland, as it accounts for almost one-third of the Union's apple production.

The situation of fruit farmers in Poland has been made more difficult still by the frost in May, which resulted in 70 to 80% losses to this year's harvest.

Does the Commission intend to support apple growers in Poland and, if so, in what way?

 
  
 

The decision to grant transitional flat rate support to surfaces under cultivation of strawberries and raspberries intended for processing reflects the fact that after recent enlargements the EU became a major producer of soft fruits on a global scale. These products were chosen because in recent years they were the most exposed to competition from third countries.

The Commission hopes that the Member States covered by the scheme decide, at least for the national part of the support, to condition access to it upon membership in producer organisations or producer groups in order to provide the incentive to organise. Fragmentation of production and marketing in the soft fruit sector was identified by the Commission as one of the structural deficiencies in its report from mid 2006. Now the producing countries have an instrument to redress the situation within a five year period.

As far as apples are concerned, the EU was already a major apple producer before recent enlargements. This is why no new support scheme is foreseen, even on a transitional basis.

The Commission would like to recall that a major objective of the fruit and vegetable reform was a more market oriented sector. This is why it was decided to remove the existing aids to products intended for processing and to not create new ones.

 

Question no 54 by Robert Evans (H-0537/07)
 Subject: Europe's endangered species
 

A recent survey by WWF concluded that there are fewer than 40 brown bears left in the Alps. What plans does the Commission have to support seriously endangered species such as the brown bear, or equally the Iberian lynx, the North Atlantic whale, the Slovakian tatra chamois and Mediterranean monk seal?
