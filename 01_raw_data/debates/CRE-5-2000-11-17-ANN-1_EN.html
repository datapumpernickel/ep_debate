Question no 19 by Pedro Aparicio Sánchez (H-0795/00)
 Subject: Educational resources in the countryside
 

Rural areas account for 80% of the EU's territory and 25% of its population. Rural depopulation is continuing, and, despite the Union's efforts to support rural development, the countryside remains at a severe disadvantage in terms of social, labour and human indicators by comparison with urban areas.

The closure of rural schools is in many cases not a consequence but a cause of depopulation. However, the successive rural development programmes have not used existing rural educational resources as dynamising agents: the Community programmes aimed at redressing imbalances in less-developed and more isolated areas have not interacted sufficiently with those aimed at improving the education and training levels of their inhabitants. By contrast, the use of the human and material resources of rural schools would require the mobilisation of key strategic factors for the realisation of EDRF and ESF objectives and, in general, those of all the various policies for regional cohesion in the Community.

Does the Commission consider, in this context, that the Socrates Programme should be linked to the existing rural development programmes?

 
  
 

Council Regulation (EC) No of 17 May 1999 on support for rural development from the European Agricultural Guidance and Guarantee Fund (EAGGF), among the rural development measures eligible for cofinancing under the programmes presented by the Member States, provides for support for vocational training intended to contribute towards improving the vocational skills and competences of farmers or other persons involved in farming or forestry activities, and their retraining. This applies, for instance, to the implementation of production methods compatible with the upkeep and improvement of the landscape and environmental protection, or to gaining the level of professional qualification required to manage an economically viable farm.

The following are, however, explicitly excluded from support under Commission Regulation (EC) 1750/1999 of 23 July 1999: any educational or training courses which form part of the standard secondary or higher education curriculum in agriculture or forestry, and, in the programmes submitted to the Commission, Member States must, in addition to outlining the eligible schemes and beneficiaries, provide assurance that no standard educational programme or scheme is put forward for Community funding. The scope of these measures for the purposes of vocational training is therefore not the same as that of the Socrates Programme which the Council, in response to the Commission proposal, has just decided to renew.

Socrates, the Community’s action programme in the field of education, the second phase of which was established by decision No 253/2000/EC of the Parliament and the Council, seeks to contribute to the development of quality education by encouraging cooperation between the Member States and by supporting and supplementing their action. According to Article 149 (ex-Article 126) of the EC Treaty, the responsibility for the content of teaching and the organisation of the educational systems lies with the Member States.

The school education chapter of the programme, Comenius, aims at enhancing the quality and the European dimension of school education throughout the Community. Comenius targets all schools of the Member States, be they rural or urban, and it seeks to encourage transnational cooperation between schools, to contribute to improved professional development of staff in the school education sector, and to promote the learning of languages and intercultural awareness.

 

Question no 20 by Martin Callanan (H-0838/00)
 Subject: OLAF
 

Can the Commission comment on how OLAF is currently operating within the Commission itself? If OLAF is so independent, why does its headed notepaper state ‘EUROPEAN COMMISSION’ at the top?

 
  
 

1. The Commission recalls that the European Anti-Fraud Office (OLAF), which was established by the Commission Decision of 28 April 19991(1), performs the tasks which are laid down in Article 2 of that Decision.

Those tasks include:

- operational activities (conducting of external or internal investigations, collection and exchange of information, contact with police and judicial authorities). In this connection, Regulation (EC) No 1073/1999 of the European Parliament and of the Council and Council Regulation (Euratom) No 1074/1999, both of 25 May 1999, concerning investigations conducted by the European Anti-Fraud Office (OLAF)2(2), lay down how the Office is to conduct these, in particular internal investigations at the Commission, the terms and conditions for which were laid down by the Commission Decision of 2 June 19993(3) pursuant to the Interinstitutional Agreement of 25 May 19994(4), to which a model decision was annexed;

- activities relating to policy on the fight against fraud and on the protection of financial interests5(5), which are the responsibility of the Commission. In this context, the Office acts like any other Directorate-General within the Commission.

2. Where the Office's operational activities are concerned, it carries out its tasks completely independently, in accordance with its establishment mechanism. A Surveillance Committee, the tasks of which have been laid down by the legislator, has been established to guarantee and strengthen that independence.

3. Where the Office's other activities are concerned, it acts in the same way as the Commission's other Directorates-General, thus under the authority of the Commissioners. This is why the title 'European Anti-Fraud Office' appears on documents after that of the Commission.

 
 

(1)1 OJ L 136, 31.5.1999, p. 20.
(2)2 OJ L 136, 31.5.1999, pp. 1 and 8.
(3)3 OJ L 149, 16.6.1999, p. 57.
(4)4 OJ L 136, 31.5.1999, p. 15.
(5)5 covering, in particular, the activities of conceptual development, preparation of legislative and regulatory initiatives and representation of the Commission.

 

Question no 21 by Michl Ebner (H-0857/00)
 Subject: Financial aid granted in response to the Resolution on Flooding in Italy and Spain
 

In the last two months various regions of Italy have been devastated by natural disasters, in September in the province of Trento and in October in the regions of Piedmont, Val d’Aosta, Liguria and Lombardy. The European Parliament subsequently adopted a resolution (B5-0807) calling on the Commission to make both technical and financial aid available without delay by drawing on a number of EU funds.

Can the Commission state to what extent it has already met the demands made in that resolution, what amount of funds have been made available and how these have been precisely distributed?

 
  
 

The Commission has already had occasion to present its views on the conditions for the application of Structural Funds in the event of natural disasters in its statement to the European Parliament of 25 October 2000.(1)

With regard to the resources of the European Agricultural Guarantee and Guidance Fund (EAGGF), the Commission has not received any official requests from the authorities of either Spain or Italy with a view to reallocating resources from this fund following the recent natural disasters.

It should be pointed out that the Council Regulation on support for rural development2(2) gives Member States the option of including in their rural development plans measures pursuant to the twelfth item of Article 33 on restoring agricultural production potential damaged by natural disasters and introducing appropriate prevention instruments. A number of Spanish and Italian regional plans have indeed included a provision of this sort for the programming period 2000-2006.

EAGGF resources could consequently be set aside on this legal basis, on the proviso that they remained less than the financial contribution of each Member State. With regard to the regions affected, and in consideration of the extent of the damage, reprogramming within the limits of the available appropriations seems probable but must be submitted to the Commission for examination and, if necessary, approval.

With regard to the Community initiative Interreg III, there have been no specific requests from the Member States concerned. This does not, however, exclude the possibility that some action with regard to natural disasters, particularly with a view to prevention, may be included in the proposed programmes for sections A and B of Interreg III which are to be submitted to the Commission in the next few weeks. The Interreg programme cannot take actual expenses related to such action into consideration until the competent national and/or regional authorities present their proposed programmes.

More specifically regarding the Italian regions mentioned by the honourable Member, on 27 July 2000, the Commission finalised the list of Italian regions eligible for Structural Fund action under Objective 2 for the period 2000-2006. The regions of Trento, Piedmont, Val d’Aosta, Liguria and Lombardy, which were affected by the flooding in September and October, have not to date officially presented their Single Programming Document proposals under this objective. As soon as these proposals have been received, the Commission will assess the possibility of undertaking action in these areas along the lines already presented to the European Parliament on 25 October 2000.

Question no 23 by Othmar Karas (H-0840/00)
 Subject: Progress and impact of euro campaign in Member States
 

Fourteen months before the issue of euro banknotes and coins, levels of public awareness and understanding concerning the single currency are by no means satisfactory. The Community public funds entered in budget item B3-300 for the PRINCE programme are for the purpose of informing European citizens and preparing them for the euro. The European Parliament's budgetary powers entitle it to be kept fully up to date on the course of that information campaign.

When will the Commission report to the European Parliament on the progress made and impact achieved by the information campaign in the euro Member States, giving an account in particular of the practical action taken and any deficiencies identified? What proposals for improving and streamlining the information campaign will it submit to Member States on the basis of its conclusions?

 
  
 

Since the honourable Member’s oral question was tabled, the Commission has implemented a raft of measures intended to improve the effectiveness of the provision of information, both centralised and decentralised.

Efficiency has been sought in the structures implemented in order to ensure cooperation and coordination. Coordination is effective at a number of levels:

- At departmental level: regular meetings chaired by the Directorate-General for Economic and Financial Affairs bring together the Commission departments directly or indirectly affected by the issue of the euro. The European Central Bank (ECB) and the European Parliament (EP) are invited to attend these meetings.

- At the level of ECB and the Eurosystem (European System of Central Banks), the Commission monitors, as an observer, the meetings of the group responsible for monitoring and directing the euro information campaign. A monthly meeting is held in Frankfurt.

- At Member State level, and, more specifically, at the level of ministries and other bodies responsible for conducting the euro information campaigns, meetings of the euro communication campaign directors are convened by the Commission (the Directorate-General for Economic and Financial Affairs). Five or six meetings are planned for next year. The last meeting, held on 23 and 24 October in Strasbourg, at the invitation of the European Parliament, made it possible to hear Mr Karas and Mrs Randzio-Plath speak, and also, for the first time, to bring together all the national and institutional operators involved in the euro information campaigns. Many Members of Parliament took part in this meeting. This experiment will be repeated in 2001, as it provides a useful opportunity to exchange ideas and experience informally and to involve the Members of Parliament. Another initiative, a scoreboard for the euro information campaigns being conducted in the Member States and by the Commission, will be drawn up and regularly updated. Details of this will be passed on to the Members of Parliament.

- At national level, the Commission representatives and Parliament services take part in euro committee meetings (or similar meetings).

- At institutional level, the high level meeting between the European Parliament and the Commission provides the political forum for coordination.

The Commission would like to stress its determination to make every effort to ensure that the information on the euro provided by the various operators, our partners, is such as to overcome the lack of information noted today and to meet the expectations of citizens. Quite obviously, the role of the Member States is primordial and, in accordance with the principles of subsidiarity and decentralisation, it falls to the national states to devote their own budget and human resources to ensuring that their nationals are informed to as high a degree as possible and trained regarding the euro.

The Commission will play its role of coordination and promotion to the full, but only to the extent that its abilities and powers allow.

 

Question no 25 by Piia-Noora Kauppi (H-0803/00)
 Subject: Oil pollution near Gotland (Sweden)
 

A serious oil spill has occurred off the island of Gotska Sandön, near Gotland in the Baltic, and the authorities have not – at least at the time of tabling this question – succeeded in ascertaining who caused it.

Several sources in the European media report that the oil spill, which occurred on Monday, 2 October 2000, has polluted some 90% of the island’s coast. The cleaning of the beach, which must of course begin without delay, will be extremely labour-intensive, because the island has no harbour and its coastal waters are shallow. The oil will have to be picked up by hand and taken back to the mainland.

The island, 3700 ha in area, has sandy beaches, and is known amongst other things for its 900 species of beetles, its plants and its grey seals. Fortunately no dead animals have yet been found on the islands. The timing of the accident was also fortunate in that most of the birds have already migrated. However, the oil presents a threat to the bird species which remain on the island.

What measures does the Commission propose to take to ensure that the person responsible for the oil spill off the coast of Gotska Sandön is found out as soon as possible and brought to justice?

 
  
 

The response to be given to marine pollution incidents primarily falls under the responsibility of the affected Member State.

In the field of accidental marine pollution, a Community action plan has been developed since 1978. The major elements of this plan are joint training initiatives, pilot projects for the development of combating means, the provision and exchange of information, and a task force including designated experts from the Member States, which is activated in case of accident.

A new decision setting up a Community framework for cooperation in the field of accidental or deliberate marine pollution should soon be finalised by Parliament and the Council. This instrument will not only bring together and consolidate the different actions carried out until now in the field of accidental pollution, but also include Community cooperation in the field of deliberate pollution at sea, in particular concerning operational spills from vessels. The financial reference for this amount for this decision for the period 2000 to 2006 is set at EUR 7 million. Once the decision is adopted, the Commission will propose concrete initiatives to be developed under this new Community framework. Such measures should in particular address reinforced cooperation in order to improve the identification of contravening vessels and further exchange of information on the efficiency of the prosecution systems applied in the different Member States. This will create synergies at Community level and should ultimately permit the application of dissuasive sanctions against the parties responsible for such criminal acts.

 

Question no 26 by Jean-Louis Bernié (H-0825/00)
 Subject: Mussel fishing in the Wadden Sea (Netherlands)
 

In March 1998 the Commission brought an action against the Netherlands before the European Court of Justice, maintaining that mussel fishing was disturbing migratory birds in the Wadden Sea NATURA 2000 site.

It appears that this action has been dropped. If so, why? If not, what stage has the procedure reached?

 
  
 

According to the Commission, the Netherlands failed to take appropriate measures with regard to the management of the cockle and mussel fishery in the Wadden Sea – designated as a special protection area under Directive 79/409/EEC on the conservation of wild birds – to ensure that the quality of the habitats of certain bird species does not deteriorate and that no significant disturbance occurs for those species, as required in Article 4(4) of Directive 79/409/EEC. The size of the area closed to the fishery was too small and limiting fishery to a level at which only at least 60% of the food requirements in the form of cockles and mussels remains available for birds in years of food shortage was considered to be insufficient.

The Netherlands failed to reply to both the letter of formal notice (sent 16 January 1996) and to the reasoned opinion (sent 23 September 1997). For this reason the Commission decided to take the case to the Court of Justice on 2 March 1998 (case registered by the Court under C-63/98).

Within the context of the Court proceedings the Netherlands responded for the first time to the allegations made in the case. This led to the Commission withdrawing the case since the substantive information provided by the Dutch authorities had to be taken into account. As a result, the allegations mentioned in the letter of formal notice and the reasoned opinion could not be considered any longer as sufficient to demonstrate a causal relationship between the fishery and possible effects on the birds, particularly since those allegations related to the period from 1993 and the Dutch shell fishery policy was under review. The Commission by letter of 30 November 1998 requested the Court to withdraw the case. By decision of 4 May 1999 the Chairman of the Court decided to delete the case. The Netherlands was condemned to pay the costs as it had failed to reply to the reasoned opinion in time which led to the Commission taking the case to the Court.

 

Question no 27 by Jonas Sjöstedt (H-0833/00)
 Subject: Safety in connection with dam accidents
 

In recent years, several very serious accidents causing environmental damage have occurred following the collapse of dams built near mining sites, e.g. in southern Spain and Romania. Another relevant example is the collapse of a dam at Aitik in northern Sweden earlier this year, which resulted in leakage of polluted water. Does the Commission have plans for any legislative initiatives in this sphere to prevent further accidents of this kind?

 
  
 

The Danube pollution caused by a cyanide spill following a damburst of a tailings pond in Baia Mare/Romania and an accident that occurred in 1998 in Aznalcóllar/Spain where a damburst poisoned the environment of the Coto Doñana National Park have increased public awareness of the environmental and safety hazards of mining activities and have also raised the question of the effectiveness of Community policies intended to prevent such disasters.

On 23 October 2000, the Commission adopted a communication on the "Safe operation of mining activities: a follow-up to recent mining accidents" (COM(2000) 664 final). This Communication describes the steps that the Commission envisages to take as a follow-up to recent mining accidents, as announced in its Communication of 3 May 2000 on Promoting sustainable development in the EU non-energy extractive industry (COM(2000) 265 final).

With regard to possible initiatives in the framework of Community environmental legislation, the Communication sets out three priority actions envisaged to improve the safety of mines. They relate to industrial risk management, management of mining waste and integrated pollution prevention and control:

- amendment of the Seveso II Directive (96/82/EC) to include mineral processing of ores and, in particular, tailings ponds or dams used in connection with such mineral processing of ores. Industrial operators performing these activities would thus be obliged to put into effect Safety Management Systems, including a detailed risk assessment on the basis of possible accident scenarios (proposal foreseen by mid-2001);

- an initiative on the management of mining waste. This initiative will take into account the results of a study launched by the Commission on the existing legislation and practices concerning the management of waste from the extractive industry. The study is to be completed by the end of 2000 for the 15 Member States and by mid-2001 for the candidate countries. The initiative on waste management will cover the environmental issues of the management of mining waste as well as the best practices, which could prevent environmental damage during the waste management.

- a reference document (BREF) under the Integrated pollution prevention and control (IPPC) Directive (96/61/EC) describing the Best Available Techniques (BAT) to reduce everyday pollution and to prevent or mitigate accidents in the non-ferrous metals mining sector. This document would provide guidance and address both industry as well as the Member States' authorities responsible for issuing permits under the IPPC Directive.

These initiatives will be prepared in close cooperation with the candidate countries for accession to the Community. The Communication and the priority actions identified will constitute an important input to the ongoing work of the Baia Mare Task Force (BMFT), an international task force set up to examine the causes and consequences of the cyanide spill into the Danube, to propose remedial actions and to identify other high risk installations in the area, to avoid similar accidents in future. The BMFT will present its Final Report to the member of the Commission in charge of Environment on 15 December 2000.

Question no 31 by Per-Arne Arvidsson (H-0823/00)
 Subject: Employers' contributions
 

The Commission has raised objections to Sweden's reduction of employers' contributions for the service industry in an Objective 1 subsidised area. In Norway, the corresponding contributions have been lowered and approved by the EFTA Court and the EFTA Surveillance Authority in order to offset the additional costs incurred as a result of long distances, sparse population, climate and high unemployment in the service sector. The Commission's reluctance to accept equivalent rules for Sweden primarily affects the area's very important tourist industry which, on the Swedish side, is largely situated close to the border with Norway and competes with Norwegian companies for the same business. Is it reasonable for different EU-associated decision-making bodies to come to different conclusions on this matter and on what grounds does the Commission take the view that Sweden should be treated differently from Norway, an EEA country, despite the similar conditions in both countries and their both having to comply with EU rules in this sector? Will the Commission, when giving the matter further consideration, take account of the conditions under which companies compete in the tourist industry in the border areas?

 
  
 

The Commission initiated the Article 88(2) (ex-Article 93) of the Treaty procedure against the Swedish reduced social contributions scheme, because it has doubts as to its compatibility with Community State aid rules.

As pointed out, the low population density regions covered by the scheme are severely affected by high transport costs. This is why the Guidelines on national regional aid allow Member States to provide transport aid in such areas. In order to avoid abuse, the Guidelines provide that transport aid should be granted exclusively in relation to the extra costs of transport of goods incurred by companies located in low population density areas. They also provide that there should never be overcompensation of the extra transport costs.

A company's social security contribution is a function of the size of its workforce, and not of the extra transport costs it has incurred. There is therefore no link between the aid under the scheme and the extra transport costs incurred by the beneficiaries. This also means that there is no guarantee that there is no overcompensation of the extra transport costs. In view of this, the Commission maintains its doubts as to whether the scheme is in conformity with the regional aid guidelines.

The honourable Members draw the Commission's attention to the fact that Norway operates a similar aid scheme in its low population density regions. The Commission is aware of the existence of this scheme. The Norwegian scheme was approved by ESA in 1997. Following the opening of the Article 88(2) procedure against the Swedish Reduced social contributions scheme, the Commission has taken up the issue with the European free trade area (EFTA) Surveillance Authority. When the Commission has taken its final decision on the Swedish scheme, it will examine with the EFTA Surveillance Authority how best to proceed to ensure that State aid rules are implemented in a consistent manner throughout the entire European Economic Area.

In accordance with the principle governing the single market, private individuals may purchase goods tax-paid in one Member State, and then transport them to their own Member State without such goods being liable to further taxation there. In order to assess whether products subject to excise duty are being held for commercial purposes rather than for personal use, Member States are authorised to consider various evidential factors, such as:

the commercial status and motives of the person holding the products,

the place where the products are located or the mode of transport used,

any documents relating to the products, and

the quantity of the products.

Where the last factor is concerned, the Member States are authorised to lay down, solely as an evidential factor, indicative levels which in no circumstances may be lower than those laid down in the directive (e.g. 800 cigarettes, 90 litres of wine, 10 litres of spirits).

It is only after considering all the factors referred to that the Member States may decide whether a person's transporting of products subject to excise duty should be deemed to be commercial in nature. This administrative approach is clearly derived from Community rules. Question no 56 by Ioannis Theonas (H-0849/00)
 Subject: Tax on fuel
 

The oil crisis which is still unfolding is having multiple adverse effects both on the economies of EU Member States and on the standards of living of various sectors of the population: workers, farmers, professionals and transporters. In addition to the rise in the international price of crude oil in US dollars, the effects of the galloping increase in the exchange rate of the dollar and the undervaluation of the euro in the international markets are making the crisis more severe. In Greece, the situation is even worse because of the fact that in addition to the already high special consumption tax on fuel, VAT is also imposed, not only on the added value of petrol and oil but also on the special consumption tax. This double taxation represents an added burden of 8% on the final price for the consumer. Does the Commission not consider that it is unacceptable to impose a tax upon a tax? What measures does it intend to take with regard to this issue?

 
  
 

The rise in fuel prices observed from the beginning of 1999 is due to the evolution of crude oil world prices. However, taxes form a significant part of the consumer price of oil products and their level varies considerably from one Member State to another. There are two kinds of taxes: (1) an excise duty in the form of a specific duty amount expressed as a fixed amount in euros and national currency per 1000 litres of fuel; and (2) VAT which is a consumption tax, levied on the total sales price of goods and services to final consumers.

As far as the excise duties are concerned, a harmonised specific excise duty (which is applied to quantities rather than values) has been introduced by Council Directive 92/82/EEC(1)1, the level of which represent the minimum rate to be applied by each Member State. Although in practice Member States apply rates that far exceed these minimum rates, for oil products used as motor fuel Greece applies rates which are very close to these Community minimum rates. They are among the three lowest rates in the Community. For diesel used as a propellant, for instance, Greece applies a rate of 252.55 euros/1000 litres, which is very close to the Community minimum rate of 245 euros/1000 litres.

Taking into account the specific nature of the excise duties it is clear that they are not influenced by inflation, or by the fluctuation of world prices of crude oil and therefore, their relative weight to the final consumer price is reducing when these prices are increasing.

As far as VAT is concerned its basis includes, where appropriate, any other taxes, levies, duties and charges, which may also be levied on the goods in question. When the price of goods increases, for whatever reason, the logical consequence is that the amount of VAT will also increase, as it is calculated as a fixed percentage of the total sales price. These rules are fully in line with Community VAT legislation (notably Article 11 of the Sixth VAT Directive). This is valid for all products and the Commission does not envisage a change to them. Greece applies a VAT rate of 18% on oil products which is below the average rate applied by the Member States.

The Commission does not share the idea of using the tax instrument in order to neutralise increases on consumer prices when the crude oil prices increase. This correspond in reality to a transfer of revenue from the oil consuming to the oil producing countries and would run counter to our environmental objectives, notably under the Kyoto protocol.

The only conceivable adjustment mechanism relates to VAT. It could be possible to stabilise VAT revenue in the event of significant fluctuations in oil prices.

Question no 58 by Charlotte Cederschiöld (H-0853/00)
 Subject: Charter of Fundamental Rights

It is my understanding that, as the Charter refers to national law, the right to strike as currently formulated in the Charter does not affect freedom of movement in the Union.

In line with the determination of the European Council, the draft Charter of Fundamental Rights unites in a single text the range of fundamental rights already in force at European Union level in order to give them greater prominence in the eyes of the citizens of the European Union.

The freedom of movement of persons as enshrined in the EC Treaty is retained in Article 45 of the draft Charter, while Article 28 thereof outlines the right of workers, in accordance with Community and national law, to take collective action, including strike action, in cases of conflicts of interest with their employers.

In practice, situations where there is a conflict between these two fundamental rights cannot be ruled out. Observations of this kind may also be made with regard to several other fundamental rights.

Article 52(1) of the Charter, by establishing a system allowing potential restrictions on the exercise of fundamental rights, makes it possible to resolve this conflict between the freedom of movement and the right to strike. This provision makes it clear that the exercise of a right may not override any other right or freedom which is also recognised in the Charter. In no case, then, is it possible to suggest that one right may be ‘more important’ than another. Quite the contrary: the authorities, in imposing restrictions on the exercise of the fundamental rights at issue here – restrictions which may be perfectly legal – are required to establish a balance between the rights at issue in order to ensure that both rights are respected, proportionately, in the specific conditions of each case.

The Commission would draw the honourable Member’s attention to the fact that the circumstances she refers to are covered by a Community regulation, insofar as the free movement of goods is concerned. Council Regulation (EC) No 2679/98 of 7 December 1998(1) 1 on the functioning of the internal market in relation to the free movement of goods among the Member States, while expressly making an exception for the right to protection of fundamental rights (including, in the terms of the regulation, “the right or freedom to strike”), seeks to overcome obstacles causing serious disruption to the proper functioning of the internal market and inflicting serious losses on the individuals affected. The Charter does not have the effect of amending this regulation, which clearly respects the various fundamental rights involved.
