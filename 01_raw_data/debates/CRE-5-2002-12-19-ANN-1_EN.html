Question no 14 by Helle Thorning-Schmidt (H-0752/02)
 Subject: Relationship with Russia
 

In the light of the fact that the EU-Russia summit on 11 November 2002 has been moved from Copenhagen to Brussels, the Council is asked to answer the following questions:

Did the Danish Presidency consult the other Member States in the Council before the meeting was moved?

Was there a common position on the issue in the Council or did moving the summit give rise to protests from the other Member States?

Does the Presidency agree that it is essential to form a common front in the Council on this issue so that defending constitutional rights is not reduced to a conflict between Denmark and Russia?

Does the Presidency intend to make a statement to the Council on the arrest of the Chechen deputy prime minister, Akhmed Zakajev, which happened on 30 October 2002 in Denmark?

 
  
 

(DA) The latest EU-Russia Summit, held on 11 November in Brussels, has been widely judged to have been a great success. Despite a difficult political context, it yielded vital agreement between leaders on both sides on how to deal with Kaliningrad. This was an important result on the way to EU enlargement. Apart from the agreement on Kaliningrad, we were able to reach agreement on Joint Statements concerning the Middle East and the fight against terrorism. We also noted good progress made on the Common European Economic Space, and on the EU-Russia Energy Dialogue. Such results would not have been possible if the Summit had not taken place.

Member States were kept informed at all stages in the preparations of the Summit. Proof that this was done completely and effectively comes from the fact that, since the Summit, the Presidency has received warm praise for its transparent handling of the preparations. On the particular question of moving the Summit from Copenhagen to Brussels, the Presidency at all times expressed the positions of the Council and of the Union. The Presidency received the full support of Member States.

The Presidency absolutely agrees that it is important for the Council to speak with one voice. It was this clarity that allowed the Summit to go ahead in a successful manner. The Council has been united and Russia has taken good note of the EU’s position. Mr Rasmussen, on behalf of the Council, made clear after the Summit that Chechnya was an important subject of discussion between the two sides. The EU was able to use the occasion to put its view that a political settlement is an essential condition to achieving a lasting peace in the region. In Copenhagen, the European Council (12/13 December 2002) recalled that the enlargement will strengthen relations with Russia

As the honourable Member will know, there have been a number of developments since this question was submitted. First, a Danish court refused a request for him to be extradited and ordered him freed. Then Mr Zakayev flew to the UK, where he was detained on arrival. He has since been remanded on conditional bail, and is due to return to Court on 9 January 2003.

Given that the case of Mr Zakajev is currently still a matter for the judiciary, it is not appropriate for anyone else to make any comment at this stage.

 

Question no 15 by Ole Krarup (H-0758/02)
 Subject: Minority rights
 

Does the Council consider it compatible with democratic principles that large ethnic minorities in several applicant countries are in reality without rights in a number of fundamental respects, including the approximately 23% of ethnic Russians in Latvia who have no nationality and no electoral rights but who are subject to visa requirements?

The situation has not changed significantly since Parliament's 1999 annual report which contains the statement that infringements of 'basic civil and political rights' and 'discrimination towards, in particular, national minorities' do take place (in Latvia). Does the Council have any plan of action for countering this discrimination?

 
  
 

(DA) As the Honourable Parliamentarian is aware, the Copenhagen European Council in June 1993 laid down the political criteria for accession which stipulate that candidate countries must have achieved stability of institutions guaranteeing democracy, the rule of law, human rights and respect for and protection of minorities. All candidate countries with which accession negotiations have been opened fulfil the political criteria. This has been of course strongly confirmed by the decision of the last European Council in Copenhagen, to conclude negotiation with Latvia, among others.

Against this background, the Union has closely followed the question of national minorities in Latvia in the past and will continue to monitor it until the accession process is fully completed. This issue has been included amongst the priorities of the revised Accession Partnership for Latvia, adopted in January 2002, and has regularly been discussed with the Latvian side in the framework of the Europe Agreement bodies, in particular the Association Council which held its latest meeting in February this year.

The 2002 Regular Report from the Commission on Latvia's progress towards accession , issued in October, concluded that Latvia continued to fulfil the Copenhagen political criteria. More specifically, this report stated that: 'Latvia has encouraged the further integration of non-citizens into Latvian society by removing language requirements in the election law, and increasing support to facilitate the naturalisation process, and through the activities of the Society Integration Foundation.'. The Report also underlined that 'the integration process needs to be accelerated and sufficient funding made available to encourage naturalisation and language training in particular'.

In conclusion, it can be said that Latvia has taken a number of concrete measures as concerns integration of non-citizens into society, and that these positive developments should be acknowledged, as also confirmed by the decision taken by the OSCE in last December not to extend the mandate of its mission. At the same time, the Union will continue to monitor the situation, particularly with regard to the implementation of the adopted measures, and to encourage Latvia to build on the progress already made. The European Council, in Copenhagen, 12-13 December 2002, underlined that monitoring up to accession of the commitments undertaken will give further guidance to the acceeding States in their efforts to assume responsibilities of membership and will give the necessary assurance to current Member States.

 

Question no 16 by Paulo Casaca (H-0762/02)
 Subject: Human rights
 

Can the Council confirm that a bilateral meeting on 'Energy' between the European Union and the Iranian authorities is scheduled to take place in Teheran on 16 and 17 November?

Will the Council clarify whether the launch of these negotiations is linked to the Council's decision of 21 October not to submit any resolution on Iran at the forthcoming session of the United Nations Commission on Human Rights?

 
  
 

(DA) The Council is not aware of any meeting between the European Union and Iran scheduled to take place on 16 and 17 November 2002. The Council can confirm, however, that a working group on energy, consisting of competent representatives of the European Commission, and of the Iranian Government, met in Teheran on 19 October 2002.

This meeting was not a 'launch of negotiations' but one of several activities in the framework of the EU-Iran Comprehensive Dialogue; it was in fact the third such meeting of this group to take place since 1998 Pending negotiation of a new contractual relationship between the EU and Iran, the Comprehensive Dialogue serves as a forum for raising issues of concern, such as human rights, but also for exploring promising areas of cooperation between the EU and Iran, such as energy. Any such co-operation must been seen in the overall global political context within which all relations with Iran now have to be considered.

There is thus no link between the meeting of the working group on energy on 19 October and the Council's conclusions of 21 October. The Council would moreover like to point out that no decision was taken on 21 October on whether or not to table a resolution on Iran at the 59th session of the UN Commission on Human Rights, due to commence in March 2003.

The Council invites the honourable Member to refer to the exact terms of this declaration which sets out its position regarding the opening of a human rights dialogue with Iran.

 

Question no 17 by Ioannis Patakis (H-0767/02)
 Subject: Use of gases by law enforcement agencies
 

The use of gases which claimed scores of victims to end the tragedy of the hundreds of citizens taken hostage in a Moscow theatre, the unknown composition of those gases and the fact that they were probably prohibited under international conventions have once again brought the issue of the production, storage and use of chemical and biological weapons sharply into focus.

Has the Council investigated, in the context of the common foreign and security policy and the European security and defence policy, whether the Member States have ratified the international conventions banning the use of chemical weapons, whether they continue to produce or hold stocks of chemical weapons, and whether it is allowed to use gases as a means of enforcement against their own citizens when their use is prohibited in the event of a military conflict with another country?

 
  
 

(DA) The Council informs the honourable Member that all EU Member States are States Parties to the Convention on the Prohibition of the Development, Production, Stockpiling and Use of Chemical Weapons and on their Destruction (CWC).

Article VIII of the Convention established the Organisation for the Prohibition of Chemical Weapons (OPCW) 'to ensure the implementation of its provisions, including those for international verification of compliance with it'. All States Parties to the CWC are members of the OPCW. This is the organisation entrusted with the verification of compliance by States Parties with the provisions of the Convention, including the case referred to by the honourable Member, notably law enforcement.

Question no 20 by Efstratios Korakas (H-0774/02)
 Subject: Persecution of communists in countries which have applied to join the enlarged European Union
 

In many EU applicant countries, the activities of certain political parties are subject to restrictions or even prohibited. For example, in Latvia, Lithuania, Poland, Romania and Hungary the activities of communist parties are outlawed. They are barred from competing in elections and freely circulating their ideas. In certain cases these restrictions are based on constitutional provisions which underpin the historically inaccurate association of Nazism with communism. Party members face prosecution or heavy prison sentences on fabricated charges (Latvia, Slovakia, Lithuania) while former members of worker collectives (Latvia) are subject to restrictions regarding the type of employment they are authorised to take up.

Does the Council not agree that this is a blatant contradiction of its expressed view that the countries in question have made great progress regarding respect for the rule of law and human rights and does it intend to take the necessary measures to ensure the free movement of ideas, freedom of activity for all political parties and an end to the persecution of communists and discrimination against them in the countries concerned?

 
  
 

(DA) The EU has attached utmost importance to the respect, by the candidate countries, of the principles of democracy, the rule of law and human rights, and has been monitoring the last political developments in the candidate countries, not least in the light of the need for them to fulfil the political criterion which is part of the Copenhagen criteria established in 1993.

In this context it has to be noted that the Regular Reports, presented by the Commission in October of this year, came to the conclusion that all candidate countries with which the EU hoped to conclude accession negotiations later this year continue to fulfil this criterion.

This has been confirmed by the decision taken in Copenhagen on 12-12 December 2002, when the Union endorsed the result of these negotiations, concluding the accession negotiations with ten countries.

The European Council, on this occasion, considered that this achievement testifies to the common determination of the peoples of Europe to come together in a Union that has become the driving force for peace, democracy, stability and prosperity on our continent.

 

Question no 21 by Robert J.E. Evans (H-0775/02)
 Subject: Religious intolerance in Hungary
 

Hungary, on the threshold of accession to the EU, still maintains a 15-year-old tradition of religious apartheid and violation of human rights.

The previous Hungarian Government introduced law LXXIV, 2001 (PA 153). This law radically alters how taxpayers' support for religious institutions is determined. It replaces the explicit annual declaration of taxpayers by ambiguous statistics derived from a recent census relating to citizens' natal faith.

This law is inequitable and unconstitutional, designed to favour a few well-established religions at the expense of other faiths.

In view of the European Union's principles of respect and tolerance, will the Council urge the Hungarian Government to repeal this law as a condition of accession?

 
  
 

(EN) The Council has attached the utmost importance to the respect, by the candidate countries, of the political criteria for accession established at the Copenhagen European Council, namely democracy, the rule of law, human rights and protection of minorities.

As regards the specific issue raised by the Honourable Parliamentarian, it should be noted that the Commission's 2002 Regular Report on Hungary's progress towards accession, which was issued in October, stated that the 'freedom of religion is enshrined in the Hungarian Constitution and no particular problems have been reported in this respect'. Furthermore, it should be noted that the criteria and methods used for granting state support to religious bodies and institutions come within the national competence of individual States and do not form part of the accession negotiations.

In any case let me repeat that the achievement of the accession negotiations, as underlined by the European Council, in Copenhagen, 12-13 December 2002, testifies to the common determination of the people of Europe to come together in a Union that has become the driving force for, among others, peace and democracy.

 

Question no 22 by Konstantinos Alyssandrakis (H-0777/02)
 Subject: Repressive measures with a view to the Thessaloniki summit meeting in June 2003
 

A report in the newspaper 'Makedonia' has disclosed the content of a memorandum of cooperation between the Greek police and Europol entitled 'Security manual for European Councils and similar events', with a view to the Thessaloniki summit meeting in June 2003. According to the manual, surveillance will be carried out in order to draw up lists of suspects and people who will be prevented from entering Greece (by invoking Article 2.2 of the Schengen Agreement), under the supervision of a body consisting of representatives of the police forces of the 15 Member States.

Can the Council confirm the existence of such a manual and that extraordinary measures are to be taken with a view to the summit? Does it consider that the measures proposed are contrary to Community legislation and democratic rights, in particular the right of assembly and demonstration?

 
  
 

(DA) After the events which took place in Gothenburg when the European Council was meeting there and after similar events which took place in Genoa when a meeting of the G7 was held there, the Council decided that a Security Handbook for the use of police authorities and services at meetings of the European Council and other similar events should be drawn up. Such a handbook has been submitted to the Council on the occasion of its meeting of 28/29 November 2002. The Council has approved the Security Hand Book whose role consists in serving as guidelines and catalogue of ideas for the Member States when undertaking the task of providing security for the related events.

It is for each Member State concerned to take the necessary measures in relation to the preparation of meetings of the European Council, in particular as far as aspects of safeguarding public order and internal security are concerned. For these aspects the Council has no competencies.

 

Question no 23 by Diana Wallis (H-0778/02)
 Subject: Proposed Legal Aid Directive
 

It appears that the Council is likely to adopt in late November 2002 the Commission proposal for a Council directive to improve access to justice in cross-border disputes by establishing minimum common rules relating to legal aid and other financial aspects of civil proceedings (COM(2002)0013/final).

If that timetable were adopted, the codecision procedure established by the entry into force of the Treaty of Nice, would be avoided.

Commissioner Vitorino has stated that, in his view, Parliament should have been consulted afresh, given the substantial changes made by the Council to the original Commission proposal on which Parliament had delivered its formal opinion (Santini report, Α5-0312/2002).

Could the Council please explain why it does not intend formally to consult Parliament afresh, in accordance with normal procedure and on the basis of working relationship between Parliament and Council, before it finally adopts the Directive?

Does not the Council think that this manner of proceeding undermines the working relationship between the two institutions?

 
  
 

(DA) The Council would point out that the EU's institutions are to cooperate as effectively as possible while fully complying with the rules adopted.

As regards the proposal for a directive on legal aid, the Council requested the European Parliament on 1 February 2002 to deliver an opinion on the basis of the Commission's proposal. It hoped to receive the opinion as soon as possible.

As amendments were made to the proposal for a directive during proceedings in the Council, and since Parliament had not yet delivered its opinion, the Council decided that it would brief Parliament in a letter of 22 September 2002 on the progress of the proceedings so that it could deliver its opinion in the light of the amendments that the Council's bodies had made to the proposal.

The European Parliament has, therefore, had the opportunity to express its opinion both on the basis of the Commission's proposal and the latest version of the text, which the Council forwarded to it. In the legislative resolution of 25 September 2002, the European Parliament refers expressly in fact to 'the revised version' which the Council forwarded ('having regard to the revised version of the Council Directive (10856/2002-C5-0423/2002')). The Council must, therefore, point out that Parliament has delivered its opinion in full knowledge of the progress of proceedings in the Council, as evidenced by the revised version. The text has not subsequently been amended so substantially as to warrant further consultation.

The legislative procedure has therefore been conducted entirely in accordance with the applicable rules.

 

Question no 24 by Alexandros Alavanos (H-0780/02)
 Subject: Situation of the Kurds in northern Iraq
 

The events of 1991 in northern Iraq, which caused enormous loss of life among the Kurds and a stream of 1.5 million outgoing Kurdish refugees, were followed by a very troubled period of civil strife and foreign intervention with tragic consequences for the population. According to persistent rumours regarding a possible future invasion of Iraq, around 30 000 Kurds will be used as backup ground forces. If this is true it will greatly compound the suffering of the local inhabitants.

In view of the above, what are the Council’s thoughts regarding the future of the Kurds in northern Iraq? What steps will it take to establish a stable administration in the area capable of resolving the enormous problems facing its inhabitants?

 
  
 

(DA) The future of the Kurds in Iraq is of course inevitably linked to the future of Iraq. The Council’s view, and indeed the view of the international community as a whole, is that Iraq must fully honour its obligations under the United Nations Security Council Resolutions. The UN weapons inspectors are now back in Iraq after nearly 4 years, working under a strengthened inspection regime, decided unanimously by the Security Council in Resolution 1441. This Resolution clearly points out the obligations which Saddam Hussein must fulfil in order to avoid serious consequences for Iraq and it sets the conditions for a peaceful solution of the crisis. The purpose is to have disarmed, peaceful Iraq.

The Council has not discussed the future of the Kurdish population in Northern Iraq and their local administration. The Council's main concern is to ensure that the prospect of weapons of mass destruction in Iraqi hands is stopped, and that those weapons are eliminated.

The European Council in Copenhagen (12-13 December 2002) confirmed that this goal remained a priority. It underlined its full and unequivocal support for the Security Council Resolution 1441 of 8 November 2002 and considered it was now up to Iraq to seize this final opportunity to comply with its international obligations.

 

Question no 25 by Marco Cappato (H-0784/02)
 Subject: April 2003 UN meeting on drugs: evaluation of the 10-year plan for a 'drugs-free world'
 

In May 1998 the United Nations General Assembly Special Session (UNGASS) on drugs set an ambitious goal in the fight against illicit drugs through a ten year plan (1998 - 2008) under the slogan 'A drug-free world: we can do it!'. After five years, a two-day ministerial segment will be included in next year's UN Committee on Narcotic Drugs session, scheduled for 8-16 April 2003 in Vienna, for a mid-term evaluation of the ten-year plan.

Which EU institutions will participate in the meeting, and will there be adequate participation by Parliament with due consideration for the different political approaches on that sensitive issue? What will be the Council's position during that meeting? Has the Council evaluated the implementation and impact of the UN plan in these first five years in the EU, and also in third countries? Does the Council not think that the UN target of a 'drugs-free world' is clearly unachievable and needs a thorough review?

 
  
 

(DA) The Council and, as I have been informed, the European Commission will take part in the two-day ministerial segment of the UN Commission on Narcotic Drugs session on 8-16 April 2003 in Vienna. The participation of the European Parliament has not yet been debated in the Council. Competent Council bodies in particular in Vienna have started to prepare the aforementioned meeting. The Council has not started to evaluate the implementation of the UN-Plan. The Council and Commission have focused recent efforts on the mid-term evaluation of the 2000-2004 EU Action Plan that is based on the same principles as the 10 year UN global action plan. These principles are: integrated and multidisciplinary approach and shared responsibility.

 

Question no 26 by Christine De Veyrac (H-0790/02)

At its meeting in Laeken, the European Council failed to reach agreement on the seat of the European Aviation Safety Agency, and the matter has not been dealt with at subsequent Council meetings.

It must be said, however, that the delay in choosing a seat is hardly likely to enhance the efficiency of the Agency's operations.

Given that the issue has not been included in the agenda for the European Council meeting in Copenhagen in December 2002, can the Council say what progress has been made on the matter to date?

 
  
 

(DA) Regulation n° 1592/2002 of the 15 July 2002 setting up the European Aviation Safety Agency (EASA), contains no provision regarding the location of EASA. The Council is continuing its efforts for a decision on the permanent location to be taken at the appropriate level in the near future.

 

Question no 27 by Dana Rosemary Scallon (H-0797/02)
 Subject: Development aid policy
 

Will the Council confirm that the Development Aid Policy by EU is within Europe's remit and in accordance with the principles and the declaration contained in the Charter of Fundamental Rights which will become legally binding as the first pillar of the EU Constitution?

 
  
 

(EN) Article 3, point r, of the Treaty establishing the European Community lays down that the activities of the Community shall include a policy in the sphere of Development cooperation.

Question no 28 by Proinsias De Rossa (H-0807/02)
 Subject: Deferral of Israeli participation in Sixth Framework Programme

The Commission will integrate the results of this study in the context of its forthcoming follow-up to the 2001 Commission communication on European Contract Law.

(EN) The Posting of Workers Directive refers to a situation where an undertaking established in a Member State, within the framework of the provision of transnational services, posts its employees to carry out work temporarily in another Member State.

In essence the Directive defines a set of terms and conditions of employment of a host state which must be complied with by the undertakings that post workers.

Three different forms of postings are covered: postings based on a service contract, intra-company postings and finally postings by temporary employment undertakings or placement agencies. Supervising and monitoring the compliance with the terms and conditions of employment is a matter for the Member States. Question no 100 by María Rodríguez Ramos (H-0827/02)
 Subject: Increase in BSE incidence in Spain
 

Reports have appeared in specialist media to the effect that 39 cases of BSE were detected in Spain during the third quarter of 2002: the number of livestock infected was double that for the equivalent quarter of 2001. These figures are extremely disturbing, given, in particular, the fact that the figure for the EU as a whole fell by 32% for the same period.

Are these data correct? What, in the Commission's view, are the reasons for Spain being the only EU Member State in which the incidence of BSE has risen at a time when it has fallen in the other Member States? Has the Commission made concrete recommendations to Spain with a view to the improvement of this situation?

 
  
 

(EN) The Commission has also noted that the number of bovine spongiform encephalopathy (BSE) cases detected in Spain is increasing. In the third quarter this year, Spain reported more than twice as many cases as during the corresponding period last year. At the same time the number of cases decreased or was stable in all other Member States except in Germany, where the Commission saw a slight increase.

Overall, the BSE incidence in Spain is increasing since the beginning of this year. The incidence is however still relatively low, around 35 cases per million adult cattle over the past 12 months, and it is not increasing rapidly. At the end of 2001, the overall BSE incidence in Spain was around 24.

The Commission does not know the exact reason for the increase in BSE cases in Spain. It can however partly be explained by the relatively high number of cases in young animals born between 1996 and 1998 reported in Spain last year. This age group of animals is now getting closer to the average age when the disease becomes clinical, which is 4-6 years, and consequently the number of reported cases is increasing.

On the other hand the Commission has already noted a shift in the age structure of positive cases towards older animals this year, which is a positive sign. This leads it to believe that the trend in the BSE incidence in Spain will reach its peak and start declining sometime next year, provided that the methods of detection remain the same. If new, more sensitive BSE tests become available, the Commission expects to see an increase in reported BSE cases in all Member States.

There is not much that Spain can do to immediately decrease the number of BSE cases being detected. The cases being reported now were infected more than three years ago and reflect the exposure of cattle to the BSE agent at that time. The effect of any measures put in place now will only be seen after 4-5 years. The current Community legislation, in particular the feed ban and the removal and destruction of specified risk material, should, if rigorously implemented, be sufficient to prevent new animals from becoming infected. BSE cases will nevertheless continue to appear in Spain, as in other Member States, for a number of years.

 

Question no 101 by Ulla Margrethe Sandbæk (H-0829/02)
 Subject: Export subsidies for milk powder
 

The EU has recognised the serious economic situation in the Dominican Republic and has taken action accordingly, including the grant of EUR 23 m for a rural development project. This project has been successful, and as a result the production of milk has increased considerably. However, the effects of the project have now been undermined by export subsidies for milk powder, since as a result of these subsidies, Arla (a Danish firm selling dairy products) can sell its milk powder at one third of the price of locally-produced milk. This competition has driven local farmers out of the market, to the extent that, according to local authorities, 20 000 milk producers have had to quit farming.

Is it correct that export subsidies amount to some DKR 5600 per tonne for skimmed milk, some DKR 8100 per tonne for whole milk and some DKR 13 000 per tonne for butter?

Could the Commission please comment on whether this information is correct? If so, will this practice be continued, or have any measures been taken to prevent the EU’s export subsidies from counteracting the interests of the EU’s development projects?

 
  
 

(FR) According to the information available, although the Dominican Republic has developed its national milk production, it is not self-sufficient in milk production and needs imports in order to meet the needs of its population.

In 2000, 50% of these needs were met by imports. Without imports, the price would be significantly higher and milk products would not be within reach of large sections of the population.

The increase in national production demonstrates that the resources implemented by the Union within the framework of development projects have had positive results.

In fact, it is precisely in order to allow such progress that the Community had agreed to restrict its exports of powdered milk within the framework of a rectification, requested by the Dominican Republic, of the concessions made at the World Trade Organisation (WTO).

For exports within the framework of this system, the Commission decided to grant refunds lower than those granted for the export of powdered milk to any other destination in the world; 70% for skimmed milk powder and 85% for whole milk powder. This means that the amounts are considerably lower than those mentioned in the honourable Member’s oral question: DKK 3379 per tonne for skimmed milk powder and DKK 6502 per tonne for whole milk powder. Butter is not covered by the system and the Union’s exports do not exceed 150 tonnes per year.

In view of these considerations, the Commission does not accept the accuracy of the information suggesting that exports of European milk powder have had harmful effects for the Dominican Republic.

In fact, in the frequent contacts held under the system agreed with the authorities of the Dominican Republic, the latter have never mentioned any negative effects on national milk production.

 

Question no 102 by John Walls Cushnahan (H-0831/02)
 Subject: Recent general elections in Pakistan
 

Is the Commission aware that, on 2 December 2002, the Pakistani newspaper ‘The News’ quoted an EU Commission source as saying that ‘the European Commission viewed the EP resolution as a document totally impervious to the political expediencies of the international fight against terror’?

What is the Commission's view of the comment attributed to it, and what is its view of the recently published EUEOM report on the general elections held in Pakistan on 10 October 2002?

 
  
 

(EN) The Commission is aware of the article in the Pakistani paper 'The News' referred to by the honourable Member which claims that 'the European Commission viewed the EP resolution as a document totally impervious to the political expediencies of the international fight against terror.'

According to the article, the quote emanates from 'sources privy to the EU decision-making process'. Whichever these ‘sources’ might be, they are not the Commission itself. The Commission would therefore like to clarify that it does not agree with this comment, which it regards as an incorrect interpretation of its views by undisclosed, and thus unverifiable sources.

The Commission considers the Final Report as a thoroughly professional document accurately reflecting the findings of the Union Election Observation Mission which was conducted in full compliance with internationally accepted criteria used for election observation. The document raises a number of serious concerns on the election process in Pakistan and sets out useful recommendations on how to improve this process. The Commission is in the process of examining what concrete measures will be taken to follow up on the report’s findings.

The Election Observation Mission report has in the meantime been distributed to the Pakistan authorities and the Member States. It has also been published on the Commission website.
