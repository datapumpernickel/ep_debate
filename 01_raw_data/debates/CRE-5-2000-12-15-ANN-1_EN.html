Questions to the Council
Question no 23 by Nicholas Clegg (H-0921/00)
 Subject: Better regulation
 

What role does the Council envisage for the European Parliament in the high-level consultative group on Better Regulation established at the meeting of Ministers of Public Administration of 7 November 2000?

What plans if any, are afoot, to create an independent agency capable of carrying out assessments of the impact of draft EU proposals before their formal adoption?

 
 

Question no 24 by Glyn Ford (H-0938/00)
 Subject: Better regulation
 

Following the meeting of Ministers of Public Administration held in Strasbourg on 7 November 2000, when will the Council ask the European Parliament to appoint a representative to serve on the high-level consultative group on better regulation?

Can the Council say what steps it plans to take in order to assess the financial implications of EU proposals before their formal adoption?

 
  
 

With regard to the question asked by Mr Clegg, I must point out that the Council has no position on the high-level consultative group referred to by the honourable Member in his question. The meeting he mentioned was a multilateral ministerial meeting, not a Council session.

I would add that the Council is not aware of any plans to create an independent agency capable of carrying out assessments of the impact of draft EU legislative proposals before their formal adoption. Such assessments are already an integral part of the Council's consideration of draft proposals.

My answer to Mr Ford's question is that the Council does not have the power to ask the European Parliament to appoint a representative to serve on the consultative group.

I can nevertheless assure the honourable Member that the Council is going to look into ways of assessing the financial implications of EU proposals within the context of a coordinated strategy, on the basis of the proposals which the Commission presents in 2001.

 

Question no 25 by Astrid Thors (H-0924/00)
 Subject: Shortcoming in the Romanian democratic system
 

The Commission's progress report on the applicant countries points out that a shortcoming of the Romanian democratic system is that so much of the incorporation of Community legislation into national law is done by way of decrees or urgent decrees.

Does the Council share the Commission's view that this is an infelicitous method by which to incorporate Community legislation into national law?

 
  
 

In December 1997, the Luxembourg European Council established the principle that the applicant countries' respect for the Copenhagen political criteria - namely stable institutions guaranteeing democracy, the rule of law, human rights and the respect for and protection of minorities - is a prior condition for the opening of any accession negotiations. The fact that, in December 1999, the Helsinki European Council decided to open negotiations with Romania, amongst other countries, indicates that the European Union considered that, generally speaking, Romania meets these criteria.

This does not mean that there is nothing left to do. The most recent Commission regular report, presented last November, highlights a whole series of areas in which Romania needs to make substantial progress. Some of these areas relate to the Copenhagen political criteria, and these include the issue of increased recourse to ordinances, as mentioned by the honourable Member.

It is true that the regular report, as such, is only the Commission’s opinion. However, the General Affairs Council held on 4 and 5 December has just adopted conclusions on the progress of the enlargement process, in which it welcomes the quality of the information transmitted by the Commission and considers that the regular reports are a necessary tool for monitoring each candidate country's preparations for accession. Clearly, therefore, the Commission will follow extremely closely the progress made by Romania in the future in the areas covered by the Commission regular report. The Union has the necessary tools to do this, in particular within the framework of the European Association Agreement and its bodies.

Question no 27 by Nelly Maes (H-0931/00)
 Subject: The EU and the conflict in Congo (DRC)
 

There is considerable uncertainty about the task allotted to Mr Ajello, whose name has been mentioned by the Council as a ‘facilitator’ in Congo. Questions are being raised in particular about the purpose of his work. Does the Council have any arguments which would justify prolonging Mr Ajello’s mandate?

Mr Ketumile Masire, appointed by the Council to help save the inter-Congolese dialogue, is regarded in the DRC as a ‘complicator’ rather than as a ‘facilitator’. What arguments does the Council have for keeping him in this job?

Regarding the resumption of structural aid, can the Council say whether political problems between the Member States have been eliminated, and when the ‘Programming Document’ (National Indicative Programme) can be signed?

What is the Council’s point of view regarding the aggression by Rwanda and Uganda? Why has the Council not yet condemned this aggression? Is restoring the integrity of the DRC, and of the other states involved in the conflict, one of the Council’s political priorities?

 
  
 

Mr Aldo Ajello was appointed European Union special envoy in the African Great Lakes region in 1996. His assignment was to support efforts to create the necessary conditions for the lasting, peaceful resolution of the crises affecting the region, by means which would include preparing an international conference on peace, stability, democracy and development in the African Great Lakes region.

During its debate on the conflicts in Africa on 22 May 2000, the Council declared that it actively supported Mr Ajello's work. Each year, the Council has voted unanimously to extend his mandate.

In view of the current state of the peace process in the DRC and in Burundi, and the many summits and meetings (Lusaka, Windhoek, Kinshasa, Tripoli, Maputo I and II, Arusha) which have taken place in recent weeks, and in view of the role which the special envoy can play in mediating and providing information, the Council feels completely justified in extending Mr Ajello's mandate.

It was the Congolese concerned (government, armed and non-violent opposition) who appointed Mr Ketumile Masire "facilitator" of the inter-Congolese dialogue. On 22 May 2000, the Council stated that it supported the inter-Congolese facilitator politically. Moreover, it welcomed the support already promised or provided by the Member States and the Commission and called upon them to examine the possibility of lending the facilitator further financial and logistic support. It is not for the Council to decide whether Mr Ketumile should continue in this role.

Finally, with regard to the resumption of structural aid to the Democratic Republic of Congo, the European Development Fund Committee recently issued a favourable opinion on the strategy document proposed by the Commission. It will hence be possible to open negotiations with the Congolese authorities with a view to signing a National Indicative Programme.

In its statement of 22 September 2000, the European Union called upon the governments of Uganda and Rwanda to enforce United Nations Security Council Resolution 1304 immediately and to implement the proposals for redeploying troops in the DRC.

The restoration of the integrity of the DRC and the other States involved in the conflict is a priority policy for the Council. The EU's main objective remains the creation of conditions which will make it possible to achieve lasting resolution of the conflicts affecting the region. This resolution will, of necessity, have to lay down respect for international law and international principles, security guarantees for all people, an end to impunity and respect for democracy, the rule of law and human rights.

 

Question no 28 by Marialiese Flemming (H-0934/00)
 Subject: Common European safety standards for nuclear power stations
 

What steps has the Council so far taken towards drafting common European safety standards for nuclear power stations for all Member States of the European Union?

The Council has set up a working party which will apparently be calling for a high-level group of experts to be established. What progress has been made towards this end?

Can the Council envisage not only that safety issues concerning nuclear power stations may be the subject of harmonisation but also that, in spite of the various technologies applied by the individual Member States, uniform and legally binding safety standards may be applied?

 
  
 

The Euratom Treaty does not contain any specific legal basis for drawing up safety standards for nuclear power stations and the Council has not to date been informed of any Commission proposal on the matter. The monitoring and regulation of safety are the responsibility of the Member States under any international obligations to which they may have committed themselves such as the International Convention on Nuclear Safety. All the Member States of the Community are parties to this Convention and have established a national nuclear safety monitoring system in their country.

With regard to the creation of a high-level group of experts referred to by the honourable Member, the creation of high-level and other groups lies within the remit of the Council and, where appropriate, Coreper, under Rule 19(5) of the Council's Rules of Procedure. The only group dealing with nuclear matters on the official list of authorised groups is the Atomic Questions Group.

 

Question no 29 by Ioannis Marinos (H-0937/00)
 Subject: Inspection of meat imported into Greece
 

The Greek press reports that there are only 250 public health inspectors in Greece, whereas the country needs at least 1000 in order to safeguard consumers' health as Greece is a net importer of meat products from within and outside the European Union. In addition to the shortage of personnel, allegations have emerged that inspections of meat and other imported produce are inadequate as they are carried out on a five-day basis and only during civil servants' eight-hour working day, whereas the largest quantities are imported at times when no inspection is possible. The Greek State Secretary for Agriculture acknowledged the laxity of the inspections when he referred to 'occasional' checks being carried out and only on weekdays.

What action will the Commission take to compel the Greek authorities to increase the number of staff and inspections to ensure that public health in Greece is effectively protected? Are there other Member States in which inspections of meat are carried out only five days a week and during office hours and, if so, which? In which Member States are inspections carried out every day and at any time that consignments are imported?

 
  
 

The Council would like to remind the honourable Member that veterinary inspections on imports from third countries are governed by Directive 97/78/EEC in the case of products and Directive 91/496/EEC in the case of animals, and by Directive 89/662/EEC (products) and Directive 90/425/EEC (animals), in the case of intra-Community trade.

The implementation of these provisions is the responsibility of the Member States. Within this framework, the organisation of controls in each State is the responsibility of that State alone.

In accordance with the above-mentioned directives on imports, the Commission is obliged, by means of the Food and Veterinary Office, to carry out control and inspection operations at border posts in order to verify:

- the application of inspection rules by the national authorities, with the aim of ensuring the uniformity of that application,

- the effectiveness of action at border posts, with regard, in particular, to the existence of the necessary infrastructures.

 

Question no 30 by Konstantinos Alyssandrakis (H-0942/00)
 Subject: Hunger strike by political prisoners in Turkey
 

816 political prisoners in Turkish jails began a hunger strike on 20 October 2000. 100 of those decided 30 days into the strike to continue until death unless their legitimate demands were met. The mothers of the political prisoners and solidarity committees in various European countries have also embarked on hunger strikes in sympathy. In Turkey and other European countries, protest demonstrations have been held in solidarity with the strikers and their demands, which are for human living conditions in Turkish prisons, the protection of prisoners' fundamental rights and refusal to be transferred to modern, 'white', F-type isolation cells, etc.

Will the Council call on the Turkish authorities to respect the prisoners' rights, stop torture, ensure that they receive medical treatment, discontinue the torment of isolation and their transfer to F-type cells and, in general, put an end to persecution for 'political' offences?

 
  
 

Since May 2000, the Human Rights Committee of the Turkish National Assembly has published new reports on the conditions in Turkish prisons and police stations. These reports indicate improvements that have been made between 1998 and 2000, particularly with regard to the attitudes of police and prison staff. They also strongly criticise the absence of monitoring and inspection of the system by governors and prosecutors. However, the prison conditions are still worrying. The fact that this debate has begun in Turkey is an encouraging sign.

It is also notable that the authorities are currently carrying out a restructuring of the entire prison system (the construction of a new type of prison, the gradual compliance with international minimum standards, collaboration with the Committee for the Prevention of Torture).

The Council has always been sensitive to the situation in Turkish prisons. This issue is included in the Accession Partnership which was the subject of a political agreement with the Council on 4 December 2000. The improvement of prison conditions is included in the short- and medium-term priorities and objectives within the framework of enhanced political dialogue and political criteria. The guarantee of the right to freedom of opinion and freedom of expression, in relation to political and religious issues, is also part of the short- and medium-term political criteria. Of course, the Council will remain vigilant with regard to all of these points.

Question no 32 by Per Gahrton (H-0946/00)
 Subject: 133 Committee
 

There is much concern today in civil society about the so-called 133 Committee and how the EU position in the WTO negotiations is decided by civil servants in a very non-transparent manner.

The following demands to improve this process have been made by NGOs and civil society movements:

- the European Parliament should be involved in trade negotiations and fully and formally informed about the activities of the European Commission and the 133 Committee;

- codecision procedures should be applied so that the Council and European Parliament share decision-making on trade and investment;

- the Article 133 Committee should be required to make all documents publicly available, ensure representation of a fair and balanced viewpoint that includes sustainability, and be open to parliamentarians.

What is the Council's view of these demands?

 
  
 

The Council would remind you that decisions concerning common trade policy, including the European Union’s positions relating to that policy in the WTO negotiations, are made by the Council, in accordance with Article 133 of the Treaty, on the basis of the Commission’s proposals.

The Council is aware of the demands of the European Parliament on the subject of the decision-making procedures relating to common trade policy. These procedures were examined within the framework of the Intergovernmental Conference. At the Nice European Council of 7-10 December, it was finally decided not to alter the procedures in this regard.

As for the remaining issues, the changes made in Nice to Article 133, on the extension of qualified majority voting in the field of services, continue to include the case of cultural and audiovisual services, of health and of education, which remain the shared responsibility of the Member States and the Community.

With regard to public access to Council documents, we would remind you that that access is the subject of Council Decision 93/731/EC of 20 December 1993, amended by means of Council Decisions 96/705/EC of 6 December 1996, and 2000/527/EC of 14 August 2000, which lay down the cases in which protection must be maintained, particularly in the commercial and industrial fields.

Question no 42 by Per Gahrton (H-0947/00)
 Subject: 133 Committee
 

There is much concern today in civil society about the so-called 133 Committee and how the EU position in the WTO negotiations is decided by civil servants in a very non-transparent manner.

The following demands to improve this process have been made by NGOs and civil society movements:

the European Parliament should be involved in trade negotiations and fully and formally informed about the activities of the European Commission and the 133 Committee;

codecision procedures should be applied so that the Council and European Parliament share decision-making on trade and investment;

- the Article 133 Committee should be required to make all documents publicly available, ensure representation of a fair and balanced viewpoint that includes sustainability, and be open to parliamentarians.

What is the Commission's view of these demands?

 
  
 

The Commission shares the desire to increase the transparency of common trade policy and democratic control by Parliament.

In fact, the involvement of Parliament not only conforms to the concept of European construction but is also a necessary supplement to the democratic control which national parliaments exercise over the governments of the Member States. Furthermore, it strengthens the Community’s negotiation position with regard to third countries. In fact, those countries know that the Commission negotiates with the full support of the democratically elected representatives of the European citizens.

That is why, as the honourable Member will know, the Commission had proposed the revision of Article 133 within the framework of the current Intergovernmental Conference. In particular, its proposal, which is supported by the European Parliament, intended Parliament to be closely involved in all the stages of commercial policy.

Unfortunately, these proposals have not been accepted by the Intergovernmental Conference. The Commission must therefore continue to do its best to ensure that Parliament is closely involved while respecting, of course, the framework laid down in the Treaty.

With regard to the Article 133 Committee, the Commission would remind you that this is a Council committee. The Commission therefore has no power to decide which documents debated in that committee can be made public. That committee’s transparency rules, with regard to other institutions and the public, depend on the decision of the Council.

In relation to its own documents, the Commission applies a great degree of transparency in its relations with Parliament.

 

Question no 50 by Niall Andrews (H-0914/00)
 Subject: International banking agreement on money laundering
 

At the end of October, 11 banks signed an agreement aimed at preventing criminal organisations from using banks to launder money and in so doing agreed a code of conduct which will place the burden of responsibility for checking the authenticity of the money they receive on the banks. Private bankers in Geneva have dismissed the idea as a public relations exercise. What is the Commission's response to this new code and does it consider that such an initiative could have helped to thwart the efforts of former Nigerian dictator Sani Abacha to launder almost £3 billion in UK and other banks, and will it request that the results of the investigation by the (London) City's Financial Services Authority into the destination of part of these funds is made public and, where possible, followed up at EU level?

 
  
 

The Commission is aware of, and welcomes, the Global Anti-Money Laundering Guidelines for Private Banking (also known as the "Wolfsberg Principles"), which were endorsed by 11 leading international banks at the end of October.

This appears to be a significant initiative given the public commitment of the prominent banks which have signed up to the principles and the involvement of Transparency International and respected international experts. The Guidelines pay particular attention to the risks involved in providing private banking services to high net-worth individuals, certain of whom may be public officials or have close links with such officials. The Commission has no information on the views of other private bankers. However, it believes that there is an increasingly wide international consensus that all countries and the whole of the financial sector must participate actively in the global fight against money laundering.

Having originated as part of the effort to combat drugs trafficking, the campaign against money laundering has recently become increasingly broad-based as regards the underlying criminality the proceeds of which are targeted.

Within the Community, the Common Position adopted by the Council on 30 November 2000 on the proposal to update and extend the 1991 anti-money laundering directive requires the Member States to oblige their financial sector and other professions to report their suspicions of the laundering of a wider range of serious crime, including corruption.

The Commission believes that this extension, together with the publicity given to cases of high-level corruption and stolen public assets, and the resulting public revulsion at such cases, will make banks even more careful as regards the origin of their clients' funds. Failure to apply adequate know-your-customer principles and to ask the necessary questions expose them not only to a serious reputational risk but also to disciplinary measures or even prosecution.

With regard to the Abacha case in particular, the Commission is aware that the British Financial Services Authority (FSA) is carrying out an investigation into the procedures followed at the banks which appear to have handled the funds in question. The FSA has stated that remedial action will be ordered where problems are found and that any evidence of criminal activity will be passed to the relevant criminal prosecution authorities.

 

Question no 51 by Michael Cashman (H-0943/00)
 Subject: Counterfeiting of ceramics
 

What measures is the Commission considering with a view to combating the counterfeiting of Ceramics?

 
  
 

Counterfeiting is of concern to the Commission because it has negative consequences on the proper functioning of the Single Market, including on innovation, employment and competitiveness. In addition, it misleads consumers and, in certain cases, puts public health and safety at risk. It also creates a barrier for European exports and investment in third countries.

To prevent counterfeit goods from being placed on the Community market, the Commission has taken a number of initiatives aiming to ensure the effective protection of intellectual property rights. These initiatives are general in nature and do not apply to particular types of products.

As regards the control of the external border of the Community, Council Regulation (EC) No 3295/94, as amended by Council Regulation (EC) No 241/1999, laying down measures concerning the entry into the Community and the export and re-export from the Community of goods infringing certain intellectual property rights, aims to prevent the passage of counterfeit and pirated goods through the external borders of the Community. This Regulation allows the national customs authorities to suspend the release of goods suspected of being counterfeit or pirated while the right holder instigates infringement proceedings before the national court.

As regards the Internal Market, the Commission adopted on 30 November 2000 a follow-up communication to its 1998 Green Paper on Combating Counterfeiting and Piracy in the Single Market. This Communication contains an Action Plan announcing a series of practical measures aiming to improve and strengthen the fight against this phenomenon in the single Market. This Action Plan will be implemented progressively and will involve all the parties concerned. As part of these measures, the Commission will put forward a proposal for a Directive harmonising the legislation of Member States on the means of enforcing intellectual property rights.

Within the World Trade Organisation (WTO) framework, the Community monitors the way in which WTO members implement the agreement on Trade-Related aspects of Intellectual Property (TRIPS), which establishes minimum standards of protection and lays down procedures for ensuring their effective enforcement. The Community also negotiates bilateral agreements with WTO and non-WTO members, which require a high level of protection and fighting against infringements.

 

Question no 52 by Mihail Papayannakis (H-0861/00)
 Subject: Revision of Article 24 of the Greek Constitution
 

A process is under way in Greece to revise Article 24 of the country's constitution concerning the protection of the natural environment with the inherent risk that this will pave the way for the removal of the protected status of woodland and its use for building. Obviously, the revision of a country's constitution is a matter solely for that country but, by ratifying the Treaty of Amsterdam (Articles 2, 174, 152 and 153), Greece has made certain specific commitments in this respect. Furthermore, there is a risk that the ostensible 'improvement' of Article 24 might nullify the 5th action programme on the environment and sustainable development, the 1992 Rio Declaration, certain specific provisions of Greek national legislation as well as international and Community rules incorporated into that legislation at a time when woodland in Greece is becoming increasingly scarce among the chaos already typical of town and country planning in Greece.

In the light of these circumstances, does the Commission consider that the revision of this controversial article – which has provoked a storm of protests from environmental organisations, associations, other bodies and eminent legal experts – is consistent with its environmental protection policy, and will it make recommendations to the relevant Greek authorities to persuade them to respect the obligations which they themselves have entered into, and the conclusions adopted as the outcome of various international procedures?

 
  
 

Constitutional revision is the exclusive competence of the Member States. The Commission may not interfere with the revision process of Article 24 of the Greek Constitution relating to the protection of the environment. However the Commission is following the matter with great interest and is confident that Article 24 if revised will not deviate from the sustainable development and integration principles enshrined in the EC Treaty. The Commission does not consider it necessary or appropriate to make recommendations to the Greek Government.

 

Question no 53 by Piia-Noora Kauppi (H-0862/00)

According to the Spanish airline pilots’ union SEPLA, numerous Spanish airports suffer from serious safety failings. At fifteen airports the technical equipment is defective, and SEPLA claims that in individual cases it is at a level more typical of developing countries. The landing radio equipment does not work, the runway lights are hardly visible and there are no fog lights at all.

This action brought together experts from 17 countries and resulted in substantial contributions (as regards research and standardisation) in Europe and beyond (e.g. in the WHO). The latest projects have recently been concluded, and their results will be given wide dissemination. A follow-up is being prepared. Two studies on the links between electromagnetic radiation and cancer have also been financed under the BIOMED 2 programme.

Under the Measurements and Testing Programme, a project was financed for measuring electromagnetic radiation from mobile telephones and masts and quantifying human exposure to radiation. The results of this project will facilitate the establishment of acceptability standards for appliances, and will also contribute to determining the degree of comparability of the results of the various research efforts now under way into risk assessment.

The total budget devoted by the Community to these studies was approximately EUR 2 million. None of the studies (which included the thermal effects of energy absorption and possible disturbances of metabolism from the various types of electromagnetic field) has pointed to adverse effects on health; nonetheless, additional results are needed if public concern is to be allayed in full.

The subject of electromagnetic radiation has therefore been specifically included in the fifth framework programme, within the working programme of key action 4 (environment and health) of strand 1 (quality of life and management of living resources). Following the 1999 call for proposals, five proposals were accepted involving total funding of approximately EUR 9 million. These projects, essentially concerning the potential carcinogenic effects of radio frequencies, got off the ground in early 2000 and are expected to bear fruit in three or four years' time.

These projects include a large-scale epidemiological study (INTERPHONE), coordinated by the International Centre for Cancer Research and aimed at determining whether electromagnetic radiation from mobile telephones can cause cancer. The other projects are aimed at improving understanding of the biological mechanisms by which electromagnetic radiation can influence human health (and, in particular, cause cancer): the means employed include using test batteries in both experiments on live mice and rats and in an in-vitro context (new molecular biology techniques).

The studies under way, which are targeted primarily at potential carcinogenic effects, will be supplemented by the 2001 call for proposals under the key action for the environment and health. This will be specifically targeted at quantifying exposure to electromagnetic radiation and at other potential effects on health. It will make it possible to obtain scientific data which can be directly used for improving risk assessment.

The text of the Council recommendation adopted on 12 July 19991(2) on the limitation of exposure of the general public to electromagnetic fields (from 0 Hz to 300 GHz) includes a provision for updating within five years of its adoption, subject to the absence of any new scientific evidence demonstrating a health risk (where this has not been taken into account).

The Commission is nonetheless continuously following the results of the latest scientific studies, as well as the opinions of its Scientific Steering Committee, to ensure that it can, at any moment, take the legislative action required to guarantee continued high standards of health protection for the citizens of the Community.

 Subject: Imports into the EU of products from occupied Palestinian territories
 

During the recent visit of a delegation of Parliament's United Left Group to the Palestinian territories and Israel, allegations were made to members of the delegation that products produced in the occupied Palestinian territories are exported by Israel as Israeli products and illegally imported into the EU in breach of the association agreement.

Are these allegations founded and, if so, what measures will the Commission take to stop such imports?

 
  
 

In May 1998, the Commission transmitted a communication to the Council and Parliament on the question of implementation of rules of origin by Israel. In particular, the Commission raised the question of goods originating from Israeli settlements in West Bank and Gaza.

As explained in the communication, Israeli settlements or occupied territories cannot be considered by the Community as part of Israeli territory for implementation of the EC-Israel agreement.

About 1600 certificates of origin (EUR 1) have been sent to Israel's custom authorities by Member States' custom authorities. The Association Agreement between the Community and Israel foresees that Israel’s custom authorities have up to 10 months to send an answer to Member States custom authorities.
