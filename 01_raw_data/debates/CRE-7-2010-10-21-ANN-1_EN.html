Question no 10 by Rodi Kratsa-Tsagaropoulou(H-0488/10)
 Subject: Basel III criteria and SMEs
 

On 12 September 2010, the Basel Committee announced new criteria for strengthening the stability of the financial system(1). These criteria, however, may result in the big banking groups taking over smaller banks which have traditionally provided finance for small and medium-sized enterprises (SMEs), which account for 99% of European businesses(2). How does the Council see the financial market and its impact on the financing of productive activities developing? Given the conditions in which the European economy is operating, has the Council drawn up a plan to support the financing of SMEs in order to offset any negative impact on liquidity and lending resulting from the Basel III criteria? To what extent are the banks prepared to meet those criteria?

 
  
 

The present answer, which has been drawn up by the Presidency and is not binding on either the Council or its members as such, was not presented orally at Question Time to the Council during the October 2010 part-session of the European Parliament in Strasbourg.

(FR) SMEs in the European Union are especially dependent on financing through the banks, which has been affected by the economic crisis. Indeed in Europe, the percentage of financing of SMEs and other entities through the banks as opposed to through the capital markets is much higher than in the United States. Some estimates place this percentage as high as 80% of all financing of the economy.

Various bodies, including the ECB and the Commission, are continuing to monitor the finance being given to the economy overall and to SMEs in particular, and regularly inform the Council on the matter. The European Central Bank has recommended among other things that the financial system should transfer funding obtained from the Member States to help it ride out the crisis to the real economy and to SMEs in particular. This recommendation was made against the backdrop of the banks also needing to clean up and strengthen their balance sheets in order to meet the increased capital requirements of the new Basel Accord announced on 12 September 2010.

The mission of the Basel Committee, which is made up of representatives of the central banks and financial regulatory authorities of 27 countries in the world, is to increase the stability and reliability of the financial system. The Committee should be submitting a series of measures to the G20 Heads of State or Government in Seoul on 11 and 12 November and has announced an impact assessment for the end of the year. Under these circumstances, it is still too early to reach a valid judgment about the long-term impact that the increased capital requirements will have on the development of the financial markets and their repercussions on financing for production activities and for SMEs in particular.

Politically speaking, the Basel Accord is crucial, but it is not legally binding. Each member of the Basel Committee therefore needs to implement it whilst taking into consideration its own limitations and situation. In the European Union, the new standards will apply to all credit establishments, not just to the major international banks as in the United States; hence the need for a detailed study before the precise figures are set. The European Commission has announced that its legislative proposal for implementation of the new Basel Accord, ‘CRD 4’ (Capital Requirements Directive 4), will in turn be accompanied by impact assessments, which will address two aspects. The first will be an assessment of the impact on the banking sector as an industry and the second an assessment to determine the anticipated overall impact on the economy.

The Council will be examining the Commission’s CRD 4 proposal, which is expected by the end of the first quarter of 2011, as soon as it is presented, and hopes there will be fruitful collaboration with the European Parliament with a view to prompt adoption. This will provide the best possible conditions for the EU’s credit institutions to prepare themselves for when the new rules come into force.

Question no 11 by Bernd Posselt(H-0492/10)
 Subject: EU - Council of Europe
 

What action is the Council taking to expedite projected EU accession to the Council of Europe's Convention on Human Rights and does it consider EU accession to the Framework Convention for the Protection of National Minorities and the European Charter for Regional or Minority Languages to be on the cards?

 
  
 

The present answer, which has been drawn up by the Presidency and is not binding on either the Council or its members as such, was not presented orally at Question Time to the Council during the October 2010 part-session of the European Parliament in Strasbourg.

(FR) Work began on the European Union’s accession to the European Convention for the Protection of Human Rights and Fundamental Freedoms (ECHR) on 17 March 2010 with the presentation of the Commission’s recommendation for opening the negotiations on accession. Parliament stated its position in a resolution of 19 May 2010, which among other things called on the Belgian and Hungarian Presidencies to do their utmost to finalise the accession at the earliest suitable opportunity and insisted that Parliament should be closely involved in the preliminary discussions and also in the conduct of negotiations on that text, in accordance with the provisions of Article 218 of the Treaty on the Functioning of the European Union. The negotiating mandate and the negotiating directives were adopted by a Council decision of 4 June 2010.

By the same decision, the Council nominated the Commission as negotiator. Under the terms of the decision, the Commission shall conduct the negotiations in consultation with the Working Party on Fundamental Rights, Citizens’ Rights and Free Movement of Persons (FREMP), as the special committee appointed in accordance with Article 218(4) TFEU. Through the negotiator’s reports, the Council follows the progress of the negotiations, which are held in Strasbourg.

The first working meeting of the informal working group of the Council of Europe’s Steering Committee for Human Rights (CDDH) on the accession of the European Union to the ECHR was held in Strasbourg on 6 and 7 July 2010. A second meeting was held in Strasbourg from 20 to 22 September 2010. Following that meeting, the working group drew up a draft list of issues to be discussed regarding the EU’s accession. The special committee (FREMP) was kept informed of both meetings.

As regards the matter of accession by the European Union to the Framework Convention for the Protection of National Minorities and the European Charter for Regional or Minority Languages, the Council has not received any recommendations on this matter from the Commission.

 

Question no 12 by Brian Crowley(H-0494/10)
 Subject: Development of a European patent
 

What concrete actions is the Council undertaking to ensure the development of a European patent?

 
  
 

The present answer, which has been drawn up by the Presidency and is not binding on either the Council or its members as such, was not presented orally at Question Time to the Council during the October 2010 part-session of the European Parliament in Strasbourg.

(FR) The Council considers that enhancing the patent system in Europe is a necessary prerequisite for boosting growth through innovation and for helping European businesses, in particular SMEs, face the economic crisis and international competition.

Such an enhanced patent system is a vital element of the internal market and should be based on two pillars: the creation of a European Union patent (hereafter ‘EU patent’) and the setting up of an integrated specialised and unified jurisdiction for patent-related disputes, thus improving the enforcement of patents and enhancing legal certainty.

In December 2009, the Council reached an agreement on its general position on the future EU patent regulation. As regards the language arrangements for the EU patent, the Council is awaiting the European Parliament’s opinion on the Commission’s proposal, and in the meantime is working hard to reach an agreement as quickly as possible. The subject was recently debated at the Council session of 11 October, and we will be returning to it soon.

As regards the second pillar, the Council has submitted a draft treaty establishing a European and EU Patents Court to the European Court of Justice for its opinion on the compatibility of the text with the Treaties. The Court’s opinion is expected within the next few months.

 

Question no 13 by Pat the Cope Gallagher(H-0496/10)
 Subject: Promotion of tourism
 

Can the Council outline what actions the EU is undertaking to promote Europe as a destination of choice for international tourists?

 
  
 

The present answer, which has been drawn up by the Presidency and is not binding on either the Council or its members as such, was not presented orally at Question Time to the Council during the October 2010 part-session of the European Parliament in Strasbourg.

(FR) Since the European Union was given no express remit in the field of tourism, its jurisdiction has been very limited in this area, which explains the fairly restricted scope of past action by the Commission and the Council to promote Europe as a destination of choice for international tourists.

With the entry into force of the Treaty of Lisbon, the new Article 195 TFEU stipulates that the European Union may complement the action of the Member States in the tourism sector, by promoting the competitiveness of undertakings in that sector and in particular by encouraging the creation of a favourable environment for their development and by promoting cooperation between Member States, particularly by the exchange of good practice. Nonetheless, this provision excludes any harmonisation of the laws and regulations of the Member States. Furthermore, any measures taken in this area must comply with the principles of subsidiarity and proportionality as set out in the Treaty.

On the basis of the above-mentioned provision, on 6 July 2010 the Commission published a communication entitled ‘Europe, the world’s No 1 tourist destination, a new political framework for tourism in Europe’.

In response to this Commission paper, the Council decided that action to support tourism could be grouped under the four priority areas set out in the Commission communication, which are respectively:

a) to stimulate competitiveness in the European tourism sector;

b) to promote the development of sustainable, responsible and high-quality tourism;

c) to consolidate the image and profile of Europe as a collection of sustainable, high-quality destinations;

d) to maximise the potential of existing EU financial policies and instruments for developing tourism.

The Council also asked the Commission to gather further evidence, consult widely, and analyse the European and plurinational added value of the actions and initiatives addressing the key issues mentioned above.

 

Question no 14 by Liam Aylward(H-0498/10)
 Subject: Young people in the EU
 

Since the economic crisis the most vulnerable people in today’s society are young people. The unemployment rate among young people has seen a sharp increase and the likelihood is that many will not succeed in securing permanent employment. The EU Youth Strategy recognises that creating new and better opportunities in the education sector and on the job market is a priority. How does the Council plan on achieving this? How much does the Council know about the progress that has been made on the structural dialogue on employment and youth (youth@work)?

 
  
 

The present answer, which has been drawn up by the Presidency and is not binding on either the Council or its members as such, was not presented orally at Question Time to the Council during the October 2010 part-session of the European Parliament in Strasbourg.

(FR) Youth employment is one of the main overarching themes for European cooperation on youth for the 18 months of the Trio Presidency.

The Belgian Presidency aims to take inspiration from the Spanish Presidency’s initiative, which led to the adoption by the Council of a resolution on the active inclusion of young people, establishing common principles in this area and stating that social and economic exclusion and all forms of discrimination are obstacles to the well-being of young people and may hinder their active participation in society and in the labour market. This resolution also integrated the ‘youth’ perspective into other policy fields such as education and employment, and the Belgian Presidency aims to continue along these lines by proposing a resolution on socio-educational activities and by calling upon the Council to adopt this resolution at its November session.

The proposed resolution will acknowledge in particular the role that socio-educational activities can play by providing opportunities for young people to develop a wide range of different personal and vocational skills that will help their employment prospects.

On 21 October 2010, the employment guidelines will also be presented to the Council for adoption. These guidelines, which will form one of the principal instruments for implementing the Europe 2020 strategy, emphasise the importance of targeting young people in the Member States’ employment and educational policies. Guideline 8 in particular states that to support young people and in particular those not in employment, education or training (NEET), Member States, in cooperation with the social partners, should enact schemes to help those people find initial employment, job experience, or further education and training opportunities, including apprenticeships, and should intervene rapidly when young people become unemployed.

Furthermore, the Presidency wishes to draw attention to the fact that continuing on from the Communication on Youth on the Move, published recently by the Commission, which proposes various measures aimed at achieving the goals of the Europe 2020 strategy (including the goal of improving employment for young people), the Council will be called upon during its November session to adopt an integrated approach (on education, youth and employment) which addresses the various socioeconomic challenges currently facing young people.

 

Question no 15 by Richard Howitt(H-0503/10)
 Subject: Human rights in the OECD Guidelines on Multinational Enterprises: Review
 

Following the Council conclusions of December 2009 endorsing the recommendations of the UN Secretary-General’s Special Representative on Business and Human Rights, will the Council fully endorse the proposal for there to be a new human rights chapter in the updated OECD Guidelines on Multinational Enterprise, based on the recommendations of Special Representative Ruggie?

Will the President-in-Office undertake consultation with other Member States with a view to supporting the paper submitted to the OECD on 30 June 2010, by Special Representative Ruggie, in which he stated that the human rights responsibilities of business go beyond first and second tier suppliers to where our businesses ‘impact’ on the world?

 
  
 

The present answer, which has been drawn up by the Presidency and is not binding on either the Council or its members as such, was not presented orally at Question Time to the Council during the October 2010 part-session of the European Parliament in Strasbourg.

(FR) Last December, in the context of its conclusions on human rights and democratisation in third countries, the Council emphasised the important role of business in achieving full respect for human rights. In this context, the Council expressed its support for the work of Professor Ruggie, the UN Special Representative on Human Rights and Transnational Corporations and other Business Enterprises.

The European Union recently reaffirmed its support for the Special Representative’s work when presenting its latest report to the Human Rights Council in Geneva in June 2010. The EU also welcomed the Special Representative’s plans to produce a set of guidelines in 2011 for the implementation of his conceptual framework ‘Protect, Respect and Remedy’ and to examine the possibility of creating an instrument within the United Nations to promote this framework. This conceptual framework is based on three fundamental principles: the state duty to protect against human rights abuses by third parties, including business; the corporate responsibility to respect human rights; and the need for greater access to effective remedy, both judicial and non-judicial. These three principles constitute a set of complementary aspects which work together to produce lasting progress.

As members of the OECD, some of the EU Member States are taking part in that organisation’s work on modernising its guidelines and making them more effective, and they generally welcomed Professor Ruggie’s proposals, including his proposal to add a specific section on human rights and the principle of due diligence. According to this principle, to avoid infringing the rights of others, companies must exercise due diligence in the area of human rights, which combines established corporate risk management practices and consideration for human rights aspects, in keeping with the fundamental criteria laid down by the United Nations.

As regards the EU Member States, the position to be adopted in the context of the OECD’s work is not coordinated between the Council’s working groups. However, the delegations within the Council are informed of the progress of this work, especially with regard to commercial aspects and aspects relating to investment.

 

Question no 16 by Laima Liucija Andrikienė(H-0506/10)
 Subject: EU representation in the UN and other international bodies
 

What is the Council’s strategy for ensuring that the recently appointed senior officials of the European Union, such as the President of the European Council, Herman Van Rompuy, and High Representative for Foreign Affairs and Security Policy Catherine Ashton, can address the UN General Assembly on behalf of the Union? After the entry into force of the Treaty of Lisbon, the President of the European Council, not the representative of the country holding the rotating presidency of the EU Council, should represent the EU at the head-of-state level internationally. What measures should be taken to implement these provisions of the Treaty of Lisbon in such international bodies as the UN?

 
  
 

The present answer, which has been drawn up by the Presidency and is not binding on either the Council or its members as such, was not presented orally at Question Time to the Council during the October 2010 part-session of the European Parliament in Strasbourg.

(FR) With the entry into force of the Treaty of Lisbon, the European Union (EU) replaced and succeeded the European Community (EC). The EU therefore inherited the status of observer at the United Nations General Assembly.

The status of observer at the United Nations General Assembly inherited by the EU carries relatively restricted rights, such as a limited degree of access to the list of speakers and certain procedural rights. These restricted rights have entailed a significant reduction in the EU’s action and profile compared with the situation prior to the entry into force of the Treaty of Lisbon, in which it was the Member State holding the Council Presidency which represented the EU. We must recognise that the EU is not able at present to fully exercise the role within the United Nations General Assembly that the Treaty on the Functioning of the European Union confers on it, nor can it promote the EU’s interests as effectively as it was able to do before the Treaty of Lisbon came into force.

Since the debate on the draft resolution on the EU’s participation in the work of the UN was postponed at the September meeting of the UN General Assembly, on 4 October the EU High Representative for Foreign Affairs and Security Policy decided to form a working party on the resolution with a view to consolidating, coordinating and supporting the work of the EU delegation to the United Nations, the work of other EU representatives in third countries and that of the services of the Commission and the Council Secretariat in Brussels, so as to establish the conditions that are required to obtain the additional rights needed for the EU to participate effectively in the work of the UN General Assembly. This working party is made up of officials from the Commission and the Council Secretariat.

The Foreign Affairs Council will be discussing a strategy on the matter on 25 October.

Question no 25 by Silvia-Adriana Ţicău(H-0467/10)
 Subject: State of implementation of the 'single European sky'
 

Regulation (EC) 1070/2009(1) on improving the performance and sustainability of the European aviation system requires Member States to take the necessary measures to ensure that a number of functional airspace blocks (FABs) are in operation by 4 December 2012, with a view to the necessary development of the efficiency and capacity of the air traffic control networks in the context of the 'single European sky', as well as maintaining high standards of safety, contributing to the global performance of the air transport system, and reducing its impact on the environment.

Commission Regulation (EU) No 691/2010(2) of 29 July 2010 lays down a performance scheme for air navigation services and network functions. Since the initial reference period begins on 1 January 2012, while on 12 August 2010 the Commission named a coordinator for the FAB system and has, in recent years, allocated significant funds from the TEN-T budget for the purpose of defining the FABs, can the Commission provide information on the state of implementation of the process of definition of the FABs and state whether it believes the 'single European sky' will be implemented on time?

 
  
 

(EN) With Functional Airspace Blocks (FABs), each Member State looks beyond national borders and provides air navigation services together with other Member States. FABs are key mechanisms of the Single European Sky (SES) to deliver the operational benefits of enhanced cooperation among air navigation service providers and, where appropriate, through an integrated provider. However, it only remains an intermediate step towards a true single European sky.

Under the SES II legislation Member States are obliged to establish FABs before 4 December 2012. Two FABs – in Sweden and Denmark and the United Kingdom and Ireland – have already been notified. Within the coming months the Commission expects the signature for the agreement on FABEC, which covers Belgium, France, Germany, Luxembourg, the Netherlands and Switzerland. To facilitate the negotiations between Member States cooperating in the same FAB, Mr Jarzembowski was appointed coordinator in August 2010.

However, more work is necessary to accelerate the Single European Sky and to meet the deadline.

To assist Member States, the Commission has established a tight work program and presented a number of regulatory measures. For example, the Commission Regulation laying down the performance scheme was adopted on 29 July 2010. This will be the vehicle to drive Member States to seek closer integration in the field of air navigation service provision while improving performance.

In addition, the Commission is now finalising its proposal for an implementing regulation on information requirements for the FAB establishment. Supporting material will also be finalised by 4 December 2010.

To conclude, the December 2012 deadline gives the Commission and the FAB coordinator strong leverage in the discussions with Member States. The Commission will then have to address possible issues if the December 2012 deadline is not met. However, at this stage the Commission is optimistic that the deadline will be met as the preparations are advancing well.

(2) OJ L 201, 3.8.2010, p. 1.

 

Question no 26 by Bernd Posselt(H-0493/10)
 Subject: Trans-European rail networks
 

What is the Commission's opinion concerning the timescale for completion of the individual sections of the main European Strasbourg-Salzburg rail link and how is this being affected by the situation regarding the Munich-Mühldorf-Freilassing line? Is it also intended to appoint a special TEN coordinator for Axis No 22, which is connected to it?

 
  
 

(EN) Priority Project 17 "Paris-Strasbourg-Stuttgart-Vienna-Bratislava" (PP17) is an east-west oriented railway axis crossing very densely populated areas in the centre of Europe. It stretches over 1,382 kilometres and crosses four Member States: France, Germany, Austria and Slovakia.

The Commission considers that with the nomination of EU-Coordinator Péter Balázs and after the signature of a declaration of intent by the Ministers for Transport of the four Member States on 9 June 2006 followed by several bilateral treaties on the cross-border sections, implementation has made good progress on most segments. The last French high-speed section should be operational in 2016; the new Vienna station will be opened in 2013; all passenger sections in Austria, designed for 200 kilometres per hour (km/h), should be operational until 2015 at the latest.

The Commission welcomes the finalisation of the works on the cross-border section between France and Germany. The Kehl Bridge will be opened on 10 December 2010 - and the ongoing works on the section Augsburg-Munich are scheduled for completion in December 2011.

But the Commission also has some concerns:

- works on the section Stuttgart-Ulm started in 2010. This section should be fully operational in December 2019, designed for a maximum speed of 250 km/h, although, as the Honourable Member is aware, the section in Stuttgart is also at the centre of a political debate;

- the connection to Munich Airport is still not financed although the Bavarian Government and the parliament (Landtag) recently agreed on a development concept for Munich ("Bahnkonzept München") to connect the railway line with the airport;

- although in July 2007 the German and the Austrian ministries of Transport agreed on jointly developing the cross-border section Freilassing-Salzburg to improve the capacity of this section, a final date for starting the works on the German side has yet to be confirmed by the German authorities;

- regarding the section Munich-Freilassing, a comprehensive approach for the whole section is expected to be a priority in the revised planning framework that the German Transport Minister is expected to present later during autumn 2010.

The Commission understands that infrastructure investments have to take account of available national resources, but equally investment in Trans-European Transport Network (TEN-T) projects is a collective commitment by all the Member States concerned: if one does not deliver its part of a link, the investments of the others are likely to be devalued. Thus the Commission will remind the German Government of its political commitments.

The Commission is happy to inform the Honourable Member that in June 2010 the Commission appointed Gilles Savary, a former prominent member of the European Parliament, as new European coordinator for Priority Project 22 (Athens–Nuremberg/Dresden). He will present his first report of his work on 27 October 2010 to the TRAN committee.

 

Question no 27 by Brian Crowley(H-0495/10)
 Subject: PSO funding for regional airports
 

Can the Commission make a statement outlining the benefits of Public Service Obligation (PSO) funding for regional airports in the European Union?

 
  
 

(EN) Regulation 1008/2008(1) allows the imposition of public service obligations (PSOs), for reasons of economic development or of territorial connection. PSOs are tools to ensure the access to isolated regions or to support regional development policy goals when the Member State finds that its objectives will not be adequately met by free play of the market forces.

These obligations are of a general nature (obligations in terms of frequencies, of capacities, of maximum prices etc…). If no operator undertakes the provision of the services, Member States can make recourse to an exclusive concession for only one operator chosen after a European call for tender. This exclusive concession can be with or without compensation, and its main objective is to effectively ensure that the regional development objectives are attained. When there is compensation, the beneficiary is the airline to whom the tender is awarded, not the airports involved.

Currently, PSOs are imposed on 200 routes within the EU. By ensuring that a minimum number of frequencies are operated on these routes, PSOs stimulate air traffic to and from regional airports. The Commission has not made a quantitative analysis of the economic impact of PSOs on regional airports, but PSOs by their nature incentivize air traffic at regional airports that would otherwise be served by less scheduled air services.

Question no 28 by Dominique Baudis(H-0500/10)
 Subject: The aeronautics industry and the White Paper on transport
 

Aeronautics is one of the rare success stories within the European venture. This sector, which provides employment for hundreds of thousands of skilled workers throughout the continent, is of key importance to our economy and countries. It acts as a real vector of technological progress and is an essential component in the mobility of our people.

What place does the Commission intend to give to this industrial sector in its forthcoming White Paper on transport?

 
  
 

(EN) Aeronautics directly employs half a million people and its yearly turnover approaches EUR 100 billion. Europe has a market share of over 38%. Therefore, this high tech industry is key for the EU.

The White Paper on the future of transport is not yet finalised and it would therefore be premature to say what it contains. In general, the aviation sector, the transport equipment industries and aeronautics will benefit from it.

First of all, the deployment of the technologies developed by SESAR (Single European Sky Air Traffic Management Research), in which many aeronautics manufacturers participate, will entail production and continuous efficiency improvements and therefore benefit the aeronautics industry. The completion of the Single European Sky will contribute to increasing air capacity with expected positive opportunities for air carriers and also for European aeronautics manufacturers.

A better integration of the transport modes, connecting air, rail and public transport, will contribute to reducing emissions linked to short haul feeding flights and to last mile connections with the cities.

Framework Programme 7 with 2.1 billion Euro,which includes the Clean Sky initiative (EUR 800 million), is already supporting the technological needs of the European industry in developing a new generation of greener, more effeicient, less costly, safer and more reliable aircraft, aero-engines and equipment as well as air transport. Also, the clean fuels strategy under development will help European manufacturers in an early and smooth transition towards a more sustainable air transport..

Last but not least, fair pricing of transport is an important policy field in the White Paper. The inclusion of aviation in the EU Emissions trading system (ETS) from 2012 will provide further incentives for innovation, accelerated fleet renewal and use of alternative fuels – not only for European, but also for major extra-EU airlines – with clear benefits for the aeronautics industry.

Overall, the Commission is committed to further increase the opportunities of this strategic sector, which creates jobs for highly skilled employees and produces technology spill-overs to other sectors.

In this context and following the good example of Vision 2020 for aeronautics research, the Commission will establish a high-level group with industry leaders from the aeronautics industry to develop a new vision for aeronautics and air transport research in Europe beyond 2020.

Question no 30 by Sarah Ludford(H-0505/10)
 Subject: Bodyscanners at airports

(EN) The Commission adopted a Communication on the use of security scanners at EU airports on 15 June 2010. This impact assessment should be ready in early 2011. The Commission will then decide whether legislative action is needed.

Question no 45 by Carl Schlyter(H-0469/10)
 Subject: Omega-3 and heart attacks
 

Research results published recently in the New England Journal of Medicine(1) from a Dutch research project in which 4800 men and women took part show that low doses of omega-3 as in, for example, table margarine do not reduce the risk of heart attack. These results should have an effect on companies which conduct advertising campaigns that claim the opposite: that the consumption of their margarine reduces the risk of heart attack.

Does the Commission consider that continued claims regarding the effectiveness of margarine are compatible with EU legislation? What measures does it intend to take to ensure that these advertising campaigns and claims are stopped or withdrawn?

 
  
 

(EN) Regulation (EC) No 1924/2006(2) on nutrition and health claims was adopted to ensure that all nutrition and health claims made on food in the EU are truthful, clear, reliable and useful for the consumer. To that end, the legislators decided that scientific substantiation should be the main aspect to be taken into account for the use of claims. Such independent scientific assessment on the truthfulness of claims is carried out by the European Food Safety Authority (EFSA).

The Regulation requires that all health claims need to be authorised at EU level on the basis of EFSA's advice.

When authorising a claim, appropriate conditions of use are set to ensure that consumption of the product on which the claim is made produces the claimed effect. For example, the conditions of use may, depending on the scientific advice, limit the use of the claim only on those foods where the effect is shown or set the minimum quantities of the active constituent needed to reach the effect.

With reference to the question, there have been authorised claims concerning certain substances that may be included in a range of products, including yellow fat spreads (margarine) and their cholesterol reducing effect. Such claims can indicate that high cholesterol is a risk factor in the development of coronary heart disease. National competent authorities are then responsible for enforcing the legislation concerning them.

Concerning claims on omega-3 fatty acids and cardiovascular health, some await an EFSA assessment; but for others that have already been evaluated, the Commission is in discussion with the Member States for a decision on their authorisation. Claims submitted for authorisation are allowed to remain on the market until a decision on them is taken.

To conclude, the Honourable Member can be reassured that, once the Regulation is fully implemented, only health claims backed by science will be allowed to be used in the EU.

 Subject: Changes to EU freedom of movement directive and prevention of sham marriages
 

As a consequence of the European Court of Justice ruling in the Metock judgment of 25 July 2008, some Member States – including Ireland – have had to drop their demands for a change in Directive 2004/38/EC(1) on the free movement of people within the European Union to prevent immigrants arranging marriages of convenience in order to stay in the EU. One of Ireland’s main marriage registrars warned recently that 10-15% of the civil ceremonies conducted across Ireland may be sham marriages aimed purely at circumventing immigration rules.

Does the Commission share Member States’ concerns about the potential for exploitation of the directive in light of the Metock ruling? Has the Commission received information from any Member State about abuse and violation of Directive 2004/38/EC and, if so, what mechanisms are in place for sharing such information with other Member States? Does the Commission believe that Member States can stamp out marriages of convenience without changes to Directive 2004/38/EC and, if so, exactly how?

 
  
 

(EN) As stressed in the report(2) from the Commission on the application of Directive 2004/38/EC(3), the Court of Justice of the European Union in its ruling in the Metock case(4) recalled that the Directive does not prevent Member States from fighting against abuse of EU rights, including marriages of convenience, as stipulated in Article 35 of the Directive.

To identify difficulties and clarify issues of interpretation of the Directive and to assist Member States to apply it correctly, the Commission established a group of experts from Member States in September 2008. The fight against abuse, exchange of best practice, statistics and intelligence on emerging patterns of abuse are on the agenda of the group which meets on a regular basis. An IT network is used to allow Member States to share statistical data and further intelligence on abuse and fraud. Recent news from the United Kingdom concerning criminal convictions of clergy involved in concluding sham marriages and successful police raids against organised criminal rings involved in sham marriages made after police investigation confirm the Commission’s opinion that there is no need to modify the existing EU legislative framework to allow Member States to take effective steps against abuse.

Question no 49 by Andrew Henry William Brons(H-0478/10)
 Subject: President of Mali
 

I should like to know the reaction of the Commission to the speech given by the President of Mali to the European Parliament last week. It will be recalled that he complained that Mali was suffering a brain drain to the developed world. He said that nearly 35% of Mali's graduates were working abroad and that a high but unspecified proportion of health service workers were working in other countries. Does the Commission believe that it is morally acceptable for developed countries to poach the medical personnel of Third World countries, who have been trained at their expense from very limited resources?

 
  
 

(EN) The Commission is particularly concerned about the critical shortage of qualified workers, in many African countries, in the health sector. It is estimated that a minimum of 2.3 health workers per 1000 population are needed in order to provide basic health care. Africa, on average, has 0.8 health workers per 1000 population and Mali even less, compared to 10.3 per 1000 population in Europe. Aware of this challenge and of the benefit which the EU derives as a destination for many of these migrant health workers, the EU has adopted a Strategy for Action on the Crisis in Human Resources for Health in Developing Countries, followed by a European Programme for Action. In 51 countries, including Mali, the EU supports health programmes dealing with human resources for health (HRH). At the global level, the EU is beginning to explore opportunities for stimulating circular migration and for introduction of other mechanisms that would make migration of health workers beneficial for development.

One sub-theme of the thematic programme Investing in People is dedicated to improving health systems and addressing the human resource crisis. Under this component, an EU project is being funded in partnership with World Health Organisation (WHO), so as to strengthen the health workforce development and tackle the critical shortage of health workforce in 57 countries, including 36 African countries, of which Mali.

Existing EU legislation and the legislation in preparation already contain a number of safeguards aiming at preventing "brain drain". For example, Directive 2009/50(1) on the EU "Blue Card" provides that Member States can refuse applications for Blue Cards in order to ensure ethical recruitment. In addition the Directive does not affect agreements with third countries which list professions suffering from lack of personnel and which are therefore subject to ethical recruitment. Third country nationals who have long-term resident status in the European Union can leave the EU territory for up to a year without losing that status, and this period is extended to two years for highly qualified migrants with a Blue Card. These provisions encourage "circular" migration between the Member States and the migrant's home country, with the accompanying transfer of skills and money.

The European Council also underlines the need to take further steps to maximise the positive and minimise the negative effects of migration on development in line with the Global Approach on Migration.
