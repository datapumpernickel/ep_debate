Question no 13 by Juan de Dios Izquierdo Collado (H-0386/03)
 Subject: Rail accident in Chinchilla (Spain)
 

An extremely serious rail accident has recently occurred at Chinchilla (Albacete province, Castilla-La Mancha region), just a few months after a similar one occurred at Tobarra, on the same line.

The seriousness of the Chinchilla accident (which, as at 5 June, had left 16 dead, 10 missing and 39 injured) was due to the fact that a passenger train collided with a goods train on a single-track line which lacked any automated traffic-control devices. Just before the accident happened a hazardous substance (sulphuric acid) had been unloaded from the goods train, which prevented an exponential increase in the scale of the tragedy.

The general public's response to such accidents is to ask what action the European Union is taking in order to investigate and prevent such occurrences.

Would the Council therefore say when the European Agency for Railway Safety will come into operation?

 
  
 

(IT) The Council would like to reiterate its deepest regret regarding the very serious rail accident in Chinchilla, Spain.

Whilst recalling that the adoption of measures to improve transport safety is an objective of the common transport policy, as established by the Treaty, the Council points out that the continuous improvement of rail safety on the European level is currently being addressed, by both European Parliament and Council, in the framework of the co-decision procedure for a Directive on safety on the Community's railways. The establishment of the European Railway Agency - which is the subject of a separate proposal - also forms an important element in this process. The Council adopted its Common on the 'Second Railway Package' on the occasion of its meeting (Agriculture) 11-26 June 2003 and has forwarded them to the European Parliament for a second reading in accordance with the co-decision procedure.

Taking into account article 42 of the proposal on a European Railway Agency, which proposes that the Agency shall be operational within 24 months of the entry into force of this Regulation, and without prejudice to the further proceedings within the context of the co-decision procedure, the Agency may be expected to become operational in 2006.

 

Question no 14 by Christos Zacharakis (H-0388/03)
 Subject: Contravention of international legal rules by Turkey, and danger of causing a serious accident to a passenger aeroplane in Greek airspace
 

On 9 June 2003, two Turkish F-16 fighter planes harassed an Olympic Airways aircraft on the Athens-Istanbul route, placing the lives of the passengers in direct danger. The aircraft’s automatic crash avoidance safety system was triggered and Greek fighter planes were forced to intercept the Turkish planes.

This action constitutes a clear contravention of the rules of international law and, more specifically, a violation of European Union airspace and thus of its territorial integrity, placing the safety of EU citizens in danger, and represents a violation of fundamental standards of international conduct, such as those enshrined in the Treaty on European Union and in the United Nations Charter and included within the Copenhagen criteria. It is a reflection of a systematic Turkish policy which leads to tension in relations between Greece and Turkey and directly threatens the peace and security of the region. In view of these facts, what urgent steps does the Council intend to take to put a stop to such aggressive acts by Turkey and to prevent their repetition in the future, and what consequences will the Turkish strategy adopted to date have on the assessment of the country’s progress towards EU membership?

 
  
 

(IT) The Council is aware of the incident mentioned by the honourable Member. It is also aware of information about claims for an increasing number of violations of Greek airspace by Turkish military aircraft, and of the sensitivity of this question - as well as of other issues relating to the Aegean - in Greece. Such incidents do unfortunately contrast with the general improvement in neighbourhood relations between Greece and Turkey over the recent years since 1999.

The Council considers that Turkey, as a candidate State, must - under the principle of peaceful settlement of disputes in accordance with the UN Charter - make every effort to resolve any outstanding border disputes and other related issues, as referred to in point 4 of the Helsinki conclusions. This is reflected in a specific priority of the revised Turkey Accession Partnership. In line with the Helsinki conclusions, the European Council will have to review the situation in this area, including its repercussions on the accession process, before the end of 2004.

In the framework of political dialogue, the Council is regularly encouraging Turkey to continue making efforts to prevent tensions - for instance through confidence building measures in sensitive areas - and to work together with its neighbour towards reaching an understanding on issues where views and interpretation of relevant international law are diverging.

 

Question no 15 by Francisca Sauquillo Pérez del Arco (H-0390/03)
 Subject: Visit by the Chilean vessel Esmeralda to European ports
 

The governments of the UK, Spain, France and Germany have authorised the Chilean vessel Esmeralda to visit their ports. In September 1973, that vessel was the scene of at least one murder and of the large-scale systematic torture of Chilean citizens by the military authorities which seized power in Chile following the 11 September 1973 coup d'état.

Under the European Convention on Human Rights, the EU Charter of Fundamental Rights, the International Convention against Torture and Degrading Treatment and Articles 1, 12 and 16 (concerning human rights) of the Association Agreement concluded between Chile and the European Union, no European government should authorise the above-mentioned Chilean training ship to visit its ports.

Does the Council consider it reasonable and politically correct for a symbol of dictatorship, torture and human degradation to have secured authorisation to visit EU territory? Does it consider that common guidelines for action by its members in cases such as this should be laid down? Is it planning to ask its Spanish, British, French and German members to withdraw their authorisation?

 
  
 

(IT) The Council is not aware of the facts mentioned by the honourable MEP concerning the vessel Esmeralda and therefore has not taken a position on it.

Furthermore, decisions allowing foreign vessels to enter Member States' territorial waters and ports lays in their exclusive competence. Therefore, the Council is not in a position to comment the facts mentioned by the MEP.

 

Question no 16 by Konstantinos Alyssandrakis (H-0396/03)
 Subject: EU position on US actions against Cuba
 

In view of the declaration by the Presidency of 5 June 2003 and the démarche made in respect of the Cuban government on the same date, will the Council say why it continues to ignore the fact that those sentenced by the Cuban courts were paid US agents and why it refers to them as 'members of the Cuban opposition and … independent journalists, being deprived of their freedom for having expressed freely their opinion'?

Why does it ignore the fact that those who were sentenced to death and executed were guilty of piracy which endangered the lives of hundreds of persons, including nationals of EU Member States?

How does it view the action taken by the US Interests Section in Havana headed by Mr Cason?

Why has it not made a single move to protest to the US administration about the continuing detention of the five Cuban patriots whose only action was to uncover plots against Cuba?

How does it view the fact that hijacked Cuban aircraft were auctioned off instead of being returned to Cuba and that the hijackers are at liberty in the USA?

 
  
 

(IT)

1. The Council does not have to comment on bilateral matters between Cuba and the US, such as the actions taken by the US Interests Section in Havana, the treatment of the so called 'Miami 5', or the auctioning off of hijacked Cuban air-crafts by the US.

2. The Council on a more general point of view, strongly underlines that on the one hand,
hi-jacking of planes or boats cannot be tolerated under any circumstances, and that on the other hand, the EU regrets that the Cuban authorities broke the de-facto moratorium on the death penalty.

3. As clearly stated in the EU's 2002 Annual Report on Human Rights,

'The EU is opposed to the death penalty in all circumstances. That stance is rooted in a belief in the inherent dignity of all human beings. The EU uses all relevant international and regional fora to advocate the universal abolition of the death penalty.

In countries that retain the death penalty, the EU aims at the progressive restriction of its scope and urges respect for the minimum standards set out in international human rights instruments and other documents, notably the 'EU Guidelines towards third countries on the death penalty', adopted by the European Council in June 1998.'

 

Question no 17 by Efstratios Korakas (H-0397/03)
 Subject: Increased provocation by the Turkish forces and Denktash's regime of occupation in Cyprus
 

The Turkish forces in Cyprus and Denktash's regime of occupation have been taking an increasing number of provocative measures, and in the last few days have:

extended their occupation to include Greek Cypriot homes in the region of Strovilia in free Famagusta,

brought in new tanks and heavy artillery through the occupied port of Famagusta, and

extended fortifications in the so-called buffer zone.

What is the Council's position in respect of these acts of provocation, how does it view the above and how does it intend to respond?

 
  
 

(IT) The Council is very closely following developments in Cyprus. It considers that any action likely to generate tensions and to damage the prospects of reaching peace and of reunifying the island must be avoided.

The best way for the European Union to respond to developments of the kind the honourable Member is pointing out is to continue to strongly support further efforts to move towards a comprehensive Cyprus settlement, in the framework of the UN. The Council stays firmly committed to this, as just confirmed at the Thessaloniki European Council.

In the 2003 Accession Partnership for Turkey, strong support to efforts to find a comprehensive settlement of the Cyprus problem has been identified as a priority.

In order to help creating favourable conditions for moving towards a settlement, the Council also supports measures likely to bring about practical improvements on the ground and to bring people from either part of the island closer together. Such measures have been announced in the recent Commission communication on promoting economic development in the northern part of Cyprus. The Council is looking forward to their early implementation.

Question no 19 by Gerardo Galeote Quecedo (H-0406/03)
 Subject: Commission and Council Office in the United Nations (New York)
 

The European Parliament has consistently expressed the need to enhance cooperation between the Commission, the Council and the Member States in third countries and international organisations in order to make progress towards the establishment of a European common external service.

Does the Council believe that its Office at the New York seat of the UN has enough budgetary, human and logistic resources at its disposal for it to fulfil effectively its tasks relating to the external representation of the EU? How many officials at each grade are currently posted to its Coordination Office in New York?

Are the Council and Commission planning to move their two offices into one location so as to ensure a common 'EU entrance'?

Is any use made of the facilities owned by Member States for EU coordination activities? Does the Council foresee the pooling of assets of Member States and EU institutions at the UN?

Has the Council reviewed the changes caused by enlargement? How does it intend to adapt in order to guarantee EU coordination and coordination with third countries at the UN?

 
  
 

(IT) The Council would recall that its Office in New York was established in 1994 after the entry into force of the Treaty on European Union, as a direct consequence of the implementation of the CFSP. The Office had to provide Secretariat support and services in order to facilitate the implementation of Articles 11, 12 and 19 of the new Treaty, and liaise with the United Nations on issues going beyond Community competence.

The subsequent development of the CFSP and, significantly, the emergence of the European Security and Defence Policy, already accounting for three crisis-management operations under UN mandate, have rendered necessary the reinforcement of the Office, which is gradually taking place. As from 1 August 2003, the staff complement will be as follows:

1 A1, 2 A4, 1 A5, 1 B1, 1 B5, 4 C

Before envisaging any further reinforcement, synergies with the Delegation of the Commission are being explored. In this perspective, it is foreseen that the Offices of both Institutions will move into the same premises, fitted with larger meeting rooms and a common entrance, by the beginning of 2004.

In the meantime, in periods of intense activity, Member States put some of their facilities at the disposal of the EU for coordination activities. Any further pooling of assets seems, however, premature: as things stand, only States are members of the UN, and the Member States have distinct legal personalities and a distinct role therein.

As far as coordination with third states is concerned, participation of the acceding countries has not had a major impact and has not required substantial adaptations. Meeting rooms are however stretched to the maximum capacity and restrictions have been imposed on the number of participants (maximum 1 per delegation at the table). The situation is expected to improve after the move to the new premises.

 

Question no 20 by Astrid Thors (H-0410/03)
 Subject: Progress relating to the situation of children in Romania
 

During this parliamentary term, the European Parliament and the Commission have been very committed to improving the situation of children in Romania. I was, however, dismayed to note the Council’s answer to my Oral Question (H-0275/03(1)): ‘which indicated that no real progress had been made in respect of the political criterion relating to children.’

A recent briefing document for the EP includes the following statement: ‘concerns persist about the significant regional differences in the implementation of the reform programme (for child protection)’. There are also frequent reports of the deaths of children and young people who have been expelled from orphanages without having a secure environment to which to return. Furthermore, on 22 May, the Romanian press reported that the moratorium on international adoptions was to continue, which means that the legislation needed is not ready, in spite of huge international efforts and aid provided by the High-Level Group on the initiative of Baroness Nicholson.

What are the reasons for the lack of progress in the reform of childcare in Romania? What measures will the Council take to inform the Romanian authorities of the EU’s serious concerns?

 
  
 

(IT) As the Honourable Parliamentarian is aware, in the case of Romania one of the specific areas covered by the pre-accession strategy is that of childcare, an important issue on which the Council has already had the opportunity to respond to questions in this assembly. The Presidency can confirm that the Union continues and will continue to closely monitor the situation in this regard.

In the revised Accession Partnership for Romania that was adopted by the Council on last 19 May, inter alia it should be underlined that the priorities and intermediate objectives include the stipulation that Romania should: 'Continue the reform of the public child care system in accordance with the National Strategy on the Protection of Children in Difficulty, by focussing on closure of old-style institutions, de-institutionalisation and prevention of institutionalisation through provision of alternative social services for families and children'. The issue was also recently mentioned at Ministerial level at the latest meeting of the EU-Romania Association Council.

The Union continues to urge the Romanian authorities to make further efforts in this area. However the progress already made should not be underestimated, as noted in the Commission's 2002 Regular Report on Romania's progress towards accession. In particular, the number of children in residential care has been reduced, from 50 000 in 2001 to 43 000 in 2002, through other solutions such as assistance to families, foster care, family type homes and national adoption.

Furthermore, according to latest information available, existing regional differences in the implementation of the reform are likely to decrease in the near future, as best practices on child protection will be extended.

As regards the current moratorium on international adoptions, the Romanian government has prepared new legislation on children's rights, including legislation on adoptions, which will be submitted to Parliament as soon as possible. An independent group of experts in family law of the Member States of the European Union is examining this legislation and will transmit their opinion to the Romanian government soon. In the meantime, in the revised Accession Partnership, the EU has maintained the view that the moratorium on adoptions should remain in place until new legislation compatible with the best interests of the child and Romania's international obligations is in place and the administrative capacity to implement the new legislation is ensured.

Question no 21 by Richard Howitt (H-0413/03)
 Subject: EU-Africa Dialogue
 

It is now three months since the second EU-Africa Summit was due to have been held in Lisbon, but it was postponed sine die. Given that it is important for the EU to maintain a strong political dialogue with Africa, that the recent G8 meeting in Evian devoted considerable attention to NEPAD and that the African Union will hold its second Summit of Heads of State or Government in Maputo in early July, could the President of the Council please inform Parliament about the steps that are being taken formally to resume the Dialogue?

 
  
 

(EN) Following the postponement of the Lisbon Summit and according to the Council mandate, the Presidency continued discussion with its South African counterpart (AU Presidency) to ensure the continuation of the dialogue, in particular to exchange views on how the dialogue between the EU and its African partners could be further enhanced and what steps could be taken to guarantee the holding of the Summit at the earliest possible date.

At this meeting both sides reiterated their commitment to ensure the continuity and enhance the effectiveness of the dialogue between EU and Africa as a whole. The EU underlined that a good preparatory work has to follow in order to ensure that the Ministerial meeting would have a positive impact on the dialogue.

 

Question no 22 by Rodi Kratsa-Tsagaropoulou (H-0415/03)
 Subject: Interim review of the Common Agricultural Policy and the situation of women in rural areas
 

At the meeting of the Council of Ministers of Agriculture of 27 May 2002(1) a number of policies and objectives regarding the situation of women in the context of gender mainstreaming in the European agricultural sector were adopted with a view to an interim CAP review.

What initiatives and guidelines has the Council adopted to date in implementation of these specific policy decisions? Has it called on the Commission to take specific measures and initiatives for integrated action to achieve gender equality in the context of agricultural and rural development policy? Will it make room for implementation of this policy in the framework of its final position on the Common Agricultural Policy particularly with a view to strengthening the second pillar of rural development policy and, if so, how?

 
  
 

(IT) The question of rural employment cannot be taken out of the wider context of the European Employment Strategy (EES), as defined by the 1997 Luxembourg Extraordinary European Council on Employment.

The 2000 Extraordinary Lisbon European Council set the goal of full employment and, in the medium-term, the goal of an overall employment rate of 70% (60% for women), to be achieved by 2010.

The 2001 Stockholm European Council set as intermediate targets for 2005 overall employment rates of 67% (57% for women).

The five-year review of the European Employment Strategy (COM (2002) 416 final) notes that over 10 million jobs (6 million of which are filled by women) were created in the five-year period 1997 to 2001.

The Council considers it of primary importance that the European Union should guarantee the application of the principle of equal opportunities for men and women living in rural areas and believes that the participation of women is vitally important to strengthen the new European rural model.

In this regard, the Agriculture Council approved the conclusions on the valuable contribution of women to rural development, which were based on the Spanish Presidency’s memorandum and the results of the work of the Conference on Gender Mainstreaming organised jointly by the Presidency and the Commission in Santander (Spain) in June 2002.

The Commission has consolidated the policies seeking to strengthen the role of women working in agriculture through the implementation of awareness-raising campaigns intended to bring about recognition of the contribution that women working in rural areas make to the economy and society, to encourage associationism, to encourage the promotion and creation of information channels and networks and to promote training and technical assistance programmes. These programmes, which have developed through Community initiatives, concern access to the labour market and training for women and provide for specific funding for women with entrepreneurial projects or the promotion of social infrastructure in rural areas. In view of these initiatives, the Commission has purposefully included specific provisions on the promotion of female employment in rural areas in the package of legislative proposals on CAP reform.

Moreover, in anticipation of a subsequent Council review, in the first half of 2003, the Presidency launched a debate on employment in rural areas on the basis of a working document, examined as a preliminary measure by the Special Committee on Agriculture on 2 June last. The debate, which addresses, inter alia, the issue of the promotion of female employment in rural areas, will be developed during the second half of 2003.

Question no 25 by Laura González Álvarez (H-0429/03)
 Subject: Colombia, paramilitaries and displaced persons in Cacarica
 

In Colombia, freedoms are being further curtailed by the day, without any substantial social progress being apparent.

Growing militarisation, accompanied by an increasingly visible North American presence, is again enabling paramilitaries to operate with impunity.

What action will the Council take to sponsor a peaceful settlement between the Colombian Government and the guerrillas? Is it promoting a humanitarian agreement, which could be a first step in this direction? How is it going about this?

 
  
 

(IT) The Council invites the Honourable Parliamentarians to refer to its conclusions on Colombia, adopted last December, which perfectly summarise the EU's position on all matters. On this occasion,

'the Council welcomed President Uribe’s efforts to reform his country’s institutions and to develop a fully functioning democratic state throughout the territory of Colombia, based on the respect for human rights and the welfare and safety of all its citizens. Question no 76 by Hans-Peter Martin (H-0426/03)

As a practical follow-up to these commitments, the Commission adopted last year the General principles and minimum standards for consultation of interested parties(3), which lay down a general framework on how to carry out consultations in an open and transparent manner. Furthermore, the Commission published on the Internet a list of formal and structured consultative groups in which civil society organisations participate.
