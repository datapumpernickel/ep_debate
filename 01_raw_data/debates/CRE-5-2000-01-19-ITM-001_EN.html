Verbatim report of proceedings
Wednesday, 19 January 2000 - Strasbourg OJ edition

1. Adoption of the Minutes of the previous sitting
 		 

  President. – The Minutes of yesterday’s sitting have been distributed.

Are there any comments?

 
  
 		 

  Sturdy (PPE-DE). – Madam President, I rise on a matter which is of great importance to everyone in this House. Yesterday afternoon, we had the Commission with their usual answering of questions. Mr Provan in the chair had a major problem because Mr Byrne took up 29 minutes answering questions. It is particularly important to this House that we have the opportunity to question the Commissioners and for a Commissioner to spend 29 minutes answering questions is, in my opinion, ridiculous. I believe that he was particularly concerned about having to answer a question which I know a number of colleagues – not myself – wanted to ask him concerning the reason why he did not instigate interim measures against the French on beef.

That apart, I would suggest that, whoever is in the chair, if we have another situation where a Commissioner is just talking his time out, then the chair should have the power to stop the Commissioner or make him shorten his answers.

 
  
 		 

  President. – Thank you, Mr Sturdy. I am going to give the floor to Mr Provan. I must say that he dealt remarkably well with a rather tricky situation, given the lack of time. For statements as important as yesterday’s, half an hour is really not enough. We should look at extending this time.

 
  
 		 

  Provan (PPE-DE). – Madam President, I am glad that this matter has been raised, but I had intended raising it in the Bureau of Parliament, which is the correct procedure. Perhaps we should have a discussion within the Bureau as to how we could properly structure this type of debate in the future.

It was not satisfactory yesterday. We over-ran our time by five minutes. As Mr Sturdy has said, the Commissioner spoke for 29 minutes and Members only had six minutes in which to put questions. It was a ridiculous situation that we must try to rectify.

 
  
 		 

  President. – I agree, Mr Provan. We will look at this during the next Bureau meeting.

 
  
 		 

  Roth-Behrendt (PSE). – (DE) Madam President, my apologies; I rarely ask for the floor in this sort of situation but I do this also to make an appeal to the House. With all due respect to Mr Sturdy, I consider that his complaint against Mr Byrne is not quite fair because, in the final analysis, we asked Mr Byrne to present the White Paper on food safety. It is a highly complicated paper and cannot be dealt with in ten minutes. Mr Byrne took 15 or 17 minutes to do so and then some of us asked questions, some of which took longer than one minute. I took less than a minute, others took more. It is up to us to ensure that the Commission has an hour, not half an hour to explain its position and that we then have sufficient time to hold a debate on it.

What Mr Provan said is indeed correct. I was here in plenary from 3.00 p.m. yesterday and it was we, the Parliament, who put the Erika oil disaster on the agenda and we, the Parliament, who put the storms in Europe on the agenda. As a result, we all talked for longer under vice-presidents Colom and Provan. It was Parliament’s fault that we ran over so badly. The vice-presidents had a difficult job and I think it is unfair to blame the Commissioner because Mr Byrne had to wait and suffered as a result. It is up to us to change our agenda and be more disciplined.

(Applause)

 
  
 		 

  President. – Thank you, Mrs Roth-Behrendt. This serves to confirm that discussions of this nature should be allocated at least an hour. We must take this into account in the future.

 
  
 		 

  Terrón i Cusí (PSE). – (ES) Madam President, this afternoon we are going to hold the annual debate on the establishment of an area of freedom, security and justice. It is included in the agenda as Questions to the Council. In reality, the oral question is intended to introduce the debate laid down in the fourth paragraph of Article 2 of the Treaty.

I would like to ask the Presidency to guarantee that the Council, which has not replied in writing to these questions, will firstly reply to the questions put by the Committee on Citizens’ Rights and Freedoms, Justice and Home Affairs, so that we can then proceed to the debate laid down in the Union Treaty in a more effective way.

 
  
 		 

  President. – Thank you, Mrs Terrón i Cusí. This matter will, of course, be discussed with the Council.

We have rather moved away from the approval of the Minutes. Are there any other comments on the Minutes?

 
  
 		 

  Howitt (PSE). – Madam President, on Minute 21 I ask you to note what I regard as a clear breach of procedure in respect of my Question No 66 last night. This concerns the labelling on European Union manufactured Nestlé tins of breast milk substitute in an inappropriate language and with confusing lettering and colouring, in clear breach of EU Directive 92/52 on infant formulae. This was first reported on 5 October last year and was still on sale in Islamabad, Pakistan yesterday, when I checked.

I know you cannot comment on the substance of the issue but the Commission's answer fails to take responsibility for the implementation of the directive as it has failed to supply the annual reports required, as it has failed to act on one previous complaint received – see my Question No 2283/99.

I ask you to write to the Commission, asking them to uphold their responsibilities on behalf of this European Parliament and on behalf of the mothers of 82 children in every thousand who die in Pakistan in infancy and for whom the use of inappropriate feed mixed with unsafe water is a danger to life.

 
  
 		 

  President. – I completely agree, Mr Howitt. Not only will your intervention be entered in today’s Minutes, but I will also write to the Commission in the vein which you suggest.

(The Minutes were approved)
