Questions to the Council
Question no 14 by Rodi Kratsa-Tsagaropoulou (H-0987/00)
 Subject: Status of islands
 

In their most recent answer concerning the status of islands, at the October 2000 part-session(1), the Council and the Commission, represented by Mr Moscovici and Mr Barnier respectively, stressed that island status does not in itself constitute a criterion of eligibility for special measures towards achieving economic and social cohesion with the rest of the 'European area', an answer which was based on a narrow interpretation of Article 158 of the EC Treaty and of Declaration No. 30 of the Treaty of Amsterdam. The Presidency's conclusions at Nice confirmed the need for special measures for island regions owing to the structural disadvantages which slow down their economic and social development.

Does the Council think that the new Treaty can provide a broader definition of the status of islands and the possibility of implementing a European policy for all the island regions?

 
  
 

The Council would like to remind the honourable Member that, in the context of economic and social cohesion, European Union island regions which meet the eligibility criteria laid down in the general regulation may benefit from Community financial support under Objective 1 (regions whose development is lagging behind) and Objective 2 (areas undergoing economic and social conversion). The Structural Funds have made it possible to improve the economic and social situation of the eligible islands regions and will continue to do so in the future. The honourable Member is reminded that the ceilings to which Community Structural Funds participation is subject have been increased for the Greek islands in the outermost regions, as they can receive up to a maximum of 85% of the total eligible cost, the current ceiling being set at a maximum of 75% for Objective 1 regions.

Furthermore, the outermost regions of the Union, a great many of which are island territories, can benefit from specific measures under Community law and under certain common policies, pursuant to Article 299(2) of the Treaty.

Moreover, the islands whose economies are dependent on agriculture or fisheries may also benefit greatly from schemes under the common agricultural policy and specific policies on fisheries or tropical foodstuffs.

It would therefore appear excessive to accuse the Council of interpreting Article 158 of the Treaty and Declaration No 30 annexed to the Treaty of Amsterdam too narrowly.

The Presidency Conclusions of the Nice European Council confirmed the need for specific actions for the benefit of island regions, in accordance with Article 158 of the TEC, within the limits of the budget resources available.

The honourable Member is also reminded that Article 158 of the Treaty of Amsterdam was not amended by the Nice Intergovernmental Conference.

 
 

(1) Report of proceedings of 24.10.2000, p. 25

 

Question no 15 by Brian Crowley (H-0990/00)
 Subject: The situation of children and young people in 2001
 

Will the Council provide a detailed overview of its proposals under the Swedish Presidency aimed at highlighting the situation of children and young people in the new media landscape and how it intends, in practical ways, to draw the attention of the Council in its work to the importance of young peoples’ creative ability and entrepreneurship?

 
  
 

I do not want to anticipate coming events. The Minister of Culture will be addressing the Culture Committee on the 24 January and present the Swedish Presidency priorities in this field, but I would already now like to mention a seminar 12-13 February 2001 in Stockholm organised by the Swedish Presidency. The seminar "Children and young people in the new media landscape" will focus on children's situation in a fast changing mediasociety. The Council meeting with the Ministers of Culture in June 2001 will be discussing this issue.

About young peoples' creativity and entrepreneurship I would like to refer to the Lisbon conclusions. To meet the challenges of the future, the European Council in Lisbon agreed on a new strategic objective, namely that the European Union should become the world’s most competitive and dynamic knowledge-based economy, with scope for sustainable economic growth, more and better job opportunities and a higher degree of social cohesion.

From a youth policy perspective it is of great importance that young peoples creativity, resourcefulness and entrepreneurship is regarded as a resource, both for the continuing of the Lisbon process and for meeting future challenges in general.

Since entrepreneurship is a cross-sectoral concept and one that includes characteristics that may find expression in a range of different areas, it fits well into the youth policies of the EU and its member States alike.

The Community action programme Youth, decided upon by Parliament and the Council, also enshrines the principle that young people should be encouraged to take the initiative and be enterprising and creative, thereby being empowered to play an active role in society.

In the light of the above, the Swedish Presidency very recently presented a presidency note on this subject. The presidency is also considering taking the initiative for a Council resolution to urge the Commission and the member States to, in various ways, devote particular attention to and promote young peoples creativity, resourcefulness and entrepreneurship in a broad sense.

The content of the latter document is not yet decided, since it will be the subject for thorough discussions in the Council.

 

Question no 16 by James (Jim) Fitzsimons (H-0993/00)
 Subject: Maritime policy
 

Having regard to Sweden’s maritime traditions, does the Council have any plans to promote a greater awareness of and cooperation between the EU’s maritime regions and ports and would it support a twinning programme between the EU’s maritime towns and ports?

 
  
 

The Council reminds the honourable Member that the objectives of the common transport policy in the maritime field include both safety and improved levels of protection of the marine environment. This may be expected to contribute to greater awareness of the common interests of the maritime regions.

At its meeting of 20 and 21 December 2000, in the course of a debate on maritime safety, the Council reaffirmed the importance which it attaches to the exchange of information, and called on the Member States to step up their cooperation with a view to improving the quality and increasing the flow of information on traffic, by submitting suitable proposals to the IMO and setting up a regional information system to link their treatment centres in order to optimise maritime traffic surveillance procedures and risk prevention for navigation and the environment.

The Council has not to date received any proposal from the Commission concerning the objectives referred to in the question.

 

Question no 17 by Liam Hyland (H-0995/00)
 Subject: Sheep farmers and Swedish Presidency
 

In the Swedish Presidency’s Programme for 2001 it is stated that Sweden intends ‘to work for continued reform in relevant areas such as the market regulations for olive oil and sheep and goat meat’. Will the Council, under the Swedish Presidency, indicate exactly how its initiatives will promote the market in sheep meat, including the wool market to the advantage of sheep farmers in Ireland?

Question no 18 by Bernd Posselt (H-0997/00)
 Subject: Transitional periods for applicant countries
 

What plans does the Presidency of the Council have as regards transitional periods for the applicant countries, particularly in the sensitive areas of freedom of movement for workers and freedom to acquire real estate?

 
  
 

The EU position in the accession negotiations is that the candidate countries must take over the acquis of the Union, be it Community legislation, common policies or other EU rules. They must transpose (where transposition is called for), implement and enforce the acquis. This should happen upon accession, and in most cases will so happen. Where transitional arrangements are deemed necessary, they must be exceptional, limited in time and scope, and accompanied by a plan with clearly defined stages for application of the acquis. They must not involve amendments to the rules or policies of the Union, disrupt their proper functioning or lead to significant distortions of competition. The safeguarding of the functioning of the internal market without disruptions is essential.

The Swedish Presidency attaches high priority to enlargement and will pursue the accession negotiations in line with the guidelines set by the Nice European Council and the conclusions of the Council on 4 December 2000, which endorsed the road map proposed by the Commission for the handling of individual negotiating chapters up to the middle of 2002. Wherever possible the Swedish Presidency is ready to move even faster than foreseen in the road map. The Swedish Presidency hopes that decisive progress can be made in the fields mentioned by the honourable Parliamentarian in accordance with the timetable laid down in the road map.

 

Question no 19 by Gerard Collins (H-0999/00)
 Subject: Economic and social cohesion
 

The Swedish Presidency has indicated that the Commission’s second report on economic and social cohesion in the Union will constitute an important contribution to the discussion on future regional and structural policy and that discussions will commence on these matters during its presidency. Will the Council provide an assurance that it will support and advance the needs of the Union’s peripheral and island regions in these discussions and, furthermore, will it ensure that the interests of regional airports and dependent tourism and industry interests are safeguarded against the ill advised ‘Open Skies’ policies being promoted by the Commission?

 
  
 

The Commission has not so far submitted the second progress report on economic and social cohesion to the Council. The Council is therefore not in a position to give an exact answer to the honourable Member's question.

The Council nonetheless stresses that it is fully aware of the problems related to the island or peripheral status of certain regions of the Community. It has always attached great importance to policies for the effective integration of those regions into the internal market.

By way of example, in the context of economic and social cohesion, the Union's least-favoured islands, which are eligible under the criteria of the general regulation on the Structural Funds, are entitled to financial support from the Structural Funds under either Objective 1 (regions whose development is lagging behind) or Objective 2 (areas undergoing economic and social conversion).

In the field of transport, the Council also takes due account of such aspects of regional policy as may arise. It is the Council's usual practice to examine the subjects submitted to it on the basis of all the elements of the dossier.

The Council is thus prepared, in accordance with the position of the Nice European Council, to support measures in favour of the island regions, in the context of the structural disadvantages which are holding back their economic and social development, on the basis of Declaration No 30 annexed to the Treaty of Amsterdam, pursuant to Article 158 of the Treaty and within the limits of the financial resources available.

On the specific subject of relations with the US in the field of aviation, the Council is aware of the position of the Irish authorities and the underlying regional policy concerns. In the context of the preparatory work for a future 'Open Skies' agreement with the US, the Council will most certainly consider all suggestions put forward to it with a view to finding a solution that meets these concerns.

 

Question no 20 by Bart Staes (H-0002/01)
 Subject: Coordinated approach to the financial repercussions of the BSE crisis
 

According to Commissioner Franz Fischler, the European ban on meat and bone meal will cost approximately EUR 4.5 billion. There is still great uncertainty surrounding compensation for the losses incurred. This means that the financial measures due to expire may create distortions of competition between the fifteen EU Member States.

Does the Council support a coordinated European approach to the financial repercussions of the BSE crisis, given that the financial measures taken by the national and regional authorities that are now due to expire are leading to the distortion of competition between the fifteen EU Member States? If not, why is the Council against a coordinated approach?

 
  
 

The Council is aware of the risks of distortion of competition which could result from the introduction of compensation systems for the collection and disposal of specific at-risk material and – following the ban introduced in December 2000 for a period of six months – meat and bone meal.

In its December conclusions, the Council invited the Commission to monitor this issue and, where necessary, to make the appropriate proposals.

In Article 36 of the proposal on health rules concerning animal by-products not intended for human consumption, sent to the Council on 19 October 2000, the Commission expressed its intention to “prepare a report on the financial support in Member States for the processing and disposal of the animal by-products, accompanied by appropriate proposals”.

The Council will treat consideration of the above-mentioned proposals, once they have been referred to it, as a top priority.

 

Question no 21 by Astrid Thors (H-0004/01)
 Subject: Plans to import nuclear fuel and radioactive waste into Russia
 

The Russian Parliament approved on first reading before Christmas legislative changes enabling Russia to import spent nuclear fuel. There has been talk of imports from Japan, Switzerland and Central and East European countries.

At question time in Parliament's Committee on Industry last November, the Commission representatives concluded that, with the knowledge available to it at that time, the Commission could not consider Russian resources or existing facilities for nuclear fuel and waste to be adequate to guarantee safety. Russia's nuclear safety authority has also opposed such imports. Furthermore, it has been claimed that the nuclear fuel/waste is to be imported via the huge harbour planned at Primorsk at the end of the Gulf of Finland.

What action does the Presidency intend to take to prevent nuclear fuel from being imported into Russia under conditions that do not satisfy international standards (Joint Convention on the safety of spent fuel management)? What action does it intend to take to guarantee that the applicant countries manage nuclear waste in compliance with EU rules?

 
  
 

The Presidency has decided to make EU-Russia cooperation, cooperation which is vital for the security and development of Europe, a high priority. The most pressing issues include the environment, nuclear safety and nuclear waste.

The EU has highlighted the importance of high standards of nuclear safety and of a high level of environmental protection from the outset of the accession negotiations with all the applicant countries by inscribing this objective in its General Negotiating Position.

Since accession implies full acceptance of the acquis, including international conventions to which the Community is party, the candidate countries will have to comply with the relevant EU provisions on nuclear waste and radiation protection upon accession. The EU closely monitors the candidate countries’ progress in adoption and effective implementation of the acquis throughout the accession negotiations. In order to support the candidate countries’ efforts in this area, the Council has encouraged the European Commission to use resources from the pre-accession funds for nuclear waste management.

Moreover, insofar as the imports to which the honourable Member refers neither originate in nor enter the European Union, the Council would point out that Community legislation on trade in these substances (Directive 92/3/Eurotom and Regulation 1493/93/Euratom) is inapplicable.

On the other hand, the timetable and arrangements for the incorporation of this legislation by candidate countries into their own law are subject to close monitoring within the framework of enlargement negotiations, particularly those relating to Chapter 22 “Environment” of the Community acquis. The same generally applies to the provisions on nuclear waste. Finally, we would point out that the majority of the countries to which the honourable Member’s question refers receive substantial assistance in the field of nuclear safety under certain aspects of the Phare or Tacis programmes and Community programmes such as the Sure programme, which is open to countries taking part in the Tacis programme and targets, in particular, the safety of the transportation of radioactive substances.

Lastly, the Commission notes that the Joint Convention on the Safety of Spent Fuel Management to which the honourable Member refers has not yet entered into force and cannot, therefore, readily be used to oppose the actions of third countries. In any case, Russia, Japan, some candidate countries and some Member States have yet to ratify this Convention.

Question no 37 by Karin Riis-Jørgensen (H-0982/00)
 Subject: State aid to Combus A/S
 

In May 1999 the state-owned Danish bus company Combus A/S received DKR 300 million from the Danish State, allowing it to compete with fares well below actual cost.

However, Combus A/S has not managed to improve its financial position and the Danish Government has just (December 2000) handed it another DKR 200 million, this time with the excuse that this is the only way the Danish Government could sell the company off.

What view does the Commission now take of the initial DKR 300 million? Was it not actual state aid at the time in May 1999 rather than a contribution following the principle of investment in the market economy as maintained by the Danish Ministry of Transport? Does the Commission intend to reappraise the DKR 300 million from May 1999?

 
  
 

On 30 November 2000, the Danish authorities notified their intentions to inject new capital into Combus A/S. The Commission is currently examining the case with regard to potential State aid. This examination includes the capital injected by the Danish State in 1999. The Commission will make every effort to finalised the case as quickly as possible.

 

Question no 38 by Konstantinos Hatzidakis (H-0989/00)
 Subject: Problems of Olympic Airways
 

What is the Commission's position on the sale of more than 50% of Olympic Airways to private buyers? Who will underwrite the company's losses? To what extent will the new airport at Spata be ready for operation on 1 March 2001, the date set by the Greek Government for its opening, in the light of the problems involved in transferring Olympic Airways to the new airport and the statement by the consortium of contractors that they will be unable to complete the aircraft maintenance and repair facilities in accordance with the original timetable?

 
  
 

For several months, the Commission has been urging the Greek authorities to begin privatisation of Olympic Airways as quickly as possible. Given the serious financial difficulties facing the company, the best solution seems to be to privatise it and make use of private capital. In any case, this is the most acceptable solution under Community law, as the “one time last time” principle is against the company receiving further State aid.

Since the process of privatisation has only just begun and is due to be completed in the spring, it is too early to make a statement on what will happen to Olympic Airway’s debts.

With regard to the new airport at Spata, it is still due to be opened on 1 March 2001 according to the information available to the Commission. However, it is likely that some maintenance and repair facilities used by Olympic Airways at Hellinikon airport will not be moved to Spata as from 1 March 2001 but during the following months. This will not jeopardise the commencement of activities of the Spata airport at all.

 

Question no 39 by James (Jim) Fitzsimons (H-0994/00)
 Subject: Alternative energies, Commission programme for 2001 and US role
 

Will the Commission outline its programme for 2001 with regard to the development of alternative energies and will it actively enlist the support of the new US Presidency for an international programme aimed at developing and using alternative energies?

 
  
 

The promotion of renewable energy continues to be a key priority of the Commission, as demonstrated by the recent Green Paper on Security of Supply.

As regards legislation, the Commission wishes to see a prompt adoption of the proposed directive on electricity from renewable energy sources as facilitated by the constructive first reading in Parliament last year. This directive will set indicative targets for the Member States for generation of electricity from renewables. The Commission looks forward to continuing the fruitful cooperation with Parliament on this politically important dossier.

Moreover, the Commission will continue to support renewable energy projects, including studies, pilot projects and monitoring schemes, in the framework of the ALTENER programme. It will also ensure the renewable energy sources continue to be given high priority in the fifth and the forthcoming sixth framework programme for research and development. Also, the Commission will continue the successful implementation of the Campaign for Take-Off, which aims at raising public awareness and private investment in this sector, for instance through a large number of Renewable Energy Partnerships.

The Commission will publish shortly the progress report on the White Paper for Community Strategy and Action Plan as published in (1) 1, providing detailed information on the progress in actions until 2010, policies and targets in the Member States, the Altener Programme and the Compaign for Take Off.

With respect to seeking United States support for an international programme to develop and promote the use of alternative energies, the Commission draws the honourable Member's attention to the fact that at the recent G8 Summit at Okinawa in June 2000, the Heads of State called for the formation of a Task Force to assess the barriers to renewable energy use in developing countries, and to recommend actions to significantly expand renewable markets. The Commission is fully involved in this work.

Question no 44 by Bernd Posselt (H-0998/00)
 Subject: Humanitarian aid for the Caucasus
 

What plans does the Commission have as regards humanitarian aid for the Caucasus in 2001? Does the Commissioner responsible intend to visit Chechnya this year and possibly open a Commission bureau there?

 
  
 

In the Northern Caucasus, the Commission intends to continue funding operations in line with its humanitarian mandate. This will ensure some relief to the people affected by the crisis, many of whom will have to spend their second winter in camps, or with host families. Obviously, the scope of the involvement will depend on the security situation and the humanitarian working condition. This concerns notably operations in Chechnya proper.

In the Southern Caucasus, ECHO will continue to phase-out the humanitarian operations. However, some funding will be made available to assist the most vulnerable strata of society, in terms of food, agricultural input, and water related assistance. It is possible, that an additional allocation might become necessary, when the full scope of last year's drought becomes clear. To determine the needs, an assessment will be conducted this spring.

The Member of the Commission in charge of Humanitarian Aid visited the Northern Caucasus in February 2000 to gain a first hand impression of the situation. Another important objective of my visit was to draw attention to the fact that humanitarian organisations must be able to work in the area – and notably in Chechnya – in an unhindered manner.

It is not foreseen to open a Commission antenna in the Northern Caucasus in the near future. The operations of ECHO's partners are monitored by the ECHO office in Moscow. To this end, the ECHO experts travel to the region as far as access is possible and the security situation permits.

 

Question no 45 by Gary Titley (H-1003/00)
 Subject: Ebola outbreak and the Gulu District of Northern Uganda
 

In the context of the recent outbreak of Ebola in Northern Uganda, culminating in the death of Dr Matthew Lukwiya, I understand that there may be a plan to relocate the Ebola Testing Laboratory from the Gulu District to the Virus Research Institute in Entebbe. This seems insensitive at a time when the Gulu District is suffering from an outbreak of Ebola, and when the capacity to diagnose and combat the disease in this area should be increased.

Could the Commission clarify the situation of the Ebola Testing Laboratory in Gulu? If it has been moved, why?

 
  
 

The Ebola testing laboratory in Gulu has been run by a team of technologists from the Centres for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC) from Atlanta, United States, who have been working in the area since the onset of the epidemic three months ago. The team is leaving as the outbreak now appears to be contained. The Ugandan Ministry of Health has more technical expertise available in the Entebbe laboratory which is close to Kampala. The transfer has not yet been completed.

Viral testing requires relatively sophisticated equipment, training laboratory staff and reliable electricity. Many African countries do not have the capacity to confirm viral diseases and samples are commonly transported to European or American laboratories. The central virus research institute would seem to be the most appropriate testing site.

Only one confirmed case has been detected in Gulu district since 22 December 2000, the baby of the previous case. No further cases have been reported in Masindi district over the past three weeks. The cumulative figures since the outset of the epidemic are for Gulu 395 cases and 150 deaths and for Masindi 27 cases and 19 deaths. While the epidemic appears to be contained, active case searching and contact tracing continues in both districts, as does a public education campaign throughout the country.

 

Question no 46 by Mihail Papayannakis (H-0948/00)
 Subject: Structural indicators and Member States' economies
 

In its communication COM(2000) 0594/final, the Commission presents a set of structural indicators measuring the progress made in each Member State's economy. On the basis of these measurements, Greece has a considerable amount of ground to make up in several areas (it has the highest energy intensity in the EU, very low labour productivity, a high unemployment rate, almost non-existent continuous learning, the lowest expenditure on education, research and the new technologies, the lowest level of internet access, very high telecommunications prices, a big gap between rich and poor Greeks, the second highest persistence of poverty, one of the highest rates of taxation of low-paid workers, etc.). These are indications that there are problems with real convergence. At the same time, reports by the Commission and other bodies advise Greece to overcome such problems by making structural changes, though these are limited to market deregulation and denationalisation.

Does the Commission believe that such measures are sufficient? Is it prepared to consider other forms of structural change such as administrative reform, regional grouping, tax reform, and agricultural cooperatives?

 
  
 

The structural indicators aim, not only to compare Member States, but to monitor their progress towards the Lisbon goal of a competitive, knowledge-based economy capable of sustainable economic growth with more and better jobs and greater social cohesion. Key areas where action is needed are identified in the Union’s annual Broad Economic Policy Guidelines.

In recent years, Greece has made considerable efforts to achieve macroeconomic stability, leading to its entry into the third stage of economic and monetary union (EMU) in January 2001. Nominal convergence has provided the basis for real gross domestic product (GDP) growth above the EU average in 1996-2000, allowing Greece to converge towards Community levels of GDP per capita. Further progress towards catching-up would be enhanced by an acceleration of structural reforms in product, labour and capital markets. Despite reforms in some areas, problems remain in relation to the opening up of product markets and the functioning of the labour market. As indicated in the question, this has hampered Greek development. However, some progress is being made.

Real convergence is also promoted by investments in human and physical capital, cofinanced by the Community’s Structural Funds and Cohesion Fund. In 2000-2006, some of the key aims of Greece’s third Community Support Framework are to improve education, training and employment services, to increase diffusion of innovation and information technologies, to upgrade core transport infrastructure, and to promote entrepreneurship. However, the impact of such investments on productivity and long-run growth depends on macroeconomic stability and structural reforms. The Commission has also agreed a series of reforms with the Greek authorities aimed at increasing the efficiency of the public administration, improving the management and legislative framework of public works, and enhancing the planning and implementation of training, education and other policies. Deepening and expanding these reforms is a necessary condition for the successful implementation of the Community Support Framework 2000-2006.

 

Question no 47 by Caroline F. Jackson (H-0949/00)
 Subject: UK VAT rate on church repairs
 

On 8 November 2000, the UK Chancellor of the Exchequer claimed in his pre-budget Statement to the House of Commons that he had asked the European Commission to reduce VAT from 17.5% to 5% for repairs to churches.

Could the Commission confirm that such a decision would have to be taken by the Council of Finance Ministers on a proposal from the Commission, and can it indicate when it will submit such a proposal? Or can the Council alter the relevant annex to the sixth VAT Directive on its own initiative?

 
  
 

The honourable Member is correct in that the Paymaster General has recently written to the Commission suggesting that it should consider making early legislative proposals to include repairs, maintenance and improvements to listed places of worship in Annex H of the sixth VAT Directive, thereby making it possible for Member States to apply a reduced rate of VAT to such services.

The honourable Member’s understanding is also correct in that any change to the VAT Directive would have to be decided on unanimously by the Council, were the Commission to propose such a change, and that Finance Ministers could not propose such an amendment on their own initiative.

The Commission can inform the honourable Member that while it has every intention of reviewing the contents of Annex H of the sixth VAT Directive, this review will only take place after the evaluation of the current experiment on the application of the reduced rate for labour-intensive services.

In this context, it should be noted that under the Commission's original proposal for an experimental reduced rate for labour intensive services(1) 1, the repair and maintenance of historic buildings could have benefited from a reduced VAT rate, had Member States requested it.

However, the scope of the Commission’s original proposal was narrowed considerably by the Council by the definition of a limited list of sectors eligible for a reduced rate. In respect of the building sector, only the renovation and repair of private dwellings can qualify for the reduced rate.

There is therefore no possibility in the short term for the United Kingdom to apply a VAT rate of 5% to repairs to churches.

Question no 48 by Alexandros Alavanos (H-0951/00)
 Subject: Risks of transmitting mad cow disease through blood donation
 

Articles in the press have expressed concern over the transmission of mad cow disease to humans through blood donation. The Commission is well aware of the potential problem of transmission of vCJD by blood and blood derivatives. The relevant Scientific Committees continuously monitor upcoming scientific evidence in that area and the Commission will propose preventive measures as necessary.

With the purpose of facilitating the free movement of goods and persons within the Community, the First Motor Directive(1) abolished the checks on green cards for vehicles normally based in a Member State entering the territory of another Member State.
