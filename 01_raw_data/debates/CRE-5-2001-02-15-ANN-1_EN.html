Questions to the Council
Question no 19 by John Joseph McCartin (H-0060/01)
 Subject: Market in beef
 

Does the Council have a strategy, in the light of the BSE scare, for balancing the supply and demand of beef and does it consider that a temporary emergency solution, such as a reduction of the suckling herd with compensatory measures, might not be preferable to the destruction of wholesome and expensive food?

 
  
 

The Council and Commission are working closely together to tackle the current problems on the beef market. Decisions taken in recent weeks include action in the public health arena to restore consumer confidence and market measures aimed at reducing the impact on farmers.

It is the Commission's task to propose further market-related measures, such as the measure which the honourable Member proposes. At its meeting of 29 January 2001, the Council welcomed the Commission's intention to submit a package of measures, before the next Council meeting, towards resolving the problems caused by the imbalance on the EU market in beef, both short and long term, while at the same time improving quality on that market.

The Council will consider these measures promptly within the framework of the budget agreed at the European Council meeting in Berlin.

As you will be aware, on 12 February 2001, the Council drew up the proposal for supplementary and amending budget 1/2001 for the funding of a package of measures relating to BSE, which was agreed by the (agriculture) Council at its meeting in December. The package includes measures for additional costs of EUR 971 million, EUR 726 million of which are made up of funds from the margin available under sub-heading 1a of the budget. Those EUR 726 million will be entirely covered by the surplus from previous years. The remaining EUR 245 million are obtained from adjustments to the estimates of agricultural expenditure as a result of an adjusted euro/dollar exchange rate in line with Article 8 of Council Regulation 2040/2000 of 26 September 2000 on budgetary discipline.

The Council has also noted and welcomed the seven-point plan presented by the Commission yesterday. The Council hopes that the Commission will soon submit the proposals officially so that it may begin to consider them and draw its conclusions. The European Parliament will, of course, be consulted in accordance with the Treaty.

Question no 23 by Yasmine Boudjenah (H-0077/01)
 Subject: Situation in Western Sahara
 

In crossing Western Sahara without the prior authorisation of the Western Saharan government, the organisers of the Paris-Dakar rally risked in an irresponsible and criminal way contributing towards bringing about a resumption of the fighting in a conflict which has still to be settled after 25 years. The event gave backing to the Moroccan Government which claims sovereignty over Western Sahara in defiance of international law. The cease-fire which has held since 1991 is now fragile. The situation could flare up at any moment and degenerate into war, destroying all the efforts made to achieve peace and harming the country's future for a long time to come.

Urgent action is therefore necessary in order to avoid any armed reopening of hostilities, to hold the referendum which has been promised, but continually postponed, since January 1992 and, finally, to achieve compliance by Morocco with the United Nations peace plan.

For the sake of the right of peoples to self-determination, and in order effectively and swiftly to help avoid an irreparable situation, is the Council resolved to play an active role to that end, and in what form?

 
 

Question no 24 by Pedro Marset Campos (H-0092/01)
 Subject: Referendum in Western Sahara
 

The extension of the mandate for the United Nations Mission for the Referendum in Western Sahara (MINURSO), approved by the UN Security Council, expires on 28 February, without the mission having fulfilled its established aims.

Given that failure to implement the peace plan in full may lead to a renewal of hostilities and instability in the Maghreb region, does the Council not take the view that, with the threat of military confrontation in Western Sahara looming, the European Union ought to take urgent steps to enforce the concept and policy of conflict prevention approved in Nice? Does it intend to urge the UN to extend the MINURSO mandate further, so as to enable MINURSO to accomplish its assignment successfully?

 
  
 

Because they are similar in character, all of the questions relating to Western Sahara shall be answered at the same time.

The Council fully supports the United Nations Secretary-General and his personal envoy, James Baker, in their endeavour to find a solution to the conflict in Western Sahara. The parties are urged to continue their efforts, under James Baker’s supervision, to solve the countless problems in implementing the UN’s settlement plan effectively and to agree upon a mutually acceptable solution to the conflict.

All the parties have approved the principles behind the UN process. The Council is therefore convinced that this process constitutes the best framework for a lasting solution which would also strengthen the stability of the whole region. Every possible solution must be based upon full respect for fundamental democratic principles and human rights.

The Council is willing to contribute actively to this process and help bring about a climate of trust and confidence between the parties concerned which can facilitate a settlement. In close consultation with James Baker and through contacts with the countries’ representatives in New York, the Council has, in this spirit, urgently called upon Morocco, Algeria and the Polisario to put a number of humanitarian principles into practice by returning prisoners of war and allowing visits from relatives. The Council has emphasised the importance attached by the EU to confidence-building measures, especially those based upon the latest report by the United Nations Secretary-General. As a result of these approaches, 201 prisoners of war were released, and there was a positive response regarding visits by relatives. We hope that the United Nations’ Commission for Refugees and the International Committee of the Red Cross will be able to arrange such visits.

The EU also addressed the question of Western Sahara at the first meeting of the EU-Morocco Association Council in October and at the Association Committee’s meeting in Rabat on 6 February 2001.

Finally, I should like to emphasise that the European Union is the largest provider of humanitarian aid to refugees from Western Sahara. EUR 20 million was paid out through the Commission’s ECHO office between 1999 and 2001.

 

Question no 25 by Myrsini Zorba (H-0068/01)
 Subject: INTERNET crime
 

The development of INTERNET and new technologies in general has revolutionised not only numerous economic and manufacturing sectors but also the everyday life of individuals everywhere on our planet. At the same time, however, we can observe that the new technologies are also being exploited in a manner degrading to the human condition, dishonouring the values and ideals forming the basis of the European concept. Swift and drastic measures must therefore be taken in response to the rapid and dangerous growth of web sites which are in general an insult to human values and include those devoted to trafficking in children, child pornography and racist propaganda.

In the light of the decisions taken at Lisbon regarding the information society, what measures will be taken to combat this problem by the Council and, in particular, the Swedish Presidency, which is known to be particularly sensitive to human rights issues?

 
  
 

As the honourable Member states, at the Lisbon Summit in March 2000, the European Council emphasised that the transition to a competitive, dynamic and knowledge-based economy was especially important and urged the Council and the Commission to draw up an action plan for eEurope in order to exploit this opportunity to the full. The action plan, drawn up by the Commission and the Council and adopted by the European Council at the Feira Summit in June 2000, includes measures for greater Internet safety and the development of a coordinated and uniform approach to Internet-related crime before the end of 2000.

The European Union has already adopted a series of measures to combat damaging and unlawful material on the Internet, to protect non-material copyright and personal data, to promote electronic commerce and the use of electronic signatures and to improve the security of transactions.

The Commission has recently submitted a communication to the Council, the European Parliament, the Economic and Social Committee and the Committee of the Regions under the heading of ‘A Safer Information Society – Safer Information Infrastructures and Combating Computer-related Crime’.

The various alternative measures available to the EU for combating computer-related crime and referred to in this communication are based upon the study’s analysis and recommendations, the conclusions from the consultation process, the new opportunities offered by the Treaty of Amsterdam and the work that has been done within the EU, G8 and the Council of Europe (which is expected to complete its work by producing the Convention on Cyber-crime this year). At Community level, the solutions chosen ought not to mean that the internal market is impeded or fragmented, nor should they entail measures that undermine the protection of basic rights.

The House ought also to be reminded that, for a long time, the EU has supported projects aimed at promoting the safety of information infrastructures and electronic transactions and at increasing public confidence in these. Research projects and operational projects aimed at promoting the special training of staff in crime-fighting organisations, together with cooperation between crime-fighting organisations and industry, have also received support within the framework of the programmes within the third pillar; for example Stop, Falcone, Oisin and Grotius. The Swedish Presidency is arranging three seminars this year on these issues.

 

Question no 26 by Anders Wijkman (H-0072/01)
 Subject: Council activities with regard to Export Credit Agencies
 

Export Credit Agencies (ECAs) play a very important role in support of infrastructure projects in the South and in Eastern Europe. During the 1990’s, ECAs’ financing through loans, project guarantees and investment insurance averaged USD 80-100 billion per annum. Roughly 60% was channelled in support of energy-intensive projects: fossil fuel power plants, oil and gas development, transportation, etc. While a few – most notably the US Export-Import Bank – weight environmental impact in deciding whether to make loans, most do not. This means that taxpayers’ money is being used to subsidise investments in carbon-based technologies. Given the seriousness of the climate problem – and EU commitments to the Kyoto Protocol – it is remarkable that so little attention has been paid to the critical role played by ECAs.

What steps will the Council take to work towards the introduction and harmonisation of strict environmental guidelines for European ECA activities?

 
  
 

There is no proposal before the Council regarding environmental guidelines for European Export Credit Agency activities.

However, Member States and the Commission are currently engaged in an international negotiation on this subject within the OECD aimed at establishing common rules for environmental review on applications. The OECD is the main international body for negotiations on export credits and all 28 members of the OECD participate in negotiations aimed at agreeing on common approaches on environment and officially supported credits.

These negotiations should, according to the OECD Ministerial meeting of last year, be concluded before the end of this year.

 

Question no 27 by Carlos Carnero González (H-0076/01)
 Subject: Failure by Spain to comply with the EU's code of practice on arms exports
 

According to a report which has been drawn up by Barcelona University as part of the ‘Farewell to Arms’ campaign (whose participants include Amnesty International, Greenpeace, Intermon and Médecins sans Frontières) and which concerns the criteria for granting or withholding authorisation in respect of arms exports, 42% of Spanish arms exports contravene the criteria laid down in the EU’s relevant code of practice. In particular, weapons are allegedly being sold to countries which violate human rights, which are involved in conflicts, which are subject to UN embargoes or sanctions or which resell weapons to third parties. These facts are serious ones which reveal the Union’s inconsistency in this area and undermine its international efforts to secure peace and protect human rights – and which are regarded by the author of this question as totally unacceptable. Is the Council aware of this situation? Is it intending to contact the authors of the report in order to seek their opinions? Will it demand an explanation from the Spanish Government regarding the above facts and, where appropriate, immediately adopt a policy which meets the strict criteria laid down in the code of practice?

 
  
 

The European Union's position on arms exports is reflected in the Code of Conduct on Arms Exports adopted on 8 June 1998. According to the operative provisions of the Code, "each Member State will assess export licence applications for military equipment made to it on a case-by-case basis against the provisions of the Code of Conduct". Decisions on whether to grant an export licence are consequently taken by each Member State individually and it does not lie in the Council's purview to exercise any judgement on such individual decisions. The Code itself and the increased dialogue and exchange of information concerning national export policies are expected to contribute to a convergence of the arms exports policies and procedures of the European Union Member States. The annual assessment of the functioning of the Code which is reflected in the annual report is an important step in this process.

The second annual report which has been adopted by the Council and published in the Official Journal confirms that the Code has ushered in a new degree of transparency between governments in the area of arms export policies and has enabled the Member States to enter into dialogue when considering national arms licensing decisions.

 

Question no 29 by Bernd Posselt (H-0079/01)
 Subject: Protection of minorities and regional languages
 

Sweden and its neighbour Finland have introduced a range of exemplary measures to protect minorities. Does the Presidency of the Council support the work being done by Parliament and the Commission to encourage greater promotion of regional and minority languages in the EU and is thought also being given to minorities in the candidate countries in this context?

 
  
 

The honourable Member is reminded that policy towards minority and regional languages is to be considered, in accordance with the principle of subsidiarity, as falling primarily within the responsibility of Member States. However, the Council fully recognises the importance of the linguistic diversity of the EU, as exemplified by the Council's conclusions of 12 June 1995 on linguistic diversity and multilingualism in the European Union and, more recently, by the fact that the European Year of Languages 2001 (jointly adopted by the European Parliament and the Council) is aimed at the promotion of language learning in general, as is clear from article 1 of the Decision designating that event. The Council has, moreover, noted the Commission's call for proposals during 2000 for measures to promote and safeguard regional and minority languages and cultures (OJ C 266, 16.9.00, p. 15) and would also examine with interest any future proposals submitted by the Commission in this context.

As far as the candidate countries are concerned, it should be recalled that they must comply with the accession criteria established at the Copenhagen European Council in June 1993. The rights of minorities are designated as one of the political criteria which are a prerequisite for the opening of accession negotiations.

Question no 33 by Olivier Dupuis (H-0105/01)
 Subject: Release of the approximately 700 Kosovar hostages still in Serbia
 

As far as international law is concerned, the approximately 700 Albanian Kosovars currently held in Serbia were literally kidnapped by Belgrade's armed forces and police forces during their UN-imposed withdrawal from Kosovo. To all intents and purposes, these people are hostages, and the sentences passed against some of them by Pristina courts now sitting in Serbia are to all intents and purposes illegal under international law. As Mr Haekkerup has rightly pointed out, UNMIK is the only institution authorised to endorse sentences which might be passed against these people. What steps does the Council intend taking to ensure that the rules of international law are finally complied with and the approximately 700 Kosovar hostages are finally entrusted to UNMIK? Will it inform the Belgrade authorities that, unless this affair is resolved quickly, they could be charged by the International Criminal Tribunal for the former Yugoslavia with complicity in the holding of hostages?

 
  
 

The Council has been much concerned about the political prisoners detained in Serbia, including the approximately 700 Albanian Kosovars, and has repeatedly pressed for their release, not least in an EU declaration of 3 January.

The Federal Government on 22 January 2001 submitted a draft amnesty law to Parliament and expects its adoption later this month. The draft amnesty law covers political prisoners, including most of the Albanian Kosovars referred to in the honourable Member's question, but, in the EU's view, while welcomed, it does not go far enough. The Council accordingly welcomes President Kostunica's announcement during his visit to Stockholm on 31 January that the cases of those prisoners not covered by the amnesty law would be reviewed. This process would take 2-3 months.

The issue was also raised by the EU Ministerial troika visiting Belgrade on 8 February 2001.

The Council will continue to act on this issue until it is finally settled.

 

Question no 34 by Gerard Collins (H-0106/01)
 Subject: The future of air travel
 

The Commission’s Communication on the protection of air passengers and the comments by Commissioner Busquin at the January Hamburg Aeronautics Days concerning the link between research and the competitiveness of the aeronautics industry demonstrate also a better awareness of the needs of passengers/consumers. Does the Council under the Swedish Presidency intend to support major funding at EU level aimed at promoting joint research programmes between Member States aimed at making EU-led research into air transport issues a showcase for the world and how can it ensure that such research is equitably shared throughout all the Member States?

 
  
 

Pursuant to Article 163, paragraph 3 of the EC Treaty, all Community activities under the EC Treaty in the area of research and technological development, including demonstration projects, are decided on and carried out in accordance with the provisions of Title XVIII (Research and Technological Development) of the Treaty, notably within the Framework Programme for research and technological development.

As the honourable Member of Parliament is aware, aeronautics research figures largely in the current RTD (5th) Framework Programme. The Commission's proposal on the next RTD Framework Programme (2002-2006) is expected to be submitted in late February. From this moment on, it will be the joint responsibility of the European Parliament and the Council, as co-legislators, to ensure that aeronautics research is given its due importance under the forthcoming Community RTD Framework Programme.

In examining the Commission's proposal, the Council will, of course, be taking into account the significant developments in the global and European air transport market, as set out in the Commission's communication "The European Airline Industry: from Single Market to World-wide Challenges", on which the European Parliament adopted a resolution in May 2000.

The Council has also noted with interest the report of the group of 14 personalities from Europe's air transport sector, submitted at the Hamburg Aeronautics Days which calls for a "European Strategic Research Agenda in the aeronautics field, firmly based on the concept of sustainable development". The Council recalls that the underlying objectives of the Community's RTD programmes are the improvement of the scientific and technological basis to enhance industrial competitiveness and provide support to other Community policies, based on the principle of sustainable development.

 

Question no 35 by Brian Crowley (H-0108/01)
 Subject: Writing-off debts of poor countries
 

Will the Council under the Swedish Presidency indicate what initiatives it is pursuing in order to alleviate economic hardship in the least developed countries and will it press for the writing-off of the debts of poor countries and, furthermore, how does it respond to the recent Reality of Aid Report, an annual survey on development published by international aid agencies, which claims that large amounts of aid are still being used to advance commercial, political and diplomatic interests rather than to reduce poverty?

 
  
 

1. The EU and the Member States are fully aware of the difficulties many poor countries, and in particular the least developed ones, face because of their excessive debt which affects their development prospects and undermines their political stability and progress towards democracy. The EC development cooperation places a special focus on the situation of the least developed and other low-income countries. In the joint declaration of the 10th of November 2000, the Council and the Commission confirms the overarching goal of EC development cooperation: the reduction – and eventual eradication – of poverty. This objective entails a determination to eliminate inequalities and support a gradual integration of the developing countries into the world economy. In this context, the implementation of the heavily indebted poor countries initiative (HIPC) is of outmost importance.

2. In order to alleviate the debt burden, the EU and the Member States have actively taken or participated in initiatives at various levels, in conformity with their competencies.

- On 6 July 1998, the Council decided to participate fully in the HIPC (Heavily Indebted Poor Countries) initiative launched by the IMF and the World Bank. This initiative was based on a commitment by the international financial community to reduce countries' debt burden to a sustainable level, provided that the country completes a period of strong economic policy performance.

- At the Cologne Summit in June 1999, Ministers agreed to an "enhanced HIPC initiative that will provide faster, deeper and broader debt relief".

- The European Council of Cologne in 1999 highlighted the importance "of finding a lasting solution to the debt problems of the poorest countries. The European Council welcomes the common European position on improving the debt initiative for heavily indebted poor countries and supports a faster debt reduction process for the poorest countries and ambitious aims as regards the extent of debt remission. It emphasises the debt relief should be more closely linked with a strategy for poverty alleviation. The Member States are prepared to support an increase in the remission percentage on commercial loans to 90% or more at the Paris Club and will seek to promote further reductions for debt from official development aid. Within this framework, with appropriate burden sharing, Europe is ready to bear its fair share of the cost of an improved debt initiative."

- At the annual World Bank/IMF meetings in Washington in September 1999, the donor community took an ambitious set of political decisions closely linking poverty alleviation strategies, structural adjustment programmes and the debt reduction initiative.

3. Following these decisions, the EU-ACP Joint Council decided, in December 1999, that unallocated programmable resources from the EDF might be used in the form of grants for meeting the outstanding debt and debt servicing obligations to the Community of the first ACP countries which qualify under the HIPC initiative (EUR 320 million), and for contributing to the overall financing of the HIPC initiative by providing up to EUR 680 million for the HIPC Trust Fund managed by the World Bank.

In July 2000, the EC disbursed the first tranche of the contribution to the HIPC Trust Fund amounting to EUR 250 million to the African Development Bank. In early September, the EU disbursed from its budget its contribution of EUR 54 million to the HIPC Trust Fund in favour of eligible Asian and Latin American HIPCs.

Similarly, a first payment of EUR 100 million was made at the end of 2000 to the Special Trust Fund established with the EIB to alleviate the burden of debt owed by the ACP HIPCs to the Community in the form of special loans and risk capital.

The mobilisation of the remainder of the up to EUR 1 billion contribution to the HIPC Initiative will depend on actual needs and contributions from other donors.

 Subject: Japanese/Russian plans to ship nuclear waste via the Artic
 

Is the Commission aware of negotiations between Japan and Russia to ship radioactive waste from Europe to Japan via the Arctic from 2002 in order to circumvent widespread international opposition to shipments of radioactive nuclear waste and is there any action the Commission can take to halt such shipments which would have to be routed via the Irish Sea or the west of Ireland to reach the Atlantic or, alternatively, through the English Channel and the North Sea?

 
  
 

The transport of radioactive waste by sea or land is an operation which is fully controlled at a technical level. It is a routine operation which has been carried out for several decades now, whether within one country or between countries, without any unfortunate incidents such as leaks or other damage. The radioactive waste shipped has to meet a number of strict criteria, in particular concerning container characteristics, as defined by the International Atomic Energy Agency and incorporated into national law. There are also various international agreements concerning accidents, safety and transport in the sphere of the transportation of nuclear material.

In the case of maritime shipment, the containers used to transport spent nuclear fuel have been designed to be totally resistant in the event of immersion, even at great depths. In the present case, the only potential problem concerns adaptation of the specifications of the transporting vessels – which have to operate in Arctic waters – to International Maritime Organisation standards. Only that organisation has the authority to issue binding guidelines on the matter.

The fact that the transport of radioactive waste between Europe and Japan is carried out by sea, across the Arctic Ocean and close to the coastline of the Russian Federation, is not in itself grounds for taking preventive action, unless it is found that this transport is infringing IMO rules.

There is no legal instrument which would enable the Commission to restrict the use of Arctic waters for transportation of radioactive waste produced by the reprocessing of nuclear fuel in Europe.

 

Question no 113 by Efstratios Korakas (H-0116/01)
 Subject: Unjustified delays in the implementation of regulations
 

Article 56 of Regulation (EC) 1257/1999(1) on support for rural development from the EAGGF provides that this Regulation shall apply in relation to Community support as from 1 January 2000. Article 50 of Regulation (EC) 1750/1999(2) laying down detailed rules for the application of the previous Regulation (EC) 1257/1999 contains the same provision. Even though over a year has elapsed since the supposed date of implementation of these two regulations, in Greece and maybe also in other countries they have not yet been implemented, at least as far as young farmers are concerned. This has caused enormous problems for young people wishing to take up farming.

Will the Commission say why the above regulations have not been implemented as from 1 January 2000, as they should have been?

 
  
 

The honourable Member is correct in stating that Regulation (EC) No 1257/1999 on support for rural development shall apply as of 1 January 2000.

This date marks the point where projects become eligible to receive aid under the ‘rural development’ operational programme submitted by Greece in April 2000 and which also includes measures in favour of young farmers.

However, Member States are very often reluctant to implement programmes until they are sure of the Commission’s approval and indeed, this programme has not yet been approved by the Commission.

One of the reasons for this delay, which the Commission is well aware of, is the operational status of the new management authorities required following the reform of the Structural Funds. In liaison with the Greek Authorities, the Commission has drawn up a methodology and a timetable for the implementation of these management authorities, which should have been allocated sufficient personnel and which should be fully operational within the first two months of 2001. This would make it possible for the programme to be officially adopted in March and subsequently for measures to be implemented from that time on. The Greek Government has just informed the Commission that the management authority responsible for the Rural Development programme shall be operational by the end of February 2001. This programme should therefore be approved shortly by the Commission.

Nonetheless, the Commission would like to assure the honourable Member that Commission Regulation No 2075/2000, amending Regulation No 1750/1999, stipulates that, as regards applications for aid made by young farmers for the first time in 2000, one of the criteria – the requirement of being under 40 years of age – must be fulfilled at the time of presenting the application and not at the time that a decision is made to grant aid in that specific case. This interim provision will make it possible to partly offset the effects of the above delay affecting young farmers who submitted an application for aid before first setting up business during that year.

Congratulations are due to the Commission for the great step made towards developing a new malaria vaccine referred to in the press release of 11 January 2001 'New malaria vaccines – made in Europe'. What action is the Commission now planning in order to ensure that the development of malaria vaccines continues in Europe and to ensure that the necessary human and institutional resources are available for the development and testing of vaccines in malaria-affected regions, in particular in Africa? Question no 115 by Lisbeth Grönfeldt Bergman (H-0119/01)
 Subject: Discrimination against e-tailers
 

A recently published study shows that, in Sweden, suppliers are refusing to supply e-tailers and are charging them higher prices than retailers who do not sell to consumers over the Internet. The reason seems to be that, because they are more cost-effective, e-tailers can keep their prices lower, which gives them a competitive edge over their suppliers' other customers. Consumers are the main losers as a result of such discrimination against e-tailers, since e-tailing enables consumers who live in areas far from big cities and specialist shops to buy goods to which they previously did not have access. They may no longer be able to get hold of the goods they want at all if e-tailing is not given the conditions it needs to survive.

Does the Commission consider such conduct on the part of suppliers to be compatible with EC law and in line with its own endeavours to improve Europe's competitiveness in the new economy?

 
  
 

Competition Law only prohibits producers refusing to supply retailers or charging them different prices in the following instances:

When the pricing policy of the producer is governed by an agreement or a concerted practice made between it and at least one other producer which may affect trade between Member States or

if the producer holds a dominant position on a relevant market within a substantial part of the common market.

If the honourable Member could provide more information on the companies involved, the markets on which they operate, their market shares and the possible effects on trade between Member States, the Commission would be happy to ascertain whether the refusal to deal and price discrimination constitute infringements of Community competition law.

 

Question no 116 by John Walls Cushnahan (H-0121/01)
 Subject: Investment in Irish Regional Airports
 

Are there any EU policies/regulations/rules which would prevent the Irish Government from providing financial aid for improving facilities at Irish regional airports such as terminal and apron development and additional car parking?

 
  
 

In the past the provision of airport capacity has been organised largely as a non-market activity. In that context the Commission has always considered the construction and development of airport infrastructure as being a measure of general policy which cannot be controlled by the Commission under the EC Treaty rules on State aid.(1)1

However, the airport industry has been going through a process of deep transformation for the last years. In the increasingly liberal and competitive environment of air transport, airport operations have also been changing in nature and have been evolving from government branches towards commercial undertakings. A major feature in this evolution is the emerging of a certain degree of competition between airports. This evolution is reflected in a recent judgement of the Court of First Instance(2)2, which clearly states that airports are undertakings submitted to competition law.

Under these circumstances, competition law, namely Articles 87 and 86 of the EC Treaty may apply to airports too. Of course, the application of the relevant provisions of the Treaty to the public financing of airport infrastructure must duly take into account the Community interest as well as the general economic interest airports may be entrusted with.

For these reasons it is difficult to give a general answer to an individual case. A case by case assessment is necessary, in order to evaluate if, in the cases at stake, the provision of financial aid has an impact on competition between airlines and between airports.

 
 

(1)1 See Commission’s Guidelines for the application of Articles 92 and 93 of the EC Treaty and 61 of the EEA Agreement to State aids in the aviation sector, OJ C 350 of 10.12.1994.
(2)2 Judgement of the Court of First Instance of 12 December 2000, in the case T-128/98 Aéroports de Paris vs Commission.

 

Question no 117 by Bárbara Dührkop Dührkop (H-0123/01)
 Subject: European vehicle licence plates
 

Is the Commission aware that the French traffic police are stopping and imposing fines of Ptas 25 000 on Spanish motorists driving in France who have not exchanged their Spanish licence plates for the new European model which was adopted several months ago in Spain? The reason given for the fine is a failure to display the new European licence plate, and has nothing to do with whether or not the vehicle displays the letter denoting its country of origin, which is compulsory under the Vienna Convention.

Given that in Spain all new cars automatically have the new model licence plates, and that older cars may retain the old model or, on payment of a flat­rate fee, may exchange them on a voluntary basis, and since there are no European rules obliging Member States to replace old licence plates with the new model, what are the Commission's views on the attitude of the French authorities described above? Will the Commission take steps to obtain the relevant information and act accordingly?

 
  
 

The requirement to display the distinguishing sign of the country where a vehicle is registered originates in Article 37 of the 1968 Vienna Convention on Road Traffic.

Council Regulation (EC) No 2411/98 provides for the mutual recognition by Member States of the distinguishing sign of the Member State in which motor vehicles are registered provided that these signs conform to the provisions laid down in the annex.

As the honourable Member states, Community law does not impose the use of the so-called Community model licence plate described in that regulation.

There are therefore no grounds for police action against drivers of vehicles purely because they are not using the European model licence plate.

If proof (e.g. copies of police reports) were to be brought to the attention of the Commission confirming that the French authorities are imposing fines in such circumstances, then the Commission would undoubtedly refer the matter immediately to the French authorities.

 

Question no 118 by Konstantinos Alyssandrakis (H-0131/01)
 Subject: Grave threat to citizens' health from increased atmospheric pollution
 

A recent study by the Institute of Public Health Studies in Valencia has confirmed what has been known for years, namely the harmful and indeed fatal consequences of atmospheric pollution for the health of inhabitants of the Planet. 6% of annual deaths are due to atmospheric pollution and, more particularly, to atmospheric pollution caused by the large quantities of sulphur dioxide, nitrogen oxide, carbon monoxide and ozone emitted. It is well established that, apart from industry, exhaust fumes from car motors constitute the main source of pollution. Given that similar studies carried out in Europe have found that atmospheric pollution is responsible not only for cardiovascular diseases but also for respiratory problems, what specific measures does the Commission intend to promote in order effectively to protect citizens' health?

 
  
 

Over the last few years the Community has agreed a substantial package of measures to protect citizens against air pollution. Directive 1999/30/EC sets ambient air quality standards for sulphur dioxide, nitrogen dioxide, particulate matter and lead. A similar Directive, adopted last year, sets standards for carbon monoxide and benzene. All are based on World Health Organisation air quality guidelines. The standards must be met by 2005 or in some cases 2010. But Member States will have to provide the public with information on levels of pollution from the start and must make public plans for improving the worst areas. Proposals setting objectives for ozone, and national emission ceilings to help meet those objectives, are currently before Parliament for Second Reading.

Directive 98/69/EC on light vehicles and Directive 1999/96/EC on Heavy Duty Vehicles are introducing progressively tighter emission limits which will help to meet air quality goals. Directive 97/68/EC is tackling off-road vehicles. Fuel standards are also subject to progressively tighter legislation and the Commission has recently completed a consultation exercise on lowering the sulphur content in fuels to below 10 parts per million.

Member States are implementing the IPPC Directive, amongst others, to reduce emissions from industry. And the proposed revision to the Directive on Large Combustion Plants, also at Second Reading, will further reduce emissions of both sulphur dioxide and nitrogen oxides.

But there is still more to be done. The proposal for a Sixth Environmental Action Programme explains that the Commission intends to set up an integrated thematic programme to develop future policy on air quality. The Clean Air For Europe Programme will aim to ensure that existing legislation is fully implemented, and will review the latest research on health effects and other matters to determine whether standards need to be revised and if so, how they could be met cost effectively. The Commission will present a Communication to Parliament and Council this session, setting out in more detail the aims and scope of the CAFE programme. It is aimed to produce a first integrated strategy by the end of 2004.
