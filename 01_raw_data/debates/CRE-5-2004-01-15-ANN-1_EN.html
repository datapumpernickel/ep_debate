QUESTIONS TO THE COMMISSION
Question no 27 by Astrid Thors (H-0826/03)
 Subject: The Commission's commitment to the Northern Dimension
 

In its various dialogues with the EU’s new and old neighbours, it seems that the Commission’s commitment is variable both in its depth and its breadth. On some issues and in some regions the Commission is very active, e.g. in the energy dialogue with the Mediterranean and South-East Europe. On the other hand, it is up to the Member States in the Baltic Sea region to implement the action programme for the Northern Dimension. It is very rarely that the Commission participates at Commissioner level in the ministerial meetings on the Northern Dimension.

What plans has the Commission to step up its commitment in the framework of the Northern Dimension?

 
  
 

(EN) The Union’s Northern Dimension policy has its own specific character, distinct from other policy frameworks operating in other geographic contexts. This has been confirmed several times by the Council and in three Ministerial Conferences on the Northern Dimension held since 2001.

Within the mandate set by several European Councils and General Affairs Councils, the Commission has consistently given its full commitment and support to the Northern Dimension, both at the political level and in the implementation of a wide range of concrete activities. This commitment, and the Commission’s leading role in the context of the Northern Dimension, has frequently been commended by Member States, Partner Countries and the main regional organisations of the Northern Dimension area.

The Commission’s commitment is also reflected in its regular participation in the work of these regional bodies, such as the Council of the Baltic Sea States, the Barents Euro-Arctic Council and the Arctic Council. Unfortunately, it is not always possible for a Commissioner to take part in high-level meetings convened by such bodies, but in such circumstances the Commission is always represented by senior officials.

 

Question no 28 by Bill Newton Dunn (H-0828/03)
 Subject: Tibet
 

When His Holiness the Dalai Lama met U.S. President Bush in the Oval Office last autumn, the State Department's Special Representative for Tibetan Affairs was present at the meeting between the two leaders.

The EU's Joint Budgetary Authority - the Parliament and the Council - have now voted for two successive years that the EU Commission should also appoint a Special Representative for Tibetan Affairs.

Why does the Commission ignore the recommendation repeated to it by both Council and Parliament? Why does the Commission believe that the peaceful values of Tibetans are not worth supporting in public? Is it afraid of what Beijing would say?

 
  
 

(EN) Channels of communication do already exist between Beijing and the Union which have allowed the latter to regularly express, be it in the framework of the bilateral dialogue on Human Rights or at the highest political level at the occasion of the Summits, its concern with respect to the preservation of the cultural, religious and linguistic identity of the Tibetan people.

The Union has repeatedly called for the establishment of a direct dialogue between the Dalai-Lama and the Chinese authorities. The Commission sees this as the only realistic manner to define a peaceful and lasting solution to the question of Tibet which would lead to genuine autonomy for this region.

In this context, the Union finds encouraging the statement made by the two Special Envoys of the Dalai-Lama after their recent visit to China in May/June 2003.

Against this background, the Commission, as well as the Member States, wonder whether the appointment of a Special Representative for Tibetan Affairs who would probably not be authorised to travel to Tibet or hold official contacts with the Chinese authorities would bring any added value to the existing dialogue mechanism. Moreover, it is of the opinion that everything which could possibly jeopardise the present dialogue process recently started and weaken the political capacity of the Union to facilitate it should be cautiously looked at.

The Union will no doubt continue to pay the greatest attention to the situation in Tibet and the Heads of Mission in Beijing will certainly be invited, as they did in the past, to further monitor developments in this region.

 

Question no 29 by Richard Howitt (H-0835/03)
 Subject: Peace process in Sri Lanka
 

Given the recent visit by the Commissioner for External Relations to Sri Lanka, what prospects would the Commission give for the ongoing peace process there?

 
  
 

(EN) The Peace Process in Sri Lanka is on hold at the moment. Just as the Liberation Tigers of the Tamul Eelam (LTTE) was getting ready to resume its participation in the Norwegian facilitated Peace Talks a political crisis over the handling of the Peace Process broke out in the Colombo between the President and the Prime Minister. The President felt obliged to take control over three key ministries among them the Ministry for Defense.

As to the prospects for the Peace Process the Commission wishes to highlight the following:

The Ceasefire has held for almost 2 years now in spite of the de facto suspension of the Peace Talks since April 2003;

Both the President and the Prime Minister remain committed to solve the political crisis and above all remain committed to the Peace Process;

The Commission has also noted that the President’s and the Prime Minister’s parties cooperated well in Parliament in December adopting among others budget for the Defence Ministry with a historical majority;

Both parties have very little to gain from a resumption of hostilities;

The people of Sri Lanka in the North and the South demand a peaceful solution to the conflict;

The LTTE also remains committed to the Peace Process awaiting a political solution in the South;

The International Community is doing its outmost to encourage a solution. All parties know that the resumption of the Peace process is a prerequisite for the fulfilment of the pledges provided at the Tokyo Conference;

But - in spite of the above and in spite of great strives by the two parties to find a common understanding including an acceptable institutional basis for the participation in the Peace Talks the situation unfortunately remains unchanged for the time being;

It is difficult to predict when a solution can be found and thus when the Peace Talks can resume.

 

Question no 30 by Myrsini Zorba (H-0836/03)
 Subject: Humanitarian aid for Iraq
 

As announced at the Madrid Donors' Conference last October, the European Union and its Member States are contributing some 700 million euros to Iraq until the end of 2004, including 200 million from the Community budget. Over the period from now until 2007, the funding for Iraq will amount to about 1.3 billion euros. According to information given by the Commission's External Relations DG, there is a rather limited number of Commission staff directly deployed in Iraq dealing with humanitarian aid issues.

How is the Commission planning to manage the allocation of the EU funding for Iraq? How is the distribution of humanitarian aid provided by the EU going to be monitored? Is the Commission intending to increase the number of its staff working directly in Iraq and is there a timetable for this?

 
  
 

(EN) As the honourable Member rightly notes, in Madrid the Union, including the Member States and acceding countries, pledged just over €1.2 billion for reconstruction in Iraq; some pledged for 2004, others made multi-annual pledges. Of this total, €200 million was promised from the community budget for reconstruction until the end of 2004. These monies come in addition to the €100 million that was committed from the budget for humanitarian assistance in 2003.

When it comes to humanitarian assistance, the European Humanitarian Aid (ECHO)’s office in Baghdad remains open, manned by 8 local staff, including security personnel. In addition ECHO has three expatriate technical assistants who, due to the security situation, are currently stationed in Amman, Jordan.

The Member of the Commission in charge of External Relations can reassure the Honourable Member, on behalf of the Member of the Commission in charge of Development and Humanitarian Aid, that ECHO local staff continue to carry out monitoring of ongoing operations - albeit at a slower pace due to the security constraints. Subject to security, ECHO's technical assistants are and will continue to make monitoring visits to Iraq from Amman and Kuwait. ECHO is also undertaking regular coordination meetings with implementing partners outside Iraq. All these actions mean that a degree of control albeit remote of humanitarian operations in Iraq has been possible.

The situation is different for reconstruction. The Commission’s special representative in Iraq – Mr Bernard Savage – was re-called after the August 19 United Nations bombing and, given the spiralling violence, a decision was taken not to ask him to return to Baghdad.

No decision has been taken on when staff might return because, without a clear indication that the security situation is stabilising, the Commission is not prepared to put Union’s visibility before safety.

The Commission is looking at creative solutions, such as attaching additional staff to the existing Commission delegation in Amman. It recognises that the lack of a presence creates constraints, not only for monitoring the impact of EC reconstruction assistance, but also for engaging with the Iraqi administration and other donors. There is need to be prepared for the day when a return becomes possible.

 

Question no 31 by Bart Staes (H-0795/03)
 Subject: Obsolete pesticides
 

The German food scandal (nitrophen-contaminated grain, June 2002) can be attributed partly to carelessness and a serious error. On the other hand, this food scandal makes it clear that more attention needs to be paid to the problem of 'obsolete pesticides'. Tonnes of these pesticides, the vast majority of which are completely banned in Europe, still linger on, inter alia in the former Eastern Bloc. They are seeping into the ground, with disastrous consequences. It is suspected that the sites where the pesticides were produced are also seriously polluted. Although no complete inventory of these pesticides exists, it is known for example that in the applicant country Poland between 50 000 and 60 000 tonnes of such pesticides remain in storage without proper management. Worldwide, it has been estimated that one million tonnes of POPs, dioxins and PCBs are in a similar situation.

Does the Commission recognise the risk to public health which these pesticides represent, and what measures will it take to solve this problem?

 
  
 

(EN) The Commission has already expressed its concerns about the environmental and public health risks caused by obsolete pesticides in a response to written questions submitted earlier by a number of Honourable Members. The importance of this matter has also been highlighted in the 6th Environmental Action Programme(1) and the Communication Towards a Thematic Strategy on the Strategy on the Sustainable Use of Pesticides(2).

The Commission would also like to note that, if requested by an accession or candidate country, capacity building or investments in safe destruction facilities of obsolete pesticides can be covered in principle by existing Community financial instruments. In addition, the Commission puts emphasis on the fact that obsolete pesticides are to be regarded as waste. Therefore the existing Community legislation on waste fully applies for Member States.

In this context the Protocol to the regional United Nations Economic Commission for Europe (UNECE) Convention on Long-Range Transboundary Air Pollution (CLRTAP) on persistant organic pollutants (POPs) and the global Stockholm Convention on POPs play a significant role, because POPs represent an important part of the total amount of obsolete pesticides. The Commission has in June 2003 adopted proposals for the ratification and implementation of these Conventions and the Council and the Parliament are already in the process of handling these proposals.

 
 

(1) Decision No 1600/2002/EC of the European Parliament and of the Council of 22 July 2002 laying down the Sixth Community Environment Action Programme
(2) COM(2002)349 final

 

Question no 32 by Alexander de Roo (H-0799/03)
 Subject: Obsolete pesticides
 

The problem of obsolete pesticides is related to the possibility of the contamination of (surface) water. Since the Ukraine is part of the Danube and Vistula basin, the framework for Community action in the field of water policy is applicable to the whole basin, taking into account the planning and execution of measures to ensure protection and sustainable use of water in the framework of the river basin. Within a river basin where use of water may have transboundary effects, the requirements for the achievement of the environmental objectives established under this Directive, and in particular all programmes of measures, should be coordinated for the whole of the river basin district. For river basins extending beyond the boundaries of the Community, Member States should endeavour to ensure the appropriate coordination with the relevant non-member States.

Is the Commission prepared to apply the Water Framework Directive 2000/60/EC(1) in order to tackle this current and future problem?

 
  
 

(EN) Article 3, paragraphs 3, 4 and 5 of the Water Framework Directive 2000/60/EC(2) require the co-ordination of the management for transboundary river basin districts with the aim of achieving the objectives of the Directive for the entire basin. Where the river basin district extends beyond the territory of the Community, the Member State shall endeavour to establish appropriate co-ordination with the relevant third country. Whereas co-ordination for the Danube river basin has been established through the International Commission for the Protection of the River Danube, the arrangements between Poland and Ukraine as regards the Vistula river are still under discussion.

Whereas the problem of obsolete pesticides could be partially addressed through the Water Framework Directive if these pesticides contribute to water pollution and thereby prevent the achievement of the Directive’s objectives, it may be more appropriate to tackle the whole problem by other means e.g. through waste legislation. In addition, since according to the Water Framework Directive the respective measures need only be implemented by the end of 2009, the Directive may not be appropriate to tackle already existing pollution problems from obsolete pesticides, in particular outside the Union.

The Commission would also like to underline that during the recently held Union-Ukraine Partnership and Cooperation agreement Subcommittee dealing with environment topics, the Ukrainian party made no mention whatsoever of the obsolete pesticides issue.

 
 

(1) OJ L 327, 22.12.2000, p. 1.
(2) OJ L 327, 22.12.2000

 

Question no 33 by Juan de Dios Izquierdo Collado (H-0802/03)
 Subject: 'Quick Start' programme
 

Why did the Commission not wait for the European Parliament's decision before drawing up the 'Quick Start' list?

Why does the list not include the cross-border Vitoria-Bilbao-Irún-Hendaya-Dax rail link, given that the Commission's proposal is that work should be completed by 2010?

 
  
 

(FR) The Commission’s report dated 11 November 2003 on the Growth Initiative does indeed constitute a Quick Start programme for certain sections of the Trans-European Networks.

The report gives the following response to the request of the European Council of 16-17 October 2003, which, in order that there might be rapid progress that would permit it to proceed to a final evaluation of the [Growth] Initiative and to come to definite decisions at its December meeting:

‘[invited] the Commission, the EIB and the relevant formations of the Council to establish, without prejudice to the priorities identified in the Commission proposal, a ‘Quick Start Programme’ identifying a list of projects in an enlarged Union based on transparent criteria, along with assessments of their significance for the integration of the internal market in the enlarged Europe, their economic and financial viability, their impact on growth and the leveraging effect on private capital;

[invited] the Commission and the EIB to present, in time for thorough preparation by the Economic and Financial Committee of the 25 November ECOFIN Council meeting, their final reports on the growth initiative.’

The projects included in this programme cover solely the cross-border sections of the priority projects proposed by the Commission on 1 October 2003, subject to their compliance with the following four criteria: (1) that they should be ready to start immediately – the objective being that substantial investments be made by the end of 2006; (2) that they should have strong cross-border impact; (3) that they should yield positive results in terms of growth, jobs and (4) should be beneficial in terms of the protection of the environment. The southern section – from Vitoria to Dax – of the Atlantic branch of the high-speed train South priority project was not included because, although the Spanish side of the project is ready, decisions have not yet been taken on the French side. The Commission regrets the lack of progress on the French side and the effects this will have on links to the Iberian Peninsula.

Delays of this kind in the realisation of cross-border projects are among the Commission’s major concerns. The 1 October proposal includes measures to speed up as much as possible the reaching of decisions on these, involving, for certain projects, the appointment of European coordinators and the production of transnational surveys.

The Commission wishes to assure the honourable Member that the ‘Quick Start’ programme does nothing to change the Commission’s priorities as announced in its proposal of 1 October 2003, and that the ultimate objective is the completion of all the 29 priority projects proposed by the Commission on that date. The Commission’s proposals were the subject of a Council agreement on a common position last December, and are now before Parliament for it to arrive at a decision on them following debate. The Commission is appreciative of Parliament’s commitment to playing its part in evaluating the proposal of 1 October.

 

Question no 34 by Nuala Ahern (H-0804/03)
 Subject: Euratom
 

How far does the proposal for a Council (Euratom) directive on setting out basic obligations and general principles on the safety of nuclear installations (COM(2003)0032/final - C5-0228/2003 - CNS 2003/0021) take into account control of the release of the radioactive pollutants from nuclear reprocessing plants such as Sellafield in the UK? Does the Commission consider it important to halt the release of the radioactive pollutant technetium-99 from nuclear reprocessing plants in order to protect the marine environment?

 
  
 

(EN) A legislation to ensure a high level of nuclear safety within the enlarged Union will be an important contribution to protect general public and workers against the hazards of ionizing radiations. The Court of Justice of the European Communities in its judgment of 10 December 2002(1) stated that it is not appropriate to draw an artificial distinction between the protection of health of the general public and the safety of the sources of ionizing radiation. Radiation protection and nuclear safety have, indeed, a common health protection objective, namely protection against ionizing radiation.

The control of the radioactive discharges in general, and from nuclear reprocessing plants in particular, is nevertheless specifically covered by the Euratom Treaty and the existing acquis communautaire derived from Title II, Chapter 3 of the Treaty.

Under Directive 96/29/Euratom laying down the Basic Safety Standards for Radiation Protection,(2) the examination and approval of plans for the discharge of radioactive effluents is a condition for authorizing the activities of – among other installations – reprocessing plants.

Under Article 37 Euratom, before authorizing discharges, Member States must submit to the Commission the general data on such planned discharges, so that the Commission can judge their possible impact on other Member States. Following a Commission Recommendation,(3) Member States periodically report to the Commission on the radioactive liquid and atmospheric waste discharges from nuclear power reactors and reprocessing plants.

At international level, the Community, as a contracting party to the OSPAR Convention (Convention for the Protection of the Marine Environment of the North East Atlantic),(4) is represented within the Convention by the Commission. Consequently, the Commission is firmly committed to the attainment of the objective of the OSPAR strategy with regard to radioactive substances, which requires the progressive and substantial reduction of all radioactive discharges, including technetium-99, by 2020.

 
 

(1) Case C29/99
(2) Council Directive 96/29/Euratom of 13 May 1996 laying down basic safety standards for the protection of the health of workers and the general public against the dangers arising from ionizing radiation, OJ L159 of 29.6.96
(3) Commission Recommendation 99/829/Euratom of 6 December 1999 on the application of Article 37 of the Euratom Treaty, OJ L 324 of 16.12.99.
(4) Council Decision 98/249/EC of 7 October 1997 on the conclusion of the Convention for the protection of the marine environment of the north-east Atlantic, OJ L 104 of 3.4.1998.

 

Question no 35 by Cristina Gutiérrez-Cortines (H-0805/03)
 Subject: Funding the Natura 2000 network
 

Directive 92/43/EEC(1) on habitats combines ambitious biodiversity protection objectives with the funding of the wherewithal to provide that protection, and its adoption was welcomed by European citizens who own land of major ecological value, since they saw it as an acknowledgement of natural values and an expression of Community solidarity and joint responsibility. As time as gone by, all the obligations laid down in the Directive are falling on the owners of Natura 2000 land, but the subsidies provided for Article 8 of the Directive are not arriving; the result has been rejection of the Directive throughout society, including on the part of the landowners who form part of the Natura 2000 network.

Is the Commission aware that the major victim of this passivity is the very biodiversity we are seeking to protect?

Is the Commission aware that the delays affecting the initiatives already underway, e.g. the Communication on the Funding of Natura 2000, are exacerbating the negative way in which Natura 2000 is viewed?

When does the Commission intend to make the funding mechanism of Natura 2000 operational, and how does it intend to proceed with this funding, provided for in Article 8 of the Directive?

What does the Commission intend to do to regain the trust and support of landowners for the Natura 2000 network?

 
  
 

(EN) In line with the principle of subsidiarity the Habitats Directive clearly places the primary responsibility for the ongoing management of the Natura 2000 network with the Member States. The Commission has consistently encouraged that the management of sites should take place within the context of management plans produced with broad consultation among all interested parties. The majority of Member States are following this approach and this is progressively reassuring rural communities that the management of the sites is handled at the appropriate regional or local level.

With respect to the commitment to funding in Article 8 of the Habitats directive, it is not true to say that no Community funds are being spent on Natura 2000 sites. Article 8 foresees that funding should come from existing Community funds and a number of these - in particular Rural Development Policy and its agri-environment measure, Structural Funds and the LIFE-Nature programme - already provide substantial support to the establishment of Natura 2000. The assessment of current funding has shown significant differences across Member States in the use that has been made of the funding instruments which are currently available. It must be stressed that these differences are not the result of Community decisions but of the choices and priorities established at national level for the use of Community funds allocated under the various Community programmes.

As the process of designating the sites for Natura 2000 is reaching completion, the Commission has committed itself to review the funding arrangements and consider how these could be organised in the future “within the limits of the resources made available under the Community’s decisions”(2). In preparing its communication on this issue, the Commission has been helped by the work of an Expert Group. However, the absence in most Member States of any detailed assessment of the likely costs of managing the network has been a significant difficulty. The Commission intends to produce the Communication early in 2004 within the broader context of its proposal on the future financial perspectives.

The Commission is fully aware of the need to improve communication and awareness of the implications and objectives of the Natura 2000 network. For those individual landowners affected this clearly must be with their full involvement via the preparation of management plans which clarify the implications for their land and provide the basis for programming the necessary financial interventions from regional, national or Community funds as appropriate.

 
 

(1) OJ L 206, 22.7.1992, p. 7.
(2) Council Directive 92/43/EEC of 21 May 1992 (12th “whereas”)

 

Question no 36 by Caroline F. Jackson (H-0807/03)
 Subject: Derogations under Directive 96/48/EC
 

Has the United Kingdom applied for derogation for any Technical Specifications for Interoperability under Article 7(b) or Article 7(c) of Directive 96/48/EC(1)? If so, which one? And, if it has not, is it too late to apply for derogation from the TSI for the control-command and signalling subsystem?

 
  
 

(EN) United Kingdom has applied a derogation from application of Technical Specifications of Interoperability (TSI) for Section 1 of the Channel Tunnel Rail Link (CTRL). This derogation was notified to the Commission on the 18th of September 2003 under Article 7(a) of Directive 96/48/EC on the interoperability of the trans-European high speed rail system.

The general objective of derogations is to avoid the application of one or more TSIs in the case of a specific project, where the application of these TSIs compromises the economic viability of this project. However, in order to maintain the compatibility of the existing rail system of each Member State, TSIs include, where appropriate, special implementing provisions in the form of specific cases. For example, the TSI for Infrastructure adopted under Commission Decision 2002/732/EC provides for specific cases for the British network, in such a way that a derogation for this TSI is not appropriate.

Should the United Kingdom encounter major difficulties in applying the TSI for the control-command and signalling subsystem adopted under Commission Decision 2002/731/EC to future projects, derogations may be requested where the conditions of Article 7 of Directive 96/48/EC are met.

 
 

(1) OJ L 235, 17.9.1996, p. 6.

 

Question no 37 by Paulo Casaca (H-0810/03)
 Subject: Down-grading of nature conservation in the post-2006 financial perspective
 

In the final report on the financing of the Natura 2000 network which has been drawn up by the working party on Article 8 of the Commission's Directive on habitats (92/43/EEC(1)), it is concluded that an annual budget of between EUR 3400 and 5700 million is needed just to run the Natura 2000 network.

The urgent need for investment in management of the natural environment was dramatically confirmed last summer when an extensive part of the Natura 2000 network in Portugal was totally destroyed by fire.

Unfortunately, the Commission has failed to reflect its working party's conclusions in its 2004 draft budget and there is no guarantee that it will do so in its proposal for the 2006-2011 financial perspective.

Can the Commission state when and how it will finally act upon the recommendations of its working party concerning Article 8 of the Directive on habitats?

 
  
 

(EN) The Commission believes that the sites included in the Natura 2000 network represent Europe’s most important wildlife areas, thus the implementation of the Habitats Directive is a major contribution towards achieving the objective of halting the loss of biodiversity by 2010, which was agreed by Heads of State and Government in Gothenburg in 2001.

Following the Report of the Working Group on Article 8 of the Habitats Directive(2), the Commission has worked on the preparation of a Communication to the Council and the Parliament, which aims to address the issue of financing for the Natura 2000 network. The Commission intends to issue the Communication on Financing Natura 2000 early in 2004. The Commission has asked Member States and candidate countries to provide their best possible estimates for the financial needs of the network and has launched a broad public stakeholder consultation on the issue.

The Commission has not proposed any additional resources specially assigned to Natura 2000 within the 2004 draft budget, due to the fact that the financial allocations in the Community budget have been fixed for the period up to 2006. However, consistent with Article 8 which foresees that funding should be from existing Community funds, the Commission would like to indicate that current Union financial instruments provide support for measures promoting nature conservation and the management of Natura 2000 sites. In particular within Rural Development programmes several agri-environmental measures apply in Natura 2000 areas. Furthermore, the so-called art. 16 measure for areas with environmental restrictions under the Rural Development Programmes providing for payments to farmers who are subject to such restrictions, is now applicable exclusively to Natura 2000 sites, after the 2003 Common Agricultural Policy Reform. The Structural Funds, as well as the LIFE-Nature funds dedicated to Natura 2000 sites, also offer substantial support to the establishment, innovative actions and management of the Natura 2000 network.

 
 

(1) OJ L 206, 22.7.1992, p. 7
(2) Directive 92/43/EEC of May 1992 on the conservation of natural habitats and of wild fauna and flora, OJ L 206, 22/7/1992, p. 7

 

Question no 38 by Niall Andrews (H-0811/03)
 Subject: Appropriate use of ERDF funds
 

The sum of €5 255 536 in ERDF funding was received for a project involving the conversion of St Michael and St John’s Church in Essex Street West, Temple Bar, Dublin 8 into cultural facilities in 1996/1997. The Viking Centre in the premises subsequently closed in February 2002 and the property has lain derelict since.

Is the Commission aware of this situation and if so, does it consider that this is an appropriate use of public funds from the ERDF? What action does the Commission propose to take vis-à-vis the public authorities concerned, to ensure that this property continues to be used for cultural activities as intended under the terms and conditions of the ERDF grant?

 
  
 

(EN) The Dublin Viking Adventure project in Temple Bar, Dublin, was funded by the European Regional Development Fund under the Local, Urban and Rural Development (LURD) Operational Programme 1994-1999. Section 5.2.5 of the Final Report on the programme drew attention to the fact that the project commenced business in May 1996 but ceased operations in February 2002. The main reason for the closure was the significant drop in visitor numbers.

In April 2002, Temple Bar Properties invited proposals from suitable cultural bodies to manage and operate the building that housed Dublin Viking Adventure. The Commission has been informed that, since the closure of the Dublin Viking Adventure Project, the premises have been in regular use as a public exhibition centre and interpretative centre.

Under the Local, Urban and Rural Development Operational Programme 1994-99, and the Tourism Operational Programme 1994-99, the ERDF funded many hundreds of projects in the domain of tourism. It is inevitable that, with such a large number of projects, there will be some that fail to achieve their objectives even after rigorous project appraisal and selection procedures have been applied. When a project that is funded by the ERDF closes, the Commission encourages the national authorities where possible to continue using the facilities for similar activities, as is the case here: according to the information the Commission received from the Irish authorities, the premises are still being used for cultural activities.

 

Question no 39 by María Luisa Bergaz Conesa (H-0812/03)
 Subject: Lada-Velilla (Castilla and León) power line
 

In April 2003 the Commission sent the Spanish authorities a reasoned opinion on the faulty application of Article 6 of Directive 92/43/EEC(1) (Habitats) and Article 4 of Directive 79/409/EEC(2) (Birds). The Madrid High Court of Justice has endorsed this opinion and annulled the authorisation to construct the power line between Lada-Velilla in the Picos de Europa in Castilla and León.

Can the Commission guarantee that no route will be accepted which has not been subjected to a prior impact assessment? Will a route for the power line which ‘goes round’ the protected natural area be considered? (FR) In the course of the previous reforms to which the honourable Member referred, the Commission in effect rejected the option of completely including cotton within the scheme for compensating producers of arable crops; its reasons for doing so were the consequent high levels of aid per hectare and the associated risks of low levels of production and ‘premium hunting’.

The reform now proposed is different in so far as support for cotton production by means of specific aid per hectare affects only part of the budget allocated to cotton producers in the course of the period under consideration.
