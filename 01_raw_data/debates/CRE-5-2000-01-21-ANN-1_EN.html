Questions to the Council
Question no 17 by Emmanouil Bakopoulos (H-0810/99)
 Subject: Cyber crime
 

In its Common Position 1999/364/JHA, adopted on 27 May 1999, the Council decided to recognise and support the Council of Europe's Draft Convention on Cyber Crime. Article 1 of the Common Position states that the Member States shall support the drawing up of the Draft Convention and shall advocate the inclusion in the Convention of provisions which will facilitate the effective investigation and prosecution of criminal offences related to computer systems and data.

What specific provisions will the Member States submit? Does the Council envisage a European body to monitor and oversee the prosecution of cyber crime at European level?

Seven months after the adoption of the Common Position, can the Council say what stage has been reached with the Draft Convention on Cyber Crime and what practical measures the Member States have taken?

 
  
 

Question 1: What specific provisions will the Member States submit?

Article 1 of the “Common Position of 27 May 1999 on negotiations of the European Union relating to the Draft Convention on Cyber Crime held in the Council of Europe”(1) expresses general support for the Draft Cyber Crime Convention, in particular for provisions regarding the investigation and prosecution of “cyber crime” offences. Momentarily, Member States contribute to the development of the Draft Convention, which is still under discussion in the Council of Europe, by supplementing the existing draft provisions where necessary. This is the case regarding provisions on substantive criminal law, but also concerning procedural law and, above all, in the field of international cooperation.

Question 2: Does the Council envisage a European body to monitor and oversee the prosecution of cyber crime at European level?

During the UK Presidency, the Council underlined in its “Elements of its strategy against high tech crime”(2) the importance of joining unbureaucratic and informal practical arrangements, such as the already existing G8 24/7 points of contact network. Until now, several Member States are part of this flexible network. It facilitates, above all, the investigation of cyber crime. At this stage, the Presidency supports this flexible approach and does not envisage setting up a proper European body to monitor and oversee the prosecution of cyber crime. However, the role of Europol in this area is kept under review.

Question 3: Seven months after the adoption of the Common Position, can the Council say what stage has been reached with the Draft Convention on Cyber Crime and what practical measures Member States have taken?

As the Draft Convention is still being negotiated, it would be premature to ask Member States to already take practical measures.

The mandate of the relative Committee of the Council of Europe (PCCY) to negotiate the Convention has been extended until the end of 2000. Especially the provisions on international cooperation are intensively discussed.

 
 

(1) Official Journal of the European Communities No L 142, p. 1 from 5.6.1999.
(2) Council document 11893/2/98, CRIMORG 157 REV 2.

 

Question no 18 by María Izquierdo Rojo (H-0812/99)
 Subject: Prospects for new policies for peace in the Mediterranean
 

What decisions, if any, does the Council intend to adopt to push forward with policies for peace and progress in the Mediterranean? Or will the Euro-Mediterranean developments which began so hopefully with the Barcelona Conference continue to slow to a stop?

 
  
 

The Council has consistently supported peace in the Middle East, through its own political initiatives, its constructive support for the efforts of the US and other key players and considerable economic EU assistance for the Peace Process. The Council welcomes the fact that the Middle East Peace Process is not only on the move but is also gaining momentum on both its Syrian and Palestinian Tracks.

On the basis of this positive development, the multilateral part of the Peace Process should soon be able to resume normal activities, starting with a meeting of its Steering Group at Ministerial level in Moscow later this month. As Chair of the Regional Economic Development Working Group, which has 53 members in and outside the region, the Council and the Commission will convene that group as soon as possible after the Ministerial meeting. The group will then be able to continue laying the foundations for the economic development and integration which is a basic condition for lasting peace in the region.

As for the Barcelona Process, the Council does not share the honourable Member's appreciation that it is “slowing to a stop”. The Euro-Mediterranean Conference of Foreign Ministers last April in Stuttgart, as well as the Ministerial Conferences on Water in Turin and on Health in Montpellier, both in the second half of 1999, on the contrary carried forward the work. We are now preparing an informal meeting of the Foreign Ministers of Industry in June in Limassol. On substance, one of the particularly far reaching projects for this year, on which work is already started, is a Euro-Mediterranean Charter for Peace and Stability.

At the bilateral level, the Council is to decide, before the end of this month, on the conclusion of the Association Agreement with Morocco. During the Portuguese Presidency, four Association Council meetings with Mediterranean countries are planned: with Tunisia, Morocco, Turkey and Israel.

Finally, the Council will continue its work on a Common Strategy of the European Union on the Mediterranean region.

 

Question no 19 by Jan Andersson (H-0816/99)
 Subject: Parliament's participation in high-level group
 

The Council's decision concerning the Commission's communication on a concerted strategy for modernising social protection (COM(1999) 0347) included setting up a high-level group. Parliament has emphasised its desire to take part in this group by requesting two observer seats.

Can the Council state its views on Parliament's wish to take part in the high-level group?

 
  
 

At its session of 29 November 1999, the Employment and Social Affairs Council effectively adopted conclusions regarding the reinforcement of cooperation with a view to modernising and improving social protection.

These conclusions provide for establishing a high-level group responsible for looking into the questions raised in the Commission communication of July 1999 regarding the modernisation of social protection, and preparing a report for the Council.

The Commission has expressed its intention to make a proposal in the near future regarding the establishment of this high-level group. At this stage, there has been no mention of the involvement of the European Parliament in the operations of this new body.

In the interim period, before starting a debate on these questions and until the high-level working party has been set up, the Council conclusions stipulate that Member States and the Commission should appoint senior officials who would be able to present an initial report to the European Council in June 2000. It is envisaged, at this stage, that this group will meet as a Council preparatory body. It will not, therefore, be open to other institutions.

 

Question no 20 by Olivier Dupuis (H-0821/99)
 Subject: The Union's policy in support of the universal abolition of the death penalty
 

Despite repeated declarations by the Council in support of the abolition of the death penalty throughout the world, the campaign pursued by the Council in Autumn 1999, which advocated the adoption by the UN General Assembly of a resolution calling for the introduction of a universal moratorium on the carrying out of death sentences, ended up as a complete shambles.

In order to avert another political and diplomatic fiasco for the Union and another defeat in the international campaign in support of the universal abolition of the death penalty, does the Council not feel that it should, with immediate effect, entrust the High Representative for the CFSP with responsibility for this issue and, in particular, with preparations for a new initiative at the forthcoming UN General Assembly in support of the introduction of a universal moratorium on the carrying out of death sentences?

 
  
 

The worldwide abolition of the death penalty is a key objective in the EU's human rights policy. The EU undertakes initiatives, in bilateral contacts and in multilateral fora, to encourage States to abolish the death penalty. If States are not yet prepared to abolish capital punishment, the EU calls on them to introduce moratoria and to respect minimum human rights standards.

The EU notes with satisfaction that its call for the worldwide abolition of the death penalty is supported by civil society representatives and NGOs within and outside Europe. It also notes that the number of countries that have abolished the death penalty has been growing over the last few years.

However, and in spite of the significant support it received from many UN Member States, the EU's initiative encountered fierce resistance from retentionist countries. The only way to get it adopted would have been to accept amendments which would have run counter not only to the immediate objective but also even to the principle of action on human rights in the UN. The EU accordingly decided after close consultation with the co-sponsors to freeze the initiative and postpone its introduction.

It is important to underline that decisions on the objective and tactics were taken by all EU Members jointly, in cooperation with other pro-abolition members of the UN. The former Presidency played its normal role in coordinating and leading on the EU's position, in accordance with the provisions of the Treaty and the arrangements for representing the EU at the UN. Worldwide abolition of the death penalty will obviously take time; initiatives to that end can achieve much in raising consciousness about this issue, even though they may not be wholly successful in the short term. The Union's commitment and determination to pursue, within the UN and elsewhere, its long-term policy for the universal abolition of the death penalty remain unchanged.

 

Question no 21 by Marco Cappato (H-0824/99)
 Subject: Serious interinstitutional problems in connection with consultation of Parliament on the Council convention on judicial cooperation in criminal matters
 

The Council has consulted Parliament on a 'Council act establishing the convention on mutual assistance in criminal matters between the Member States of the European Union'. In this connection, Parliament has been confronted with the following problems: it was consulted on the proposal before any consensus on substance or form had been reached within the Council, given that the proposal has subsequently been modified repeatedly; the Council turned down Parliament's initial request for an extension of the deadline for examination of the proposed convention; the Council failed to take account of the fundamental issues that emerged during discussion of this matter in the presence of Council representatives; the Council did not send Parliament the preparatory texts and documents on which the proposed convention was based.

What are the reasons for this hostile attitude to collaboration with Parliament? What does the Council intend to do in future to restore a relationship of trust with Parliament based upon the principles of democracy and transparency?

 
  
 

The Council wishes to emphasise that it is firmly convinced that the establishment of the area of freedom, security and justice cannot be achieved in a harmonious fashion unless there is close interinstitutional cooperation, particularly between the Council and the European Parliament.

This is the spirit in which the Council has always been concerned to consult the European Parliament, pursuant to the terms of the Treaties, regarding proposals put forward by the Commission or initiatives from Member States.

Like the procedure for all other proposals put forward in other areas, the Council consults the European Parliament as quickly as possible and, in most cases, virtually as soon as it receives these proposals itself. This is the result of its desire to keep the European Parliament informed at the earliest possible stage of work in progress and to give Parliament the opportunity to give the Council’s operations added value in due course.

As regards the proposed convention on judicial cooperation in criminal matters mentioned in the specific question which the honourable Member has asked, the Council wishes to state that, following the entry into force of the Amsterdam Treaty, it consulted the European Parliament by asking it to issue an opinion within an appropriate time limit, in accordance with the provisions of the Treaties.

Following the requests made by the European Parliament, the Council issued a new text to the European Parliament, following the Council meeting of 2 December 1999, regarding the proposed convention and is now awaiting the opinion of Parliament.

The Council awaits with great interest Parliament’s forthcoming opinion on this subject, testifying to its political will to involve the European Parliament, in compliance with the rules established by the Treaties, in establishing the area of freedom, security and justice.

 

Question no 22 by Bernd Posselt (H-0830/99)
 Subject: EU-Egypt
 

How is the Barcelona Process coming along, and what progress has been made in particular in negotiations with Egypt?

 
  
 

Egypt has always played a particularly active and constructive role within the Barcelona Process. We are now preparing an informal meeting of the Foreign Ministers within the Barcelona Process scheduled to take place on 25-26 May in Lisbon. It will prepare the ground for the Barcelona IV meeting later this year in France. On substance, an interim report on the progress made in the drafting of a Euro-Mediterranean Charter for Peace and Stability will be presented to Ministers in Lisbon. A successful outcome of the Middle East Peace Process will have a positive impact on the Partnership as a whole.

As regards the negotiation of an Association Agreement with Egypt, the Council, in Luxembourg on 21 June 1999, approved a final package and endorsed the state of negotiations. However, due to the need for further consultations between Egyptian government departments, the Agreement has not yet been initialled. The Presidency raised this point again with Egypt during its recent trip to the region.

 

Question no 23 by John Bowis (H-0001/00)
 Subject: Civil rights in Tunisia
 

Is the Council aware of the case of the Tunisian Miss Radhia Aouididi? Is it aware that she was sentenced to three years in prison for leaving the country without a valid passport, having been refused one because her fiancé, who lives in France, and her brother, who lives in London, are members of al-Nahda, although she herself does not belong to that organisation?

Is the Council further aware that, since her release from prison after two and a half years, her movements within Tunisia have been restricted and that she has still not been allowed to obtain a valid passport? Will the Council investigate this matter, with the Tunisian authorities, and ensure that justice is being done in this case?

 
  
 

The Council has no detailed information about the particular case to which the honourable Member refers.

In its various contacts at every level, the Council has been attempting for some years to impress upon the Tunisian authorities how much importance it attaches to respect for human rights and fundamental freedoms. Difficulties have arisen in the past because the Tunisian authorities have refused to meet Union representatives.

However, a number of developments in recent months have been sufficiently encouraging to warrant attention. In the first place, the Association Agreement entered into force on 1 March 1998, providing a basis on which to pursue the necessary dialogue at ministerial level. The EU-Tunisia Association Council held its first meeting on 14 July 1998 and at the dinner given immediately afterwards, the Austrian Presidency raised the question of human rights. The second Association Council meeting will take place on 24 January 2000 and likewise be followed by a dinner at which discussions will focus on the next stages of the political dialogue. The Union will also take the opportunity to voice its specific concerns about respect for human rights. The case mentioned in the honourable Member's question could accordingly be dealt with on that occasion.

In addition, several breakthroughs appear to have occurred where human rights in Tunisia are concerned. First of all, a Minister whose brief includes human rights was appointed in November� 1999. Initial contacts have been made with the Minister, who has said that he wishes to foster progress in the sphere of human rights, freedom of the Press and freedom of expression. He has given to understand that he is ready and willing to conduct an ongoing constructive dialogue with the EU on all of these sensitive matters, marking a departure from the previous attitude. Furthermore, after years of efforts to bring such talks about, the UN special rapporteur on promotion and protection of the rights of freedom of conscience and expression, Mr Hussain, has just visited the Tunisian authorities and will report to the next meeting of the Commission on Human Rights in Geneva.

Question no 25 by John Walls Cushnahan (H-0014/00)
 Subject: Falun Gong practitioners
 

Is the Council monitoring the treatment of Falun Dafa practitioners by the Chinese authorities? What action has it taken to defend the basic human rights of Falun Gong practitioners?

 
  
 

The Council has closely monitored the measures taken by the Chinese Government against Falun Gong practitioners. The Council expressed its concern about the situation and called on the Chinese authorities to respect human rights of individuals, including those who are followers of the Falun Gong. It urged the Chinese not to act against the principles contained in the UN Covenants signed by China, in particular those relating to the freedoms of expression, assembly and association. In this context, the EU expressed concern about the number of arrests and the heavy sentences imposed on members of the China Democratic Party, as well as on members of the Christian Church and the Falun Gong movement. The EU called on China to review these harsh sentences and to ensure that safeguards of a fair trial, including adequate legal representation, are fully respected, in conformity with international human rights standards and the principle of the rule of law.

The Council will continue to closely monitor the situation.

 

Question no 26 by Benedetto Della Vedova (H-0016/00)
 Subject: New tasks and undertakings for the Council concerning human rights, fundamental freedoms and measures to combat discrimination in the EU
 

Under Articles 6 and 7 of the EU Treaty and Article 13 of the EC Treaty, the EU is required to take more incisive and resolute action to safeguard human rights and fundamental freedoms and to combat discrimination in the EU. With this in view, the Council is drawing up an annual report on respect for human rights and fundamental freedoms in the world and in the European Union and its Member States.

How will the Council fulfil the new tasks in the field of human rights, fundamental freedoms and anti-discrimination measures which it is required to perform under Articles 6 and 7 of the EU Treaty and Article 13 of the EC Treaty?

Does the Council not think that the EU should continuously monitor human rights violations, particularly within the EU, so as to draw up specific standards and policies that will remove the underlying causes of violations and the means used to perpetrate them?

The tasks of monitoring and putting forward proposals could be performed in the most effective and objective manner by a monitoring unit attached to the Commission, and which is given a similar brief and structures to the existing European Monitoring Centre on Racism and Xenophobia, assisting the Commission in formulating legislative proposals.

In any case, should not the Council’s report on respect for human rights spell out the exact commitments, measures and provisions which the EU intends to adopt in every case at political and legislative level, bearing in mind the above-mentioned articles?

 
  
 

As the honourable Member points out, the new treaty attaches greater importance to matters relating to respect for human rights, fundamental freedoms and the combating of discrimination.

On the basis of this new treaty, a number of actions have been undertaken by the Council pursuant to Articles 6 and 13.

Article 6 of the EU Treaty stipulates that:

1. “The Union is founded on the principles of liberty, democracy, respect for human rights and fundamental freedoms, and the rule of law, principles which are common to the Member States.

2. The Union shall respect fundamental rights, as guaranteed by the European Convention for the Protection of Human Rights and Fundamental Freedoms signed in Rome on 4 November 1950 and as they result from the constitutional traditions common to the Member States, as general principles of Community law.”

In accordance with the new powers thereby conferred on it, the Council has adopted a first annual report on human rights, both in the European Union and internationally. This report has, in fact, been published by the General Secretariat of the Council.

Furthermore, the European Council in Cologne decided that a Community Charter of Fundamental Rights would be drawn up in accordance with the terms specified by the European Council in Tampere, which make the procedure the responsibility of both the Council and Parliament.

Finally, the Commission has just presented the Council with a raft of proposals based on Article 13. According to Article 13 of the EC treaty, “Without prejudice to the other provisions of this Treaty and within the limits of the powers conferred by it upon the Community, the Council, acting unanimously on a proposal from the Commission and after consulting the European Parliament, may take appropriate action to combat discrimination based on sex, racial or ethnic origin, religion or belief, disability, age or sexual orientation.” The European Parliament has been consulted on these proposals. The underlying principles which the Commission used in drawing up the two directives and the programme are explained in a communication. In the same document, the Commission mentions the great amount of consultation work carried out within Member States, particularly involving civil society, whether it be NGOs or both sides of industry.

The first directive, which targets discrimination of all forms, with the exception of sexual discrimination, is a directive with a view to the creation of a general framework in favour of equal treatment in employment and at work.

The second directive concerns the implementation of the principle of the equal treatment of peoples regardless of race or ethnic origin. This is a legal framework intended to enable Member States to intensify the fight against discrimination in all forms.

As for the programme, it sets itself three objectives, which are as follows:

“(a) improve the understanding of issues related to discrimination through improved knowledge and measurement and through the evaluation of the effectiveness of policies and practice;

(b) develop the capacity of target actors (in particular Member States, local and regional authorities, independent bodies responsible for the fight against discrimination, the social partners and non-governmental organisations) to address discrimination effectively, in particular through support for the exchange of information and good practice and networking at European level;

(c) promote and disseminate the values and practices underlying the fight against discrimination.”

This programme will therefore make it possible to monitor breaches of the principle of non-discrimination more closely.

The proposals based on Article 13 are, in the opinion of the Council, of considerable political importance, and the Portuguese Presidency has made it clear that this is one of its priorities. The Presidency intends to take the examination of these proposals as far as possible during the first six months in order to achieve the adoption of these measures, if possible.

These proposals are also representative of the way in which Community institutions may give substance to a general principle. This process will contribute towards giving the Charter of Fundamental Rights greater credibility.

Finally, the European Monitoring Centre on Racism and Xenophobia, which was also created by Council Regulation No 1035/97, has just published its first report. At this stage it does not seem the right time to extend its mandate to make it a monitoring centre for human rights. This idea, although very interesting, seems premature in view of the short lifetime of the monitoring centre in its present form and in view of the fact that the process of drawing up the Charter of Fundamental Rights is still in its initial phase.

The Council would like to assure the honourable Member once again of its deep commitment to this issue.

 

Question no 27 by Luisa Morgantini (H-0017/00)
 Subject: Products imported from Israel: possible violations of the rules of origin
 

In its Communication to the Council and the European Parliament of 12 May 1998, the Commission informed the Council and Parliament that the territorial scope of the EC-Israel Interim Agreement on Trade and Trade-related matters currently in force is confined to the territory of the State of Israel which, under applicable international law, binding on all of the Community’s Member States and on Israel, does not include the territories occupied by Israel since 1967, namely the West Bank, together with East Jerusalem, the Gaza Strip and the Golan Heights. Apparently, the territorial clause establishing this scope in the current Agreement is repeated in the EC-Israel Association Agreement initialled by Israel and the Council and now undergoing ratification by the Member States.

Have the current or previous Israeli governments instructed Israel’s customs services to apply the rules of origin as laid down in the territorial clause of that Agreement, as the Agreement requires?

Does the body of international law governing the application and interpretation of all EC bilateral agreements permit Israeli settlements, or any part of the Occupied Territories, to be treated as a part of the State of Israel?

Is the territorial clause and the obligation to apply it in certifying the origin of exports to the EC under that Agreement an essential element of the Agreement?

If this territorial clause and the rules of origin are being breached as a matter of official Israeli policy, what does the Council intend to do about it?

 
  
 

According to Article 38 of the EC-Israel Interim Agreement, it applies “to the territory of the State of Israel”. In addition, Protocol 4 on Rules of Origin provides that the term “Israel” shall also cover the territorial waters, which surround Israel, and under certain conditions also some sea-vessels. No further definition of that notion is contained in the agreement. The EC has always considered that the agreement applies solely to the territory of the State of Israel within its internationally recognised borders in accordance with the relevant UNSC resolutions. This issue is thus linked to the final settlement of the Middle East Peace Process.

Responsibility for monitoring the implementation of all international agreements entered into by the Community lies with the Commission. This applies also to the Agreements concluded with Israel and with the PLO.

In a Communication, which was also addressed to the European Parliament, of May 1998, the Commission pointed out the following: “Evidence suggests that the practice of exporting Palestinian products as “made in Israel” under Israeli certificates of origin applies to a substantial proportion of total exports into the EC of Palestinian originating products”.

The Council took note of this Communication and considered that these issues should be resolved on a technical level in a constructive dialogue with Israel. It asked the Commission to report back on the outcome of its talks with the Israeli authorities and to keep it fully and promptly informed of all relevant developments, including of further measures which might be under consideration.

Since then, the Commission held several such technical discussions and reported to Member States in the Customs Committee on a regular basis, and at a more political level last December. It appears that all these issues are still being discussed. I am sure that the Commission will also keep the European Parliament fully informed.

 

Questions to the Commission
Question no 46 by Alexandros Alavanos (H-0779/99)
 Subject: Application of Directive 98/35 on the minimum level of training of seafarers
 

Directive 98/35(1) on the minimum level of training of seafarers requires the Member States to provide mandatory training for masters, officers, ratings and other crew of ro-ro vessels so that they are capable of: dealing with a large number of passengers in emergency situations onboard ship; ensuring the safety of passengers, cargo and the integrity of the vessel; assisting and instructing passengers in correct conduct.

In the light of the recent accident involving the Superfast III which set sail from the port of Patras, will the Commission say whether, and in what manner, Greece is implementing Directive 98/35, which has been mandatory since 25 May 1999? Are there training programmes in operation which cover the minimum requirements of the above guidelines laid down in the Directive? If not, what measures has the Commission taken or does it intend to take?

 
  
 

Concerning the implementation of Directive 98/35 amending Directive 94/58 on the minimum level of training for seafarers, Greece notified the Commission on 8 November 1999 of its national implementing measures taken to comply with the Directive. The Commission is currently examining the Greek notification. This examination will, in particular, look into the Greek Presidential Decree no 243 of 29 July 1998 and specifically its Article 5 which requires that the master, officers and personnel designated on the muster list to assist passengers should undergo the different types of special training at shore-based training centres.

At the time, the Commission has no information on whether training programmes are already in place in Greece.

 
 

(1) OJ L 172, 17.6.1998, p. 1.

 

Question no 47 by Manuel Medina Ortega (H-0783/99)
 Subject: Nuclear power station in Morocco
 

On the basis of the reply I received from the Presidency-in-Office of the Council (H-0555/99) on 17 November 1999(1) on EU monitoring of nuclear programmes in associated countries, and Morocco's programme in particular, would the Commission be prepared to examine, in collaboration with the Kingdom of Morocco, what risks and dangers the nuclear power station currently under construction at Tan-Tan might involve, both for Morocco itself and the neighbouring Community region of the Canary Islands?

 
  
 

Apparently, in view of the possible construction of a nuclear power station to be used for the desalination of seawater at Tan-Tan, the Kingdom of Morocco has already sought technical advice from at least two competent bodies in the field of nuclear safety, the IAEA (International Atomic Energy Agency) and DSIN (the French Nuclear Installations Safety Directorate).

Should Morocco wish to add the Union’s technical advice to that already sought as regards monitoring the observance of safety standards in this field, the matter would certainly be examined by the Commission in the most positive light.

 
 

(1) Verbatim Report of the sitting of 17.11.1999, p. 94.

 

Question no 48 by William Francis Newton Dunn (H-0787/99)
 Subject: Over­the­counter selling of fashion contact­lenses
 

The British Government is reported to be planning later this year (by amending the UK Opticians Act of 1989 through Section 60 of their proposed new Health Bill) to restrict the right to supply the public in Britain with ‘fashion contact lenses without power’ (which merely change the colour of the wearer's eyes) to sales by registered opticians only. It will, however, still be possible to purchase these products face-to-face over the counter of a fashion-house in the rest of the European Union, via mail-order or via the Internet. Would such a proposed restriction by the British Government be contrary to the rules of the Single Market?

 
  
 

The honourable Member is asking whether the planned UK measure to restrict the sale to the public of “fashion contact lenses without power” by registered opticians only, is contrary to the rules of the single market. He points out that the sale of these products in the other Member States is made over-the-counter, or via mail-order or via the Internet.

It has to be examined, therefore, whether such a measure would infringe Article 28 of the EC Treaty, which prohibits all quantitative restrictions on imports of goods between the Member States and measures having equivalent effect.

The European Court of Justice in its Judgement Keck of 24 November 1993 (C-267 and 268/91) ruled that national provisions restricting or prohibiting certain selling arrangements of products, fall outside Article 28.

It follows from the jurisprudence of the Court (Commission v. Greece C-391/92) that measures determining by whom or where goods may be sold are considered as selling arrangements.

As declared in the Judgement Keck, national measures on selling arrangements do not hinder trade between Member States, so long as they apply to all relevant traders operating within the national territory and so long as they affect in the same manner, in law and in fact, the marketing of domestic products and of those of other Member States.

Consequently, unless there is going to be discrimination in favour of the sale of nationally produced fashion contact lenses in the UK, such provisions would escape the scope of application of Article 28 of the Treaty.

These extensive efforts to find a solution to this issue have not yet yielded a satisfactory result, but discussions are still under way. It is to be hoped that the new Israeli Government, in the context of the revitalised peace-process, will be as eager as the Community to overcome these problems. Indeed, by far the best way forward for all, Israelis, Palestinians and Europeans alike, would be that a mutually satisfactory solution is found in the coming months within the framework of the permanent status negotiations.
