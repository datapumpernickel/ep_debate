QUESTIONS TO THE COUNCIL (The Presidency-in-Office of the Council of the European Union bears sole responsibility for these answers)
Question no 11 by Simon Coveney (H-0065/06)
 Subject: Accelerating broadband uptake by SMEs
 

The Lisbon Strategy focuses on improving competitiveness of European businesses and increasing economic growth in the EU. Accelerating broadband uptake by SMEs will allow SMEs faster access to customers and suppliers and therefore is central to achieving increased levels of growth and competitiveness in the EU.

Would the Council consider organising a conference under the Austrian Presidency to examine the challenges and barriers to broadband uptake by SMEs and looking at ways to ensure that SMEs, especially in peripheral areas, have broadband access?

 
 

Question no 12 by Liam Aylward (H-0077/06)
 Subject: Digital broadband
 

Figures produced in the year 2005 show that digital broadband is available to 90% of the urban population within the European Union while it is only available to 62% of the rural population within Europe.

Can the Council state what initiatives it is undertaking to ensure that this imbalance in terms of access to broadband is redressed within Europe in as short a timeframe as possible?

 
  
 

(DE)Secure high-speed broadband networks are vitally important to the Commission’s ‘i2010’ strategy for boosting growth and jobs in the digital economy. Provision of broadband services acts as a catalyst for the realisation of the following goals of the i2010 strategy: (i) completion of a single European information area promoting an open and competitive internal market for the information society and media, and (ii) achieving an inclusive European information society that promotes growth and jobs in a manner that is consistent with sustainable development and prioritises better public services and quality of life. Bridging the digital divide in the area of broadband, in other words ensuring widespread availability and use of broadband services, calls for diverse, mutually agreed and coherent measures at different levels of administration.

All of the national reform programmes (NRPs) submitted by the Member States to the Commission following the relaunch of the Lisbon Strategy last year deal with the subject of broadband provision and expansion, with competition viewed as the most important driving force for the development of broadband technologies. In less-developed areas of the Union, on the other hand, public funding is helping to accelerate the introduction of broadband. Many of the NRPs put forward extensive broadband programmes (AT, IE, EE, FI, FR, HU, IT, LU, LT, PT, SI, ES), and the topic of broadband connections for SMEs is dealt with in the broadband strategies of the Member States, of the Commission and of the Council.

As the Council emphasised in its conclusions of 1 December 2005, the i2010 strategy is a shared responsibility between Member States, the European institutions and stakeholders. That being the case, the Council invited the Commission, among other things, to ‘review the impact of digital convergence on the creation, circulation and distribution of European content, and if appropriate, bring forward proposals to promote a rich and diverse digital content market, increased use of broadband and the development of Next Generation Networks (NGNs)’, and to ‘prepare a European initiative on e-Inclusion in 2008 addressing issues relating to [inter alia] regional divides’. Furthermore, the Council called on the Member States to ‘promote the roll-out of advanced seamless networks through the rapid implementation of national strategies, aimed at increasing broadband coverage and multiplatform access, and stimulating take-up, making use, where appropriate, of EU structural funds, in line with the Commission Guidelines’, and to ‘encourage effective use of ICTs by public services and businesses (especially SMEs), by promoting the necessary skills needed for their deployment, promoting interoperability and open standards, and effective on-line public services through the introduction of organisational change’. The proposed Competitiveness and Innovation Programme (CIP) also includes a programme for ICTs, including measures to improve interconnectivity, and is currently under examination by the European Parliament and the Council.

In its communication of 20 March 2006 entitled ‘Bridging the Broadband Gap’, the Commission states: ‘The benefits of broadband are such that the inability to have access to it is an issue which should be addressed urgently.’ The communication contains a range of instruments aimed at improving the availability of broadband access. One of the Commission’s conclusions is that local/regional authorities are in the best position to plan broadband projects that take account of local needs and technical requirements. Measures might include strengthening national broadband strategies (as part of the Commission’s strategy for growth and employment as well as of the overall i2010 strategy), improved sharing of good practice, not least through the collection and shared use of information about projects and invitations to tender relating to extension of broadband services, as well as drawing up innovative, wide-ranging, pan-European pilot projects. A number of instruments are already available at EU level, including the implementation of the regulatory framework for electronic communication, aimed at promoting competition and bringing state intervention into line with competition rules. The Commission’s communication is based on the Digital Divide Forum Report of 15 July 2005 and the ensuing public consultation and includes proposals for further policy guidelines for the Member States and the Commission. The Council will in due course look at all the proposals and examine whether further measures should be considered.

Two important conferences on this subject will be held during the Austrian Presidency. The annual Conference on the European Charter for Small Enterprises will take place in Vienna on 13 and 14 June 2006, where the state of implementation of the Charter will be discussed, and in April a seminar on ‘ICT-Tools for Providing Information, Advice and Services for rural SMEs’ will be held in Oulu (Finland).

In the longer term, and with a view to the imminent review of the electronic communications regulatory framework (Commission proposals are expected by the end of 2006), the Council will take full account of the convergence aspect of broadband technologies and ensure that the plans for an open, competitive and innovative market for electronic communication services are treated appropriately.

 

Question no 13 by Gay Mitchell (H-0243/06)
 Subject: ICTs
 

Has the Council noted the findings in the Ahoa report which state that Europe has failed to capitalise on the application of Information and Communications Technologies (ICTs) and is losing out as large firms globalise their research and development? How does the Council intend to address this situation?

 
  
 

(DE)The honourable Member will undoubtedly agree that Information and Communications Technologies (ICT) make a substantial contribution to growth and employment in Europe and are thus an important resource for the attainment of the Lisbon Objectives. Moreover, investment in research and innovation is vital in order to enable the ICT sector to continue to make its contribution to the economy, in both the short and the long term.

In 2005 the Commission presented its strategy 'i2010' for a European information society, the second pillar of which is innovation and investment in research. In reply to the Commission, the Council stressed in its conclusions of December 2005 that the i2010 strategy was a matter for which the Member States, the European institutions and interested parties bore joint responsibility. The Council asked the Commission and Member States to promote investment in research and development in the field of ICT and to provide greater support for research and innovation by promoting private investment and cooperation between research and innovation programmes.

After the publication of the Aho report, the European Council called on the Member States at its spring summit to allocate more public funding to innovative research and to promote R&D investment by the private sector in order to make more and better resources available for research and innovation. It also called for a broad-based innovation strategy for Europe, to translate investment in knowledge into products and services. In this context it called on the Commission and Member States to implement the new i2010 strategy energetically.

The Commission proposal for a decision establishing a Competitiveness and Innovation Framework Programme (2007-2013) is currently being considered by the European Parliament and the Council. For the information society, Chapter II of this proposal ('The ICT Policy Support Programme') seeks to promote innovation by making greater use of ICT and by means of investment in this field. The provisions of the programme serve as an instrument to support the measures indicated in the i2010 strategy. As regards Community support for research and technological development, the honourable Member will be aware that the Commission proposal for the Seventh Framework Programme is currently being considered by the European Parliament and the Council under codecision. It is proposed that research and technological development in the ICT sector should be one of the main thematic priorities of this programme.

Finally it may be noted that on 22/23 March 2006 in Vienna the Austrian Presidency also organised a conference on 'Investing in ICT Research and Innovation'. The conference was intended as a follow-up measure to the Commission's i2010 initiative and served the purpose of identifying practical measures to increase Europe's innovativeness in the ICT sector. At present a final report on the results of the conference and the recommendations of the eminent delegates is being drafted.

 

Question no 18 by Dieter-Lebrecht Koch (H-0113/06)
 Subject: Transrapid
 

Is it conceivable that a consortium similar to those set up for Galileo and Airbus could also be set up for Transrapid?

 
  
 

(DE)As the honourable Member rightly observes, enterprises have, in the past, pooled their resources in consortia with the purpose of operating the Galileo satellite navigation systems and setting up Airbus Industrie. In the same way, Siemens and ThyssenKrupp have united their work on Transrapid in a common enterprise bearing the name of Transrapid International.

Between 1997 and 2000, Transrapid International carried out feasibility studies into the possibility of using Transrapid’s system of electromagnetic levitation in rapid transit systems in Central and Eastern Europe and further eastwards. These studies were financially supported by the European Community, which also, in 2003, provided funds from the TEN-T budget for a study into a possible link between the main railway station in Munich and the city’s airport.

Should additional measures at European level in support of Transrapid’s development in Europe prove necessary, the Council is certain that the Commission will initiate the action required.

Question no 21 by Othmar Karas (H-0277/06)
 Subject: Directive on HGV charges
 

What action will the Presidency take with regard to the Directive on HGV charges (1999/62/EC(1)) and the model for internalising external costs, which the Commission is to devise?

 
  
 

(DE)The honourable Members have posed a number of questions on the relationship between the environment and transport.

Economic growth and the resulting increase in transport demand have an impact in a number of areas, with implications in terms of traffic congestion, traffic safety, health problems and environmental damage. In response to the 2001 White Paper 'European transport policy for 2010: time to decide', the Council endorsed a shift from road transport to more environmentally-friendly modes of transport such as clean buses, ships and rail transport. Relevant concrete measures include guidelines giving priority to trans-European rail and inland waterway networks and legislation on the opening up of the market for rail freight services in Europe.

With a view to deciding on the most appropriate measures to be taken in future, the Council is awaiting the communication on the mid-term review of the White Paper. As this is expected to be submitted by the Commission shortly, the Austrian Presidency already has plans to present the communication at the Council meeting on 8/9 June 2006 and to hold an exchange of views on it.

The Council supports the use of the trans-European transport networks, the European Regional Development Fund and the Cohesion Fund as European instruments for investing in transport. In accordance with the subsidiarity principle, cities with excessive traffic congestion may apply to the Commission for funding. The total budget available for these instruments in the period 2007-2013 will, of course, depend on the outcome of discussions on the financial perspective.

As regards the levying of charges for the use of roads, the European Parliament and the Council adopted a directive on 27 March 2006 amending the directive in force on the charging of heavy goods vehicles for the use of certain infrastructures (Eurovignette). A particularly notable feature of this amending directive is a new provision allowing a mark-up to be added to the tolls for specific sections of the trans-European road transport network in mountainous regions which suffer from acute traffic congestion or the use of which by vehicles causes significant damage to the environment. The revenues raised by the mark-up are to be invested in priority TEN projects (mainly rail projects) which will contribute to reducing the problem. In addition to this provision on cross-financing of alternative infrastructure, the directive also provides for further possibilities for applying different charges within the trans-European network in order to achieve environmental objectives, and it specifies that the Member States are free to levy charges in order to combat traffic congestion and/or environmental impact (for example, the London congestion charge).

Pursuant to this directive the Commission is also required to submit within two years a model for the assessment of external costs. The model is to be accompanied, if appropriate, by legislative proposals, which - if submitted - will be examined by the Council.

 
 

(1) OJ L 187, 20.7.1999, p. 42.

 

Question no 20 by Reinhard Rack (H-0240/06)
 Subject: Marco Polo II
 

The positions of Parliament and the Council on the Marco Polo II programme are now very close. Does the Council Presidency see a possibility of producing a joint result at first reading on the basis of the current draft?

 
  
 

(DE)In April 2005, the Council adopted a partial general approach to the proposal for a Parliament and Council regulation on the second ‘Marco Polo’ programme for the granting of Community financial aid to improve the environmental performance of the freight transport system (‘Marco Polo II’).

The ‘Marco Polo II’ grant programme, which is intended to facilitate a changeover to alternative modes of transport, is of particular significance and high priority in view of the disproportionate increase in the transport of goods by road. When it is borne in mind that Marco Polo II will be able, between 2007 and 2013, to bring about the transfer of some 140 billion tonnes per kilometre from roads to means of transport with less of an environmental impact, with a corresponding reduction of 8 400 million kg of CO2 emissions and an environmental and social benefit amounting to EUR 5 billion, the importance of this programme is plain to see.

On 15 December 2005, the European Parliament’s Committee on Transport and Tourism adopted its own position on the proposal, and the Presidency has already embarked on the examination of the amendments that Parliament proposed.

It is indeed the case that - as the honourable Member points out – the standpoints of Parliament and of the Council have moved much closer together. The Presidency’s concern is that agreement should be reached as early as first reading, although the amendments proposed by Parliament have yet to be discussed in greater detail by the Council. It should be noted that the financial aspects of the proposal referred to above cannot be settled until agreement is reached on the Financial Perspective for 2007 to 2013, and that there is a need for a successor programme to run on seamlessly from the first ‘Marco Polo’ programme. The presidency looks forward to honourable Members’ continuing constructive support in this regard.

In view of the difficulties relating to scientific and technological development which are encountered by the regions furthest away from the EU's heartland (such as polar, insular, outlying and extremely remote regions), what measures could - in the Council's view - compensate for those regions' imbalances and contribute to the development of their technological and innovatory potential?

Question no 26 by Sajjad Karim (H-0214/06)
 Subject: EU-India Strategic Partnership and the knowledge game
 

As the European Commission unveiled its plans for a world-class institute of technology, intended to boost the EU’s economic competitiveness and rival the likes of the US’s Massachusetts Institute of Technology (MIT), it also noted that China and India are the ‘coming players in the knowledge game’. Aside from the famed Indian Institutes of Technology and the Indian Institutes of Management, there are approximately 20 other centres of excellence in science, engineering, medicine and the arts.

In its opinion on AFET’s EU-India Relations: A Strategic Partnership (A6-0256/2005), the International Trade Committee called for support to be given to promote dialogue and cooperation in science and technology, an important factor of which was to expand university contacts and exchanges. What role does the Council think that these proposals could play in the European Institute of Technology, and what does the Council think India and the EU have to offer each other, within the remit of the Strategic Partnership, in this regard?

 
  
 

(DE)Naturally the Council has noted the report of 1 August 2005 by the Committee on Foreign Affairs (AFET) and the European Parliament Resolution of 29 September 2005 on the strategic partnership between the EU and India.

The Council attaches considerable importance to closer cooperation between the European Union and India in many fields, including university education. The joint action plan between the EU and India (document 11984/05) states that the EU and India believe that cooperation between institutions of higher education and exchanges of students play an important part in raising the level of knowledge on both sides. It is therefore proposed that, on the basis of existing programmes, new initiatives be planned between India and the EU Member States to give students on both sides more opportunities to study at universities in the other region.

As regards the proposed European institute of technology in particular, however, it should be noted that the Heads of State or Government of the EU have yet to adopt a formal position on the Commission's plans and that a detailed proposal by the Commission on the establishment of such an institute is expected only later in the year. At present, therefore, it is probably too soon to assess the possible impact of such an institute and its possible role in promoting this cooperation.

 

Question no 27 by Jonas Sjöstedt (H-0215/06)
 Subject: Banning of the Gay Pride Festival in Moscow
 

In mid-May 2006, Russia will take over the Presidency of the Council of Europe for the next half-year. The organisation consists of 46 Member States and its proclaimed purposes include defending human rights.

It was planned to hold the first Gay Pride Festival in Moscow at the end of May, just a week after Russia assumes the Presidency of the Council of Europe. Now the festival has been banned. The city's mayor has said previously that he is against the festival because in his opinion homosexuality is unnatural.

Has the Council conveyed to Russia any criticism of the ban on the Gay Pride Festival, and what measures will the Council take to persuade the country to respect human rights, including freedom of assembly and freedom to demonstrate?

 
  
 

(DE)The Council shares the honourable Member’s concern with regard to the freedom of assembly and the free expression of opinion in the Russian Federation and reiterates its position that discrimination on the grounds of sexual orientation is not acceptable.

There is no practical justification for the ban imposed on a ‘Gay Pride Festival’ in Moscow comparable to those already held in many EU Member States. The question therefore arises of whether the ban is defensible in terms of the European Convention on Human Rights and of other relevant international agreements on human rights, such as the International Covenant on Civil and Political Rights.

The Council will therefore give consideration to raising this topic at one of the forthcoming meetings with its Russian partners.

 

Question no 28 by Marie Panayotopoulos-Cassiotou (H-0219/06)
 Subject: Economic performance and family policy
 

Does the Council believe that competitiveness and sustainable economic development can be fostered through specific policies designed to counter negative demographic trends in the European Union?

What specific measures is the Austrian Presidency planning with a view to reconciling family and working life, improving conditions and removing the obstacles which prevent Europeans from having as many children as they would wish?

The enlargement of the Union in 2004 prompted the Commission to propose the now agreed European Neighbourhood Policy (ENP), under which Action Plans have already been agreed with five partners in the Mediterranean, and consultations are ongoing with further two partner countries.

These ENP Action Plans foresee concrete cooperation to enhance good environmental governance in the partner countries, to address sector specific environment issues (e.g. water management, waste management, marine environment, desertification, nature protection, air quality, sustainable maritime transport) as well as vigorous pursuit of international and regional environment cooperation (including ratification and implementation of multilateral environmental agreements, including the Kyoto Protocol and the Barcelona Convention and its Protocols).

The implementation of the Plans is pursued in the context of the Environment and Transport Subcommittees set up under the Association Agreements. In this context, we will be supporting, as well as monitoring, the implementation of the agreed actions of the Action Plans, including with regard to implementing the obligations the partner countries have undertaken under the Barcelona Convention and other relevant legally binding environment agreements. It contributes to bridging the gap between the Mediterranean countries that are not members of the EU and those that are members, through promoting a coherent, effective and uniform implementation of the relevant international conventions and rules aimed at better protection of marine environment in the Mediterranean region by preventing pollution from ships. To this end, in January 2006 the SAFEMED project(2) (€4,5 million financed under the MEDA(3)) started in order to promote the achievement of the above objectives.

The proposed legislative action, along with the fact that the implementation of the above Strategies requires the cooperation of non-EU States, make this process extremely useful as platform for environmental cooperation at regional scale.
