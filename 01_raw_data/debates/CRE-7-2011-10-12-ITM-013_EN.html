Verbatim report of proceedings
Wednesday, 12 October 2011 - Brussels OJ edition

13. Order of business
Video of the speeches
PV
 

  President. – The final draft agenda, as drawn up pursuant to Rule 137 of the Rules of Procedure by the Conference of Presidents at its meeting of Thursday, 29 September 2011, has been distributed. After consultations with the political groups, the following changes have been proposed:

Wednesday/Brussels part-session:

1. Winding up the debate on the preparation for the European Council meeting on 23 October by tabling motions for resolutions. The following deadlines have been set: motions for resolution: Tuesday, 11 October, at 12 noon;

amendments and joint motions for resolutions: Wednesday, 12 October, at 10.00;

amendments to the joint motions for resolutions: Wednesday, 12 October, at 11.00;

requests for split votes: Wednesday, 12 October, at 19.00.

2. Inclusion of the oral questions tabled by the Group of the Alliance of Liberals and Democrats for Europe, the Confederal Group of the European United Left – Nordic Green Left and the Europe of Freedom and Democracy Group in the debate on the accession of Bulgaria and Romania to Schengen.

3. Inclusion of Syria and Egypt in the debate on the situation in Yemen and Bahrain.

4. Inclusion on the agenda, just after the debate on the situation in Yemen, Bahrain, Syria and Egypt, of a statement by the High Representative of the Union for Foreign Affairs and Security Policy and Vice-President of the Commission. A resolution on this subject will be put to the vote at the October II part-session.

5. Insertion of the one-minute speeches immediately after the debate on current developments in Ukraine.

6. Extension of the sitting to 22.00.

Thursday/Brussels part-session:

Inclusion at voting time of the report by Sharon Bowles on the appointment of a Member of the Executive Board of the European Central Bank. No deadline has been set for tabling amendments.

 
  
 

  Bruno Gollnisch (NI). – (FR) Mr President, I have a comment to make about today’s order of business. It includes a debate on the situation in Yemen and Bahrain, which has been extended to cover Syria and Egypt. These issues are very important, but they concern states over which we have no direct influence. The fact is, Mr President, Member States’ armed forces, under NATO control, are today continuing to kill, in our name, the last remaining supporters of the former regime in Libya. This is a far cry from the mandate that was granted by the United Nations. I have no idea how many victims Europe’s bombs have currently claimed in Sirte or in the surrounding areas, and I feel that we should address this issue. We are more than just the weapon bearers of NATO and the United States.

 
  
 

  Mario Mauro (PPE). – (IT) Mr President, ladies and gentlemen, as you rightly complained, the worsening situation in Egypt has affected the large minority of Coptic Christians in that country and poses a problem that also involves the legitimacy of, and our hopes for, the process of democratisation connected with the Arab Spring, namely, the re-constitutionalisation process in these countries.

It is precisely for that reason that we had hoped to have the opportunity to vote tomorrow through the resolution. However, we understand both the technical difficulties and the needs of the other groups to focus on drawing up a text. We ask that this text have as its pre-eminent and clear-cut object the situation of the Coptic Christians in Egypt, when the resolution is put to the vote in Strasbourg.

 
  
 

  Hannes Swoboda (S&D). – (DE) Mr President, it depends what the motion is. However, I would like to put forward the following suggestion: that we do indeed vote in Strasbourg as planned, but that, before the vote tomorrow, you could perhaps give a brief statement expressing our concern about what has happened to the Copts, so that we can combine a clear opinion from you with a resolution in the next Strasbourg part-session.

 
  
 

  President. – You are aware that I did mention this matter in my opening remarks today, but I understand there is a desire for a more robust and more detailed statement on the matter, and I accept this comment. I would like to stress that the request of the Group of the European People’s Party (Christian Democrats) is about setting a deadline for tabling motions for resolutions. This is the main point, the main idea of this request. Is there someone who would like to speak against the request?

 
  
 

(Parliament agreed to the request)

(The order of business was thus established)(1)

 
  

(1) Other amendments to the order of business: see Minutes
