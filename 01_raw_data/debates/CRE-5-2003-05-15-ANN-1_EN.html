Question no 15 by James (Jim) Fitzsimons (H-0245/03)
 Subject: World Energy Council conference and new initiatives
 

As the Council is aware the EU is the world’s largest energy importer and the world’s second largest energy consumer. Does the Council consider that the recent World Energy conference held in Brussels offered any new initiatives aimed at developing a single energy market for the EU based on exploiting renewable energy sources, reducing energy wastage (including energy wastage in buildings) and moving away from the nuclear option, and how does the Council see our relations on energy issues developing with other countries and organisations such as OPEC?

 
  
 

(EN) The Council is not a member of the World Energy Council nor has it discussed the outcome of the conference mentioned by the MEP.

There is however no doubt as to the Council's long-standing commitment regarding the promotion of renewable and energy efficiency as attested by the adoption of Directive 2001/77/EC on the promotion of electricity from renewable energy sources in the internal market in electricity (1) and Directive 2002/91/EC on the energy performance of buildings (2) as well as the provisions supporting renewable electricity and energy efficiency retained in the common position of the Council with a view to the adoption of a Directive concerning common rules for the internal market in electricity (3).

This support for renewable energy and energy efficiency is without prejudice to Member States' individual decisions regarding their energy mix.

Regarding the EU relations with third countries in the field of Energy, the Council is well aware that addressing security of supply issues requires the diversification of energy sources both in terms of fuel and origin, and firmly believes that dialogues with third countries respond to this objective and should be further promoted. The support given by the Council to the establishment of the International Energy Forum, involving OPEC countries, is a good example in this respect.

There are at present four dialogues/relations in the field of energy:

- EU - Russia

- EU - Euromed countries

- Northern Dimension

- EU - South East Europe

On 14 May, the Council will adopt a set of detailed conclusions covering these four dialogues/relations.

(2) OJ L 1, 4.1.2003
(3) OJ C 50, 4.3.2003

 

Question no 16 by Claude Moraes (H-0248/03)
 Subject: Member States' progress in the transposition of Directives 2000/43/EC and 2000/78/EC
 

Will the Council please set out its views on the current state of progress by Member States in their transposition of Council Directive 2000/43/EC(1) implementing the principle of equal treatment between persons irrespective of racial or ethnic origin and the transposition of Council Directive 2000/78/EC(2) establishing a general framework for equal treatment in employment and occupation in the Member States.

 
  
 

(EN) Council Directive 2000/43/EC of 29 June 2000 implementing the principle of equal treatment between persons irrespective of racial or ethnic origin obliges Member States to comply with it by 19 July 2003.

For the implementation of Council Directive 2000/78/EC of 27 November 2000 establishing a general framework for equal treatment in employment and occupation this date is 2 December 2003, with the possibility to prolong the implementation period by 3 years if this is necessary to take account of particular conditions.

In addition both Directives confine to the Commission the task of drawing up a report on the implementation based on information by the Member States which have to communicate this information in 2005.

Anyhow, taking these implementation periods into consideration it appears to be too early to evaluate the implementation by the Member States.

Question no 17 by Glenys Kinnock (H-0249/03)
 Subject: Zimbabwe
 

Now the EU sanctions against Zimbabwe have been rolled over, does the Council envisage a strengthening of sanctions to include, for instance, more family members of the ZANU-PF regime and business people who finance ZANU-PF, and rescinding rights of residence in Europe and access of family members to employment and educational institutions?

 
  
 

(EN) On 18 February 2003 the Council decided to extend the period of application of the restrictive measures adopted last year for a further period of 12 months. The measures applied remained unaltered. The measures were adopted last year because of serious violations of human rights and of the freedom of opinion, of association and of peaceful assembly in Zimbabwe. A more immediate reason was the attempts by the government of Zimbabwe to prevent free and fair elections, notably by refusing access for international election observers and for the media. The Council decided to extend the sanctions because it found that no improvement had taken place in the situation in the country over the last 12 months.

The Council has stated that it will be willing to revoke the measures once conditions prevail to ensure respect for human rights, democratic principles and the rule of law, but also that the EU reserves the right to take additional restrictive measures. In the meantime, the EU is following developments closely in Zimbabwe, it continues its contribution to operations of a humanitarian nature and projects in direct support to the population and it has stated its desire to pursue dialogue with Zimbabwe.

 

Question no 18 by Olivier Dupuis (H-0251/03)
 Subject: Chechnya
 

Following a meeting between the Greek Presidency of the EU, the High Representative of the CFSP and the Russian Foreign Minister on 15 April 2003, the Union Presidency welcomed the positive signs in Chechnya, particularly the substantial turnout in the constitutional referendum of 23 March. After the meeting, it was also decided not to include an item on Chechnya on the agenda for the EU-Russia summit to be held in Saint Petersburg on 31 May 2003.

Is the Council familiar with the countless media reports detailing the very grave irregularities which marked both the build-up to the so-called referendum and the event itself? In particular, is it aware of the existence of ‘supplementary electoral rolls’, which made it possible to inflate still further the size of the electorate, already swollen by the inclusion of tens of thousands of Russian soldiers, to the point where even foreign journalists were able to take part in the ‘vote’? Furthermore, is the Council familiar with the report by the pro-Russian Chechen administration of Mr Kadyrov, especially as regards the number of Chechen civilians who have been the victims of summary executions, the number of mass graves and bodies already discovered, and the number of killings perpetrated daily by military and paramilitary Russian forces?

 
  
 

(EL) The Council follows attentively the current situation in the Chechen Republic of the Russian Federation. There are continued reports of human rights abuses and reprisals against civilians by Russian armed forces and their Chechen allies, as well as rebels.

The Council is aware of some of the irregularities that took place before and during the referendum on 23 March, as reported by Ambassador Strohal in his oral report to the Permanent Council on the OSCE/ODIHR Expert Visit to the Chechen Republic during the referendum.

The EU has expressed its concerns at several occasions and will continue to do so at all levels. The EU has noted the declaration of President Putin according to which the referendum should become a new start for the reconstruction and consolidation process in the Chechen Republic of the Russian Federation. The Council will continue to watch closely the follow up of this declaration. It will also continue to encourage Russia to adequately investigate all allegations of human rights abuses in a prompt, independent and impartial manner.

The Council believes that a permanent international presence is essential in this context. It therefore supports the Dutch OSCE chairman in office’s on going efforts in achieving a renewed, constructive and long term engagement of the OSCE in Chechnya, mainly designed to strengthen democratic institutions and to promote the rule of law. It is certainly in the interest of Russia to maintain a continued OSCE field presence in Chechnya with a broad mandate.

 

Question no 19 by Proinsias De Rossa (H-0261/03)
 Subject: Rights and dignity of persons with disabilities
 

Could the Presidency outline the state of negotiations within Council regarding the proposed United Nations legally-binding instrument to promote and protect the rights and dignity of persons with disabilities, indicate the EU's likely position on the matter, including the rights and principles the EU would like to include in the instrument, clarify whether the Irish Government has opposed the adoption of a united EU position in support of this instrument at Council working group level; and indicate its response to the Commission's recommendation to the Council authorising the Commission to take part in the negotiations on this instrument?

 
  
 

(EN) The EU is committed to promoting and protecting the rights of persons with disabilities and will continue to play an active role in the work of the Ad Hoc Committee that is scheduled to meet in New York from 16-27 June 2003. The Council has submitted an EU position paper to the UN Secretariat late March 2003 for inclusion in the UNSG’s report on proposals for a comprehensive and integral international convention to promote and protect the rights and dignity of persons with disabilities.

This EU position paper (doc. 8997/03), that was agreed by all Member States, sets out the EU’s view on a new legally binding instrument. It discusses inter alia the objectives, principles, scope, definitions, elements to be included, monitoring and evaluation, as well as the relationship of a new instrument to existing international human rights instruments. The paper states that a fundamental principle in elaborating a new legally binding instrument on the rights and dignity of persons with disabilities is that it should have a rights-based approach. Furthermore, it should not duplicate but rather reaffirm and tailor existing human rights rules and standards to the specific situations faced by persons with disabilities. The main purpose of a new instrument should be to seek to ensure the equal and effective enjoyment of human rights and fundamental freedoms of persons with disabilities.

The Council is in the process of preparing in more details the upcoming meeting of the Ad Hoc Committee and is still discussing whether should it or not come forward with more detailed proposals for a Convention or should it rather at this stage work on the basis of other proposals. It has not yet discussed the recommendation from the Commission to the Council in order to authorise the Commission to participate in the negotiations of an international legally binding instrument to promote and protect the rights and dignity of persons with disabilities, but will do so in due course.

 

Question no 20 by Patricia McKenna (H-0269/03)
 Subject: SARS and the Special Olympics in Ireland
 

The Irish authorities have been very slow to react to the SARS virus, with Foot-and-Mouth Disease still being treated as a greater threat to public health. What is more, a suspected SARS victim was treated in a way that breached WHO guidelines.

Given the Irish Government's lack of preparation for any SARS outbreak in the country, what steps will the Council take to ensure that the Special Olympics may be held without the spread of the disease being exacerbated?

 
  
 

(EN) The Council thanks the Honourable Member for her question and refers her to Article 152(5) of the Treaty which states that Community action in the field of public health shall fully respect the responsibilities of the Member States for the organisation and delivery of health services and medical care. The Council is therefore unable to comment on any government's response to Severe Acute Respiratory Syndrome.

The Honourable Member is also referred to the Council Conclusions of 6 May 2003 on Severe Acute Respiratory Syndrome (SARS) (1)(2) in which, inter alia, the Council:

stated its opinion that the network for the epidemiological surveillance and control of communicable diseases in the Community has provided a very effective basis for the rapid exchange of information within the EU and for a co-ordinated response, in which the acceding countries have fully participated;

recalled that the application of health protection measures falls to the authorities of the Member States, and confirmed the benefits of effective co-ordination of such measures through the Network Committee; and

called on Member States to ensure, inter alia:

prompt and effective identification of all suspect and probable SARS cases, and their appropriate isolation in hospitals;

appropriate protection of hospital personnel treating the patients;

provision of guidance and information to health care workers and the public;

measures, consistent throughout the European Union, to provide and acquire information and traceability of international travellers arriving or in transit from affected areas; and

timely and accurate reporting and sharing information with the Commission, WHO, other countries and other partners (e.g. airline companies).

The Council invites the Honourable Parliamentarian to address her question directly to the Irish government.

Question no 21 by Astrid Thors (H-0275/03)
 Subject: Institutional care in Bulgaria
 

Bulgaria has the highest rate of children in institutional care in Europe. Altogether, in 2002, there were 22 000 children (aged 0-18) living in institutional care in Bulgaria. The vast majority of these children do have parents and should not be in these residential homes at all. The continued use of institutions, and the conditions within them, indicate serious breaches of Bulgaria's commitments under the UN Convention on the Rights of the Child (UNCRC). There is an urgent need for a full reform of the child care system.

Why has Bulgaria not been required to address this situation, as was the case with Romania? Has the Council taken any measures to promote the devising of long-term solutions in the form of community-based alternatives with a simultaneous downsizing of the institutional system? What has been done to promote the regulation of adoption processes so as to ensure that they are transparent and in the best interests of the child?

 
  
 

(EL) The Council attaches the utmost importance to the respect, by candidate countries, of the political criteria for accession established at the Copenhagen European Council, namely democracy, the rule of law, human rights and protection of minorities. The protection of children is therefore a requirement for all candidate countries.

The Commission’s 2002 Regular Report on Bulgaria’s progress towards accession concluded that Bulgaria “needs to strengthen its efforts to reform the child care system, to make sure that the best interests of the child are reflected and reduce the number of children in institutions”. This is also one of the priorities highlighted within the revised Accession Partnership with Bulgaria, which was given political agreement by the Council on 14 April 2003. In fact Bulgaria is now party to the relevant international conventions and has established a legislative framework for ensuring the rights of children. The Bulgarian government is making significant albeit slow efforts to tackle the question of childcare in the country’s harmonization with the Acquis. On the contrary, in Romania’s case, within the Political Criteria, the Section regarding children has still to bear fruit.

Furthermore, in the context of the accession negotiations Bulgaria has signed and introduced a number of bilateral agreements with Member States providing for measures aiming at fighting against child abuse. However, sustained efforts are still required to ensure that this legislative framework is properly implemented and that the necessary structures are put into place.

The Union will continue to closely monitor the situation in this regard, in particular within the framework of the bodies established by the European Agreement.

 

Question no 22 by Herman Schmid (H-0280/03)
 Subject: The Lisbon strategy and the Council's employment task force
 

The Council has recently set up a task force group on employment policy which is responsible for proposing practical measures. Is this an alternative to an effective long-term employment strategy or a supplementary, more practical policy initiative which will operate within the framework of the European employment strategy and simply consider how the EES can be implemented more effectively at the present time?

Some observers have expressed their concern that the Council will abandon its drive for quality in employment and production, which is a cornerstone of the Lisbon strategy, and instead propose 'mini-jobs', reduced wage costs and lower social security expenditure of various kinds as the traditional way of getting more people into work and more activity into the economy.

Can the Council confirm that those observers are wrong and that the Council continues to support the Lisbon strategy to promote knowledge, quality and high productivity as the way forward for Europe?

 
  
 

(EL) The Spring European Council (Brussels, 21 March 2003) reaffirmed its strong commitment to the timely and effective delivery of reforms across the three pillars of the Lisbon strategy – economic, social, and environmental.

The promotion of sustainable growth and the creation of more and better jobs must remain firmly at the top of the Union's agenda, despite the economic slowdown. This involves taking decisive action to increase employment and modernise the European Social model.

In this context, the European Council endorsed the initiatives by Heads of State and Government of certain Member States for the establishment of a European Employment Task Force.


Pursuant to the conclusions of the Spring Summit, the European Commission, and not the Council, has set up a European Task Force for Employment.


This Task Force will carry out an independent in-depth examination of key employment-related policy challenges and will identify practical reform measures that can have the most direct and immediate impact on the ability of Member States to implement the revised European Employment Strategy and to achieve its objectives and its targets.

It is intended, therefore, that the Task Force will be an additional instrument in achieving the Lisbon targets.

On 6 March 2003, the Council (EPSCO) adopted the "Key messages on the future of the European Employment Strategy", which are part of the Joint Employment Report 2002. The Council supported the three overarching objectives of the Strategy: full employment by increasing employment rates, quality and productivity at work and cohesion and an inclusive labour market.

Therefore, the Council has no intention of abandoning its drive for quality in employment and production and is fully committed to supporting the Lisbon strategy, in order to build the most competitive and dynamic knowledge-based economy in the world, capable of sustainable economic growth with more and better jobs and greater social cohesion.

 

Question no 23 by Jan Dhaene (H-0282/03)
 Subject: Cross-border rail travel
 

Cross-border rail travel is more expensive than normal train services. This is an anomaly in a Europe without frontiers. It should however be underscored that any such subsidy should respect the relevant Community legislation, including the rules pertaining to competition.

 

Question no 24 by Rodi Kratsa-Tsagaropoulou (H-0284/03)
 Subject: Tackling the crisis in European tourism
 

The adverse impact of the Iraq crisis on European tourism was immediately apparent, particularly in the southern Mediterranean countries. Does the Council intend to take practical measures to support tourism policy and, in particular, to deal with the consequences of the crisis in the Gulf in the short term? (EL) The honourable Member will already be aware of the efforts being made by the Commission to deal with the adverse impact of the Iraqi crisis on European tourism.

Question no 38 by John Bowis (H-0262/03)
 Subject: Epilepsy and EU development policy
 

Given the priority given to epilepsy by the WHO in its global campaign, ‘Out of the Shadows’, how is the Commission ensuring that the EU plays its part in this campaign through its development aid policies?

 
  
 

(EN) The Commission recognises that epilepsy and other neurological disorders are an important but largely unrecognised health problem in many developing countries, and that while effective treatment is available, access to such treatment is frequently very limited. There are a variety of reasons for this, of which arguably the most important are the cultural beliefs and associated stigma that surround the condition.

The Commission therefore welcomes “Out of the Shadows”, the global campaign against epilepsy, and notes that it is a partnership between the World Health Organisation (WHO) and two international NGOs, the International League against Epilepsy (ILAE) and the International Bureau for Epilepsy (IBE). These organisations are active in advocacy about epilepsy, and assisting Ministries of Health to implement activities such as education, training, and research.

The Commission itself has no specific comparative advantage in the field of epilepsy, and the main focus of its development and health policies remains accelerating progress to meet the Millennium Development Goals for health.

The Commission increasingly provides sectoral or global budgetary support to countries rather than project-type assistance. One of the main aims of doing so is to make increased resources available for health systems to tackle major health problems according to nationally agreed priorities. Decisions about what these priorities are vary from country to country, but in some, there may be a good case to include epilepsy and other mental health problems.

 

Question no 39 by Astrid Thors (H-0279/03)
 Subject: EU's contribution to the eradication of polio
 

Work is in progress under the auspices of the WHO to eradicate polio. The work has been successful and, according to the latest available report on the campaign, only 483 new cases were discovered in 2001. New cases are found primarily in India, Pakistan and their neighbouring countries, as well as Ethiopia, Eritrea, Nigeria and some other African countries.

A concerted effort is now needed if we are to succeed in eradicating polio. Commissioner Nielsen has reportedly recently promised grants of 12.9 million to Nigeria and 25 million to India. However, this is far from making up the shortfall in funding which now totals almost 300 million US dollars.

What intentions and means does the Commission have to provide further grants for the campaign this year and next so that polio can be eradicated?

 
  
 

(EN) The Commission will continue to contribute to the efforts to eradicate polio.

The Commission’s activities in this field are embedded in our ongoing support for health and the strengthening of essential public health services such as immunization programmes, in developing countries.

Where polio is still endemic, the Community has also supported specific projects supporting immunisation programmes at country level, such as the Partnership to Reinforce Immunization Efficacy programme in Nigeria; and regional levels, such as the ARIVA programme in West Africa, aimed at strengthening capacities for greater national and regional independence in vaccination programmes. The Commission specifically supports polio eradication efforts in India and Nigeria for a total of € 37.5 million, where over 90% of the confirmed polio cases were found during 2002.

Some of the countries where polio is still endemic undergo complex emergencies. In this context, the Commission plays a leading role in the support to essential health needs, including immunisation activities, through European Community Humanitarian Office (ECHO).

At present, the Commission does not intend to support further specific programmes or projects in this area.

 

Question no 41 by Alexandros Alavanos (H-0216/03)
 Subject: Support for the tourist industry from the budget
 

Insecurity caused by the war in Iraq and the economic recession in Europe have already led to a serious crisis in the tourist industry in the European Union. In the Greek market, bookings are down by up to 30% as compared with the same period last year and fears are being expressed that prolongation of the war in Iraq may have a serious effect on the summer period too.

Can the Commission provide data on tourism trends in the European Union, particularly for the Mediterranean countries? What measures can be taken to boost the tourist industry through the Community budget?

 
  
 

(EN) Council Directive 95/57/EC on the collection of statistical information in the field of tourism, and certain weaknesses in its implementation by Member States, make it impossible to analyse the impact of the war in Iraq on tourism trends in a way that allows a rapid reaction. However, in the wider Mediterranean region of the Community, more or less stable latest trends can be seen for Spain and Italy. Portugal is reported as currently undergoing a slightly negative trend. While data for France are only available until the end of 2001, increased domestic demand there seems to offset a drop in foreign visitors, if any. Greece has transmitted full data only until 1999 inclusive.

On a medium term basis (1997-2001), all Member States in the wider Mediterranean region recorded significant tourism growth, expressed in nights spent. In first place Spain is found with a yearly increase of more than 7%, followed by France, Italy and Portugal, where the rate amounts to 4% and 3%, respectively. In Greece the past annual growth of tourism activity can be estimated at about 1%.

Regarding Community financial support in favour of Europe's tourism industry, despite the current situation, the Commission does not intend to provide specific assistance. A wide range of existing Community financial instruments in the various policy fields provides possibilities of enhancing the competitiveness of the sector, its enterprises and tourist destinations.

The Basque Country and the whole of north-eastern Spain have been taken by surprise at newspaper reports on the route followed by B-52 bombers between their base at Fairford (England) and Iraq. Does the Commission not consider the overflying by these aircraft of densely populated towns and cities to be an infringement of public safety?
