  Der Präsident. – Als nächster Punkt der Tagesordnung folgt die Aussprache über den Bericht von Andreas Schwab im Namen des Ausschusses für Wirtschaft und Währung über die Wettbewerbspolitik – Jahresbericht 2021 (2021/2185(INI)) (A9—0064/2022).

 
  
 

  Andreas Schwab, Berichterstatter. – Herr Präsident, Frau Vizepräsidentin, liebe Kolleginnen und Kollegen! Der Jahresbericht zur Wettbewerbspolitik ist immer ein guter Gradmesser darüber, wo Europa in Sachen Offenheit seiner eigenen Märkte und Wettbewerbsfähigkeit seiner eigenen Wirtschaft steht. Bevor wir allerdings zu den einzelnen Inhalten kommen, erlaube ich mir zunächst mal, den Kollegen aus dem Wirtschaftsausschuss, aber natürlich vor allem auch dem Kollegen Andrea Caroppo, der die Stellungnahme des Binnenmarkt-Ausschusses verfasst hat, zu danken.

Ich glaube, dass man einleitend sagen muss, dass die Ukrainekrise, die russische Aggression gegen die Ukraine, wirtschaftlich und wahrscheinlich auch im Wettbewerbsrecht und bei der Wettbewerbspolitik vieles von dem, was wir als Gewissheit angesehen haben, infrage stellt und sich deswegen viele wettbewerbsrechtliche und wettbewerbspolitische Fragen in den kommenden Jahren neu stellen. Aber das ist etwas, was viele Politikbereiche betrifft, und deswegen verdient es heute hier vielleicht noch nicht die besondere Aufmerksamkeit.

Colleagues, the report is focusing on four major points that have to be addressed soon by the European Commission. The first is – and that’s a bit the backward—looking part of the report – post—COVID—19 phasing out of specific pandemic support measures.

Secondly, achieving the Green Deal and digital transformation. Here we have to look for recovery and the Green Deal to be combined. C, or third, the rise of e—commerce and the importance of large online platforms for competition rules. We have already discussed about that in this committee with a lot of colleagues that are here today when addressing the key proposals of the digital market act.

Finally, as a fourth element, mergers and antitrust: how new digital markets require an update of the notice and the definition of relevant markets.

Colleagues, first I would like to stress that one of our priorities was to mention the necessity to phase out pandemic support measures step—by—step. I am a bit unhappy that today my group chairman has fallen ill because of COVID. It will hopefully be very easy, but we still hope that it will go away fully and, at least in competition policy, we believe that we have to call for a phase—out of the pandemic support measures.

In this regard, the report highlights that the pandemic is ending and that we must return to market discipline. Markets need to do their work again to deliver innovation, growth and more choice for consumers. Moreover, colleagues, the European Parliament affirmed the importance to have competition policy tools that are well adjusted and complement one another. They will facilitate the twin transition between green and digital and will guarantee a level playing field, price signals for innovation and fair economic conditions.

In our report, together with my colleagues, the shadow rapporteurs, we assessed the crucial importance of a review of competition policy instruments in the light of then twin transition and COVID pandemic. Especially important was the planned revision of the general bloc exemption regulation.

Another key element we achieved in the report has been the reference to enforcement of the DMA and DSA. We firmly pointed out that the Commission’s priority must be to ensure and to enforce DSA and DMA strongly, to show that Europe delivers contestable markets, fairness and the rule of law on the Internet. This is a very strong commitment that we share with all our colleagues.

Therefore, we expect the Commission to provide adequate resources, and we expect the Member States to give the Commission the money to allow for adequate resources in that respect. In this regard, I brought to the attention of the Commission the necessity to create more positions to face the growing portfolio of responsibilities since, for the first time, we are giving the Commission a direct enforcement power for two harmonisation instruments. The letter that we have been addressing, together with shadows, to the French Presidency is known by you.

Colleagues, the two other elements that I have been mentioning at the beginning are as important as the last one, and I will come back to that after your comments. I am looking now forward to an open debate and I thank you for your attention.

 
  
 

  Margrethe Vestager, Executive Vice-President of the Commission. – Mr President, honourable Members, I thank you, Andreas Schwab, as rapporteur, and the shadows for this comprehensive report that we are discussing today. I think it provides an excellent basis for the discussion that we are going to have. It identifies the main challenges and opportunities, and I welcome the very constructive and supportive tone.

This annual debate is again taking place in the midst of a crisis. Just as we were starting to get back to normal life after the pandemic – or the pausing of the pandemic – we were faced with unprecedented aggression, with an unprecedented attack on our democratic values – values I think we took for granted. They are now being tested. Can we stand up for them? And can we mirror their brave countermeasures, as we see in Ukraine?

I’m glad that our reaction was swift. The sanctions that we imposed are effective, and they are effective because of the size and the strength of our economy. The effects of sanctions and counter-sanctions can only be cushioned by a well-functioning and resilient single market. That is part of our defence. This is why competition policy and enforcement play a key role. In the current context of prices going up, it’s hitting purchasing power hard in many families, especially, of course, those who are most vulnerable when it comes to their budgets.

We need to make sure that markets remain competitive, to deliver affordable prices, and to make sure that no one takes an undue advantage. That work, of course, is never complete. There is no resting on any laurels when it comes to that. We must continue working to further strengthen the single market, to complete it, and to make sure that it’s working every day of the week. And we must make it future—oriented and ready for the green and digital transition.

The draft report also recognises that EU competition policy and its vigorous enforcement play a crucial and instrumental role in this endeavour. I hear the calls of the Parliament for an in-depth review of existing competition instruments, and we are acting on this call. Recent challenges show that, overall, our rules remain relevant, useful, enabling, on a day—to—day basis. They serve us well and they can be swiftly adapted if necessary.

I think the COVID State aid temporary framework is a very good example of that ability to adapt. The framework has enabled Member States to provide necessary and proportional support to businesses hit by the crisis. At the same time, the fact that we had rules, that we assessed what was going on, helped us maintain the level playing field in the single market. So far, within the framework of the COVID temporary framework, the Commission has approved a total amount of State aid – a budget, so to speak, of State aid – of almost EUR 3.2 trillion. Based on a survey covering from mid-March 2020 to June 2021, the actual spending was not the budget; the actual spending was almost EUR 730 billion, but not in the size of trillions. I think that is really, really important to take note of because it also means that, while we had fears or concerns that we could have had a serious disturbance of the level playing field, that has actually not materialised because the survey results also show that no Member State has disproportionately outspent another.

Now we are actively considering the progressive phase-out of this temporary framework – something that the Parliament has called for, as was just mentioned by the rapporteur. The Commission was also quick to put in place a temporary crisis framework in the context of the Russian invasion of Ukraine. To make sure that our tools are fit for purpose, we have engaged in an unprecedented review of 20 competition rules and guidelines across the three instruments: antitrust, mergers and state control. The recently adopted climate, environmental protection and energy aid guidelines are key enablers of the green transition. Let me thank the Parliament for its input in the run—up to these guidelines.

Let’s not forget the casework. A recent example is the Council decision from last year, by which the Commission imposed heavy fines on a number of carmakers who colluded to slow down the deployment of technical development potential to reduce car pollution levels. And, last but not least, competition also supports the digital transition. It keeps the power of dominant companies in check, especially through antitrust rules that prohibit the abuse of dominance. We have continued vigorously enforcing competition rules. You will know the ongoing cases that we have: a Google case, a Facebook case, three Apple cases, two Amazon cases – all high priority.

Last but not least, as Parliament has pointed out several times, investigations take time. But now the enforcement has also shaped the content of the Digital Markets Act, both on process and on substance. The Digital Markets Act could not have been delivered so fast without the experience that we have accumulated over so many antitrust cases. I think the DMA is proof that democracy works because, with the excellent work of Parliament, here we are with a strong political agreement on this landmark proposal.

Last but not least, since we are in times of change, we have revised the State aid rules on important projects of common European interest. We can discuss that, of course, in greater detail. You know the many projects that we are about to approve. Tomorrow, we will start the trilogues on the regulation on distortive foreign subsidies.

I am really happy to see the speed at which Parliament has been working and the engagement. With that engagement, of course, I also look forward to this debate.

  Eva Kaili, on behalf of the S&D Group. – Mr President, so, the rapporteur did excellent work and I think we have achieved our ambitions, with other shadow rapporteurs, to explain what Parliament’s reflections on the legislative and enforcement developments are and also to communicate our priorities to the Commission.

Last year was indeed a dense year for competition policy: we saw 1 000 state-aid decisions, 11 cartel decisions, 14 merger cases entering into phase two and EUR 1.7 billion in fines imposed, also delivering the ambition to complement antitrust enforcement with competition policy through the DMA (Digital Markets Act).

What we achieved actually with this report provides a path to the next chapter of the European economy by emphasising the importance of effectively assessing market power in digital ecosystems and calling for an inclusive and future-proof revision of the market definition notice – basically to translate offline to online and modernise our rules to meet the new digital challenges. We also highlighted social protection priorities, for example, on self-employed workers, offline and online again.

Furthermore, in this section we suggest and we call on the Commission to conduct a market study also on online advertising technology or the practices that exist, and also to integrate privacy and data protection into the assessment of anti-competitive behaviour.

Parliament pays great attention against potential uneven level playing fields from different state-aid support strategies or tax policies that could be indirectly state aid. And we emphasised the need to consider the socioeconomic impact also of energy prices now.

Finally, the report touches upon the important issues of enforcement, calling for more resources at both national and European level, and also for closer cooperation of authorities and consumers in data protection law to break down the silos that we have identified. It basically builds on our commitment to reach consumer choice, safeguard the internal market and make our economy a fair, competitive and future-proof ecosystem.

  Margrethe Vestager, Executive Vice-President of the Commission. – Mr President, thank you very much for this very dynamic debate. A lot has been said and at great pace. I think everyone has been listening here. They have almost lost their breath because of the richness here.

I just want to make a few comments. It was said quite early in the debate that Europe should not miss the innovation train, that we should actually be in the first carriage. I think that is one of the many reasons for supporting a competitive market and for supporting competition in the market, because we know from experience and from what we see that competition is one of the most important drivers.

If there is no risk that someone will come up with something better, why bother to innovate? Why not just continue doing what you have been doing and try to make the most of it? We see that, unfortunately, in some mergers, for instance, there is a risk that someone is buying the pipeline of a competing pharmaceutical product to continue the blockbuster and not enabling the new product to get to market. That, of course, is really important.

This is why, also in our merger enforcement, innovation plays a role because it’s a long time ago, if ever, that competition law enforcement was just about prices. Yes, it’s about prices, and for many families these days of course prices are important, but it is also about quality, about choice and about innovation because competition in innovation is one of the main drivers. If you look at the legislative proposals that are supporting what we do, you also see this because the DMA will enable competition and will enable businesses to get to market based on their own ideas, their work ethic, and the funding that they have available. They will not have to depend on the gatekeeper.

If you look at the AI proposal, the point of that is to create trust in technology and, by creating trust, open markets; for instance, that these fantastic public sectors that we have in Europe, they are open for the use of artificial intelligence and because of that, there is a drive to innovate in the use of artificial intelligence for a better purpose within the Union.

Several speakers tonight have talked passionately about small and medium—sized businesses for very good reasons because this is not just for festive speeches, it is in everyday life that small and medium—sized businesses provide jobs, growth and innovation. If you look at everything we do, I do hope that you see that we have implemented the slogan of thinking small first, because every business starts small and a lot of businesses remain in the category of being small and medium sized. They need a fair chance in a single market. Here we need to make sure that they are not being squeezed out, dominated or held back by bigger companies.

When you look at what we can do in extraordinary times – actually during COVID, we did not suspend state-aid rules because we wanted to make sure that, while necessary aid was given to companies which were asked to close their doors and customers were asked to stay at home, so that the two couldn’t meet and, because of that, had to hibernate, that really necessary support was given in a way that would still enable the single market to function and that would limit the unlevelling of the playing field. This is why we do not suspend state-aid rules, but we make them more flexible. We enable more support to be given, but always in a way that is proportionate and necessary.

Here, if you look at what we have done in the temporary framework for the COVID pandemic, you will see that we changed it six times. We changed it because we found that the situation on the ground was changing. New needs were appearing, and of course we will do the same when we follow the use of the temporary crisis framework that we have put in place to answer the war that we have in Europe and the energy crisis that has come with it. We will look at what is happening, and we will do one more thing.

We will give guidance to companies if they need to cooperate in order to better help out in the supply chains or in a situation that may occur because of the sanctions. But, at the same time, we have sent a very clear signal that we will be vigilant and look to see if anyone is trying to take advantage of the situation because we don’t want anyone, in the shadow of war, where people are being killed, to try to make a profit that is not due.

Competition law enforcement and regulation have also been touched upon by many, and I will be the first to say that competition law enforcement alone cannot solve every competition problem. But we cannot do without it and this is why competition law enforcement and regulation must go hand in hand. We have just had a judgment from the European Court on bpost, which exactly establishes that the two can, and should, exist together. That’s a great encouragement for what we have ahead of us.

On the question of connectivity, I think this is really important because this is one Union and it should not be the case that someone feels forgotten. This is, of course, why one can establish a public service obligation that can be on airline connectivity, that can be on ferry connectivity, that can be on any connectivity needed in order to make sure that, for instance, out of season, citizens who live on an island that may not have perfect connectivity can feel that they are part of this Europe that we all rely on.

Last, but not least, I think it’s really important to make sure that competition rules are fit for purpose. We have had this big project of making sure that we are fit for the digital world, we’ve had this big project to make sure that we are fit for the green transition and for the green world that we have ahead of us, but we are also recognising that competition law enforcement, no matter how effective, cannot solve everything. Europe and citizens still rely on this House to set the rules of the game when it comes to social, when it comes to environment, when it comes to how we deal with things, and within that democratically legitimate framework, then to go compete in a single market that we will do our best to preserve every day.

Thank you very much for this debate. It has indeed been encouraging to be part of it.
