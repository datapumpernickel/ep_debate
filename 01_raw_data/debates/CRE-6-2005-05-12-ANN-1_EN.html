QUESTIONS TO THE COUNCIL
Question no 25 by Eoin Ryan (H-0349/05)
 Subject: Countries affected by the tsunami in the Indian Ocean
 

Will the President-in-Office outline what follow-up to date has taken place on the EU Action Plan for the countries affected by the tsunami in the Indian Ocean?

 
  
 

(FR)On 31 January 2005, the Council adopted an EU Action Plan to help the countries which had been devastated by the tsunami in the Indian Ocean(1) which provided for enhanced cooperation between the Commission and the Member States with a view to enabling the Commission to monitor in an appropriate fashion the commitments given by the Member States. The Commission was instructed to report back to the Council on a regular basis.

At operational level, monitoring and coordination of commitments given in connection with humanitarian aid are the responsibility of the European Community Humanitarian Office (ECHO) using, firstly, a networked system (known as the 14-point system) which enables the financing decisions taken by the Member States to be entered directly and, secondly, regular reports relating to the financing decisions taken by the Community as a whole.

With regard to reconstruction, the Commission has established a system of focal points so that information on financial commitments and financing decisions taken by the Member States may be exchanged between the Commission and the Member States, centralised and regularly updated.

At the Council meeting held on 25 April 2005, the Commission presented two communications(2), (3), one with a view to reinforcing the European Union's capacity to react to disasters and crises, the other on ways of improving the Community Civil Protection Mechanism.

The Council also noted the report by the Secretary-General/High Representative, Mr Javier Solana, on the follow-up to the EU Action Plan dated 31 January 2005(4), which covered in more detail the following two aspects:

- reinforcing the EU's rapid reaction capability by using military resources;

- reinforcing consular coordination.

Finally, at the Council meeting on 23 and 24 May 2005, to be attended by the Foreign Ministers and by the Development Cooperation Ministers, the Presidency intends to submit a document giving an initial progress report on the implementation of the EU Action Plan dated 31 January 2005 on the basis of the 17 points identified in the Action Plan, including a report on the documents submitted by the Commission and by the Secretary-General/High Representative to the GAERC on 25 April 2005.

 
 

(1) Doc. 5788/05.
(2) Communication from the Commission to the Council, European Parliament, the European Economic and Social Committee and the Committee of the Regions entitled 'Reinforcing EU Disaster and Crisis Response in third countries' (Doc. 8382/05).
(3) Communication from the Commission to the Council, European Parliament, the European Economic and Social Committee and the Committee of the Regions entitled 'Improving the Community Civil Protection Mechanism' (Doc. 8430/05).
(4) Doc. 8204/05.

 

Question no 26 by Diamanto Manolakou (H-0351/05)
 Subject: Violation of human rights in Afghanistan
 

According to the report by the independent expert sent to Afghanistan by the UN, Cherif Bassiouni, a large number of serious human rights violations are still being committed there by 'coalition' forces, private security firms and the Afghan police. Among other things, the UN expert refers to sexual abuse, beatings, torture and use of force resulting in death. He also cites witness statements reporting that hoods are placed over prisoners' heads so that they do not know where they are, that they are deprived of sleep and food, and that they are forced to remain in painful positions for long periods of time. The report also protests against the fact that the 'coalition forces' continue to deny UN envoys access to their installations.

Is the Council aware of these facts, and what steps will it take with a view to safeguarding human rights and opposing those who violate them?

 
  
 

(FR) The Council is certainly fully aware of the report of the independent expert on the situation of human rights in Afghanistan, M. Cherif Bassiouni. The EU has very serious concerns at the content of this report, and continues to monitor closely the human rights situation in Afghanistan.

The EU has been vigorous in support for the independent expert. The EU was the prime mover behind the Chair's statement on Afghanistan at UN CHR 61 in Geneva under agenda item 19, following close consultations with the government of Afghanistan and other interested parties. This statement covers in detail recent developments and commitments undertaken in the field of human rights, inter alia on the basis of the report of the independent expert (E/CN.4/2005/122).

Through the Chairperson's statement, the UN Commission on Human Rights requests the Afghan Government to continue cooperating fully with all special rapporteurs and human rights mechanisms of the UN and to implement their recommendations. It also requests the UN High Commissioner for Human Rights to monitor the human rights situation in Afghanistan, to compile a report on it and to expand its programme of advisory services and technical cooperation in the field of human rights and the rule of law. The final text of the statement can shortly be found on the website of the Office of the UN High Commissioner for Human Rights (www.ohchr.org).

The EU fully agrees with the need for continuing monitoring and reporting on human rights violations in Afghanistan, however they may arise. It is important that the local Office of the High Commissioner on Human Rights receive proper funding.

The Council also receives its own regular reporting from the EU Special Representative, Francesc Vendrell, on developments in Afghanistan, including those in the field of human rights. This informs decisions taken within the Council on EU policy towards that country.

More generally, the Council is committed to safeguarding human rights in all countries of the world. All individuals should be entitled to the same protection, regardless of their circumstances.

Question no 27 by Athanasios Pafilis (H-0352/05)
 Subject: Pain-inducing weapon for the US army
 

According to an article published in the newspaper 'Imerisia' on 4 March 2005, the US army is funding the development of a weapon with a range of two kilometres which causes excruciating pain for a certain period of time. This weapon, which is classed as a biological weapon, is designed to deal with demonstrators with the aim of causing extreme pain without causing injury, but it is feared that it will also be used for torture. This revelation, which was made public by a body active in the field of biological weapons research, has sparked protests and deep concern among scientists, both on account of the ethical aspects linked to the construction of such a weapon and on account of its long-term side-effects on the human body.

Is the Council aware of the construction of this weapon? Does it approve of the use of biological weapons as a means of repression? What is its position on the fact that this pain-inducing weapon is being funded by the US army?

 
  
 

(FR)The Council has not received any information about the weapon described in the honourable Member’s question.

All EU Member States, plus the United States, are parties to the Biological and Toxin Weapons Convention (BTWC). The EU sees this Convention as the cornerstone of its efforts to prevent the development and use of biological agents and toxins as arms. The BTWC celebrated its 30th birthday on 26 March 2005.

The BTWC was the first Convention to ban an entire category of weapons of mass destruction, and it is a key component in the international non-proliferation and disarmament regime. A Review Conference on the Convention is set to take place in 2006.

All EU Member States attach the highest importance to strengthening the Convention and to complying with its provisions, and have undertaken to channel their efforts into arranging the Review Conference due to take place in 2006. The conference will be an excellent opportunity for all parties to assess the functioning of the Convention, to reaffirm their commitment to international standards on biological weapons and to adopt measures aimed at strengthening the BTWC, encompassing the most recent developments.

 

Question no 28 by Georgios Toussas (H-0354/05)
 Subject: New round of NATO interventions
 

The recent statements by the US Secretary of State, Condoleeza Rice, on the need for 'further democratic reform in Russia', the description of Belarus as the 'last dictatorship in Europe' and the decisions taken by the meeting of NATO Ministers in Vilnius, Lithuania, mark the beginning of a new phase of expanding NATO's role and field of action by means of interventions in independent countries. NATO declares itself 'open to a possible role in the Middle East'. On the pretext of promoting 'freedom and democracy', the sights are trained on the countries of the Caucasus, the Middle East and North Africa in a bid to control their wealth-producing sources and exploit their people.

What measures will the Council take to ensure respect for the non-negotiable right of peoples to choose their own future?

 
  
 

(EN)NATO’s intentions and decisions regarding possible interventions are the responsibility of the Atlantic Alliance and its Members. It is not for the Council to make statements of opinion on this matter. The relationship between the EU and NATO is based on cooperation, on complementarity and on respect for each other’s decision-making autonomy. The EU is pursuing, and will continue to pursue, an approach to intervention that falls within its scope in the regions in question. Democracy and the rule of law, human rights and fundamental freedoms, peace-keeping and reinforcing international security, in accordance with the Charter of the United Nations, with the principles of the Helsinki Final Act and with the objectives of the Paris Charter, including those relating to external borders, are at the very core of the Union’s common foreign and security policy (CFSP), and, accordingly, of the European security and defence policy, which is an integral part thereof. These form part of the objectives of the CFSP, as laid down in Article 11 of the Treaty on European Union. Promoting democracy is one of the strategic objectives that form part of the European Security Strategy.

Question no 42 by Georgios Papastamkos (H-0304/05)
 Subject: NUTS II regions in the EU
 

What is the average population size in the NUTS II regions of the EU-25 and in Greece, respectively? Does the size of the bigger regions covered by the programme have a positive effect on their development? In the Commission's view, what is the optimum size?

Is it possible to form inter-regional entities under the programme within a Member State in order to achieve a more competitive framework for development?

What method is used to calculate the per capita gross regional product in order to determine the eligibility for one of the three Objectives of a regional entity covered by the programme?

Commissioner Hübner notes in her answer to my previous question (P-0108/05) that the procedure for revising the list of NUTS II regions also applies where there is a substantial reorganisation of the relevant administrative structure of a Member State. By what process can such a reorganisation take place?

 
  
 

(EN)The average NUTS 2 population for the EU-25 (in 2002) is 1.790.000 inhabitants ; in Greece the average population at NUTS 2 level (in 2002) is 842.000 inhabitants. I do not believe that the size of a region covered by a programme is in itself a factor which affects the development of that given region. In that respect I do not think that we can define an «optimum» size for a region either.

It has to been recalled that at the current programming period, Member States (with the exception of Member States with a federal structure) have opted for regional operational programmes at NUTS II level and for sectoral and / or horizontal programmes with a national coverage. In the 2007 – 2013 period, according to the draft Regulations, Member States shall establish the Operational programmes at the appropriate geographical level and at least at regional level NUTS II (under the Covergence objective) and at least at regional NUTS I or NUTS II level under the “Regional competitiveness and employment” objective, in accordance with the institutional system specific to the Member State. While respecting those provisions, Member States are therefore free to form inter-regional entities under a programme. By doing so, they will nevertheless have to pay particular attention to ensuring the best possible definition of strategy, existence of appropriate structures and coordination mechanisms in order to define the scope and priorities of the programme as well as its implementation, monitoring, control and evaluation.

Gross Domestic Product (GDP) per capita determines eligibility of a region for Objective 1 support in the 2000 - 2006 programming period (and will do so also for the future convergence objective in the 2007-2013 programming period). Regions with a GDP per capita (in Purchasing Power Parities, which adjusts for differences in the cost of living between countries) below 75% of the community average are eligible. This eligibility decision is based upon the data for the three most recent years at the moment the decision is taken.

Member States willing to request a revision of the NUTS nomenclature should closely cooperate with Eurostat. Eurostat will examine on a case-by-case basis whether an internal reform of the administrative structure of a Member States corresponds to a “substantial reorganisation” as defined under Article 5, §4 of the Regulation on the establishment of a common classification of territorial units for statistics (NUTS) (Regulation(EC) n°1059/2003 of the Parliament and the Council.

 

Question no 43 by Panagiotis Beglitis (H-0308/05)
 Subject: Support from the European Union Solidarity Fund for the prefecture of Evros following the damage caused by the February floods
 

The flooding which occurred on 17 February 2005 in the prefecture of Evros caused enormous damage to road building, farming infrastructure, flood-prevention projects, agricultural holdings, stockfarms, housing, small craft industries and industrial plants.

The European Union Solidarity Fund intervenes in the event of a major natural disaster with serious effects on the living conditions, the natural environment or the economy of a region of a Member State of the European Union.

Since a major natural disaster is defined - where the damage has not reached the national limit of 0.6% of gross domestic product - as a disaster which affects the majority of the population of a region and has serious and lasting effects on the living conditions or economic stability of the region, particularly in the case of outlying or isolated regions, does the Commission intend to finance the restoration of the damage in the prefecture of Evros in full or in part?

 
  
 

(EN)The Commission is aware of the disaster that struck the prefecture of Evros in February of this year and, first of all, the Commission wishes to express its the deep sympathy for the people that have suffered or are still suffering from the consequences of this event.

The Council Regulation establishing the EU Solidarity Fund does not allow the Commission to activate the Solidarity Fund upon its own initiative. The Commission can only act if the national authorities of the affected country formally apply for assistance within 10 weeks of the beginning of the disaster. Contacts have been established between DG Regional Policy and the Greek authorities several weeks ago and my services have given the Greek authorities detailed advice on the possibility and procedures for an application to mobilise the Solidarity Fund.

On 22 April the Greek authorities have made a formal application for assistance from the Fund. The relevant letter, however, contains no detailed information on the disaster. The Commission cannot assess this application until this information has been received. Therefore it is not able to comment today on its final position on this application.

The Commission, on this occasion, underlines that the Solidarity Fund was created to assist Member States and candidate countries in the event of major disasters with direct damage exceeding a rather high threshold – which for Greece is currently at € 918 million. If this threshold is not reached the possibilities to mobilise the Fund are extremely limited and subject to a number of very strict conditions imposed by the Regulation.

The Honourable Member may be interested to know that - with now over 30 Solidarity fund applications received since the creation of the Fund in 2002 - the Commission has come to the view that the current thresholds to mobilise the Fund are excessively high. In order to address this issue, a lower threshold has been established by the Commission in its recent proposal for a new Solidarity Fund regulation adopted on 6 April.

The Commission may still add that, for the Honourable Member’s information, in the framework of the Regional Operational Programme «Oriental Macedonia & Thrace » 3 projects totalling around 4 Million € are co-financed by the Structural Funds to reduce the risk of flooding in the Evros Region.

 

Question no 44 by Claude Moraes (H-0310/05)
 Subject: Mid-term review of the Lisbon Strategy
 

What is the Commission's view of the mid-term review of the Lisbon Strategy as it assesses the impact of Structural Funds in achieving higher rates of employment, particularly in poorer regions and in inner city areas such as my constituency in London?

 
  
 

(EN)The Commission would like to refer the Honourable Member of Parliament to the answer given by President Barroso on behalf of the Commission to his previous written question P-0620/05

The Lisbon strategy set out a broad range of objectives and policy tools the basic aim of which is to turn the European Union into the world’s most competitive economy by 2010. At mid-term in this process, the results are manifestly disappointing although that is not to say that the results are entirely negative. Although the poor economic performance has partly been the result of the cyclical slowdowns at both world and European level, much more can be done to stimulate economic growth. Structural reforms are especially important in view of the challenges of globalisation, ageing populations and enlargement that are now facing the EU.

The contribution of cohesion policy to the Lisbon Strategy is unquestionable. Cohesion policy not only has an important redistribution function in favour of the less developed Member States and regions, which has a significant macroeconomic impact; it also contributes to the competitiveness of regions where there is underutilised potential and therefore has a direct influence on EU-wide economic performance in line with the Lisbon targets.

In the framework of cohesion policy almost EUR 80 billion have been disbursed since 2000 to support the Lisbon’s strategy main aims, with the European Regional Development Fund (ERDF) and the Cohesion Fund set to invest for the period 2000-2006:

EUR 39,5 billion on transport infrastructure (out of which the trans-European networks for transport account for EUR 9,8 billion) and 19,4 billion on environmental infrastructure;

EUR 21,6 billion on the development of SME’s and 3 billion for risk capital for SMEs;

EUR 9,6 billion on research, technological development and innovation;

EUR 6,2 billion on infrastructure in the telecommunications and information society areas;

EUR 1,5 billion on educational and vocational training infrastructure

Issues such as the downsizing of urban areas in certain Member States or the concentrations of population in certain urban areas in other Member States (such as inner London) illustrate the breadth of disparities across the EU’s territory. To combat problems of social exclusion, rising crime rates, or a further worsening of quality of life in deprived urban areas, the Structural Funds support the development of participative and integrated strategies to tackle the high concentration of economic environmental and social problems affecting urban agglomerations.

For the next generation of cohesion programmes, the Commission proposes a more strategic approach in an effort to ensure that their content is targeted on growth and jobs, thus the Commission proposals for the operation of the Structural Funds in 2007-2013 entail strengthening the relationship between Structural Fund interventions and the Lisbon Strategy.

 

Question no 45 by Lidia Joanna Geringer de Oedenberg (H-0328/05)
 Subject: Funding for culture from the Structural Funds
 

The aim of the European Regional Development Fund (ERDF) is to ensure economic growth. Investment in sports infrastructure and cultural services has a positive socio-economic impact in the regions, improves the environment for job creation, attracts tourism and helps to foster a healthy life style. What are the Commission's views on a broadened scope for Structural Fund assistance for culture promotion purposes? Can the scope for ERDF funding of culture-related infrastructure and services be broadened, and if so, to what extent?

 
  
 

(EN)The Treaties provide for the support of cultural projects through Structural Funds, albeit within the designated limits.

These funds do of course need to be reoriented towards supporting and profiting from the Lisbon Strategy. However, it goes without saying that cohesion policy must find a balanced policy mix which is adapted to the current circumstances. Cultural projects must find their place in this next funding period.

Together with tourism such projects contribute both directly and indirectly to regional development. Investment in both cultural heritage and tourism activities is usually labour intensive, with comparatively high levels of local and regional demand.

The main focus of cohesion policy is on regions lagging behind in their development; the so called convergence regions. Within this group, we find regions that suffer from multiple disadvantages, having characteristics of being low income, landlocked or island, peripheral or ultra-peripheral regions. Cohesion policy must also offer suitable development strategies for these regions and while progressing towards the core targets of the Lisbon process, it must take into account the fact that tourism will remain a major source of income for most of them. The main elements of tourism strategies are the region’s natural and cultural assets. The “mise en valeur” of cultural assets is therefore of utmost importance, especially in those regions lacking significant natural assets.

Culture makes a significant contribution to the overall economy and the interface between art (including contemporary art) and related businesses offers much scope for Structural Fund activity, particularly through innovations and SMEs. It is normally a part of the economy which is rather labour intensive and is often suitable for private-public partnerships. Support for harnessing such potential, including relevant network and territorial cooperation elements, is to be sought through cohesion instruments which focus on the business sector and instruments for cross-border activities.

Consequently, ERDF regulation proposes that convergence regions include cultural assets as a means to develop sustainable tourism and to protect and enhance cultural heritage and therefore support economic development. The suggested cross-border strand of the “European territorial cooperation” objective addresses culture in two ways, firstly by encouraging entrepreneurship and SME development and secondly, by developing the capacity and joint use of infrastructures in the cultural sector.

 

Question no 46 by Alexander Lambsdorff (H-0331/05)
 Subject: Aid for regions in North-Rhine Westphalia
 

Is the Commission aware that there are regions in North-Rhine Westphalia which are facing structural problems more critical than those in other European major regions which, even after the changes made to the European aid structures from 2007 onwards, will continue to receive EU funding? For example, unemployment rates in Gelsenkirchen (16.7%) and Duisburg (26.1%) are higher than those in the five new German Länder and also higher than in some regions of the Central and Eastern European accession countries. In addition, the planned sugar market reform will require significant structural adjustment measures to be taken in the Lower Rhine region, in the Aachen Bight and in the Cologne Basin.

What will the Commission do to ensure that such disadvantaged regions continue to receive European aid in the future?

 
  
 

(EN)In July 2004 the Commission presented its draft regulations for the next funding period 2007 - 2013. In its proposal the Commission has kept its clear focus on the least developed regions, the so called “convergence regions”, having a per capita GNI below 75 % of the average EU GNI.

Nevertheless, the Commission is of the opinion that there are regions and parts of regions outside the convergence objective that would also need support from the Structural Funds. This is why it proposed a share of more than 17 % of the Structural Funds for an objective called “Regional competitiveness and employment”.

In contrast to the current funding period the Commission will not determine the geographical concentration for interventions within the objective “Regional competitiveness and employment”. In the future the regions themselves will be able to choose where to concentrate their spending in geographical terms. However, the Commission considers that a thematic concentration of spending (e.g. for innovation and research) is necessary. This should ensure a high leverage of Structural Funds investment and eventually boost Europe’s potential for growth.

As to the Honourable member’s specific concern, namely high unemployment in Gelsenkirchen and Duisburg, the Commission can assures that it is well aware of the situation in Northrhine-Westphalia and its structural problems. Partnership is an essential principle of cohesion policy, which ensures close relations with the regions in order to remain receptive to their problems and needs. In February this year the Commission visited Northrhine-Westphalia and on that occasion it went to Gelsenkirchen. The Lord Mayor Baranowski provided the Commission with examples of the efficient use of the ERDF as well as examples of where further support will be necessary.

In the next programming period, Northrhine-Westphalia will be eligible under two objectives; “Regional competitiveness and employment” and “European Territorial Cooperation”. Therefore, Northrhine-Westphalia will be able to benefit from EU support for growth enhancing investment such as innovation, accessibility, environment and human resources.

The Commission is positive that the EU Structural Fund support will help Northrine-Westfalia to overcome the difficulties it is currently facing.

 

Question no 49 by David Hammerstein Mintz (H-0276/05)
 Subject: Free movement of pet animals
 

In light of the recent confiscation of dogs that were being sent from Crete to Germany for re-homing despite being in possession of the necessary and relevant identification under Regulation (EC) No 998/2003(1) allowing free movement of pet animals in the EU, does the Commission feel that Greece is implementing and enforcing the Regulation properly? And, is it possible for Greece to prevent legal trade in pet dogs from its country?

 
  
 

(EN)The Commission does not have sufficient details about the alleged confiscation of dogs in Greece mentioned in the Question to give an exhaustive answer and would therefore ask the Honourable Member to supply additional information about the facts.
