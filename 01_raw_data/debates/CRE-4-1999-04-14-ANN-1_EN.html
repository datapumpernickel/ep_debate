Wednesday, 14 April 1999 - Strasbourg OJ edition

ANNEX

1. Questions to the Council

 

Question No 19 by Brian Crowley (H−0259/99)

Subject: Computers and the year 2000

In view of the continuing concerns relating to the computer bug in the year 2000, will the Council urge each Member State to insist that all operators of major utilities, hospitals, airports, banks, social security systems, traffic control networks and police authorities immediately report to them any serious problems encountered relating to their computer systems so that the situation can be assessed and all relevant information exchanged and coordinated at a European level through the offices of the European Commission in order to counteract potential disasters?

Answer

1. The Council is well aware of the problems arising from the fact that many computer systems and programmes will not be able to cope with the change of date at the millennium. The Council is also aware of the impact this will have on certain sensitive infrastructures and sectors such as major utilities, hospitals, airports, banks, social security systems, traffic control networks and police authorities.

2. I should like to remind the honourable Member that the Council has stressed the importance of this matter on a number of occasions: for example, the European Councils of Luxembourg, Cardiff and Vienna. The Council has also urged the Member States and all persons concerned to take action with a view to minimising any adverse effects of the millennium problem on the safety of European citizens and on the functioning of essential public services.

3. With regard to further consideration of this problem, the Council welcomes the Commission's intention to organise a meeting with representatives of public infrastructure facilities from the Member States in order to ensure that sufficient attention is paid to the cross‐border aspects in the EU.

4. The Telecommunications Council will be adopting a resolution on 22 April 1999 dealing in particular with the importance of the continuing viability of critical cross‐border infrastructure. The resolution also considers the need for emergency planning to ensure the continuity of essential services in the event of a major crash.

Question No 20 by Eva Kjer Hansen (H−0261/99)

Subject: Appointment of officials without holding a competition

It appears from ‘Agence Europe' of 5 March 1999 that the Council intends to appoint a group of employees from the ‘Schengen secretariat' without requiring them to pass either a competition or a probationary period, which would appear to conflict with the Staff Regulations.

The use of an alternative appointment procedure based on Article 24 of the 1967 Merger Treaty would mean that the Council, on specific conditions and on the basis of a proposal from the Commission and after consulting the European Parliament, could appoint outside staff as officials without competition, but the Council presumably did not consider using this procedure here.

Can the Council explain why it in this case considers it legitimate both to flout the current Staff Regulations and to ignore the Commission's right of initiative laid down in Article 24 of the Merger Treaty?

Question No 21 by Mihail Papayannakis (H−0268/99)

Subject: Incorporation of Schengen staff into the General Secretariat of the Council

With a view to incorporating the Schengen secretariat, the Council has opted for an ad hoc decision to recruit Schengen staff en bloc as officials of the General Secretariat of the Council, without following Staff Regulation procedures, in particular with regard to recruitment. According to the opinion of the Council's legal service (6 March 1998), this option is very questionable from a legal point of view in that it is not consistent with the Treaties and is contrary to the Staff Regulations. The Council is incorporating the Schengen staff despite the fact that the Secretary‐General and the Council's Staff Committee had already agreed in 1997 that Schengen staff would be incorporated on the basis of competition in line with the provisions of the Staff Regulations in this respect. The Council would seem determined, therefore, to act contrary to the Staff Regulations and the principle of the independence of the European civil service. Moreover, this decision has had serious adverse consequences within the Council. Does the Council not consider that this approach, which is not in keeping with the relevant provisions of the Staff Regulations, threatens to compromise its relations with its staff and tarnish its public image at a time when the European institutions are already being criticised over their transparency and respect for the law?

Joint answer

With the entry into force of the Treaty of Amsterdam on 1 May 1999 the Schengen Protocol will also be applied. On the basis of Article 7 of this Protocol the Council is obliged by virtue of primary legislation to take responsibility for the recruitment of the Schengen staff. To this end the Council is authorised to adopt the detailed arrangements for the integration of the Schengen Secretariat into the General Secretariat of the Council.

In the Council"s view the examination of the professional qualifications of the Schengen staff who are to be recruited and the trial period provided for will be sufficient to guarantee that the principles of a legally correct and transparent recruitment procedure are adhered to in the context of this special procedure for which provision is made in the Treaty.

Question No 25 by Jan Andersson (H−0285/99)

Subject: Action to prevent Structural Fund grants from contributing to job losses

Lear, the motor vehicle components company, has transferred operations from Bengtsfors in Sweden to Palema in Portugal as a direct result of receiving EU aid. As Lear is a major employer in Bengtsfors, the closure will have very serious consequences for the whole district. All over Sweden, people are quite rightly up in arms about EU resources being used to shed jobs. The EU's inability to do more to ensure that structural aid and regional development grants do not distort competition is quite unacceptable.

Will the Council say what steps are being taken to stop Structural Fund grants causing distortions of competition resulting in job losses, as has happened at Bengtsfors?

Joint answer

I would like to give a joint answer to the questions raised by Mrs Eriksson, Mrs Schörling and Mr Andersson as they all refer to the same case involving European Structural Funds. I would emphasise that the Council has no knowledge of the facts involved in this case and has therefore never discussed them.

All I would say is that implementation of Structural Fund aid is not the responsibility of the Council but of the Commission, and it is therefore to the Commission that you should address your questions.

I would refer in this context to Articles 169 and 170 of the Treaty, which state that the Commission ensures that the Member States fulfil their obligations under the Treaty and point to the possibility of recourse to legal action should this fail to be done.

Legislation to force manufacturers to make the necessary modifications is supposedly in the pipeline but has been repeatedly delayed. Given that around 8000 pedestrians are killed each year in Europe, with particularly high numbers in rural districts with poor roads, can the Presidency give an assurance that the rapid introduction of this vital legislation be given a high priority, and will the Presidency outline the timetable envisaged for the implementation process?

Question No 27 by Olivier Dupuis (H−0278/99)

Subject: Tunisia: the case of Mr Khémaïs Ksila

Mr Khémaïs Ksila, vice‐chairman of the Tunisian League for the Protection of Human Rights, has been imprisoned for almost two years for his activities as a human rights militant. His situation is a cause for concern as he is a diabetic and has a heart condition. He was prosecuted on charges of ‘defamation against public policy and the public authorities', 'spreading false reports likely to cause a breach of the peace' and ‘incitement of citizens to break the law of the land' and sentenced, under scandalous circumstances, to 3 years' imprisonment and a heavy fine by the Tunis Criminal Court on 11 February 1998. This sentence was upheld on appeal on 25 April 1998 and his appeal on a point of law was hastily dismissed on 28 May 1998. In the unanimous opinion of his Tunisian lawyers and 9 international observers, Mr Khémaïs Ksila is the victim of a genuine miscarriage of justice made worse by the way in which the authorities have hounded him and his family. What steps has the Council taken since Mr Ksila's arrest to persuade the Tunisian authorities to release him, stop all forms of harassment of him and his family and as well as anyone militating for the establishment of democracy and the rule of law in Tunisia and for the observance of fundamental rights? Since any steps which may have been taken have been fruitless, what further steps does the Council intend to take to obtain the rapid release of Mr Ksila? Finally, does the Council not believe that in the light of such conduct on the part of the Tunisian State it would be advisable to review relations between the EU and Tunisia?

Answer

The Council is aware of the circumstances of the Khémaïs Ksila case referred to by the honourable Member.

The Council places great political importance on the spread and observance of human rights in Tunisia and is therefore paying close attention to developments there.

Tunisia was the first of the Mediterranean partners to conclude a EURO‐MED association agreement with the European Union, which established a direct political dialogue in which both sides can openly discuss political issues, including human rights.

The Council has, in various approaches, repeatedly raised the issue of human rights and fundamental freedoms with Tunisia and it will continue to do so in future, including in relation to the Khémaïs Ksila case.

Furthermore, human rights in Tunisia will be discussed in the course of the next meeting of the CFSP working group in May.

Question No 28 by Manuel Medina Ortega (H−0279/99)

Subject: Fishing agreement with Argentina

What is the Council's policy with regard to the present fishing agreement with the Argentine Republic, the Argentine Government's failures to comply with it and the prospects for renegotiation?

Answer

The Council is aware of the problems that have arisen as a result of the Argentine Republic"s new fisheries regulation. To solve these problems a meeting of the EC‐Argentina Joint Committee was held in Buenos Aires on 4/5 March 1999.

At the Joint Committee meeting the Community called upon the Argentine Government to comply with all the provisions of the fishing agreement with the European Community. It urged in particular that the discriminatory elements of the fisheries regulation (zoning of the hake fishing area) be withdrawn. The Community also expressed its regret that the agreement is no longer a balanced one as a result of the discriminatory legislation.

The negotiations on a new agreement are the responsibility of the Commission, which has already held exploratory talks with the Argentine Government on this issue. Following the conclusion of these exploratory talks the Council will establish an appropriate mandate for new negotiations in due course.

Question No 30 by Anna Karamanou (H−0284/99)

Subject: Organisation of impartial investigations into the disappearance of 3000 people in Algeria

According to Amnesty International, the problem of disappearances in Algeria has been shown to have intensified following revelations of arbitrary arrests and illegal detention. More particularly, during the last six years, about 3000 men and women have disappeared and only very few of them have been traced following prolonged period of secret detention. The authorities and the security services are refusing to provide any information concerning the fate of detainees to their families, who are desperately searching not only hospitals and army camps but also mortuaries and graveyards for even the slightest clue as to their whereabouts. What steps will be Council take to launch a full, impartial and independent inquiry into all these disappearances and to ensure effective protection for human rights in Algeria?

Answer

The human rights situation in Algeria has improved significantly in the last year. This is also clear from reports by independent human rights organisations.

The disappearances date mostly from the mid−1990s; recently there have been hardly any such cases.

The Algerian Government is showing an obvious willingness to cooperate in this area and has now contacted the ICRC in order to deal with the subject of disappearances.

The Algerian foreign minister, Mr Attaf, has already agreed, during the EU‐Algeria consultations in October 1998 under the Austrian Presidency, to examine the cases raised by the European Union.

This topic has been dealt with on several occasions in the CFSP working group responsible. The presidency last presented Algeria with documented individual cases for examination in March. The dialogue with the Algerian Government and independent human rights organisations will be continued by the Council, including the Heads of Missions in Algiers.

Question No 31 by Mark Killilea (H−0290/99)

Subject: Monitoring of equal opportunities across the Structural Funds

The European Employment Strategy, adopted at the Luxembourg Summit, is committed to creating labour markets that provide equal opportunities and freedom from discrimination. Can the Council indicate what specific mechanisms will be put in place to ensure adequate implementation of such measures and how monitoring of Member States' activities in this respect can be carried out?

Answer

The Employment Policy Guidelines adopted in 1998 following the European Council of Luxembourg contain equal opportunities as one of the four pillars of the new employment strategy.

In the light of experience of the first year of transposition of the directives, significant importance is attached to the equal opportunities aspect of the 1999 Employment Guidelines (OJ C 69, 12.3.1999, p. 2). Directive No 19 extends equal opportunities so that it also covers the other three pillars of the strategy (employability, entrepreneurship and adaptability).

The details of transposition of the Employment Policy Guidelines are left to the discretion of the Member States. They can present, in the National Action Plans to be submitted in June 1999, those measures with which they are endeavouring to achieve the objectives set out in the Guidelines.

To ensure appropriate monitoring of transposition of the Guidelines, the introduction to the text of the 1999 Guidelines stresses the need for common indicators which must be drawn up at Community level and on the basis of comparable statistics. The Committee on Employment and the Labour Market, with the technical support of the Commission's services, is actively engaged in identifying such indicators.

Question No 32 by Alan Gillis (H−0292/99)

Subject: Angola

Given that Angola has now slipped back into civil war and the actions of both sides in this conflict are having a disastrous effect on the people of Angola in areas controlled both by the Governing MPLA and UNITA, it is the ordinary people yet again who are paying the price. What steps can the Council take to bring pressure on the warring sides to come to the conciliation table and therefore bring this suffering to an end?

Answer

The Council fully shares the honourable Member's deep concern at the current situation in Angola. We have made known our position on Angola on numerous occasions and we have repeatedly called on both parties – the government and UNITA – to honour their obligations under the Lusaka Protocol. We have repeatedly called on the government not to seek a military solution.

In its Resolution 1229 of 26 February 1999 on extending the mandate of MONUA, the UN observer mission, the UN's Security Council made it clear that UNITA bears the main responsibility for the present situation because of its failure to honour the Lusaka Protocol of 1994.

The Lusaka Protocol remains the only legal framework for a peaceful negotiated solution to the conflict. The Council is of the opinion that the EU should continue the policy it has pursued hitherto of supporting the efforts at conciliation by leading politicians from the UN and from the region and of ensuring effective implementation of the UN sanctions in Angola.

The Council is all too aware that it is the ordinary people of Angola who have to pay the price of this conflict. The EU is making efforts, both via ECHO and bilaterally on the part of the Member States, to ease the suffering through humanitarian aid. However, the only possibility of a solution to the conflict lies in an end to the fighting and in UNITA pursuing the path of peaceful negotiations.

Question No 35 by Niall Andrews (H−0297/99)

Subject: Involvement of all ‘stakeholders' in the development of EU‐supported Urban strategies and initiatives in cities such as Dublin

The Commission has submitted to the Council and the European Parliament an important Communication on ‘Sustainable Urban Development in the European Union: a Framework for Action' in which it argues that all stakeholders, including citizens, should be involved in the formulation of integrated urban strategies for sustainable development. It also argues that EU policies need to be made more ‘urban sensitive'.

With 80 % of the EU population living in towns and cities, and with urban areas having to bear the brunt of economic, social and transport problems, will the Council give an assurance that the Urban Initiative, supported from the current Structural Funds, will be central to future policies aimed at developing successful urban strategies, a matter which is vital for a rapidly expanding city such as Dublin?

Answer

The Council is aware of the specific problems of urban areas and it has always attached particular importance to strategies for successful town planning.

In the context of the talks on Agenda 2000, the European Council in Berlin also reached political agreement on reform of the EU's structural funds.

Accordingly, the European Council has adopted the Commission's proposal to reduce the number of Community initiatives to three: Interreg, EQUAL and Leader. The objectives of the Community initiatives which are to be discontinued – including URBAN – can be pursued under the new Objectives 1, 2 and 3.

These areas include those with a high population density which are suffering from above‐average unemployment, high levels of poverty, environmental damage and crime, etc.

Subject to the legislative procedure with Parliament, it is the Council's view that the future structural aid period will assign high priority to urban development policy, and that it will therefore allay the honourable Member's concerns.

Question No 39 by Gary Titley (H−0308/99)

Subject: POWs still missing following the Gulf War

Eight years after the end of the Gulf War, the fate of over 600 POWs (mainly Kuwaitis) remains unknown.

Under the terms of UN Resolution 687, Iraq is obligated to cooperate with the International Committee of the Red Cross and account for and release all POWs. In December 1998, however, Iraq stopped collaborating with the ICRC.

Will the Council now bring pressure to bear on Iraq to account fully for all POWs it took during the Gulf War and release immediately and unconditionally any POWs it still holds?

Answer

It is barbaric that eight years after the end of the Gulf War there are still prisoners of war and civilian detainees and that no information is available on their fate.

This is an item which will remain on the agenda of the international community until it has been resolved.

Implementation of Security Council Resolution 687 (the cease‐fire resolution of 1991) is, in the first instance, the responsibility of the Security Council itself which acts on behalf of all the UN's member states. In return, the member states implement the measures mandated by the Security Council: in this case, the sanctions.

The question of POWs is one which is on the agenda of the Security Council. The third of the panels set up on 30 January has just published its report, which is largely devoted to this question. The report will be one of the bases for the Security Council's future stance towards Iraq. As stated above, the issue will remain on the agenda until it is resolved.

At the same time, the honourable Member will be aware of how difficult if is to exert pressure on the Iraqi leadership with any prospect of success – and if it is difficult for the Security Council it is even more so for the Council.

Against this background, the Council respects the approach of the ICRC, which treats the fate of the POWs and – to use the ICRC's wording – 'persons whose whereabouts is not known' as a humanitarian and not a political problem, and which conducts its negotiations in the utmost confidentiality, and once again it calls on Iraq to resume cooperation within the Tripartite Commission.

Question No 43 by Christine Oddy (H−0316/99)

There is considerable agreement between the Member States on the substance of the draft regulation on the European Company Statute. Question No 44 by Carlos Bru Purón (H−0319/99)

Subject: Introduction of a European eco‐tax

The Federal Republic of Germany has decided to levy a tax on the use of hydrocarbons, varying from 0.2 DM per kw/h to between 0.06 and 0.32 DM per kw/hour, according to circumstances.

Is the Council not of the opinion that the time has now come to introduce this eco‐tax, or a similar one, throughout Europe?

Answer

Germany did indeed introduce an environmental tax reform on 1 April 1999, and the products which are being taxed are diesel, petrol, domestic fuel oil, petroleum gas and electricity. The tax revenue is returned in full to the taxpayers by means of reductions of 0.8 % in pension insurance contributions. What measures does the Commission propose to take to ensure that the use of structural fund and relocation aid does not result in the kind of distortion of competition we have seen in the case of Lear?

Joint answer

In a Community where one of the basic principles is the freedom of establishment, any enterprise can decide to set up where it wants to or to transfer its activity from one area to another. Most often, this kind of decision is taken in a broader context of rationalisation or development of an investment plan. The elements which are taken into account by a firm in deciding on localisation are numerous and, in general, unknown to the Commission. The possibility of obtaining aid is but one of them.

As regards national aid for regional development purposes, the Commission considers that this aid is compatible with the single market and in conformity with Articles 92‐94 of the EC Treaty if it conforms with the rules adopted by the Commission under its competition policy. These rules restrict inter alia the geographical scope and the admissible intensity of the aid. At the request of the Member States and subject to the Commission's approval, Community Structural Funds may part‐finance the approved national aid. In doing so, the Structural Funds compensate for the lack of resources available in disfavoured regions to assist their enterprises.

Concerning the investment by Lear corporation in Setubal, Portugal, the Commission can confirm that, according to information provided by the Portuguese authorities, public aid of 10.7 million euro will be paid in accordance with the PEDIP programme approved by the Commission in 1994. Of this amount 75 % is co‐financed by the Structural Funds. Of these 8 million euro, 2.5 comes from the European Regional Development Fund and 5.5 from the European Social Fund. In addition, the Portuguese authorities have agreed to provide up to 5 million euro in fiscal credits. Setubal being a region which qualifies as a least‐favoured region both under Article EC 92.3.a and under Objective 1 of the Structural Funds, this assistance complies with the Community competition rules on State aid.

It should also be emphasised that, according to the information available to the Commission, the investment is part of a wider strategy of the enterprise to expand its activities in Europe. The investment in Portugal amounting to 73.5 million euro, it is expected to create 4022 new jobs. At the same time, the company operates a reorganisation of its production facilities in Sweden. This includes the transfer to Thailand of most of the sewing activities of the Bengstfors plant, representing 280 jobs, whereas the production and assembly activities of the plant are transferred to Göteborg, which is a non‐assisted area. These elements indicate that there is no direct link between the company's reorganisation in Sweden and its greenfield investment in Portugal. It follows that, in the present case, the Commission is not of the opinion that Community funding has been used for the relocation of jobs into Portugal at the expense of jobs in Sweden.

On a more general level, the Commission agrees that efforts should be made to discourage ‘subsidy shopping' by investors and to avoid investments being (re)located purely as a result of the availability of aid. What is more, the Commission has already taken concrete steps in that direction:

* In 1997, the Commission has approved new Guidelines on national regional aid. The new Guidelines contain a special provision requiring Member States to make the granting of regional aid conditional on the maintenance of the investment concerned for a minimum period of five years. Member States have until 1 January 2000 to bring their existing regional aid schemes in line with this new provision.

* With respect to the next programming period, the Commission has proposed, in Article 29 of the draft Regulation laying down general provisions of the Structural Funds, that the Member States shall ensure that an operation retains the contribution from the Funds only if that operation does not, within five years of the date of the decision of the competent national authorities or the managing authority on the contribution of the Funds, undergo a substantial modification resulting notably from a cessation or change in location of a productive activity.

* Finally, in the recently issued guidelines for the next programming period, there is a statement of principle that the Structural Funds should not be used for the mere relocation of existing activities.

Question No 83 by Manuel Medina Ortega (H−0280/99)

Subject: Fishing agreement with Argentina

What action has the Commission taken to ensure compliance with the present fishing agreement with the Argentine Republic, and what safeguards are there for maintaining the activity of the Community fleet in those waters?

Answer

1. What action has the Commission taken to ensure that Argentina complies with the fisheries agreement?

The fisheries agreement between the EU and Argentina, which entered into force on 24 May 1994 for a period of 5 years, provides for the establishment of joint enterprises and temporary joint ventures.

A total of 27 joint enterprises have been set up involving 29 vessels that have been transferred permanently to Argentina; 15 of these fish for ‘hubbsi" hake. The three temporary joint ventures that were formed, involving three vessels, are now coming to an end and the vessels are returning to the Community.

Recently, since the entry into force of the fisheries agreement, Argentina"s catch of ‘hubbsi" hake has increased so much that its survival is threatened. As a result, the Argentinian Government has proclaimed a ‘fisheries emergency" and is adopting a series of management and conservation measures.

During the seventh meeting of the joint committee – held in Buenos Aires on 4 and 5 May 1998 – the parties recognised that the scarcity of the resources was not due to the activities of vessels that had been transferred to Argentina under the fisheries agreement, but rather to the inadequacies of the Argentinian system of managing and controlling its resources.

However, the Community has always supported any conservation measures aimed at ensuring that sustainable and rational use is made of resources. The Commission has often stressed that it supported all conservation efforts that were not discriminatory and that were adopted in a transparent manner on the basis of objective scientific criteria.

Through Resolution 2/99 of 5 January 1999, the Argentinian Government adopted a conservation measure reducing each vessel"s maximum catch for 1999 by 50 % compared with 1997. It also established three fishing zones according to the type of vessel.

First of all, this resolution affects the fishing activities of the vessels operating under the agreement. It was adopted without consulting the Community, either before or immediately following its adoption; it therefore contravenes Article 9(1) of the agreement.

Secondly, as regards its substance, the Community has established that this measure represents an average reduction of more than 65 % in the individual quotas allocated to these vessels. In addition, the Community considers that the establishment of fishing zones has no scientific basis and is not provided for in the agreement. It also prevents those involved from fully exercising the fishing rights they were granted under the agreement.

As a result, this resolution, which was adopted unilaterally by Argentina, disrupts the overall balance of the agreement.

As the Commission explained to the Committee on Fisheries at its meeting on 15 and 16 March 1999, it has made its position clear to the Argentinian authorities on several occasions, including in a letter of 25 January 1999 from Mr Cavaco, the Director‐General of DG XIV, to Mr Auguste, the Argentinian Deputy Secretary of State for Fisheries. In addition, talks were held on 2 February between Mrs Bonino, the European Commissioner, and Mr Alonso, the Argentinian Secretary of State for Fisheries, and a letter was sent on 1 March from Mrs Bonino and Vice‐President Marín, the European Commissioner, to the Argentinian Minister for Foreign Affairs, Mr Di Tella. Moreover, in accordance with Article 10 of the agreement, the Community asked that an extraordinary meeting of the joint committee be convened.

The letter from Vice‐President Marín and Mrs Bonino protested against the new measures adopted by Argentina and asked that the appropriate changes be made to preserve the overall balance of the agreement.

During the meeting of the joint committee, which was held in Buenos Aires on 4 and 5 March 1999, the Commission reasserted its position and asked that the reductions should be applied on the basis of the quotas allocated to each vessel and that the zones should not be introduced.

The Argentinian delegation showed no flexibility in trying to rectify the situation and reestablish the overall balance of the agreement. It reminded the Community that EUR 6 million had yet to be paid in the context of scientific and technical cooperation.

2. What safeguards are there for maintaining the activity of the Community fleet in Argentinian waters?

As the honourable Member is aware, under the agreement substantial financial aid was paid to Community vessel owners who formed a joint enterprise in Argentina with a view to transferring vessels there permanently. As a result, we cannot talk about a Community fleet that operates in Argentina; we should instead look on it as a section of the Argentinian fleet operating under the fisheries agreement that expires on 24 May 1999.

The vessels transferred under the fisheries agreement are Argentinian vessels and should benefit from the same safeguards as the rest of the Argentinian fleet. The Community will continue to ensure that these vessels are not discriminated against in any way, in accordance with the provisions of Article 9 of the fisheries agreement.
