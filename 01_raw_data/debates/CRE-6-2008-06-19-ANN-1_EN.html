Question no 9 by Gay Mitchell (H-0368/08)
 Subject: Overseas Development Assistance funding
 

The EU Member States have undertaken to bring their Overseas Development Assistance spending to 0.56% by 2010 and 0.7% of GNI by 2015. The OECD has found that total official development assistance (ODA) from EU Member States fell from 0.43% of GNI in 2006 to 0.4% of GNI in 2007 - a fall nearly 6%.The EU average is now below the OECD average of 0.45%. From these figures it is increasingly likely that EU countries will fall a long way short of their commitments. This does not sit well with the importance placed by the EU on development policy and our solidarity with the world's poorest people. Will the Council make a statement on this matter?

 
  
 

This answer, which was drafted by the Presidency and is binding neither on the Council nor on the Member States, was not given orally during questions to the Council at the June 2008 session of the European Parliament in Strasbourg.

In the second half of 2008 three important international meetings will be held: the Third High-Level Forum on Aid Effectiveness (Accra, 2-4 September), a high-level event on the millennium development goals (New York, 25 September) and an international conference on monitoring financing for development to review implementation of the Monterrey Consensus (Doha, 29 November-2 December).

On 27 May 2008 as part of the preparations for the participation of the EU at these meetings, the Council adopted decisions on the EU as a global partner for development: speeding up progress towards the millennium development goals, in which the ‘The EU strongly reaffirms its long-term financial commitment with developing countries to achieve a collective ODA target of 0.56% GNI by 2010 and 0.7% GNI by 2015, as set out in the May 2005 Council Conclusions, the June 2005 European Council Conclusions and the European Consensus on Development’.

In 2002 and 2005 the European Union adopted commitments of historic importance, in order to contribute to the progress and success of international development financing, but is seriously concerned about the reduction in the amount of common official development assistance (ODA) from EUR 47.7 billion in 2006 to EUR 46 billion in 2007. Despite this, Europe remains the biggest donor in the world, especially in Africa, and judging from the Commission report the reduction in assistance is only temporary. If the forecasts from Member States for 2008 are accurate, the level of assistance this year should again reach a record level.

In order for the EU’s commitments to be more credible in the eyes of the international community, the Council has strongly urged Member States, taking into account the monitoring conference in Doha, to formulate as soon as possible a continuous orientational multi-year time frame, which will show how they intend in national budget procedures and in the context of current competences to achieve the ODA targets.

As for the millennium development goals, the EU will – if possible by the June 2008 meeting of the European Council – draw up an action plan that will define the specific landmarks and measures, along with time frames, in key areas such as education, health, gender equality, water, agriculture, growth and infrastructure, which will contribute to fulfilling the millennium development goals by 2015.

 

Question no 10 by Sarah Ludford (H-0370/08)
 Subject: Ahmadiyya community in Indonesia
 

How is the Council responding to recent events in Indonesia involving violence directed towards the minority Muslim Ahmadiyya community, including setting fire to a mosque, vandalisation of a religious school and attacks on individuals? These despicable acts appear to be tolerated by the Indonesian authorities, since they reflect official hostility to Ahmadiyya Muslims in Indonesia, which is expected to result in a decree banning the Ahmadiyya community and placing restrictions on their civil liberties.

Will the Council give assurances that it will prevail upon the Indonesian Government to condemn the violence and vandalism, to respect the right to religious freedom and to refrain from a ban or any restrictions on the civil liberties of Ahmadiyyas?

 
  
 

This answer, which was drafted by the Presidency and is binding neither on the Council nor on the Member States, was not given orally during questions to the Council at the June 2008 session of the European Parliament in Strasbourg.

The Council of the European Union is aware of the position of the Islamic movement Ahmadiyah (Jamaah Ahmadiyah) in Indonesia. The Council is concerned at reports of violence and intolerance towards this movement, and also because it would appear that the Indonesian Government is considering a ban or restrictions on its activities.

We know Indonesia primarily for its democratic, pluralist and tolerant society, and these developments could stain its reputation, which of course it has earned.

On 9 June the Indonesian Government decided that Ahmadiyah could continue to function in the future as a religious community. It had to be enabled to profess its faith and hold religious rites. It was necessary, however, to prohibit all activities of this movement aimed at spreading different interpretations of the ‘basic teachings of Islam’.

The EU Council’s Working Party on Human Rights (COHOM) raised the issue of attacks on the Ahmadiyah community in its May meeting. On 28 May 2008 the EU troika delivered to the Indonesian Director of Human Rights and Humanitarian Affairs at the Indonesian Ministry of Foreign Affairs a coordinated demarche in which it called upon the Indonesian Government to do its utmost to provide for the safety of members of the Ahmadiyah community and to protect their mosques, as well as to conduct a proper investigation into a fire at one mosque. The Director responded to the concern of the EU regarding the attacks and possible ban on the movement with her own expression of concern, and gave an assurance that the Indonesian Government would address this issue seriously and at a high level.

 

Question no 11 by Zdzisław Kazimierz Chmielewski (H-0372/08)
 Subject: Promoting the use of energy from renewable sources
 

An examination of the proposal for a directive of the European Parliament and of the Council on the promotion of the use of energy from renewable sources (COM(2008)0019) shows there to be a disparity between the requirements resulting from the proposal and those laid down in the proposal for the amendment of Directive 98/70/EC(1), which provides for a 10% reduction in the level of life-cycle greenhouse gas emissions from transport fuels between 2011 and 2020. The latter requirement will in practice require biofuels to account for a significantly larger share of the energy mix than is provided for in the proposal for a directive on the promotion of the use of energy from renewable sources.

Would the Council not agree that the requirement for life-cycle greenhouse gas emissions from transport fuels should be brought into line with the biofuels share provided for in the proposal for a directive on the promotion of the use of energy from renewable sources?

 
  
 

This answer, which was drafted by the Presidency and is binding neither on the Council nor on the Member States, was not given orally during questions to the Council at the June 2008 session of the European Parliament in Strasbourg.

The Council is aware of the burning issues to which the Member has drawn attention. However, the debates on both directives are still at a very early stage. For the moment the three institutions have not yet agreed on appropriate levels for both targets. One of the Council’s objectives in further negotiation will be to ensure the harmony and proportionality of the agreed targets.

It is very hard to make a direct link between the two targets. The target of the proposed directive on renewable energy sources relates to all types of renewable fuels for transport, involving not just biofuels, but for instance also hydrogen and electricity obtained from renewable sources. It also relates to all forms of transport, including air and maritime transport. However, the aim of the fuel quality directive is to reduce greenhouse gas emissions from fuels used to power road vehicles and off-road mobile machinery. The use of biofuels is one of many possibilities that the relevant industries could use to reduce greenhouse gas emissions. Other possibilities include reducing flaring off at wells and increasing the efficiency of refineries. The scope for using biofuels therefore depends on their availability, relative costs and the saving from greenhouse gas emissions.

The three institutions have also agreed in principle that biofuels must adhere to sustainable criteria for reducing possible negative direct effects that might arise through production. The substance of these criteria, which are still a subject of debate, would also influence the spread of biofuels in the market.

Question no 13 by Brian Crowley (H-0415/08)
 Subject: The political situation in Burma
 

Could the Council give an updated assessment of the political situation in Burma at this time?

 
  
 

This answer, which was drafted by the Presidency and is binding neither on the Council nor on the Member States, was not given orally during questions to the Council at the June 2008 session of the European Parliament in Strasbourg.

In the weeks following Cyclone Nargis the EU focused on providing emergency assistance. In close cooperation with the European Commission, on 14 May 2008 the Presidency called an extraordinary session of the GAERC Council (development), at which the humanitarian conditions in Burma/Myanmar were addressed. In its conclusions the Council called upon the authorities of that country to enable unimpeded access to areas in Burma/Myanmar that had been affected by the cyclone.

At the May meeting of the GAERC Council, development ministers again appealed to the authorities in Burma/Myanmar to fulfil the promises they gave to ASEAN and the UN at the donors’ conference in Rangoon (25 May 2008) concerning access for humanitarian workers and humanitarian assistance to the affected areas. The Council took the view that it would be more appropriate if special attention was focused on humanitarian issues, since this could help more victims of the disaster.

The Council publicly regrets that the house arrest of Daw Aung San Suu Kyi was extended on 27 May, and again calls for the immediate release of all political prisoners and detainees. Furthermore, the EU regrets that the authorities of Burma/Myanmar conducted the constitutional referendum on 10 and 24 May in an overall climate of intimidation; for this reason the results lack credibility.

The EU trusts that multiparty elections, which the authorities have announced for 2010, will be prepared and carried out in a way that ensures a credible transition to democracy. Only real dialogue with all politically interested parties will help Burma/Myanmar achieve stability and prosperity. The EU will continue to strive in this direction in the future (including in UN forums), specifically through expression of support for the further activities of UN Secretary-General Ban Ki-Moon and Special Adviser Ibrahim Gambari, and through the actions of the EU’s special envoy Mr Fassin, who has just returned from Southeast Asia.

In any event we may hope that based on positive experiences with international assistance the country will recognise that a more open policy would be beneficial.

 

Question no 14 by Marie Anne Isler Béguin (H-0375/08)
 Subject: Resolution of the conflicts in Mali and Niger
 

In Mali and Niger, the armed conflicts between the rebels and the authorities have resumed. In Mali, despite the Algiers Agreement of July 2006 and the mediation by Algeria and Libya, violence has escalated, with the murder of two Tuareg office-holders on 11 April 2008 and attacks on barracks, including those on 21 May 2008, causing the flight of civilians to neighbouring countries. In Niger, the background to the violence is the redistribution of income from uranium extraction by the French undertaking AREVA and the impact of its operations on health and the environment.

In connection with the Strategic Partnership between Africa and the European Union (agreement signed in Lisbon on 6 December 2007), will the Council propose mediation to help resolve the conflicts in these two countries?

 
  
 

This answer, which was drafted by the Presidency and is binding neither on the Council nor on the Member States, was not given orally during questions to the Council at the June 2008 session of the European Parliament in Strasbourg.

The Council is greatly concerned at the continuously deteriorating situation in the north of Mali and Niger. This situation actually signifies a continuation of the major crisis resulting in part from drought, desertification, insufficient development and the infiltration of political Islam, which has affected the entire Sahel, from Somalia across to Mauritania.

The Council is therefore striving to be able to contribute to resolving this crisis. The deployment of European forces (EUFOR) in Chad (and in the Central African Republic) is contributing in a certain way to stabilising the entire Sahel. If the Chad containment collapses, however, the entire Sahel would be threatened by anarchy and violence.

This issue, which especially affects Mali and Niger, is regularly addressed within the Council. The issue is also being closely followed by the SG/HR: both the Malian president and the prime minister of Niger have been received in Brussels. The SG/HR was also represented at the investiture of President Touré. His envoy discussed the Sahel issue for a long time with responsible persons in leading positions in Mali. In talks with the highest authorities of Algeria and Libya, which are playing a key role in the current efforts to calm the crisis, the SG/HR regularly addresses this matter. In this connection it should be pointed out that all efforts are based on the agreements concluded in Algiers in May 2006. The SG/HR also addressed this issue with the executive secretary of ECOWAS, Dr Mohamed Ibn Chambas, specifically at the recent meeting of the troika with that body, and on 23 May 2008 the SG/HR published a statement on the recent attack in Abeibara.

Question no 16 by Athanasios Pafilis (H-0390/08)
 Subject: Provocative prosecutions of anti-fascists in Estonia
 

After the 'war' waged in 2006 over the anti-fascist monuments, the authorities in Estonia are now conducting trials against anti-fascists, such as the 89 year-old decorated 'Hero of the Soviet Union', Arnold Meri, and four young members of the social organisation which in 2006 opposed the removal of the anti-fascist monument from the centre of Tallin. The latter have protested that they were beaten up after being arrested, while one witness for the prosecution admitted in court that their original testimonies had been obtained by violence used against them by the Estonian security forces.

What are the Council's views on these provocative actions by the Estonian authorities, which have no regard for history? What does it intend to do to put an end to this flagrant anti-communism and anti-Sovietism which leads to vindication of the nazis and the persecution of anti-fascists?

 
  
 

This answer, which was drafted by the Presidency and is binding neither on the Council nor on the Member States, was not given orally during questions to the Council at the June 2008 session of the European Parliament in Strasbourg.

The Council has always affirmed, and does so now, that human rights take precedence; it is committed to preventing and eradicating all forms of degrading and discriminatory treatment, and will continue to be so committed in the future.

Question no 17 by Leopold Józef Rutowicz (H-0398/08)
 Subject: Rising energy and fuel prices
 

Energy and fuel prices are rising sharply on EU markets. At the same time, electricity and fuel companies are increasing their profits. All the indications are that the current trend towards higher prices will continue.

What measures have been taken to curb the price rises, which particularly affect EU citizens on lower incomes and small and medium-sized enterprises?

 
  
 

This answer, which was drafted by the Presidency and is binding neither on the Council nor on the Member States, was not given orally during questions to the Council at the June 2008 session of the European Parliament in Strasbourg.

The Council is well aware of the consequences of rising energy prices for European citizens, as well as their negative impact on the European economy; this issue was also addressed at recent meetings.

For some time the Council has supported the active policy in the area of energy efficiency and diversification of energy supply, which can contribute to a slowing down in the growth of energy prices, while the different routes of energy supply concern countries and regions, transport routes and types of fuel.

In 2003 the Council, together with Parliament, adopted Directive 2003/30/EC on the promotion of the use of biofuels or other renewable fuels for transport; this directive expressly provides framework targets for a minimum proportion of renewable fuels that Member States would have to place in their markets within specific deadlines. The Council advises that at the spring session of the European Council in March 2007, the action plan ‘Energy Policy for Europe’ was adopted. This sets the binding target of a 20 per cent share of energy from renewable sources in the entire consumption of energy in the EU by 2020. In line with these targets it is currently studying the Commission’s proposal for a directive on promoting the use of energy from renewable sources, which will, when it is adopted, be an additional contribution to reducing the use of fossil fuels.

Another important area of action is energy efficiency, which in its appeal for greater energy efficiency was also confirmed by the spring European Council of March 2007 – specifically that 20 per cent savings in energy consumption be achieved by 2020. The valid legislation is contributing to the fulfilment of this target, for instance the directive on energy end-use efficiency and energy services, which was adopted in 2006 by Parliament and the Council. Moreover with the aim of promoting energy efficiency on a global level the Council actively supports the establishing of an international platform for cooperation in the area of energy efficiency.

The Council emphasises that there is a need to improve the transparency of oil markets, promote competition in energy markets and strengthen dialogue with oil-producing countries. The Council would also draw attention to the agreement reached in 2005 in Manchester, under which we should avoid any tax-related and other political intervention that would distort competition, since this would prevent economic subjects from adapting appropriately. The measures intended to mitigate the consequences of higher oil prices for the poorer sections of society should be short-term and goal-orientated, and should not distort competition.

 

Question no 18 by Pedro Guerreiro (H-0405/08)
 Subject: Situation of five Cuban citizens being held prisoner in the United States - the Miami Five
 

Following on from the Council’s answer (H-0132/08)(1) concerning the plight of Antonio Guerrero, Fernando González, Gerardo Hernández, Ramón Labañino, and René González, Cuban patriots who had sought to defend their country in the face of terrorist acts being fomented and mounted by US-based organisations in Miami, one point that needs to be clarified is whether the UN Working Group on Arbitrary Detention has ever commented on the issue of visas to the relatives of these five Cuban patriots imprisoned in the US.

Because of obstructionism on the part of the US authorities, and their systematic refusal over the past two years to grant them to the relatives, visas now take longer to obtain: the waiting time required has risen on average to about a year and can run to as much as two years, as in the case of Elizabeth Palmeiro, the wife of Ramón Labañino, who has not been visited by family members since June 2006. Antonio Guerrero has been denied a visit from his relatives for more than 17 months, and Fernando González has not received any visit for over a year. As a result of the actions of the US authorities, Adriana Pérez has been prevented from visiting Gerardo Hernández, her husband, since 12 September 1998, and Olga Salaneuva has been unable, for the same reason, to visit her husband, René González, since August 2000.

What will the Council do to ensure that the five Cuban patriots locked up in US jails can enjoy the most basic human rights, including the right to receive visits from family members?

 
  
 

This answer, which was drafted by the Presidency and is binding neither on the Council nor on the Member States, was not given orally during questions to the Council at the June 2008 session of the European Parliament in Strasbourg.

The Council is aware of the fact that the US authorities are not permitting family members and other persons to have contact with five Cuban citizens convicted by those authorities. In its answer to written question E-6094/07 the Council has already regretted the fact that not even Members of the European Parliament were able to obtain permission to visit them.

The sentence passed is an internal matter for which the courts of the USA have jurisdiction. Decisions regarding the grant of visas for entry into the territory of a country also fall within the internal jurisdiction of that country. The Council is therefore not competent to make any statement on these issues.

Nevertheless the Council wishes to emphasise that it does not approve of any case in which human rights and respect of persons cannot be guaranteed to a satisfactory extent.

Question no 19 by Ryszard Czarnecki (H-0410/08)
 Subject: Situation in Macedonia
 

What is the Council's view of the situation in Macedonia, where Sunday's elections were marked by violence, killings and riots?

 
  
 

This answer, which was drafted by the Presidency and is binding neither on the Council nor on the Member States, was not given orally during questions to the Council at the June 2008 session of the European Parliament in Strasbourg.

In its decisions adopted on Monday, 16 June 2008, the Council condemned the violent incidents and other serious shortcomings associated with the elections of 1 June and identified by the Office for Democratic Institutions and Human Rights (ODIHR) of the OSCE. sustainable progress in reforms. The Council also called on all political parties to promote and participate in a constructive and inclusive dialogue, and to participate in such dialogue and to implement the priorities identified in the Accession Partnership.

Question no 20 by Eoin Ryan (H-0413/08)
 Subject: Common position of Economic Partnership Agreements
 

Given the heated discussions and the marked discord and disagreement that has hung over the issue of Economic Partnership Agreements for some time, can the Council tell us what the state of play is at the moment and if the EU and ACP countries are any nearer to a common position on EPAs?

 
  
 

This answer, which was drafted by the Presidency and is binding neither on the Council nor on the Member States, was not given orally during questions to the Council at the June 2008 session of the European Parliament in Strasbourg.

In accordance with the directives adopted by the Council in June 2002, the Commission is responsible for negotiations regarding economic partnership agreements on the part of the EU. The Commission conducts negotiations upon consultation with a special committee appointed by the Council within the context of these directives, which the Council may amend at any time. This involves an individual assessment of each case. The Directive also stipulates that previous criminal convictions could not in themselves constitute grounds for taking such measures. National legislation which introduces penalties applicable, in a general way, to all landlords who, for financial gain, intentionally assist illegal immigrants to reside within the territory of a Member State constitutes implementation of Directive 2002/90/EC. Legislation that goes beyond implementation of this Directive is a matter for national law. The Commission notes that the Charter of Fundamental Rights of the European Union, in accordance with its Article 51(1), applies to the Member States only when they are implementing Union law.
