QUESTIONS TO THE COUNCIL
Question no 20 by Liam Hyland (H-0296/01)
 Subject: Rural emergency
 

Given the rural emergency in Ireland and other countries as a result of the outbreak of foot-and-mouth disease, will the Council outline its current views on the origin of the outbreak and what plans it considers are needed to restore consumer confidence, and will it support the introduction of an emergency package for rural economies affected by foot-and-mouth disease?

 
  
 

The Council (formation Agriculture) discussed the current outbreak of foot-and-mouth disease at both its February and March sessions.

On both occasions, the representatives of the Commission and affected Member States provided information on the spread of the disease and measures taken to deal with it. The Council expressed its sympathy for those Member States that the disease had stuck and its appreciation for the prompt and decisive protective measures introduced by the Commission and national governments.

It is not the Council’s role to seek to manage the current outbreak. Existing legislation provides an appropriate framework within which the Commission and Member States can act, including on compensation. The Council has, however, already begun to discuss the lessons to be learned from the current outbreak, and will take account of these when considering any proposals from the Commission.

 

Question no 21 by David Robert Bowe (H-0298/01)
 Subject: Revision of the Directive on material for the propagation of the vine
 

With regard to the Commission proposal to revise Directive 68/193/EEC(1) on the marketing of material for the vegetative propagation of the vine, so that it covered genetically modified vines, and to its rejection by the Council of Agriculture Ministers on 29-30 January 2001, will the Council state what it now intends to do with regard to this matter?

 
  
 

I would begin by emphasising to the honourable Member that the Directive in question does not deal solely with aspects relating to genetically modified vine varieties, but that its prime purpose is to adapt to technical progress Community rules on material for the vegetative propagation of the vine which date back more than thirty years, and thus to ensure the freest possible movement of this type of product within the Community.

Secondly, I would point out that the marketing of a transgenic vine variety is already a possibility, subject to compliance with the Directive of the European Parliament and of the Council on the deliberate release to the environment of genetically modified micro-organisms, which was amended just recently on the basis of a joint text agreed between our two institutions.

The future Directive does not, therefore, constitute any fundamental innovation in this area, and merely confirms the new approach that has been adopted at Community level to improve the control of GMOs, whereby they are regulated sector by sector, following a so-called vertical approach. In giving its opinion on this proposal, your institution moreover confirmed its support for such an approach.

Lastly, let me make it clear that the Agriculture Council in January did not reject the proposal, as stated in the oral question, but merely deferred adoption to one of its subsequent meetings in order to have more time to achieve the broadest possible consensus on the future Directive.

Question no 22 by Gerard Collins (H-0301/01)
 Subject: India
 

Will the Council provide an up-to-date assessment of the development of EU-India relations following the first-ever EU-India Summit which took place in Lisbon, Portugal, in June 2000?

 
  
 

The first ever EU-India Summit, held in June 2000 in Portugal, was a milestone on the road towards a broader and deeper relationship, and has created a strong momentum that both the EU and India are building on. The Summit adopted a declaration on the EU-India partnership in the 21st century and agreed on an "Agenda for Action". Both documents set out an ambitious working program and identified key areas, in which both sides wish to enhance their cooperation, namely :

enhanced political dialogue, including further regular Summits ;

protection and promotion of human rights ;

prevention of and fight against terrorism, drug trafficking and drug abuse ;

eradication of chemical and biological weapons ;

closer coordination in international fora and organisations ;

creation of a Civil Society Round Table and of a Think Tank network ;

strengthening of the high level economic and commercial dialogue ;

support for Indian economic reforms ;

enhanced cooperation in the information technology, bio-technology and telecommunications ;

protection of the environment and promotion of sustainable development ;

development of infrastructure and removal of obstacles to trade and investment ;

trade liberalisation and strengthening of the WTO.

Since the Lisbon Summit, significant progress has been made in the implementation of several of the agreed initiatives. Political dialogue meetings have been held at Senior Officials level (Paris 06.11.2000) and at Ministerial level (Stockholm, 04.04.2001). Dates for the second Political and Business Summits, to be held in New Delhi on 23.11.2001, have been agreed upon. First contacts between the Council Working Group on Terrorism and Indian experts will take place in May 2001. The first meeting of the EU-India Round Table took place in New Delhi on 29.01.2001 and will be followed up by a second meeting later this Summer in Brussels (tentative dates 16/17.07.2001). Based on its findings, the Round Table intends to make recommendations to the next Summit. The first meeting between European and Indian think tanks is currently being prepared.

As far as Community activities are concerned, several of the agreed initiatives have been advancing well, too. High level meetings of the working groups on environment and the "information society" were held in Delhi earlier this year. The negotiations on a science and technology cooperation agreement are well advanced. The 11th session of the EC-India Joint Commission took place in New Delhi on 06.02.2001 and discussed a broad range of trade and development cooperation issues. Both sides welcomed the increased trade in the year 2000 and took stock of the progress made in their economic and development cooperation. India welcomed the EC's announcement of € 200 million Euro to be available for India's elementary education system. India and the Community underlined their determination to intensify cooperation in the social sector development and the fight against poverty, notably developing basic health services and the education system. In the context of the high level economic dialogue, and following up a first round of talks held in Brussels in October 2000, the Joint Commission launched a second round of high level consultations on WTO. Both sides further agreed to enter into discussions on a maritime transport agreement, to start negotiations on a customs cooperation agreement and to explore opportunities to further enhance market access for textiles. Finally, the Joint Commission discussed extensively the preparation of the next EU-India Summit, and in particular the European suggestion for a joint initiative to enhance trade and investment flows. Both sides agreed to invite the Indian and European business community to examine a limited number of strategic sectors and to draw up a pro-trade and pro-investment action plan in time for the Summit.

In the light of this positive evolution, the Council is content with the overall development of EU-India relations, even if some trade irritants and areas of disagreement on foreign policy issues like nuclear issues and the CTBT persist. The Council will maintain its efforts to further develop the relationship with the world's biggest democracy, while at the same time continue to stress certain foreign policy matters such as these mentioned.

 

Question no 23 by Pedro Marset Campos (H-0304/01)
 Subject: Missile shield
 

During his recent visit to Brussels the US Secretary of State, Colin Powell, announced that the United States would expect Europe to be involved in the future missile shield and the NATO Secretary-General Lord Robertson added that they agreed that there was a ‘threat’ of attack from ‘irresponsible countries’.

What is the Council’s view about the existence of this ‘threat’? What, in its opinion, is the criterion for considering a country to be ‘irresponsible’ or ‘threatening’?

 
  
 

As the honourable Member may know, there is no specific criterion according to which the EU would consider a country a "threat" to international security. However, in the framework of the Common Foreign and Security Policy, Member States regularly exchange information and discuss regional issues, addressing in particular issues related to risks of proliferation of weapons of mass destruction.

Furthermore, the EU has regular dialogue at various levels with a large number of countries. Through this dialogue, in particular with countries which are not yet engaged in international agreements on disarmament and non-proliferation, the EU has repeatedly expressed its concerns about certain regional developments it considers endanger global security.

The subject of missile defence has been raised on several occasions during discussions between the European Union and the United States. The EU representatives have expressed the EU's concern about the potential consequences of a possible deployment of the National Missile Defence System that might involve a violation of the ABM Treaty. The EU would like to see the ABM Treaty preserved and echoed the views expressed by a number of Member States on the importance of maintaining global stability through an agreed security framework and on the need for full and transparent consultations between the US and its European friends and allies and also with the Russian Federation and China on this issue.

 

Question no 24 by Konstantinos Alyssandrakis (H-0310/01)
 Subject: Continuing human rights violations by Turkey
 

Human rights are still being violated in Turkey in the most barbaric and inhumane manner. People are being killed for demanding humane living conditions and for opposing torture and fighting for democracy. The hunger strikers in Turkish prisons are continuing their struggle, and one more prisoner has died, joining the long list of those who have been killed. Cengiz Soydas who had been on a death fast for 153 days in the SINCAN F-type prison has died in the hospital to which he had been transferred.

What measures does the Council intend to take to protect human rights in Turkey so as to prevent the hunger strike claiming further victims?

 
  
 

The Council recalls to the honourable Member that human rights issues, including torture and prison conditions, figure prominently among the short-term priorities and intermediate objectives of the recently adopted Accession Partnership for Turkey (adopted on 8 March 2001; OJ L 85, 24.3.2001, p. 13). The Turkish National Programme for the Adoption of the Acquis (NPAA) is an important landmark in Turkey's preparation for EU membership and the first stage in a far-reaching programme of political reform. It is a starting point for the fundamental transformation of Turkey into a modern democracy.

With regard to the political criteria for EU membership, a preliminary analysis by the Commission suggests that further efforts will be required in a number of areas. The implementation of the Accession Partnership, in the light of the Turkish NPAA, will be closely monitored by the Council and the Commission. Human rights issues are also part of the enhanced political dialogue between the EU and Turkey.

The EU deeply regrets that the hunger strikes in various prisons in Turkey have led to the loss of life and has urged the Turkish authorities to take all appropriate measures to avoid further fatalities. It has also reminded them of recommendations of the Committee for the Prevention of Torture in this regard.

The Council welcomes the intention of the Turkish authorities to continue cooperation with the Council of Europe in the context of prison reform, in particular with its Committee for the Prevention of Torture, and their decision to make the preliminary observations of this Committee (following visits in December 2000 and January 2001) available to the public.

 

Question no 25 by Inger Schörling (H-0313/01)
 Subject: Subsidised transport of live animals
 

It emerged during a hearing at the European Parliament that a large amount of aid is paid out to subsidise the transport of animals for slaughter, without proper checks being carried out to verify compliance with the rules on the transport of animals, that only a fraction of EU-subsidised animal transport operations are checked and that infringements of EU rules are a regular occurrence. What does the Council propose to do to put a stop to this situation?

 
  
 

The Council would remind the honourable Member that Directive 91/628/EEC of 19 November 1991 on the protection of animals during transport, as amended by Directive 95/29/EC of 29 June 1995, introduces a set of rules designed to implement a welfare policy for animals during transport. In fact, the Community does not subsidise the transport of live cattle but it grants export refunds for export of cattle and beef.

The Council completed this set of provisions by adopting on 18 December 1997 the Regulation (EC) 2634/97 of 18 December 1997 amending Regulation (EEC) 805/68 on the common organisation of the market in beef and veal (OJ C 356, 31.12.1997) stipulating that, in the case of export of live animals, the payment of the export refund shall be subject to compliance with the provisions of Community legislation concerning animal welfare and, in particular, the protection of animals during transport. Council Regulation (EC) 1254/99 on the common organisation of the market in beef and veal currently in force also stipulates the above-mentioned criteria.

The Council is not aware of the dysfunctions referred to in the question and underlines that it has not received any proposal from the Commission to modify current legislation. To that extent, the honourable Member is invited to address her request directly to the Commission, which is in charge of the implementation of EC legislation.

 

Question no 26 by John Walls Cushnahan (H-0317/01)
 Subject: Football - false passports scandal
 

Will the Justice and Home Affairs Ministers take action in response to the 'false passport' scandal involving some leading European football clubs?

Is the Council aware that such false documentation is being used both to flaunt the restrictions on non-European players and to facilitate the exploitation and 'human trafficking' of very young South American and African footballers?

 
  
 

I am sure that the honourable Member will agree that it is not for the Council as a body to address individual cases concerning the use of fraudulent documents. Of course the Council condemns any recourse to the use of such documents and is seeking actively to address the problem of illegal immigration in a broad and comprehensive manner. I would remind the honourable Member of the measures taken over recent months to tackle illegal immigration and in particular to combat the networks involved in trafficking human beings, starting with the seminar organised in July last year under French Presidency on the fight against illegal immigration networks. Initiatives proposed by France with regard to penalties for persons abetting illegal immigration and on carriers transporting persons lacking the necessary documents into the EU are currently under examination.

Measures are also taken both at national level and in the framework of programmes such as Odysseus or TACIS to ensure that officials, not only in the Member States but also in the candidate countries and in certain third countries which benefit from Community assistance, are trained in the detection of false documents.

It remains, however, the responsibility and competence of national authorities to commence proceedings in respect of persons suspected of having committed criminal offences.

Question no 28 by Bernd Posselt (H-0319/01)
 Subject: Kaliningrad/Königsberg
 

In the light of eastwards enlargement in the near future, how does the Council Presidency view the possibility of obtaining special status for the Kaliningrad/Königsberg region, and what are the EU's thoughts on contributing to the economic development and demilitarisation of the region and to combating crime there?

 
  
 

The Council is well aware of the challenges and difficulties related to the Kaliningrad region of the Russian Federation. The Common Strategy of the European Union, adopted in 1999, already called for closer cooperation with Russia on this issue. Subsequently, an Action Plan for the Northern Dimension in the external and cross-border policies of the European Union was prepared by the Council and endorsed by the European Council in Feira. It invited the Commission to follow-up on Kaliningrad, among other issues.

As a response, the Commission adopted on 17 January 2001 a Communication to the Council on the ‘EU and Kaliningrad’. After a preliminary examination, the Council at its meeting on 26 February 2001 welcomed the Commission communication as providing a good basis for developing further EU cooperation with Russia and neighbouring countries. It should, in fact, ease the addressing of specific problems related to the Kaliningrad region, regarding the movement of goods and people, energy supplies, fisheries, economic development, governance, democracy and the rule of law, environment and health. Currently the communication is subject to a more detailed examination in the relevant Council bodies.

On the Russian side, both federal authorities in Moscow and local authorities in Kaliningrad have welcomed the communication as providing a good basis for future discussion. The same applies to Lithuania and Poland.

The EU and Russia have agreed that the question of Kaliningrad will be examined jointly in the relevant bodies of the Partnership and Cooperation Agreement. Such discussions have already started, and will continue on a regular basis. Furthermore, to gather first-hand information, Anna Lindh, the Swedish Minister for Foreign Affairs on behalf of the Presidency and Chris Patten on behalf of the Commission visited the region in February this year.

Discussions on Kaliningrad with the neighbouring countries, Lithuania and Poland, will continue in the context of the relevant bodies under the Association Agreements. Finally, the issue of Kaliningrad can also usefully be addressed in the context of the Northern Dimension initiative.

While it is premature to indicate what type of solutions can be identified to various problems related to the Kaliningrad region and what instruments could be used to address these problems, the issue remains a key priority in EU/Russia relations. The Council will therefore keep it under close scrutiny.

Question no 43 by James (Jim) Fitzsimons (H-0295/01)
 Subject: Exporting nuclear waste to Russia
 

Two EU member States are among a number of countries world-wide considering the export of nuclear waste to Russia. The site most likely to be used for the storage of imported spent nuclear fuel is thought to be Mayak, the world’s largest nuclear complex and one of the most radioactively contaminated sites in the world. What are the Commission’s views on this unsatisfactory situation?

 
  
 

With the entry into force of the International Atomic Energy Agency Joint Convention on the safety of spent fuel management and radioactive waste, Member States will be prohibited from exporting shipments of radioactive material for reprocessing or disposal to third countries that do not have the “technical, legal or administrative resources” to manage it safely. It would therefore be for the relevant authorities in Member States to assess whether this condition is met before any nuclear waste could be shipped to the Russian Federation. The convention builds on principles that already apply under Euratom Directive 92/3 of 1992 to shipments of waste.

Although outside the direct competence of the Commission, there are very considerable doubts in the international community over whether the facility at Mayak is at present able to accept and safely process spent fuel or radioactive waste from outside Russia.

The Commission has not received any official notification from Community nuclear facilities of exports of fissile materials to Russia. Under Euratom legislation, the Commission is required to validate such declarations, in particular to ensure compliance with international agreements.

 

Question no 44 by Hans Karlsson (H-0312/01)
 Subject: The Middle East peace process
 

The recent events in the Middle East are well known to us all. The need for a renewed peace process, not only involves putting an end to the suffering of the peoples concerned or stabilising regional security, it also involves future European security, even international security. Every additional person killed or injured means one step further away from a peace agreement and mutual understanding between the belligerents. The excessive use of violence has to be stopped immediately.

The continued peace process cannot rely on the decreasing attempts made by the US Administration. The EU must take the responsibility and play an active role in creating the conditions for a just and comprehensive peace. That constitutes not only a request from its partners in the region, but a responsibility that we can no longer avoid.

What will the Commission do in order to facilitate the conditions for a just and comprehensive peace in the Middle East? What will it do in order to stop the excessive use of violence that results in the daily killings in the West Bank and Gaza?

 
  
 

As the Commission has indicated on previous occasions, it is seriously concerned about the loss of life on both sides. Too many people have fallen victim to an excessive use of force. The Commission has condemned the extra-judicial killings perpetrated by Israeli security forces as unacceptable behaviour and as a threat to the peace process. But it is also deeply concerned by the killing of innocent Israeli civilians by Palestinians and deeply deplores the acts of cruel, cold hearted and destructive terrorism which have been perpetrated over the past week.

The Commission has little reason to hope that the parties will resume discussions soon. There is a wide consensus however on the need to launch simultaneous discussions on economic security and political issues: it is far from clear how this complex process can be started. The Commission is ready to assist in any way to facilitate talks. It supports the principles and mutual understandings which have been reached thus far in negotiations and it has urged the parties to abide by their obligations under agreements reached so far.

The Commission is distressed by the deteriorating humanitarian and economic situation in the West Bank and Gaza, about the destabilising impact the Israeli closure is having on the economic and social fabric of the Palestinian community and on the institutional framework of the Palestinian Authority. The collapse of the Palestinian Authority will set the cause of peace back even further, threaten Israeli security, and jeopardise efforts to promote cooperation and stability in the Middle East. The Commission has urged the Israelis to resume the transfer of tax revenues owed to the Palestinian Authority and lift the economic blockade. This is clearly stated in the Stockholm European Council conclusions.

As discussed at the European Council in Stockholm, it is needed to assess now how the Union can contribute to a resumption of the peace process. It will continue the dialogue with both sides and will repeat its pleas for an end to violence and to the economic blockade.

The Commission is providing aid to the Palestinian Authority to help it meet its minimum obligations. To this end the Commission will provide €60 million in budgetary aid in order to sustain the Palestinian Authority over the next six months. It is working with other donors to ensure that they also contribute to this support.

 

Question no 45 by Caroline Lucas (H-0315/01)
 Subject: EU relations with Colombia
 

Given Parliament's recent resolution R5-0055/2001(1) which calls for the involvement of the local population in the choice and delivery of projects for building peace, would the Commission please explain which regions of Colombia the EU is engaged in and how and why these regions were chosen? Further, what is the extent of the human rights violations perpetrated by paramilitary organisations in these regions, especially in the light of the recent revelations from the UN Office in Bogota that the paramilitary has grown by 81 % in the last two years? Will it also explain what the human rights criteria are for these projects, who has selected the criteria, who is monitoring them and what training has been provided for the personnel on the ground who are carrying out these projects? Finally, can the Commission guarantee that the EU assistance package will remain wholly separate from the US 'Colombia Plan'?

 
  
 

The Colombian peace-process is, indeed, a major concern for the Commission. The member of the Commission in charge of External relations has just come from Colombia, where he met President Pastrana on 27 March. The best has to be done to make the peace process irreversible so that it becomes a state policy. The Union's political and financial commitment to the peace process is crucial to this end.

The Community support package for the peace process in Colombia will be oriented towards actions that underpin the peace process itself. 105 M€ of programmable aid will be made available by the Commission over the period 2000-2006: it will be dedicated to modernising administration, strengthening democracy and the rule of law, respect for human rights, protecting the environment and developing alternative crops. In addition, it is hoped that Member States’ bilateral aid focuses in these areas so that a coherent and properly coordinated package of aid can be presented. The exact geographical location of these new, planned projects in support of the peace-process is not yet clearly defined. We have a technical mission in Colombia right now: the delegation for cooperation will naturally depend on the results of this mission. A proposal for a "peace-laboratory" in the Magdalena Medio area - where ELN acts - is currently being studied and we are also looking for possible projects in non ELN areas.

So much for the projects planned for the future. As to the ongoing Community projects, they are financed under different budget lines: financial and technical cooperation, ECHO (to assist displaced persons), NGO-cooperation, tropical forests and environment, human rights and others. These projects are carried out almost all over the country.

The intervention areas for the ECHO projects are selected by taking into account the number of displaced people and their vulnerability. Financial and technical cooperation projects are elaborated together with the beneficiary country, (which also provides significant counter-part funding). Projects carried out by NGOs follow a "demand-driven" logic. They are submitted by NGOs who work in the field. In all these cases, the local population is involved in the design of the project.

The Human rights violations committed by the paramilitaries. As it can be seen on one of the enclosed maps, the paramilitaries are present in many areas of Colombia (and are expanding even further). Human rights violations committed by the paramilitaries are increasing fast. The situation is a matter of extreme concern, both in terms of the human rights violations committed by the paramilitaries, but also with regard to the guerrilla groups.

The Commission would like to stress that in all projects the promotion of human rights and international humanitarian law is essential. Personnel are well trained and they of course monitor the human rights situation. But the honourable Member needs to be aware of the fact that the security conditions in the field are extremely difficult, which may complicate the implementation of the package.

A final word on "Plan Colombia". As repeatedly stated, the Commission does not intend, within the framework of its aid package, to take part in any initiative with a military dimension. Plan Colombia remains a Colombian domestic issue. The package aims to support the Colombian peace process and the efforts of the Colombian Government to this end.

Question no 46 by Glenys Kinnock (H-0321/01)
 Subject: Burma
 

On 20 March 2001, 53 Burmese organisations from 28 countries called on the international community to intensify efforts to ensure that talks between the Burmese military regime and democratic opposition are transformed into a genuine tripartite dialogue between the military regime, the democratic opposition and ethnic minority groups.

How does the Commission intend to respond to this statement, to increase pressure on the junta to commit itself to a genuine tripartite dialogue?

 
  
 

Like the participants of the meeting which took place in Bangkok on 20th March 2001, the Commission welcomes the current "talks" between Aung San Suu Kyi and the State Peace and Development Council (SPDE) and sincerely hopes that they will soon lead to concrete steps towards national reconciliation, democracy and the respect for human rights for the benefit of all the peoples of Burma.

In order to achieve these goals the SPDC, the National League for Democracy and all the ethnic groups should be involved in a genuine dialogue. A lasting solution must be based on agreement between the SPDC, the democratically elected leaders and the representatives of the ethnic minorities.

Not having reached yet a stage of real "dialogue", the contacts between Aung San Suu Kyi and the SPDC are still at a very sensitive stage. Both sides have undertaken not to reveal their contents, a decision which must be respected. But naturally the Commission supports the talks, encourages them, and hopes to see progress soon. It is by doing this, and by continuing to encourage both sides to make progress, that the Commission can make the best contribution at the present time.

Question no 60 by John Bowis (H-0282/01)
 Subject: The precautionary principle and food
 

Does the Commission agree with Sir John Krebs, Chairman of the UK Food Standards Agency, that, in order to be effective and credible, the 'Precautionary Principle' must incorporate the 'Proportionate Principle', whereby any proposed action in response to a perceived or assessed risk must be proportionate to the likely scale and scope of the risk?

 
  
 

The Commission’s Communication on the precautionary principle has initiated a lively debate both within the Community and internationally. The Commission has taken concrete action to ensure that this principle becomes better understood and correctly used. The proposal from the Commission for a Regulation on General Food Law and the establishment of the European Food Authority clarifies the role of the precautionary principle in the management of risks in the food and feed supply. In addition, the Commission is actively pursuing an international consensus in several fora including in Codex Alimentarius.

In the majority of risk management situations, a full set of scientific information is available on which to base a decision. However, this is not always the case. Essentially the precautionary principle is a risk management tool which comes into its own in those specific circumstances where, following an evaluation of all the available scientific information, a risk to health is identified but it is clear that further scientific information is needed.

Notwithstanding some lacunae in the scientific data, if adverse health or environmental effects have been identified, decision-makers have to act and cannot wait for every morsel of scientific data to be sought and evaluated. However, measures based on the precautionary principle must be kept under review while further scientific data is collected.

This principle should not be misunderstood. Risk management decisions based on the precautionary principle are not based on an uninformed perception of a problem but are based on an evaluation of all the available pertinent data, scientific and otherwise.

The principle of proportionality is a legal principle common to all decision making whether the precautionary principle has been invoked or not.

In the guidelines to the use of the precautionary principle, as incorporated in its Communication, the Commission explicitly states that the principle of proportionality must be respected.

The Nice European Council supported the Commission’s Communication and also referred to the need to ensure that the principle of proportionality is respected. Indeed the resolution calls upon the Commission to apply systematically its guidelines on the conditions for use of the precautionary principle which includes respect of the principle of proportionality.

In concrete terms, how this principle is expressed, the nature of the action that must be taken to protect health or the environment, will depend very much on the nature of the risk, and the level of protection needed.

This may fall anywhere in-between the removal of a product from the market to much milder action, which nevertheless, protects against adverse health or environmental effects. Any measure should not only be proportionate, but its application must be practical and feasible, and where relevant, the implementation of controls must be possible.
