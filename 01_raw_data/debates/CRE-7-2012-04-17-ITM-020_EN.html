Verbatim report of proceedings
Tuesday, 17 April 2012 - Strasbourg OJ edition

20. One-minute speeches (Rule 150)
Video of the speeches
PV
 

  President. – The next item is the one-minute speeches on matters of political importance in accordance with Rule 150 of the Rules of Procedure.

 
  
 

  Anna Záborská (PPE). – (SK) Mr President, we have focused on human rights this afternoon. In this context, I would like to highlight two points. In April 2012, we are assessing human rights in the world of 2010: we are considering something that should no longer be current. I would understand this if the report on human rights were drafted by students with no right to a fee. I do not understand, however, why the Council Secretariat only published the report in September 2011, and I have absolutely no idea why we are only discussing it in today’s plenary. My second comment concerns Belarus. With regard to human rights, Lukashenko’s regime has changed for the worse since 2010. I would therefore like to call upon the International Ice Hockey Federation not to organise this year’s World Championship in Belarus. Belarusians play good ice hockey and have the right to host this sport’s championship. Dictator Lukashenko, however, must not use this as proof that the world has accepted his government.

 
  
 

  Izaskun Bilbao Barandica (ALDE). – (ES) Mr President, on 26 April, we will commemorate the 75th anniversary of the bombing of Guernica during the Spanish civil war. At the time, the German air force was testing techniques for large-scale attacks on the civilian population, which were perfected during the Second World War. Franco, for his part, was attacking Basque institutions and national identity, which remained one of his objectives throughout his whole dictatorship.

Guernica is now a symbol of peace in the world, made stronger by Picasso’s famous painting, which should be exhibited in the Basque Country.

In 1977, the German State apologised to the Basque people for its part in that massacre.

Today, in Spain, far-right revisionist historians are denying the truth, and many politicians are quietly acknowledging that pro-Franco impunity was the key to Spain’s successful transition to democracy. Perhaps that is why the only judge who has tried to investigate the dictator’s crimes has been expelled from the legal profession.

I am therefore using this 75th anniversary as an opportunity to call for justice, an apology, and recognition for the victims. Seventy-five years later, it is vital that we settle our debt with the millions of people whose lives, dignity and freedoms were stolen from them by supporters of Franco.

 
  
 

  Marek Henryk Migalski (ECR). – (PL) Mr President, I would like to refer to the issue discussed by Ms Záborská, that is, Lukashenko and the dictatorship in Belarus. In recent days, two political prisoners, Sannikov and Bondarenko, have been freed. This is a good move, but I believe that the reason that it is so good is that the European Union showed that, from time to time, it can be firm and unequivocal in its condemnation of the regime in Belarus.

Let us not forget, however, that at the present time, there are more than a dozen political prisoners in Belarus and that the policy decided on in Parliament over the last couple of weeks should be maintained as it has begun to bear fruit. I hope that neither our Chamber nor the European Union as a whole will show signs of weakness or that for which our institution has unfortunately been well known, namely, a certain changeability that manifests itself as taking one step forward in order to take two steps back. Let us maintain fixity of purpose in this regard and this will bring about positive effects, both for the inhabitants of Belarus and for ourselves.

 
  
 

  Claudio Morganti (EFD). – (IT) Mr President, ladies and gentlemen, in recent weeks, a few episodes have taken place that I would simply call curious, were it not for the fact that they represent an extremely serious and dangerous idea. In Italy, a group of pseudo-researchers, that has, however, won European and international awards, has announced its intention to reconsider, or even ban, the reading of the Divine Comedy, Dante’s masterpiece known and appreciated for centuries by millions of citizens worldwide. In their view, the work has too many racist, anti-Semitic and Islamophobic references and should therefore be revised in a modern and politically correct form.

In Vienna, the city’s Restaurateurs’ Association has proposed banning the names of some traditional dishes, such as gypsy cutlets or Negro bread, so as not to offend the sensibilities of others. These episodes are not isolated, but represent a common and widespread feeling in Europe, in other words, the need to deny our roots simply to please other cultures, but a tree without roots dies, and that is the risk Europe runs if it does not immediately change direction and defend its origins and traditions.

 
  
 

  Marisa Matias (GUE/NGL). – (PT) Mr President, last week, Portugal voted in favour of the treaty or, rather, the budget ultimatum, as it is worth pointing out that it is not, in fact, an EU treaty. The vote in favour of the government parties reflects the accumulation of decades and decades of stupidity and an attitude typical of a good boot-licking student who does not think and obeys bad teachers. The Socialist Party’s position, and that of its leader, changed from an extremely dangerous and violent abstention to a grovelling vote in favour. This alliance makes our Portuguese Parliament the first parliament to accept the humiliation of having one of its most important functions, for which the people elected it, taken away: deciding the country’s annual budget. This is yet another blow from the European Union, yet another, in which financial power is increasingly out of reach of citizens’ votes. I ask, Mr President, is it democratic for someone to vote for not being able to vote any more?

 
  
 

  Crescenzio Rivellini (PPE). – (IT) Mr President, ladies and gentlemen, the situation in Italy is dramatic and our involvement in Europe is at a crisis point. This is because Italian workers do not understand Europe when they lose their jobs because big companies are shutting down in our country and opening up in European countries with lower wage costs. They do not understand Europe when the ECB, on paper, finances small and medium-sized enterprises, whereas in fact, it only helps banking institutions.

They do not understand Europe when a government that was democratically elected by the Italian people is removed and replaced by technocrats, whose only proposal to solve the crisis has been to raise taxes and increase the price of fuel. They do not understand Europe when Italian fishermen on the border with Croatia are penalised by rules which the Croats, who will soon be entering the common market, do not respect. They do not understand Europe when countries which have no connection with our continent come here to exploit our resources. Europe has boundaries which are clearly defined by history: the rocks of Stonehenge to the west, the wheat of Budapest to the east, the Brandenburg Gate to the north and Castel del Monte to the south: this is Europe.

 
  
 

  Ioan Enciu (S&D). – (RO) Mr President, the alleged problem of secret CIA detention centres and flights in Europe has come to the attention of the European Parliament once again, which is a positive thing, given that it is vital that the truth comes to light.

The fight against terrorism cannot and must never be regarded as more important than the protection of human rights. Therefore, any abuses of these rights must be condemned. On the other hand, however, we must be mindful that repeated accusations against states, without any real evidence, also constitute an abuse. Romania, for example, was repeatedly accused of hosting secret CIA detention centres, whilst the only evidence for these allegations was a series of articles in the press. Parliamentary and civil inquiries into the matter have since proved that there have been no such detention centres. Therefore, I want to call attention to the fact that the truth about CIA detention centres will not be discovered through launching frequent and unfounded accusations, but through conducting a reasonable analysis of real and objective facts, which, at least in Romania’s case, disproved the existence of these detention centres.

 
  
 		 

  Pat the Cope Gallagher (ALDE). – (GA) Mr President, I believe that ratifying the fiscal treaty will be the foundation stone of economic recovery in Ireland and in the euro area.

(EN) The economic problems facing Europe in recent years has required a response across a range of different policy areas.

(GA) For the past couple of years, the Union is implementing new rules to improve the regulation of the financial services sector. And now the Union is proposing a treaty that would give more stability to the euro area. Without a stable euro area, economic recovery will be very difficult to stimulate.

Financial stability would create the economic conditions needed for job creation. Countries that ratify the Fiscal Treaty will have access to the European Stabilisation Mechanism. This aspect of the Treaty will be particularly important for Ireland when the country returns to the international financial markets. Those opposed to the Treaty must show how Ireland can succeed in returning to the markets without being a member of it.

 
  
 

  Rui Tavares (Verts/ALE). – (PT) Mr President, a sad reality is being repeated in Guinea-Bissau: once again, instead of defending the nation or the constitutional order and human life, the military are attacking them all to make way for their dirty dealings.

A UN report in 2008 warned that the uninhabited islands of Guinea-Bissau were being used by drug traffickers operating with total impunity with the agreement of key military figures, based on corruption and complete lawlessness. What has been done since 2008? What has the EU done? It ordered the withdrawal of the mission that was in Guinea-Bissau to work with the Guinea-Bissau national army, to try to instil a civic mentality in that army. It was a very serious error. We already have a failed state and if, overnight, we are faced with a very serious situation that is difficult to resolve and has consequences for the EU, with drugs and arms trafficking in that country, we and our inconsistent strategy in Guinea-Bissau will also be at stake.

I would like to send a message of hope to the people of Guinea-Bissau, who we know want peace, reform and moderation in their country and want to rebuild and go about their lives.

 
  
 

  Ryszard Czarnecki (ECR). – (PL) Mr President, ladies and gentlemen, two years ago, an aircraft carrying the President of Poland, government leaders and the most senior military leaders from my country crashed near Smolensk in Russia. Two years have passed and the wreckage of that plane is still in the hands of Russia, which does not want to return it to Poland. Likewise, it does not want to hand over the black boxes. Both these items are important pieces of evidence in the investigation. Practically speaking, the investigation cannot make any progress. It is an extraordinary situation that, in the 21st century, it is not possible to identify the causes of this disaster, which was so tragic for my country. I hope that the European Union will become involved in this matter and make the investigation an international one. This is absolutely vital to clarify what happened.

 
  
 

  Gerard Batten (EFD). – Mr President, jobs which could easily be saved are being lost in London because of EU legislation. The Tate and Lyle sugar refinery in the borough of Newham has been forced to cut back on production and lay off 30 staff. This is because, under EU quota rules, they cannot buy the raw sugar they need within the EU.

However, the Commission has the power to change the rules and allow Tate and Lyle to buy what they need on the world market. I wrote to Commissioner Cioloş on 14 March, asking him to urgently meet with Tate and Lyle so they could put their case for resolving the problem. So far, I have not had a reply.

We cannot afford to lose these jobs in one of the poorest parts of London. I urge Commissioner Cioloş to meet with Tate and Lyle as soon as possible in order to reach a solution.

 
  
 

  Søren Bo Søndergaard (GUE/NGL). – (DA) Mr President, I would like to appeal to the President on behalf of the human rights activist, Abdulhadi al-Khawaja, who has now been on hunger strike in a prison in Bahrain for 69 days. He is doing this in protest against the king’s dictatorship and against his detention and torture. What was his crime, you might ask? It was to fight for democracy and human rights – not with violence, but with words and peaceful demonstrations. After being on hunger strike for 69 days, Mr al-Khawaja is on the verge of death. This must not be allowed to happen. His fight must be our fight. Therefore, I would call on the President to ensure that an urgent appeal is sent from the European Parliament to the King of Bahrain. Mr al-Khawaja must be released immediately. Every day counts, and tomorrow might be too late.

 
  
 

  Seán Kelly (PPE). – (GA) Mr President, thank you for giving me the opportunity to say a few words about an issue that is troubling me, Irish MEPs and particularly my colleague, Phil Prendergast, from the Socialists.

An Irish citizen was murdered by Bolivian forces exactly three years ago now.

Despite what the Bolivian authorities said, it would appear that Michael Dwyer was unarmed, yet he was shot in the heart and three times in the back by the Bolivian authorities. Naturally, the family want answers to questions but, despite three years having elapsed, the Bolivian authorities are not willing to grant an open, independent, international inquiry into the situation.

His parents, Martin and Caroline, and his sister, Aisling, were here last week. In fairness, they got a good hearing here in Parliament, especially from Lady Ashton, who is supportive of a démarche between both countries, but we need an independent, international open inquiry. We are giving EUR 234 million in aid to the Bolivian authorities and that should be used to force them to give closure to this situation and grant the inquiry.

 
  
 

  Emer Costello (S&D). – Mr President, as a member of Dublin’s North Inner City Drugs Task Force, I have received an increasing number of reports of violence, harassment and intimidation targeted against the families – including elderly parents – of drug users and against addicts over drug debts, very often involving small sums of money.

In 2008, the Dial to Stop Drug Dealing and Threats service was launched in Ireland. It provides a confidential service for reporting drug dealing. Since then, there have been over 9 000 calls to the service, resulting in two and a half thousand reports to the Irish police’s drugs unit.

I would like to urge the Commission to now consider introducing a common Europe-wide Dial to Stop Drug Dealing and Threats service, similar to the Europe-wide numbers for missing children, child help lines, crime victims and non-emergency medical calls.

I would also urge the Commission to support and fund this very useful public service under the EU drugs action plan, the Daphne Programme, and other similar EU initiatives, so that we can deal with this issue on a transnational and Europe-wide basis.

 
  
 

  Ramon Tremosa i Balcells (ALDE). – Mr President, yesterday, a new chapter was written in the long history of nationalisations. Argentina decided to nationalise YPF, an oil company and the property of Repsol. This unilateral move will make the government of Argentina the biggest stakeholder in the company with a 51% participation and will leave Repsol with only 6% instead of the 57% it has now.

This attack against the rule of law and private property creates juridical insecurity and signifies a loss of confidence for international investors in Argentina. This is an action that brings Argentina closer to Cuba and Venezuela than to successful and open Brazil.

Therefore, I ask this Chamber, the Commission and the Council to support Repsol and all other European investments in Argentina against this situation, to put trade negotiations temporarily on hold, and to put in place measures and strong mechanisms to ensure that it is not repeated in the future.

 
  
 

  Nikos Chrysogelos (Verts/ALE). – (EL) Mr President, as today is the Global Day of Action on Military Spending, we need to learn from the mistakes made by Greece in this sector. Between 1974 and 2011, Greece spent around USD 218 billion on defence and arms programmes. In fact, between 2001 and 2011, despite the reduction in arms and defence spending in 2011 due to fiscal adjustment, Greece was the fifth biggest importer of military equipment in the world after China, India, South Korea and the United Arab Emirates, spending USD 10.3 billion. However, over the five years from 2007 to 2011, Greece was the biggest recipient under German and the second biggest recipient under French arms programmes.

We certainly need to learn from our mistakes, because those who benefited from the arms programmes were mainly the arms industry and the senior servants in the Greek administration as well as Greek politicians responsible for selecting the various arms programmes. This culminated, of course, in the arrest of the former Greek defence minister, Mr Tsochatzopoulos.

 
  
 

  Jacek Włosowicz (EFD). – (PL) Mr President, a moment ago, we concluded the debate on negotiations for association agreements with Armenia and Azerbaijan. It is, however, also a matter of considerable concern, I believe, to all Members of the European Parliament to see the current situation in Iran, a country that borders on this region. According to media reports, the talks conducted by six powers with Iran in Istanbul on Saturday could provide some grounds for optimism. Apparently, the Iranian side signalled its readiness to engage in discussions on its nuclear programme.

What does worry me, however, is the fact that, on the other side, we can see a concentration of Russian forces on the southern frontier of Russia, namely, in the Caucasus. I believe that we should do everything possible to prevent Iran from enriching uranium, even though it is claimed that this is for civilian requirements in the fields of medicine and power generation. We should continue negotiations in Baghdad next month and, at the same time, keep Russian activities under constant surveillance, so that it does not profit from a situation where attention is focused on Iran in order to take control over former Soviet republics and, in particular, over their natural resources.

 
  
 

  Gianni Vattimo (ALDE). – (IT) Mr President, ladies and gentlemen, I would like to remind you that, this week in Washington, many members of civil society and various political parties from around the world will be meeting to call on President Obama to perform an act of justice: to release the five Cubans arrested in 1998 in Florida and accused by the US Government of committing espionage and infiltrating into the US as secret agents of a foreign State, even though none of this could ever be proven.

Tried in a climate of great hostility in Miami, they were sentenced to extremely harsh prison terms, in violation of the International Covenant on Civil and Political Rights. The UN has declared their trial invalid, as it lacked the minimum standards for a fair trial, and has called on the US Government to release them. Amnesty International has repeatedly criticised the US over its treatment of the five prisoners as being contrary to all human rights.

Today, procedures at all levels of the courts have been completed and the Cubans have been in prison for 14 years. It is now up to President Obama alone to remedy this gross injustice.

(The President cut off the speaker)

 
  
 

  Ana Miranda (Verts/ALE). – (PT) Mr President, preserving biodiversity is one of the European Union objectives that, unfortunately, is not always effectively met. This is the case in Galicia, where 16 000 hectares have burnt this year and where the fires have razed to the ground areas of incalculable economic value, seven of them part of the Natura network. A month ago, this was how the Fragas do Eume national park, one of the main Atlantic forests in Europe, looked. Today, this is the forest’s bleak appearance. We therefore call on the Commission to promote common fire prevention policies and territorial policies to prevent land abandonment, and to ensure that the master plans to protect and conserve these ecosystems are implemented. We want to leave a green country for the future.

 
  
 

  Andrea Zanoni (ALDE). – (IT) Mr President, ladies and gentlemen, I would like to draw your attention to what might soon be happening in one of Italy’s finest forests, the Cansiglio, located between the Veneto and Friuli regions. This is an area of great natural importance, part of the Natura 2000 network. It is here in this forest, once protected by the Most Serene Republic of Venice, that for the first time – after a ban that has lasted for centuries – hundreds of deer will be hunted, under proposals from the provinces of Treviso and Belluno and the Veneto and Friuli regional administrations. Pregnant does, on the point of delivery, will also be brutally killed, as set out in the culling plans.

Under the pretext of overpopulation, disputed by the Worldwide Fund for Nature and many other associations, the Italian authorities are giving the green light to these killings. The EU must more effectively ensure compliance with directives protecting biodiversity, providing appropriate sanctions for any local administrations that violate them.

 
  
 

  Inês Cristina Zuber (GUE/NGL). – (PT) Mr President, the social and economic situation of the Portuguese people is deteriorating badly, and their living conditions are rapidly declining to subhuman and unbearable levels. Meanwhile, the troika, of which the Commission is part, with the servile agreement of the Portuguese Government, believes that Portugal is on track. However, let us see what track that is: does increased debt and economic dependence, a deepening recession with the consequent rise in unemployment – which has already reached 15%, with over 200 000 job losses forecast for 2012 and 2013 – and increased poverty with a quarter of Portuguese people living on the poverty line mean that Portugal is on track? Do you believe, ladies and gentlemen, that the decline in the Portuguese standard of living, with an estimated 7.3% drop in private consumption in 2012, the theft of public sector workers’ and pensioners’ holiday pay and Christmas bonuses, and the freezing and cutting of wages is because Portugal is on track? Do you believe that the closure of multiple public health services with a view to their privatisation, as well as many other public services, and the increased number of Portuguese with nutritional deficiencies, particularly school-age children, is a sign that Portugal is on track?

 
  
 

  Kristiina Ojuland (ALDE). – Mr President, the Kremlin authorities are negotiating the acquisition of a EUR 20 million piece of land in the middle of Paris. It is meant for the construction of a Russian Orthodox Church under the leadership of the Moscow Patriarch Cyril, who is not content with the existing historic Alexander Nevsky Cathedral in Paris that welcomes believers.

The Russian state has endorsed the programme of construction of 200 churches in 10 years, discussing plans for the construction of 500 in future within this state-church fusion project.

I think the separation of the church and state is a core element of any democracy. It is our duty to protect our citizens from confessions to the Kremlin’s agents under the charming Orthodox golden cupolas. We have to decide whether we allow these ambivalent Putin regime establishments to be created in the EU or not.
