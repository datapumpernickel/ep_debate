Question no 19 by Rodi Kratsa-Tsagaropoulou (H-0778/03)
 Subject: Euro-Mediterranean Ministerial Conference in Naples
 

The 6th Euro-Mediterranean Ministerial Conference was held in Naples on 2 and 3 December 2003. Among the subjects discussed were a number of important issues concerning the future and the promotion of the Barcelona process, focusing on the Euro-Mediterranean Parliamentary Assembly, the European Investment Bank and the European Foundation for the Dialogue of Cultures.

To what extent is the Council satisfied with developments and the decisions taken in relation to these three topics? Will it take further practical initiatives during 2004 to promote and strengthen the Euro-Mediterranean partnership, in particular by establishing a free trade area (reciprocal liberalisation of trade in agricultural produce, free trade in services, regional integration)? Does it believe that the signing and implementation of the Geneva Accord can contribute, and in what way, towards regional integration in the Mediterranean basin and how will it use this positive development towards resolving the problem of the Middle East to the benefit of the Euro-Mediterranean partnership?

 
  
 

(IT)

1. The Council believes that the decisions of the Naples Ministerial Conference in relation to the three main topics mentioned by the Honourable Member ie, the Euro-Med Assembly, the FEMIP and the Euro-Mediterranean Foundation represent a crucial step towards strengthening the Barcelona Process. The transformation in early 2004 of the Parliamentary Forum into a Euro-med Assembly will give the process enhanced visibility and transparency, thereby bringing it closer to the interests and the expectations of public opinions in the region. The reinforcement of FEMIP (Euro-Mediterranean Investment and Partnership Facility), as decided by the ECOFIN Council and endorsed at the Naples Conference, will provide further support to the private sector in the region. The setting up of the Euro-Med Foundation will foster the much needed dialogue between cultures. It will do so through a network of national networks co-ordinated by a Director assisted by a limited staff structure.

2. The Council will pursue its efforts to promote and strengthen the Euro-Mediterranean partnership, throughout 2004, by implementing the orientations contained in the Naples conclusions, as well as continuing work with regard to the commitments referred to in the Valencia Action Plan of April 2002. In that respect and with regard to the establishment of a free trade area in the region, the Council will do its utmost to ensure that the negotiations for an Association Agreement with Syria be completed, the Agadir Protocol be signed and ratified and that the Protocols on rules of origin to all association agreements be replaced by the pan-Euro-Mediterranean Protocol.

3. Regarding the reinforcement of the economic and financial dialogue, the Conference agreed that meetings of Ministers of Finance/Economic affairs should be convened in principle, every two years; furthermore, with regard to free trade in agricultural products, the Commission will propose in the first half of 2004, a precise roadmap for building on existing arrangements to achieve a higher level of progressive reciprocal liberalisation of trade in agricultural products, while negotiations aiming at further liberalisation of trade in processed agricultural products will be pursued.

4. Regarding services, the forthcoming Trade Ministerial meeting in 2004 will review the progress achieved in trade liberalisation in this area.

5. The establishment of a Framework Protocol common to all Mediterranean countries will constitute a basis to ensure coherence. Regarding regional integration, strong support will be provided to all initiatives aiming at integration at a sub-regional level on issues such as trade, infrastructure networks, and migration policies.

6. A Mid-term meeting of Ministers of Foreign Affairs will take place in Dublin on 5-6 May and will assess the state of progress of the above.

7. As to the Geneva Accord, the Council is of the view that such initiatives from civil society are welcome contributions to the effort to promote rapprochement, confidence building and the search for lasting peace. The Middle East Peace Process and the Euro-Mediterranean Partnership are complementary.

8. On the occasion of its meeting in Brussels on last 12 December, the European Council welcomed initiatives from civil society on both sides, including the Geneva Peace Initiative, and declared it was ready to further assist in the efforts to promote rapprochement, confidence building and the search for a long lasting peace.

 

Question no 20 by Ioannis Patakis (H-0783/03)
 Subject: Relations between the EU and Cuba
 

At the meeting of the EP Committee on Foreign Affairs of 25 November, the Italian Foreign Minister and President-in-Office of the Council, replying to a question by a Danish MEP as to whether the EU Council had at its recent meeting considered the question of political prisoners in Cuba, stated that this had not been the case since it had just discussed the matter with Colin Powell, who had indicated that the USA was studying ways of resolving the problem which it believed would also satisfy the EU. For this reason the EU was awaiting the solution to be proposed by the USA.

On the basis of what principle of international law is the Council empowered to intervene in Cuban internal affairs? Has the EU entrusted to the USA the question of its relations with Cuba? Has it transferred to the USA some of the responsibility for implementing its foreign policy to the USA and on the basis of what decision?

 
  
 

(IT) The Council is inclined to believe that the Honourable Parliamentarian's question seems to be based on a misunderstanding, since it is obviously confusing the answer given by Minister Frattini on the situation of political prisoners in Cuba and the answer he gave relating to the prisoners the US hold at Guantanamo Bay. As concerns the political prisoners in Cuba, the EU repeats that it has intervened various times with Cuban authorities in order to request their release and, in the meantime, to avoid ill treatment. The EU will continue to do so, monitoring the situation very closely.

Regarding the legal aspects contained in the question, let me remind you that the development and consolidation of democracy, the rule of law and respect for human rights and fundamental freedoms are among the objectives of the Common Foreign and Security Policy of the EU and of the Community Policy in the sphere of Development Cooperation (Article 11, TEU; Article 177, TEC). Moreover, Cuba has signed and ratified a number of UN human rights instruments, including the Convention Against Torture (1995). The EU seeks to promote adherence to international human rights norms and standards in its relations with all third countries.

Finally, as concerns the more political aspects, I would like to underline that the EU establishes its policy with Cuba, as with other countries, obviously under its own responsibility and does not condition it on the approval by others. However, this does not prevent the EU assessing situations and positions together with other interested partners, such as Latin American countries or the USA, in particular in the framework of Political Dialogue meetings.

 

Question no 21 by Konstantinos Alyssandrakis (H-0784/03)
 Subject: Increase in the repression and persecution of demonstrators and trade unionists
 

Greece has seen an alarming increase in the political persecution of demonstrators taking part in demonstrations against the war, military intervention and the anti-worker policy pursued by the EU and the government. In the last few months alone peace activists have been put on trial for taking part in demonstrations against the passage of foreign troops through the country, trade unionists have been sentenced for taking part in demonstrations against plans to curtail pension rights and demonstrators who expressed their opposition to EU policy at the Thessaloniki summit have been held in custody and only released after five months and a lengthy hunger strike without even a date being set for their trial.

Will the Council say whether these unacceptable actions have taken place as part of a crackdown on demonstrations and the so-called anti-terrorist policy, and whether they are dictated by Community and international agreements, such as that with the USA?

 
  
 

(IT) Article 33 of the Treaty on European Union bars the Council from taking any position as far as the maintenance of law and order and the safeguarding of internal security in the Member States is concerned.

This equally applies to the protection of meetings of the European Council; the measures applied by the host State outside the venue of the meeting of a European Council are not within the competence of the Council.

The Council adopted conclusions on the issue of security at meetings of the European Council and other comparable events in July 2003.

The Council also invites the Honourable Parliamentarian to refer to the answer given to the H-0627/03 question raised by Alexandros Alavanos(1), during the last November session and in particular the reference to the draft resolution of the Italian Presidency which is aimed at installing cooperation on law and order and security on the occasion of EU Summits.

The Council is not informed of the facts related in the question and did not express views on them.

 
 

(1) Oral answer of 19.11.2003.

 Subject: Basic human rights in the Baltic countries
 

In his reply to a question I had tabled, the President of the Commission stated that he fully endorsed the statement by Commissioner Verheugen in support of the unacceptable approach to Russian speakers taken by the Latvian and Estonian governments and considered that under no circumstances had the prohibition of a Communist Party in a candidate country given rise to particular problems or censure in respect of political criteria.

Question no 37 by Michl Ebner (H-0735/03)
 Subject: Support meaasures for vehicles powered by alternative fuels
 

The issue of hydrogen-driven vehicles has already been raised in my Written Question E-1697/01(1). However, nearly every vehicle with a petrol engine can be retrofitted to run on natural gas, also known as methane (CNG), or on liquefied petroleum gas (LPG), a mixture of propane and butane. Public awareness of this environmentally sound alternative to liquid fuels such as petrol or diesel is still too low, and many believe there are still too many economic and technical limitations. The cost of retrofitting a car with an LPG system is between EUR 1 500 and EUR 2 600, depending on factors such as engine type, engine performance and number of cylinders. Some countries, such as Italy, offer financial aid to make this environmentally friendly alternative more attractive to the public. However most Member States offer either insufficient aid or none at all.

Does the European Union provide additional support for this purpose? Would it be possible to establish a single funding programme? What steps are being taken in general to promote these alternative fuels?

 
  
 

(EN) The Commission agrees that alternative fuels, including natural gas (CNG) or liquefied petroleum gas (LPG) have several attractive features. These fuels are addressed in the November 2001 Commission communication on alternative fuels. The Commission is currently studying the potential of these fuels to provide an alternative to petrol and diesel, with the primary aims of reducing CO2 emissions and improving security of supply in the road sector.

Part of this work is being carried out with a stakeholder group. Their preliminary results indicate low awareness of these fuels’ benefits and that CNG seems to be fully competitive if the vehicles and fuelling equipment are produced at a comparable scale and are used to a comparable degree as with petrol and diesel. Part of the reason for their modest success is that they suffer from the higher cost of any niche product unless introduced on a large scale. Higher levels of market penetration are only likely if incentives are granted as permitted in the directive on taxation of energy products. The group will report before the end of the year and after examining the report the Commission will make the necessary proposals to continue the developpment of its strategy.

In addition, the transport element of the new Intelligent Energy – Europe programme, STEER, contains a Key Action which covers both CNG and LPG. Proposals under this area could cover issues such as fuel standards, norms and promotional schemes. This Key Action will be opened in the second call in 2004.

Finally, the Community research and technological development (RTD) programme supports research and demonstration on alternative fuels. In the 5th framework programme some € 50 million are spent on the first CIVITAS initiative involving 19 cities, around a third of whom are operating CNG/LPG vehicles, as well as over € 10 million RTD projects related to CNG production, development and safe use. Both fuels are also eligible within the second CIVITAS call for proposals with a similar budget which closes in December 2003. Alternative fuel activities featured in the first call of the 6th framework programme included an integrated project to produce hydrogen-rich gases from biomass which is still under negotiation. A later call will address demonstration activities with a higher priority.

 
 

(1) OJ C 40 E, 14.2.2002, p. 80.

Question no 39 by Astrid Thors (H-0741/03)
 Subject: Transport of radioactive cargo in the Baltic Sea
 

The Russian Prime Minister, Mr Kasyanov, has signed an order decreeing which of the country's ports may be visited by vessels carrying radioactive cargo. The list includes Kaliningrad, St Petersburg, Ust-Luga and Vysotsk.

Did the Russian authorities inform the Commission of the decision before it was taken? Is there any information to indicate a link with imports of radioactive waste for reprocessing in Russia?

What measures has the Commission taken to ensure that the planned shipments of radioactive cargoes in the Baltic Sea and the Gulf of Finland do not endanger the environment or human beings, e.g. in the context of HELCOM? In which areas of the Baltic Sea will SRS (Ship Reporting Systems) operate, in addition to GOFREP (the Gulf of Finland Ship Reporting System)?

 
  
 

(FR) The Commission has received no information that enables it to establish a link between the Russian order specifying the ports in the Baltic Sea and the Gulf of Finland in which ships carrying radioactive cargos could potentially dock and the decision authorising the import into Russia of irradiated fuel or radioactive waste.

Shipments of radioactive waste between Member States and non-Member States are governed by Council Directive 92/3/Euratom of 3 February 1992, which specifies that shipment operations must conform to Community and national provisions as well as to international agreements concerning the transport of radioactive material. Furthermore, any obligations associated with safety control (Safeguards) must be respected.

As regards the route across the Baltic sea and the Gulf of Finland that ships carrying these cargos would take, neither Member States nor transporters of radioactive material are bound by Community legislation to give prior notice to the Commission regarding the schedule and routes of shipments of nuclear matter or other radioactive material.

In accordance with Article 18 of the above-mentioned directive, however, Member States must submit regular reports to the Commission on the situation with regard to shipments of radioactive waste.

Ships carrying radioactive cargos participate in the ship reporting system in the Baltic Sea in accordance with locally applicable rules, which generally stipulate that the competent coastal authorities should be informed of the nature of the hazardous or pollutant cargo being shipped, including radioactive material.

It should be noted that the implementation, from 5 February 2004, of Directive 2002/59/EC establishing a Community vessel traffic monitoring and information system, will permit closer monitoring of ships and more effective transmission between Member States of information concerning hazardous cargos. In this context, the Commission will support in principle the initiatives of Member States of the International Maritime Organisation, aiming to bolster or establish traffic organisation and ship reporting systems in European waters.

Question no 41 by Jan Dhaene (H-0767/03)
 Subject: Use of pure vegetable oil as fuel for ships
 

Shipping disasters confront us with the dangers of oil pollution at sea. Without wishing to minimise the consequences of such disasters, I should like to point out that there is also a serious problem due to diffuse pollution of freshwater systems with polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons (PACs) from inland shipping and pleasure craft. This is caused, inter alia, by leaks, spillage and precipitation of combustion gases. Directive 2003/30/EC(1) of the EP and of the Council of 8 May 2003 has the aim of promoting the use of biofuels in transport. If the first problem mentioned here is viewed in conjunction with the EU's biofuel objectives, some interesting opportunities come to mind. An increase in the use of pure vegetable oil in inland shipping and by pleasure craft could reduce diffuse pollution of freshwater systems with PACs.

Can the Commission encourage the use of pure vegetable oil specifically by ships? If so, have any steps already been taken in this regard?

Can the Commission initiate a research project to study ways of converting ordinary diesel engines on ships simply and cheaply into engines which can run on pure vegetable oil? If so, have any steps already been taken in this regard?

 
  
 

(EN) Biofuels, blended or pure, carry a number of environmental advantages compared to conventional transport fuels. One of these, as noted in the Honourable Member’s question, is that they cause less damage to freshwater plants, fish and animals when released into the water.

Through the biofuels directive(2), the Union has adopted the indicative target of increasing the share of biofuels share in transport fuels from less than 1% today to 5.75% in 2010. The directive applies to diesel used in inland waterway and rail transport, as well as to road transport. It is for Member States, suppliers and users to make the decisions which will determine the contribution each of these sectors will make to achieving the objective.

Projects to develop the use of biofuels in waterway transport are eligible for support under the Community’s Framework Programme for Research and Technological Development. However, no project proposals have been received to date.

Concerning the conversion of small diesel engines for the use of biofuels, it should be noted that commercial products have been available for a number of years, including also dual-fuel solutions.

 
 

(1) OJ L 123, 17.5.2003, p. 42.
(2) directive 2003/30 on the promotion of the use of biofuels or other renewable fuels for transport

On 4 November 2003, the ‘Unión de Agricultores y Ganaderos del País Valenciano’ (COAG), ‘Ecologistas en Acción Murcia’, ‘Ecologistas en Acción País Valenciano’, ‘Amigos de los Humedales del Sur de Alicante’ (AHSA), ‘Grupo ecologista Caralluma (Murcia)’, and ‘Comisión Pro-Río de Orihuela (Alicante)’ sent a letter to Commissioners Wallström and Fischler objecting to the seriously inefficient water management policies applied in Murcia and Valencia, which have generated expectations for new transferred water resources from the River Ebro.

How is the Commission going to take account of these aspects in its assessment of the Spanish National Hydrological Plan?

 
  
 

(EN) On 16th and 17th October 2003, the Commission held a technical meeting to discuss various aspects of the flow of water in the lower river Ebro. The Chairman’s report of that meeting has been sent to Parliament’s Environment Committee and Petitions Committee. The Commission’s task is to ensure that the proposed Ebro transfer is compatible with relevant Union legislation and policies. To the extent that the issues raised in the letter of 4th November 2003 are relevant to making this assessment, they will of course be taken into account.

Question no 57 by Mario Mantovani (H-0763/03)
 Subject: Health services and freedom of movement for patients
 

The judgment of the Court of Justice in the Müller-Fauré/Van Riet case (C-0385/99) made a clear distinction between out-patient and in-patient hospital treatment. In the report on the future of health care for which the questioner was rapporteur (12/2002 - A5-0452/2002) the European Parliament asserted that the achievement of the objectives of sustainability, accessibility and quality of health services requires, inter alia, the creation of an internal market in health services and products, which must guarantee high quality health care accessible to, and affordable for, all, taking into account the viability and capacity of the Member States’ systems, and guaranteeing free movement of citizens and access to services in all the countries of the Union, whilst respecting the principle of subsidiarity.

In particular, how can this be reconciled with the status of European citizens, whose right to healthcare is, as a result of that judgment, fully guaranteed only in the case of out-patient care and not for in-patient care, on the basis of purely economic considerations? How is this two-tier system of free choice in the case of out-patient care and compulsory prior authorisation for in-patient care to be reconciled with guaranteeing high-quality health protection as sanctioned in the Treaties?

 
  
 

(FR) As the honourable Member points out, in the Müller-Fauré/van Riet(1) judgment, the Court ruled on the issue of authorisation of reimbursement in the case of care provided in another Member State. This judgment comes after a series of other judgments given by the Court on this issue since 1998(2). It should be pointed out that the Court has again ruled on this issue in the Inizan(3) case. The questions put to the Commission must be considered in light of all this case-law.

In these judgments, the Court recognised the right of patients to receive reimbursement in the case of medical care received in another Member State. However, the Court made this right subject to conditions intended, in particular, to guarantee the financial stability of social security and to ensure a high level of health protection. These considerations are at the heart of the distinction made by the Court between in-patient care and out-patient care.

As regards out-patient care, the Court recognised patients’ freedom of choice, pointing out that the requirement of prior authorisation for reimbursement was not justified and should be removed. It confirmed that, in order to avoid endangering the financial stability of the social security of the Member State of affiliation, this care should be paid for within the limits of the health insurance cover patients receive in their Member State of affiliation. Thus, the Member State of affiliation is only bound to reimburse care provided in another Member State insofar as this care is paid for by its social security system when provided on its own territory. This care is reimbursed in accordance with the tariffs applicable in the Member State of affiliation and the conditions to which the Member States subject the provision of out-patient care on their territory, such as the requirement of prior consultation by a general practitioner before consulting a specialist, remain applicable in the case of care provided in another Member State.

As regards in-patient care, the Court admitted that the requirement of authorisation was justified by the planning needs of Member States in order to allow them to guarantee sufficient and permanent access to a balanced range of quality in-patient care on their territory and to avoid, as far as possible, any waste of technical and human resources. Thus, upholding the authorisation requirement in this case serves the interests of patients who can receive adequate treatment in their own Member State. Furthermore, the Court pointed out that if this was not the case, for example in cases where there are waiting lists on national territory to receive the care required by the patient’s state of health, which cannot be provided in due time, refusal of authorisation for treatment in another Member State would not be justified. The Court therefore put protection of the patient’s health first over purely economic considerations which, according to consistent case-law, could also constitute a barrier to the fundamental principle of the free provision of services.

Thus, citizens can now obtain financial cover for in-patient care required by their state of health in cases where the care is provided in another Member State. The possibility of going abroad to be treated is therefore no longer reserved only for patients with financial means that allow them to bear these medical costs without the help of social security.

It should be noted that Council Regulation (EEC) 1408/71 of 14 June 1971, on the coordination of national social security systems(4) already lays down within the framework of Article 22, conditions for the reimbursement of care received in another Member State. Since this is care a person plans to receive in another Member State, the Regulation lays down cases in which Member States may not refuse prior authorisation for financial reimbursement of care received abroad. Consequently, this leaves all options for Member States to determine other circumstances in which authorisation may be granted. Furthermore, a political agreement was found at the Council, on 1 December 2003, on the modernisation and simplification of this Regulation. Within this framework, amendments made to the chapter on health insurance supplement the medical criterion used by the Court in its case-law in favour of patients.
