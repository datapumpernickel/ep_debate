QUESTIONS TO COUNCIL
Question no 10 by Claude Moraes (H-0387/05)
 Subject: Promotion of fundamental rights and minority rights
 

Following the Luxembourg Presidency's positive statement on promoting fundamental rights in the EU (European Parliament public hearing promoting EU Fundamental Rights Policy, 25th to 26th April 2005), what lessons has the Luxembourg Presidency learned about the promotion of fundamental rights and minority rights?

What is the Presidency's view at this stage of the establishment of a new Fundamental Rights Agency and its future role?

 
  
 

(FR)The European Union is a community of values founded on the principles of liberty, democracy, respect for human rights and fundamental freedoms, and the rule of law. Like every Council Presidency, the Luxembourg Presidency has endeavoured to respect and promote European Union policy on and the positions that the Union has taken in the field of human rights.

The Council has also reaffirmed on many occasions that it attaches priority to the establishment of an area of freedom, security and justice.

To that end, five years after Tampere, the European Council has adopted a new multiannual programme for the Union, to be known as the Hague Programme, the objectives of which include the improvement of the common capability of the Union and its Member States to attain the Union's objectives in that area.

On 3 June 2005, the Council adopted a Council and Commission Action Plan which includes the specific measures that will be referred to the European legislative body with a view to the implementation of the Hague Programme.

In this connection, the Heads of State or Government of the Member States of the European Union, meeting in Council in December 2003, advocated the conversion of the European Monitoring Centre on Racism and Xenophobia into the European Union Fundamental Rights Agency to which the honourable Member refers.

The Commission has said that it will draft a proposal for a regulation on this issue. The Council will give full and in-depth consideration to such a proposal.

Finally, the attention of the honourable Member is drawn to the fact that, on 27 April 2005, the Committee of Permanent Representatives (Part 2) approved the establishment of an ad hoc working party with a remit to consider the proposal for a decision establishing, for the period 2007-2013, a specific programme on fundamental rights and citizenship (as part of the general fundamental rights and justice programme), which the Commission submitted to it on 26 April 2005.

 

Question no 11 by Eoin Ryan (H-0391/05)
 Subject: Forced labour: 'a social evil which has no place in the modern world'
 

According to the most recent report from the International Labour Organization (ILO), at least 12.3 million people throughout the world are subjected to forced labour. Of that total, some 9.5 million are in Asia. Furthermore, according to the same report, more than 2 million forced labourers, many of them children, are victims of human trafficking.

Does the Council Presidency agree with the findings of this report? What is being done by the European Union to contribute to eradicate this 'social evil which has no place in the modern world'?

 
  
 

(EN)The Council is aware that an alarming number of people is still subject to forced labour, a phenomenon that indeed should have no place in this world.

In order to raise awareness and combat this and related phenomena, the EU regularly raises these issues in its relations with third countries, both at a bilateral and at a multilateral level, as well as with international governmental and non-governmental organisations.

For instance, as part of its efforts to promote effective multilateralism in the world, the EU puts a lot of effort in promoting ratification and implementation of the key international human rights instruments, many of which include provisions on labour standards and the protection of children.

These efforts include actions undertaken in the field of trade and development (for instance, the Generalised System of Trade Preferences (GSP), the international commodity agreements where the EC and its Member States are parties and most Partnership Agreements with third countries include human rights clauses) and justice and home affairs (combating crime and trafficking in human beings).

As an example, the preferences provided for by the GSP may at any time be temporarily withdrawn, in whole or in part, in cases of practice of any form of slavery or forced labour as defined in the Geneva Conventions of 25 September 1926 and 7 September 1956 and International Labour Organisation Conventions No 29 and No 105. In application of this provision, the benefit of the tariff preferences applicable to Myanmar has been withdrawn since 1997, following a report by the Commission on forced labour in that country.

 

Question no 12 by Brian Crowley (H-0393/05)
 Subject: Romania's orphans
 

In connection with the ongoing accession negotiations with Romania, will the Council indicate what discussions, if any, have taken place at political or official level with the Romanian authorities concerning the level and quality of care for children in Romanian orphanages? Furthermore, does the Council believe that much more needs to be done to improve the lot of these Romanian children, including measures to facilitate their adoption?

 
  
 

(FR)As has already been pointed out in answers to similar questions, it is important to draw attention to the importance which the Union, and more particularly the Council, has always attached to the issue of child protection in Romania.

In its 2004 report on the progress made by Romania on the road to accession, the Commission noted that regular progress had been observed as regards the reorganisation of child protection, in particular through the closure of the large, old-fashioned institutions and the establishment of alternative facilities. The Union, for its part, has provided financial support for the efforts made by Romania in improving the quality of state-run child welfare facilities.

It should be noted that the new legislation on children's rights and adoption, which entered into force on 1 January 2005, complies for the most part with the standards set by the United Nations Convention on the Rights of the Child and the practice followed by EU Member States.

Although there is still some progress to be made in developing administrative capacities with a view to the proper and effective implementation of these new provisions, all the evidence shows that conditions have on the whole significantly improved and that the Romanian authorities have undertaken to continue their efforts to fully implement the reform of the child protection system.

The Council can assure the honourable Member that the Union will continue to closely monitor Romania's efforts and the outcome thereof.

 

Question no 13 by Seán Ó Neachtain (H-0395/05)
 Subject: Millennium Development goals - reduction of child mortality
 

Bearing in mind that the Millennium goals hope to reduce the mortality rate of children under the age of five by 2015, can the Council state what exactly are its political and financial commitments towards promoting a reduction in child mortality in accordance with the Millennium Development goals?

 
  
 

(FR)The Council is well aware of the alarming situation facing millions of children in developing countries, and in Africa in particular, owing to their vulnerability vis-à-vis famine, violence, conflicts and disease. The Council would point out that children are directly or indirectly mentioned in the 8 Millennium Development Goals (MDG) whose implementation will be the subject of an initial report on the occasion of the United Nations Summit in September 2005.

The Council believes that it will not be possible to achieve these goals by 2015 unless a coherent policy is adopted with regard to children in the developing world.

Specific attention needs to be given to children in the implementation of the European Union's development policy. The Council has taken a series of recent initiatives to this end:

1. The Council and the representatives of the Member States meeting within the Council approved, in their conclusions of 24 May 2005, a set of measures aimed at speeding up the progress towards implementation of the MDG.

The Council welcomed the fact that the Member States are well on the way to achieving the 0.39% of official development assistance objective contained in the Barcelona commitments by 2006.

The Council also took a crucial decision regarding a collective and individual increase in the volume of official development assistance by 2010, as a further step towards collectively contributing 0.7% of Community gross domestic income by 2015.

To this end, the Council decided that the European Union would collectively contribute 0.56% of its gross domestic income to official development assistance by 2010. In concrete terms, this is equivalent to a EUR 20 billion increase, which the EU will effect each year from 2010 in order to support the world's poorest countries. In making this collective commitment, the agreement has taken account of the specific situation of those Member States which joined the Union in May 2004.

In addition, the Council decided that the Member States would undertake to reach the 0.7% figure by 2015 and those which have already reached this figure have undertaken to remain above this rate. Those Member States which joined the EU after 2002 will endeavour to reach 0.33% by 2015. This is the first time that a clear timeframe has been set for achieving the 0.7% collective threshold.

2. At the same meeting of the General Affairs and External Relations Council, the Council expressed its firm support for the European Action Programme to combat HIV/AIDS, malaria and tuberculoses through external actions, submitted by the Commission at the end of April 2005. This programme provides for special attention to be devoted to children's rights and the needs of orphans and vulnerable children, as well as safety at school, especially for girls. A joint action by the EC and the Member States, in cooperation with the main international organisations in this area (UNICEF and UNFPA), together with teachers and parents' associations in the partner countries, is also planned.

3. The process of revising the joint declaration of November 2000 on the European Union's development policy is in progress. A Commission communication on future EU development policy is expected for the end of June. Many subjects were identified during the extended consultation process as possible thematic priorities, including child protection in particular.

Child protection is a vital aspect of EU development policy and the Council intends to ensure that this priority is adequately reflected both in the context of preparations for the September 2005 summit in New York on the implementation of the MDG and in the process of formulating future EC development policy.

 

Question no 14 by Liam Aylward (H-0397/05)
 Subject: A 'Weather Insurance' scheme for Ethiopia
 

The World Food Programme (WFP) says that it is planning to launch a weather insurance scheme in Ethiopia early next year which will receive technical support from the World Bank and which will address the recurrent problem of famine and malnutrition in Ethiopia by basing insurance contracts on a rainfall index.

According to the WFP, if rainfall measurements fall to a level that suggests severe drought, insurance companies will pay out. Premiums will be met by aid donors.

Would the Council comment on this initiative and indicate if it has had any direct or indirect involvement in the proposed scheme?

 
  
 

(FR)The Council is aware of the work in progress at the World Bank to prepare a Global Index Insurance Facility (GIIF). A recent meeting of the International Task Force on management of commodity risks, held in Interlaken, Switzerland, in May 2005, considered this proposal from the World Bank, on which we are expecting a concept report. According to the information available, index-based (for example, rainfall index) insurance for weather-related risks will be covered by the Facility proposed by the Bank. This Facility will be aimed at linking the demand in developing countries for insurance against risks relating inter alia to natural disasters with the world insurance market.

In the proposals concerning the use of the conditional billion from the 9th European Development Fund (EDF), the Commission proposed that the Council set aside an indicative amount of up to EUR 25 million to help finance the management of commodity risks.

This initiative followed on from the Council's approval (Doc. 8972/04) in April 2004 of an EU action plan proposed by the Commission (Doc. 6454/05) on commodities, which includes a proposal to improve producer access to commodity risk insurance and trade finance.

This funding could contribute to the establishment of the Facility proposed by the World Bank and the participation of other donors to supplement the amount put forward by the Bank, which is EUR 100 million. Its aim would be to help ACP countries to gain access to available market instruments for the management of commodity risks through temporary co-funding of their premiums. This would help to reduce the vulnerability of beneficiary ACP countries to fluctuations in commodity prices and natural disasters and to set up and eventually adopt instruments to manage commodity risks. A joint EU-ACP committee on agricultural commodities would supervise arrangements for the establishment and implementation of the programme.

As with all decisions regarding the use of EDF resources, such a contribution will require a decision from the ACP-EC Council, whose next session is to be held in Luxembourg on 24 and 25 June 2005. The EU Council is continuing its work to reach a position on all the Commission's proposals regarding the conditional billion with a view to this joint ministerial meeting. The ACP side is also due to establish its position at a ministerial meeting due to take place in mid-June.

 

Question no 16 by Vittorio Agnoletto (H-0401/05)
 Subject: European Court of Human Rights and the Öcalan case (Turkey)
 

On 12 May 2005 the European Court of Human Rights delivered its judgment on the conditions of arrest and forced transfer of former PKK leader Abdullah Öcalan, the conduct of his trial and ill-treatment inflicted on him (application 46221/99 to the ECHR). The Court held that Öcalan had not had a fair and impartial trial in Turkey, ruling that various articles of the European Convention on Human Rights had been violated and suggesting that Turkey hold a new trial. It also established that there had been serious violations of Öcalan's right of defence, with further substantial violations of the European Convention in this case.

What overall conclusions does the Council draw from the ruling of 12 May 2005 of the European Court of Human Rights? Does the Council agree, in particular, that Öcalan must be given a new trial before 3 October 2005, and does it not consider that the holding of this second trial is of essential importance if Turkey is to show that it adheres to the principles underpinning the rule of law?

 
  
 

(FR)The Council has been informed of the ruling of the European Court of Human Rights (ECHR) referred to by the honourable Member. It is not for the Council to express an opinion on the substance of this ruling. Obviously, Turkey is, however, expected to respect the Court’s ruling and to comply with it. The Constitutional Review of May 2004 established the principle of the primacy of international and European conventions in the field of human rights and therefore confirmed the capacity of the Turkish judicial system to give direct effect to the rulings of the ECHR. The need fully and quickly to implement the decisions of the European Court of Human Rights was, moreover, recently pointed out again by the EU in the position it adopted for the EU-Turkey Association Council of 26 April 2005.

Furthermore, as a candidate country for accession to the EU, Turkey is also expected generally to pursue the reform process and to implement it effectively in all its aspects, particularly with regard to fundamental freedoms and full respect for human rights. The European Council of 16 and 17 December 2004 clearly indicated that the full and effective implementation of the reform process will continue to be monitored closely by the European Union.

The Council can assure the honourable Member that it will carefully monitor the ways in which this issue develops.

 

Question no 17 by Ivo Belet (H-0404/05)
 Subject: Implementation of the Council Resolution of 26 May 2003 on the horizontal aspects of culture
 

In its Resolution of 26 May 2003 on the horizontal aspects of culture, the Council invites the Member States and the Commission to enhance synergies with Community areas and activities other than cultural ones from the earliest possible stage of preparation of any action or policy to its implementation, through an assessment of these actions at all appropriate levels, and to examine the methods through which the exchange of good practices in relation to the economic and social dimension of culture could be developed. The Council agrees to take stock of the follow-up to this Resolution by the end of 2004.

What initiatives has the Council undertaken in order to take stock of the follow-up? What were the findings/conclusions of this stocktaking?

 
  
 

(FR)The Resolution to which the honourable Member refers, which formed part of the 2002-2004 work plan on culture, was intended to enhance synergies with areas and activities other than cultural ones, as laid down, moreover, by the Treaty. The Treaty establishing the European Community states in Article 151(4), that ‘the Community shall take cultural aspects into account in its action under other provisions of this Treaty, in particular in order to respect and promote the diversity of its cultures.’

During the study into the implementation of the 2002-2004 work plan and the implementation of this resolution, the Council felt that it was right to focus on certain areas in order to achieve tangible results in areas outside the confines of the purely cultural sphere.

The Council accordingly decided to base the new work plan for culture 2005-2006, adopted in November 2004, on five major areas, mostly concerning the economic and social dimensions of culture, which are as follows: to look into the contribution of creativity and Europe’s cultural industries to achieving growth and cohesion in Europe; to introduce coordination in the digitisation of European works of art, so as to allow them to be disseminated and to ensure that they can play a part in establishing a knowledge-based economy; to foster the mobility of works of art, becoming involved in the area of insurance, for example; and, lastly, to remove obstacles to artists’ mobility and, more specifically, obstacles caused by taxation of mobile artists.

The Council believes that implementing this work plan will make it possible to achieve results in those areas in which joint action with other sectors is required.

Recently North Korea said that it had taken steps to increase its nuclear arsenal. According to the Commission in a release from March this year, the EU should not 'overreact' to statements from the capital Pyongyang. Question no 21 by Philip Claeys (H-0416/05)
 Subject: Presidency declaration rejecting the systematic forwarding of information on criminal records
 

The Luxembourg Presidency has declared that the systematic forwarding of information between the Member States on criminal judgments would be too time-consuming and too expensive. According to the Presidency, it would lead to national criminal records offices becoming overloaded and result in more bureaucracy, higher costs and delays.

Nevertheless, in the past, both the Council and Parliament expressed their support for the sharing of information on criminal records.

Why is the Luxembourg Presidency now abandoning the idea? Is it doing so on the basis of the findings of a feasibility study which also proposed solutions to the problems referred to above?

 
  
 

(FR)It is important to emphasise that establishing an area of freedom, justice and security requires information on convictions and disqualifications to be properly shared among the Member States’ judicial authorities.

The obligation to provide information on convictions already exists on the basis of Articles 13 and 22 of the 1959 Convention on Mutual Assistance in Criminal Matters, supplemented by Article 4 of the Additional Protocol to this Convention, dated 17 March 1978.

It is also laid down in the draft Council Decision on the Exchange of information extracted from the criminal record, which is in the process of being adopted.

It should also be noted that, in adopting the Hague Programme, the European Council of 4-5 November 2004 prioritised the issue of the exchange of information on convictions between Member States and called on the Commission to draw up fresh proposals.

Consequently, on 25 January 2005, the Commission tabled the ‘White Paper on exchanges of information on convictions and the effect of such convictions in the European Union’. On the basis of that document, meetings were held to look into the feasibility of setting up an effective and complete system of exchange of information between national criminal records. Whilst the results of the feasibility studies demonstrated that setting up the exchange system is a complex process and that it places extra burdens on the national authorities in the Member States, such a system is an indispensable means of guaranteeing security, justice and effective cooperation between the national judicial authorities in the European Union.

The exchange of information and the interconnection between national criminal records were the subject of the deliberations of the Justice and Home Affairs Council in April.
