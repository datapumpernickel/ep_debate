Questions to the Commission
Question no 16 by Richard Howitt (H-0778/00)
 Subject: Progress on reforming the management of External Assistance: The Quality Support Group
 

Why was the establishment of an interdepartmental Quality Support Group for External Assistance programmes, as set out in the Commission Communication of 16 May 2000, not included in the most recent Progress Report on the Reform Actions dated 10 July 2000? Will the Commission now comment on the progress made in the establishment of an interdepartmental QSG and outline its exact membership and terms of reference? What criteria have been identified to assess ‘quality’ in programming? How will results be disseminated? Will the Commission clarify these details in a written statement to Parliament? Does the lack of priority given to implementing this proposal mean that the Commission is not keeping its promise given by Commissioner Neilson, that development aspects would be given priority in all its trade and external assistance activities?

 
  
 

The Communication of 16 May 2000 on the reform of the Community's External Assistance identified the establishment of an Interdepartmental Quality Support Group (QSGI) as one of the main actions to improve the consistency and quality of programming.

The mandate of the QSGI was adopted by the Commission in July 2000 and the personal representatives of the Commissioners have now been nominated. The first meeting of the QSGI is taking place this week.

The establishment of the QSGI was one of the top priorities in the implementation of the reform of external assistance. There has been a short delay compared to the initial calendar to make sure that the best people were appointed as the personal representatives of the Commissioners. In no way does this short delay, however, question the Commission's commitment to development policy priorities.

One of the very first tasks of the QSGI will be to define the substance and methodology for an enhanced system of multiannual aid programming by country and by region. This methodology will, inter alia, assess whether the analysis of six common elements contained in the Framework for Country Strategy Papers has been carried out in a thorough way and is relevant to and consistent with the proposed strategy. The Framework should be adopted at one of the next General Affairs Councils (GAC) or Development Councils, after fruitful coordination between the Commission and the Member States. Furthermore, the QSGI will examine the usual four criteria of pertinence, feasbility, viability and impact for programming documents.

 

Question no 17 by Nuala Ahern (H-0785/00)
 Subject: Construction of further nuclear power plants in the Ukraine
 

In the light of the extraordinary decision taken by the Commission at its meeting of 6 September 2000 to support the construction of further nuclear power plants in the Ukraine using the support of EU taxpayers' money, will the Commission explain why non-nuclear options, such as modern electricity systems, planning and energy efficient technologies for delivering better energy security to the Ukraine, were not given support and why Euratom's expertise is being diverted to the development of new nuclear plants outside the EU when it has failed to ensure proper safety and environmental protection measures at the Sellafield and La Hague nuclear reprocessing plants inside the EU?

 
  
 

The December 1995 "Memorandum of Understanding on the closure of Chernobyl by 2000" between the G7 and the Ukraine includes a number of commitments for both sides. In particular, these require the Ukraine definitively to close the Chernobyl plant in 2000. The Commission welcomes the recent confirmation that this will happen by 15 December this year. In the discussions with the Ukrainian authorities the Commission will continue to press for improved levels of nuclear safety throughout the country and for reform of the energy sector more generally. In this context there is also included in the Memorandum a point related to the energy investment programme. This programme is intended to identify least-cost power supply investments to meet Ukraine's future national power requirements. Besides the completion of the Khmeltnisky 2 and Rovno 4 nuclear plants other non-nuclear projects are also included, like thermal and hydro plants rehabilitation and pumped storage projects.

Licensing of nuclear installations within Member States is the responsibility of national safety authorities. Under Chapter 3 of the Euratom Treaty, the Community is responsible for ensuring the protection of the health of the general public as well as of workers in the nuclear industry. In this context, the Commission has issued several Directives and Regulations, in addition to the specific provisions on environmental protection laid down in the Treaty itself. These competences do not directly relate to nuclear safety of the installations, but do address the possible consequences of an emergency, including on the territory of other Member States.

 

Question no 18 by Jean-Louis Bernié (H-0711/00)
 Subject: Guersac-Pontchâteau very high voltage power line project
 

A 12 km section of the planned Guersac-Pontchâteau very high voltage (225 000 volts) power line will cross the Brière Regional Park, an area of special plant and animal interest (French acronym: ZNIEFF), a bird conservation area (ZICO) and land which is being considered for classification as a special conservation area (ZSC).

This project will disfigure the Brivet valley and will inevitably have an adverse impact on its fauna and flora. As the body responsible for safeguarding the importance of the Brière Regional Park in terms of its environment, landscape and as a tourist centre, and without calling into question the advisability and economic value of the project, the Pays de la Loire Regional Council has urged that the power line should be built using innovative, alternative technological procedures which reflect the concerns of the various authorities involved in running the park, with a view to making an effective contribution to its sustainable development.

Does the Commission take the view that the EDF project, the value of which has not been demonstrated, given that no independent energy study of the Basse-Loire region has yet been carried out, is compatible with the conservation aims being pursued in this wetland area?

 
  
 

The matter of the planned Guersac-Ponchâteau very high voltage line, likely to have an adverse impact on an area of special plant and animal interest (ZNIEFF), a bird conservation area (ZICO) and land which is being considered for classification as a special conservation area (ZSC) has been referred to the Commission.

It must be made clear, first of all, that the ZNIEFF and ZICO areas are scientifically classified areas which are not covered by Community nature conservation legislation.

If the project were likely to have an adverse impact on a Site of Community Importance (SCI) proposed by the French authorities, then these authorities would have to comply with the provisions of Article 6 of the ‘habitats’ directive.

Given the scant information available to the Commission, it is not possible to make a conclusive statement as to whether this project complies with Community nature conservation legislation.

The Commission has opened a case detected by the Commission itself in order to investigate the legal case. The honourable Member is therefore encouraged to provide the Commission with any further information on the project in question.

What action has the Commission taken in relation to the arrest of journalists from CNN/Channel 4 on charges of alleged espionage in Liberia?

 
  
 

Four foreign journalists were arrested in Liberia on 18 August 2000. The four men – two British, a South African and a Sierra Leonean – had been in Liberia for some three weeks working on a documentary for London-based Channel 4. (The Commission is not aware of any journalists working for CNN having been arrested.)

 Subject: Financial support for Agenda 21
 

Given that the main objectives of the Fifth Environment Action Programme and the Union's environment policy have consistently included realising integrated projects and programmes with the participation of the public, can the Commission state what forms of financial support are proposed for the realisation or dissemination of Agenda 21, which is one of the few programmes in which agreement and consensus are actually inherent to the working method?

- The Structural Funds and the Cohesion Fund promote a more balanced socio-economic development in the Community. The Funds are increasingly drawn on for environmental improvement projects, to clean up polluted areas, to promote environmentally friendly agriculture, and to provide support for conservation, public transport and environmental management training. Recent reforms mean that environmental concerns must now be addressed systematically in all programming and evaluations.

- LIFE is the financial instrument supporting Community environmental policy. It co-finances actions both in nature conservation and in other environmental fields, both within the Community and outside it. For additional information, please refer to

- The Commission also has other mechanisms to finance sustainability within the Community, including information and awareness. For further information, please see

In addition, the Commission contributes a substantial amount to NGO activities in line with the objectives set out in Agenda 21, also with regard to public awareness raising.

At the global level, the Community is a major aid donor: together, the Community and its Member States provide more than half of all development aid. Environment and, more generally, sustainable development concerns, have become a priority in development cooperation policy. Sustainable development provisions have been built into programmes financed under the Lomé and the new Cotonou Conventions, cooperation with Asian and Latin American States (ALA), the Mediterranean, and the Phare and Tacis programmes. In addition, there are specific budget lines for NGO cooperation and for supporting environment and tropical forest projects in developing countries.

 

Question no 21 by Sebastiano (Nello) Musumeci (H-0732/00)
 Subject: Mutual compatibility of small-scale and industrial fishing in the Mediterranean
 

In the course of a visit to Catania arranged by the FAO, Mr Segre Colet, lecturer in anthropology at Hamburg university and Mr François Feral, lecturer in public law at Montpellier university, replying to a journalist from the Sicilia newspaper (on 28 August 2000) on the subject of how, and to what extent, industrial fishing had affected, and continued to affect, the fauna of the Mediterranean, stated that `industrial fishing should completely disappear from the Mediterranean´. The two FAO experts said that industrial fishing vessels did not comply with the periods laid down for fishing and were exploiting maritime resources in such a way as to drive certain species of fish to extinction. Moreover, by failing to adjust to the Mediterranean ecosystem, industrial fishing had interfered with species' reproductive cycles.

Does the Commission endorse the opinion of the two experts in question? If so, what action is it taking – or does it intend to take – to protect the fauna of the Mediterranean? Does it consider that small-scale and industrial fishing are compatible in the Mediterranean?

 
  
 

There are two concepts, sometimes confused, associated with the phrase “industrial fishing”.

Fisheries which exploit various species for conversion to fish meal and fish oil are referred to in northern Europe as industrial fisheries. Such fisheries are not known to the Commission to take place in the Mediterranean Sea.

Otherwise, predominantly in southern Europe, large-scale and non-artisanal fisheries are referred to as industrial fisheries. In this context, the Commission does not share the cited opinion of the experts. Such fisheries should be able to co-exist in this area and, indeed, in any other area provided that appropriate management measures are taken.

 

Question no 22 by Rodi Kratsa-Tsagaropoulou (H-0734/00)
 Subject: Infringement of the Schengen Agreement
 

On recent Olympic Airways flights from Athens to Germany, on Saturday, 2 September to Munich (flight OA 175) and on Wednesday, 6 September to Frankfurt (flight OA 165) I witnessed a breach of the Community's principle of the free movement of persons and in particular the application of the Schengen Agreement in the European Union: German police officers were stationed at the aircraft exit and requiring the passengers to show their passports before allowing them to leave the aircraft. In response to my efforts to explain to them that this action was a breach of the Schengen Agreement they replied provocatively that they were carrying out the orders of the airport police authorities and were not concerned about any consequences.

What action does the Commission intend to take to remedy this situation, which is an affront to the dignity of the European public and will undermine its belief in European unification?

 
  
 

In order to correctly assess the case reported by the honourable Member, we should mention the principles of the Schengen Agreement with regard to the crossing of internal borders:

- Article 2(1) of the Schengen Convention stipulates, “Internal borders may be crossed at any point without any checks on persons being carried out.” According to a decision by the Executive Committee in 1994, that means that all travellers, whatever their nationality, are exempt from checking carried out by border police on the grounds that they are crossing a border. Since 26 March 2000, this has also been applicable to passengers on flights between Greece and Germany.

- The terms of Article 2(2) authorise a Contracting Party, subject to public order or national security requirements, to reintroduce border checks appropriate to the situation for a limited period. In principle, if this safeguard clause is implemented, it should be initiated through a consultation procedure. According to the information available to the Commission, Article 2(2) was not implemented by the German authorities at the internal borders mentioned in the honourable Member’s question.

- Article 2(3) of the Schengen Convention stipulates, “The abolition of checks on persons at internal borders shall not affect … the exercise of police powers by the competent authorities under each Contracting Party's legislation throughout its territory.”

Internal borders and the bordering area are not therefore a proscribed area where no checks may be carried out.

In accordance with the Executive Committee’s decision, however, substitute border checks are incompatible with the terms of the Schengen Convention on the elimination of checks. Substitute checks are understood here to be systematic checks carried out on people on the grounds that they have crossed a border in an area in close proximity to the border or in determined border areas. As far as the Commission is concerned, it is clear that this principle defined by the Executive Committee regarding internal land borders is also, by analogy, applicable to airports.

All these Schengen instruments have been integrated into the European Union framework, and the Council has determined the legal basis for these in the first pillar. Monitoring the implementation of Article 2 of the Convention applying the Schengen Agreement is therefore carried out according to the principles governing the monitoring of the application of Community legislation.

At this stage, the Commission does not have sufficient information regarding the reasons why, and the conditions in which, the checks at Munich airport reported by the honourable Member were carried out, which would make it possible to determine whether these checks were “substitute border checks” or general police checks of the type which may be carried out throughout the territory. The Commission cannot, consequently, give an immediate opinion as to whether these checks are concordant with the above-mentioned principles or not.

The Commission will not fail to request clarification from the German authorities regarding the case raised by the honourable Member.

 

Question no 23 by Anneli Hulthén (H-0736/00)
 Subject: European Monitoring Centre for Drugs and Drug Addition (EMCDDA)
 

As long ago as 1997 the Council conclusions on the health aspects of the drugs problem agreed with the Commission that the EMCDDA 'must continue to give high priority to supplying the Community and its Member States with objective, reliable and comparable information'. Does the Commission consider that the statistical data and annual report produced by the Centre meet these requirements? If not, what does the Commission plan to do about it?

 
  
 

The first article of the regulation on the creation of the European Monitoring Centre for Drugs and Drug Addiction (EMCDDA) stipulates that the objective of the Monitoring Centre is to provide the Community and its Member States with ‘objective, reliable and comparable data’ at the European level on drugs and drug addiction, and the consequences thereof. This objective has not, however, been achieved in full and efforts still need to be made in order to make a genuine comparison between the situations in the various Member States possible.

The European Union Action Plan on Drugs 2000–2004, which was endorsed by the Santa Maria Da Feira European Council, stipulates that the Monitoring Centre must, by the end of the year 2000, complete its work on the five epidemiological indicators and also the indicators for drugs-related crime, the availability of illegal drugs and social exclusion related to drugs, and shall present an annual report to the Council’s Horizontal Group on Drugs on the convergence of the main indicators, the progress made in this area and proposed actions for the following year intended to resolve any outstanding problems.

The Monitoring Centre made a commitment to submit to its Management Board by January 2001 a report on its views on the criteria and definitions to be used to implement the key five epidemiological indicators at national level, thereby making it possible to overcome the omissions in definitions and methodologies. The implementation of these criteria by the Member States will make it possible to carry out the necessary harmonisation to be able to carry out a comparison of epidemiological statistics on drugs between Member States.

The Commission therefore considers that genuine progress has been made by the Monitoring Centre in meeting the requirements established by the Council.

Question no 25 by Mihail Papayannakis (H-0742/00)
 Subject: Genetically modified cotton
 

On 11 September 2000 the Greek Ministries of Agriculture and of the Environment, Regional Planning and Public Works announced the measures to be taken concerning genetically modified cotton which has been illegally imported into and cultivated in Greece. As part of these measures the Ministry of Agriculture has arbitrarily set the contamination level of the seeds at 1% and decided that the crop of fields sown with less than 1% of the genetically modified product need not be destroyed, while in the case of fields in which the degree of contamination of the seeds is above 1% the cotton should be ginned in separate ginning plants and exported. These measures are in breach of Council Directive 90/220/EEC(1) on the deliberate release into the environment of genetically modified organisms and pre-empt a final decision by the Community institutions. It should also be pointed out that by this announcement the two ministries in question are violating undertakings to destroy crops of genetically modified cotton which account for 6% of the total seeds on the Greek market.

How does the Commission intend to intervene with the relevant Greek authorities so that they immediately destroy crops of genetically modified cotton and ensure that the farmers concerned receive compensation from the seed import and production companies in question?

 
  
 

To date, no genetically modified (GM) cotton varieties have received authorisation for placing on the market under Part C of Directive 90/220/EEC although two varieties are currently pending approval.

Seed of conventional varieties of cotton has been found in Greece to contain impurities of GM seeds. The Greek authorities sampled 14,000 tonnes of seed and examined 3,000 samples. They found that 560 hectares were planted with seeds with GM impurities higher than 1 %.

In a press release of 11 September 2000 the Greek Government stated that:

the harvesting of the area will take place separately,

the product of the harvest will go to two selected ginning (processing) industries,

the cotton fibre will be separated from the kernels,

the kernels will be destroyed (approximately 1,000 tonnes) and farmers will be compensated for the loss of income (a total of 25 million Greek drachmas).

the fibres will be exported, although there is no way to find out whether or not they derive from GM plants( the "raw" cotton fibre is an elongated single cell containing a dead nucleus – so there is no chance to identify the DNA).

Inspection and control measures to ensure compliance with the Directive 90/220/EEC lie with Member States and the question concerning financial compensation of the farmers concerned should also be addressed by the national administrative body and legal system.

As indicated in position 75 of the Annex to the Commission’s White Paper on Food Safety, the Commission has the intention to adopt, under the existing Community seed legislation, standards for purity concerning the adventitious presence of genetically modified seeds as well specific labelling requirements for seeds in respect of genetically modified material.

Question no 28 by Paulo Casaca (H-0748/00)
 Subject: Application of Article 299(2) to the Autonomous Region of the Azores
 

The new Article 299(2) of the Treaty makes provision for specific adjustments to Community policies to take account of the fact that the outermost regions are heavily dependent on a restricted number of economic activities.

Economic and, in particular, agricultural activity in the Azores depends heavily on milk production, and this dependency is often total in the smallest and most isolated islands.

At the partnership meeting of 23 November the Commission promised to draw up practical measures to adapt the milk quota system to the Autonomous Region of the Azores.

However, the Commission has still not said precisely what action it will take to bring about such an adjustment.

Can the Commission say when and in what form it will draw up this proposal?

 
  
 

Just a few weeks after taking office, the new Commission wished to indicate its determination to continue the Commission’s action in favour of the outermost regions.

To this end, the Commission organised a partnership meeting on 23 November 1999 bringing together Members of the European Parliament from these regions, the representatives of Member States, regional and local authorities, the Commission President and several other Commissioners and Commission departments.

This event enabled the Member States to present the broad guidelines of their memoranda, and gave the various participants the opportunity to draw the Commission’s attention to any particularly sensitive areas or to their priorities. The purpose of this day’s event was to initiate constructive dialogue with a view to preparing the Commission’s report on the implementation of the new Article 299(2) of the EC Treaty. At this time, the Commission did not make an express agreement to draw up practical measures to adapt the milk quota system to the Autonomous Region of the Azores, as the honourable Member claims.

This report was adopted on 14 March 2000, and does not contain any specific reference to the Azores milk quota system. It should be noted that, neither in the memorandum on implementation of the new Article 299(2) nor in its requests for revision of POSEIMA, did Portugal include any specific request regarding the Azores milk quota.

At the beginning of April 2000, the Portuguese authorities informed the Commission of a problem involving exceeding the milk quota in the Azores. This is an important and problematic issue, in view of the Community regulations on milk quotas. The matter is currently being examined in detail within the Commission, but as yet no definitive conclusions are available.

 

Question no 29 by Gerard Collins (H-0749/00)
 Subject: Regional policy and transport
 

The cost of transporting goods from peripheral, island regions, is a significant factor for exporters from countries such as Ireland. What new initiatives does the Commission consider it can bring forward to create a level playing field for Irish exporters to continental Europe?

 
  
 

The Commission is well aware of the potential difficulties of lack of accessibility faced by certain islands and peripheral regions. For this reason, in the case of Ireland, programmes to improve transport links have from the beginning featured prominently in the Community’s interventions under both the Structural and Cohesion Funds.

In the period of the first two Community Support Frameworks, from 1989 to 1999, actions to reduce the impact of peripherality covered almost every aspect of transport infrastructure : the upgrading of strategic road corridors and main railways, the development of regional airport and port infrastructure and improving traffic management. In accordance with Council Directive 95/21/EC on Port State Control (PSC), the inspection of foreign flagged vessels by the port State consists of a document check and a general examination of the overall condition of the ship. This examination may be extended, in breadth and depth, if clear grounds are established to question the vessel's compliance with the international safety standards.

When deficiencies found are clearly hazardous to safety, health or the environment the ship must be detained. The detention order can be lifted only when the hazard is removed.

This means that, notwithstanding the certification issued by or on behalf of the flag State, when there are clear grounds to believe that a vessel does not comply with the applicable requirements, the port State is authorised to take action to ensure that the vessel does not sail until it can do so without danger.

The inspections by the state where ports are located are rightly considered as a fundamental tool to reduce substandard shipping. The Commission action in this field continues to be rigorous. It should be noted that the package of legislative proposals adopted in March with the "Communication on the Safety of Seaborne Oil Trade", foresees a general strengthening of the PSC inspection regime. In particular, the possibility is also proposed to ban from European waters certain vessels which, on the basis of the number of their detentions, are considered to represent a high level of risk.
