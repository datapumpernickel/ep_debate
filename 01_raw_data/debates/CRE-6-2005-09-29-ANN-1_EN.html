Question no 9 by Proinsias De Rossa (H-0675/05)
 Subject: Temporary Agency Workers Directive
 

Recent research by the European Foundation for the Improvement of Living and Working Conditions indicated that just 23% of temporary agency workers receive paid 'on-the-job' training or are sent on training and courses by their employer, as against 35% of permanent employees. The research concluded that, since new skills and occupational competencies are the key to employability, it is more difficult for employees with non-permanent work contracts to make the transition to the regular jobs market or to have access to new jobs and opportunities.

Given these findings, is the Council Presidency making any efforts to achieve progress on the Draft Directive on Temporary Agency Workers, which has been blocked in Council for over two years and which would seek to give temporary agency workers the same opportunities and benefits as their colleagues who have permanent jobs?

 
  
 

(EN) I would like to thank the Honourable Member for his continuing interest in this complex and politically sensitive matter.

The main difficulty, as you know, is finding a solution which reflects the concerns of Member States and labour market diversity across the EU. A solution is needed which finds the right balance between giving temporary agency workers adequate protection and allowing the agency market to develop and grow so that it can contribute to meeting the Lisbon goals of increasing employment and providing better jobs. Member States have recently renewed their commitment to achieving these goals, I believe they can, be achieved in ways which respect the different practices and traditions in the national labour markets.

As you are probably aware, the Netherlands Presidency in the latter half of last year held discussions at various levels to ascertain whether positions remained the same on the Directive since the last substantive debate in June 2003. In the last few weeks, the UK Presidency has been in contact with the Member States. Unfortunately, the UK Presidency, in common with previous Presidencies, found that very little had changed and that views remain very polarised. At this stage therefore, the UK Presidency does not envisage putting this item on the agenda of the Council in December, although it is keeping the issue under review.

I would like to stress that the UK Presidency wants to work constructively to find a solution which is acceptable to all Member States. However, it is clear from our bilateral contacts that many Member States still have problems with the current text.

Question no 11 by Manuel Medina Ortega (H-0679/05)
  Subject: Safety requirements in the case of budget airlines
 

What action is the Council proposing to take in order to ensure that budget airlines which operate into or out of the European Union comply with safety requirements and carry out the checks prescribed by the international civil aviation authorities?

 
  
 

(ES) Low-cost airlines must conform to the same safety requirements and their aircraft must undergo the same aviation inspections as aircraft of other companies.

EU airlines and their aircraft must meet the applicable national or Community requirements(1), including those prepared by the European Aviation Safety Agency (EASA) and adopted by the Commission. Non-Community airlines and aircraft must also be authorised or approved by the States responsible for them, according to requirements that are at least equivalent to the international standards laid down by the International Civil Aviation Organisation.

 
 

(1) See, amongst others, Regulation (EEC) No 3922/91 of the Council, of 16 December 1991, on the harmonization of technical requirements and administrative procedures in the field of civil aviation (OJ L 373 of 31.12.1991, p. 4), Regulation (EC) No 1592/02 of the European Parliament and of the Council, of 15 July 2002, on common rules in the field of civil aviation and establishing a European Aviation Safety Agency (OJ L 240 of 7.9.2002, p. 1) and Directive 2004/36/EC of the European Parliament and of the Council, of 21 April 2004, on the safety of third-country aircraft using Community airports (OJ L 143 of 30.4.2004, p. 76).

 

Question no 12 by Panagiotis Beglitis (H-0684/05)
 Subject: Violation of human rights and democratic freedoms by Turkey - prosecution of Turkish writer Orhan Pamuk
 

We are informed today that the Public Prosecutor of Istanbul has initiated criminal proceedings against the renowned Turkish writer Orhan Pamuk for 'denigrating Turkish identity' because he had the courage to state in a Swiss newspaper that Turkey should recognise that 'a million Armenians and thirty thousand Kurds were killed by the Turks' and should come to terms with its history. The trial is to take place in Istanbul on 16 December and the Turkish writer may be sentenced to between 6 and 9 months in prison under the Penal Code.

Since this action by the Turkish authorities constitutes a flagrant violation of fundamental human rights, democratic freedoms and Turkey's commitments, as a candidate country, to respect the Copenhagen political criteria, what immediate, practical measures will the Council take to persuade the Turkish political leadership to withdraw the charges against the writer Orhan Pamuk?

Is the Turkish Penal Code, even after its recent reform, consistent with Community legislation and European democratic principles?

 
  
 

(EL) Τhe Council is aware of the events referred to by the honourable Member. Freedom of expression in Turkey is a sector in which, despite the improvements achieved, there are still numerous problems. The need for more constant efforts to be made, especially on the general framework for the exercise of fundamental freedoms, such as the freedom of expression, has already been emphasised repeatedly by the European Council.

In its position for the meeting of the EU-Turkey Association Council on 26 April 2005, the European Union stated that, although there had been cases in which superior courts had acquitted persons who had been prosecuted for non-violent expression of opinion, in various other cases journalists, publishers, writers and other citizens were still being prosecuted. The Council further noted that the new penal code only achieves limited progress in this sector and expressed its satisfaction with Turkey's declared intention of introducing amendments with a view to improving the relevant provisions relating to freedom of expression and freedom of the press.

The new penal code issued on 1 June could be considered a positive step. The new penal code is, as a whole, an important step forward and introduces numerous positive amendments in sectors such as gender equality and respect for women's rights and the combating of torture chambers and maltreatment. Nonetheless, the postponement of the entry into force of the penal code was not used to make important amendments to the text; certain provisions of the code relating to freedom of expression and the freedom of the press are still formulated in somewhat unclear terms and could be interpreted in a restrictive manner by judges or public prosecutors. That is why the European Union will monitor the application of the penal code closely, especially the provisions of the code relating to the media and freedom of expression, in order to evaluate its efficacy in practice.

Generally speaking, and in full keeping with the conclusions of the European Council in December 2004, the Commission will continue to monitor closely the actual and overall application of the political reform process in Turkey and will submit regular reports to the Council. It goes without saying that this includes the question of freedom of expression. Consequently, the Council can assure the honourable Member that it will monitor this question closely and will raise it at all the appropriate levels, especially within the framework of the continuing political dialogue with Turkey and within the framework of the various bodies set up on the basis of the EC-Turkey association agreement.

Question no 14 by Antonios Trakatellis (H-0686/05)
 Subject: Protection of public health from an influenza pandemic: the Union's response capability and the functioning of the European Centre for Disease Prevention and Control
 

The avian influenza pandemic (virus subtype A(H5N1)) is before the gates of the European Union and will very likely cause an influenza pandemic among humans which will have an immediate impact on the health of European citizens and the social and economic structure of Member States. Given that this virus may be spread in the immediate future particularly to the Balkan countries (Romania and Bulgaria) by migratory birds from Russia and Kazakhstan, will the Council say:

Beyond the conclusions of June 2004 containing recommendations to make preparations to tackle an influenza pandemic, to suspend imports of live poultry and feathers from countries affected by the pandemic and to impose restrictions on poultry farming, what measures does the Council, acting in conjunction with the Member States, recommend to protect public health? What capability does the Union have to respond to an influenza pandemic, for instance a network of laboratories, mechanisms, financial measures, vaccines and anti-viral drugs? Has the newly established European Centre for Disease Prevention and Control been consulted, and does the Council intend to issue recommendations for determining priority groups which should be vaccinated and receive anti-viral drugs?

 
  
 

(EL) Naturally the Council also is still very worried about avian influenza.The Member States are responsible for the measures which are taken at national level to control diseases which occur, such as influenza pandemics. The Commission is informed of progress with their programmes and it is the Commission that coordinates action at Community level.

In order to strengthen coordination between veterinary measures and measures to protect public health, a meeting was held between the heads of the medical services and the heads of the veterinary services on 22 September 2005, at which the need was emphasised for the ΕU to continue to work in close liaison and to coordinate closely with the competent international bodies.

As regards the financing measures, the Council is examining, within the framework of the future financial debate, a Commission proposal for a new regulation on the EU Solidarity Fund. The proposal includes the facility to contribute to the cost in the event of a pandemic, including the cost of vaccines and antivirals.

Networks of veterinary laboratories and human health laboratories have been created in the ΕU with the objective of combating the threat of influenza viruses. As the honourable Member perhaps knows, the ΕU has a surveillance network which was set up with Community cofinancing and is known as the European Influenza Surveillance Scheme. The Early Warning and Response System links the competent authorities in the Member States, the European Centre for Disease Prevention and Control and the Commission in the event of danger from this sort of disease.

The European Centre for Disease Prevention and Control will offer advice and support on public health issues in order to guarantee the compatibility and standardisation of surveillance and protection measures throughout the ΕU.

The next ΕU/WHO conference will be held on 24 to 26 October 2005 in order to review progress in preparations by the Member States since the last conference in March 2005 as regards the possible influenza pandemic. The European Centre for Disease Prevention and Control will take the opportunity to establish how the Member States intend to decide which social groups are most vulnerable and, by extension, what the order of priority will be for inoculations and antivirals.

In addition, a pandemic influenza exercise (Exercise Common Ground) has been scheduled for 23 and 24 November 2005, in order to test the communications systems and the preparedness of the Member States to deal with a possible influenza pandemic.

 

Question no 15 by Syed Kamall (H-0688/05)
 Subject: EU quotas on textile imports from China
 

What guarantees can the Council give that retailers and consumers in the EU will not continue to be penalised by EU quotas on textile imports from China, designed to protect inefficient producers?

And furthermore, can the Council give details of when these quotas will be completely abolished?

 
  
 

(EN) The Commission has signed two documents with the Chinese Ministry of Commerce which contain "agreed levels" for exports of certain Chinese textile and clothing products to the EU.

The first of these documents was signed in Shanghai on 10 June 2005 and is commonly referred to as "the Memorandum of Understanding" (MoU): its full title is: "Memorandum of Understanding (MoU) between the European Commission and the Ministry of Commerce of the People’s Republic of China on the Export of Certain Chinese Textile and Clothing Products to the European Union".

The second document is commonly referred to as "the Minutes" or "the Agreed Minutes" and was signed on 5 September 2005 in Beijing. Its full title is as follows: "Minutes of the consultations regarding the establishment of transitional flexibility measures on the Memorandum of Understanding between the European Commission and the Ministry of Commerce of the People's Republic of China on the export of certain textile and clothing products to the EU".

Annex 1 to the Memorandum of Understanding sets out the agreed levels of exports for 10 product categories for the years 2005, 2006 and 2007. These levels have been partly adapted by the Agreed Minutes.

 

Question no 17 by Jonas Sjöstedt (H-0692/05)
 Subject: Fisheries agreement with Morocco
 

Since the mid-1980s, the EU has had a fisheries agreement with Morocco, including Western Sahara which has one of the world's richest fishing grounds. The United Nations, among others, defines Western Sahara as the last colony in Africa. The exploitation of Western Sahara's natural resources by Moroccan and other foreign companies can, therefore, be seen as illegal.

On 28 July 2005, the EU signed a new fisheries agreement with Morocco, which will also cover the territory of Western Sahara. This differs, for example, from the USA, which has excluded from its free trade agreement with Morocco all products originating from Western Sahara.

In the light of this situation, and if the Council shares the view that Western Sahara is the last colony in Africa, is it justifiable to include Western Sahara in the agreement with Morocco?

 
  
 

(SV) The issue of Western Sahara is at present handled within the context of the work of the United Nations. The Council supports the UN process.

Article 2 of the fisheries agreement, which was signed on 28 July 2005, states that what is at issue are the Moroccan fishing grounds that come under the Kingdom of Morocco’s sovereignty or jurisdiction. The definition of the agreement’s geographical scope is identical to that in the previous agreement, which expired in 1999, and does not in any way pre-empt the issue of Western Sahara’s status.

Question no 20 by Manolis Mavrommatis (H-0706/05)
 Subject: Possible printing of euro outside the EU
 

The information that from 2007 the euro will be printed in Asia, and more specifically in Malaysia, gives rise to a great many doubts and questions. Statistics show that there is a huge problem with the quality of the current banknotes and that their shortcomings are becoming increasingly obvious. Does the Council confirm and approve the transfer of euro printing to countries outside the EU? On what criteria will any possible decision of that nature be based and how will transparency be ensured in view of the fact that the 12 printing plants in the Member States will lose that prerogative as well as jobs and revenue? What guarantees do the European Central Bank and the EU have regarding possible counterfeiting of the euro 'made in Asia' compared with those given by the Member States already producing the coins and banknotes? Does this decision contravene or not the criteria of the Treaty of Maastricht and of the EU's monetary policy, in particular Article 106 concerning the printing of the euro?

 
  
 

(EL) According to Article 106 of the Treaty, the European Central Bank is competent for this matter.

The ECB's guidelines of 16 September 2004 on the procurement of euro banknotes state that the exclusive right to authorise the issue of euro banknotes within the Community also includes the competence to define the legal framework for the procurement of banknotes.

Consequently, the Council is not the competent body to express an opinion or to take decisions on questions relating to euro banknotes.

 

Question no 21 by Ryszard Czarnecki (H-0709/05)
 Subject: Respect for human rights in Kashmir
 

How does the Council assess the situation regarding the observance of human rights in Kashmir in the context of the strategic partnership between the EU and India?

 
  
 

(EN) The EU is naturally troubled by continuing reports of human rights abuses in Jammu and Kashmir. In this context, the EU appreciates the public commitment by the Indian leadership to ensure that India's security forces take further steps to improve their approach towards respect for human rights in Kashmir. The EU condemns all acts of violence in Kashmir, and urges the militants to recognise that their actions cannot bring about a resolution to the Kashmir issue. Rather, a durable settlement can only be achieved through dialogue between India and Pakistan, taking into account the wishes of all the peoples of Kashmir.

Both India and the EU, through their Strategic Partnership, are committed to upholding human rights and fundamental freedoms, and have ratified the major international human rights instruments. These shared values, based on democracy, pluralism and respect for the rule of law gives strength to the relationship between India and the EU.

Through their Joint Action Plan, both the EU and India propose to:

Continue in a spirit of equality and mutual respect, the dialogue on Human Rights both in a multilateral and bilateral context, with the objective of building greater mutual understanding and expanding common ground in order to strengthen the foundations of the strategic partnership;

Consult and discuss positions on human rights and democracy issues and look at opportunities for co-sponsoring resolutions on thematic issues in relevant fora such as UN Commission on Human Rights or UNGA Third Committee;

Look together for possible synergies and initiatives to promote human rights and democracy.

 

Question no 22 by Christopher Heaton-Harris (H-0711/05)
 Subject: Integrity of the Commission's accounts and accounting system
 

Since before 1997 when Labour entered government in the UK, the Court of Auditors has failed to give a positive 'DAS' (Statement of Assurance) on the European Commission's accounts. Throughout this time the Council has always signed off the Commission's accounts seemingly without question.

Can it confirm that the integrity of the Commission's accounts and accounting system is important to the Presidency - what suggestions have been made for improvements and how many times has this been discussed? Has the UK Presidency expressed any concerns to the Commission about its accounts for 2004?

 
  
 

(EN) The integrity of the Commission's accounts and accounting system has always been of great concern to the Council. It has, in recent years, consistently underlined to the Commission the importance of modernising the accounting system.

While the Honourable Member was present in the Budgetary Control Committee on 15 March 2005, the President of the Council, my colleague from Luxembourg Mr Luc Frieden, took up this issue before Parliament when he presented the Council discharge recommendation on the Commission accounts for 2003. The Minister stated that he was encouraged to hear that the Commission would be introducing its new IT accounting system, ABAC, from 1 January 2005. He trusted that the system would become completely operational according to the planned schedule. The Commission announced in January 2005 that the transition to the new accounting system had been successful.

The Council continues to attach the greatest importance to achieving a positive statement of assurance for the Commission accounts and it is currently examining the recent Commission communication on a "roadmap to an integrated internal control framework". In this context, the UK Presidency, together with the Commission, has established a Panel of Experts which will help prepare draft Conclusions for ECOFIN in November.

 

Question no 23 by Claude Moraes (H-0713/05)
 Subject: Data retention
 

Following the terrorist bombings in London, the Council has come forward with a number of proposals and enhancements of existing proposals to fight terrorism.

Can the Council indicate what progress is being made with particular reference to money laundering issues and the financing of terrorism in the EU? Can the Council also indicate what progress is being made on data retention proposals in relation to the fight against terrorism?

 
  
 

(EN) The creation of common rules on the retention of telecommunications data is a key element of the EU Counter-Terrorism Action Plan and remains a priority for the UK Presidency. The Council has therefore been negotiating a draft Framework Decision on data retention since April 2004 which will be submitted to the JHA Council on 12 October. The draft Framework Decision will be discussed by the Council on 12 October 2005 with a view to achieving as much progress as possible on the substance of the initiative. On that occasion the Commission will present the proposal for a Directive on data retention that it put forward on 21 September 2005.

In the Council Declaration on the EU response to the London bombings, several proposals and enhancements of existing proposals for fighting terrorism were mentioned. Progress has been made as regards the proposals on money laundering and the financing of terrorism in the EU.

The Third Money Laundering Directive was adopted by the Council on 20 September 2005 and will be published in the Official Journal at the beginning of October 2005.

The draft Regulation on Wire Transfers was adopted by the Commission on 26 July 2005 and discussions in the relevant working group of the Council have started.

The Commission is working on a Code of Conduct to prevent the misuse of charities by terrorists. It has announced its intention to set out a series of transparency and accountability measures with the intention that these would become a benchmark for the sector. A broad public consultation is ongoing, as are discussions with experts from the Member States and with representatives of the non-profit sector. The presentation of a Communication on this issue and a proposal for a Code of Conduct is planned for December 2005.

Furthermore, in order to review the EU's performance in the fight against terrorism financing, the Council intends to publish a report by December 2005. In order to draft this report, a questionnaire has been sent to all Member States and the process of analysing the answers is well under way.

Several months ago, when I asked Commissioner Frattini about the regular publication, possibly by Europol or by Eurojust, of the list of the EU's Top Ten Most Wanted Criminals in order to involve the public in the fight against organised criminal gangs across our open internal borders, he promised to come back to me about the idea.

Is the Commission now ready to take a decision on this perfectly good idea ?

 
  
 

(FR) All police officers in the European Union, plus Norway and Iceland, have, or in the case of the new Member States, will have access as from mid-2007 to all the data entered in the Schengen Information System concerning individuals wanted for arrest by the authorities of those same countries.

For its part, Interpol regularly publishes on its website (www.Interpol.int/public/wanted) a list of persons wanted throughout the world on the basis of the notifications forwarded to it by the Interpol member countries. This site, which is freely accessible to the public, refers visitors, for example, to the sites which give details of persons wanted in eight Union Member States (AT, BE, CZ, FR, DE, EL, ES, UK). The use made of the tool provided by Interpol should therefore be considered with all the EU Member States. The issue of public support for the fight against organised crime should not be neglected. The importance of the contribution made by UK citizens to the rapid progress of the investigation into the terrorist attacks perpetrated in London on 7 July 2005

Responsibility for disseminating information concerning wanted persons generally rests with police forces - Eurojust cannot therefore be involved in that process. As regards Europol, the task conferred on it by the convention focuses on the analysis and cross-referencing of information forwarded to it by the Member States prior to the judicial proceedings during which coercive measures, such as the issuing of an arrest warrant, are taken.

 

Question no 94 by Luisa Morgantini (H-0749/05)
 Subject: Humanitarian assistance financed by the Community
 

I have in my possession a report that notes that an agency implementing assistance to the Palestinians in the Occupied Territories was hiring storage facilities located in an Israeli settlement(1). Does the Commission know if any programme of humanitarian assistance financed by the Community is using Community funds to procure goods or services from illegally-established settlement-based enterprises?

Has the Commission taken any measures to prevent Community funds provided to humanitarian aid agencies and programmes from being used to finance the procurement of goods and services from illegally-established settlement based enterprises? Has the Commission taken any measure to prevent Community funds from being used to finance the procurement of goods and services from enterprises actively engaged in activities that violate international humanitarian law?

Will the Commission include a provision in the rules governing humanitarian assistance grants which specifically require Community-funded programs to be implemented in strict conformity with the principles and requirements of international humanitarian law? If not, why not?

 
  
 

(EN) The Commission is aware of the report referred to by the Honourable Member, and it has met the authors both during its preparations and after it was published. The Commission has taken note that the report refers, on page 33, to the alleged "case of one agency using storage facilities in the east Jerusalem settlement industrial zone of Atarot".

According to what the Commission was able to establish, the information pertains to the World Food Programme (WFP). The latter explained that, whilst 85% of the food aid that it distributes in the Palestinian territories goes directly from the port of Ashdod to the West Bank and the Gaza Strip, 15% of it is in fact stored at Atarot. This concerns, according to WFP, mainly food aid funded by the United States, but also some quantities of EU funded food aid (less than 3%). As far as the Commission could establish, there would be at the moment 83 MT of sugar funded under the EU budget line for humanitarian aid and 243 MT of chickpeas funded under the EU budget line for food security that are stored at Atarot. According to WFP, the main reason for this is operational, since Palestinian drivers have no access to the Ashdod.

The Commission was not aware of any programme of assistance that is using funds provided under the EU budget lines for humanitarian aid and food security to procure goods and/or services from illegally-established settlement-based enterprises, including the hiring of storage facilities at the above-mentioned settlement or in any other settlement. The Commission will take the appropriate action towards WFP in order to avoid that the storage facilities at Atarot continue to be used for the storage of EU funded aid.

In respect to the last sub-question, the Commission draws the attention of the Honourable Member to the fact that Article 16 of its Framework Partnership Agreement with humanitarian organisations, which is the basis upon which financial aid for the implementation of humanitarian operation is granted, already prescribes, inter alia, that: "Humanitarian aid operations shall respect and promote the enforcement of international humanitarian law and humanitarian principles". It also draws the attention on preamble 2 of Council Regulation (EC) N° 1257/96 of 20 June 1996 concerning humanitarian aid(2), which reads: "Whereas civilian operations to protect the victims of fighting or of comparable exceptional circumstances are governed by international humanitarian law and should accordingly be considered part of humanitarian action".

(2) Official Journal L 163 of 2 July 1996

 

Question no 95 by Antonios Trakatellis (H-0759/05)
 Subject: Ultraviolet (UV) radiation; skin cancer and protection of public health
 

Given that the increased incidence of skin cancer recorded worldwide is alarming and that epidemiological studies worldwide revealed a correlation between the increase of skin cancer incidence and UV exposure in white populations, and that increasing UV radiation is caused by stratospheric ozone depletion - reported to be 3% - over large areas of the globe (65 degrees North to 65 degrees South) according to the latest study of the United Nations Environment Programme and given that not only are people increasingly exposed during their leisure time but that there are groups that are exposed also during their work, such as farmers, builders etc., is there a recent epidemiological study available of the incidence of skin cancer in Europe (non-melanoma and melanoma) and a recent study of the UV radiation levels across Europe and how these vary? What measures does the Commission intend to take in order to protect public health?

 
  
 

(EN) The Commission is aware of three recent epidemiological studies(1),(2), 3 of time trends in melanoma incidence and mortality in Europe, which are in line with European trends of an increase of melanoma incidence and mortality in the population as well as some improvements generally attributed to skin cancer prevention actions.

These studies have been supported by the Community action programme “Europe against Cancer” 1996-2002.

In addition, regarding prevention of all skin cancers the Commission supported the review and re-launch in 2003 of the European Code against Cancer, which includes the review of the actual epidemiological data and the scientific justification for evidence-based recommendations also on the prevention of skin cancer in its recommendation N°64.

Nevertheless, the Commission is not aware of any long-term epidemiological studies on the relationship between the increase of natural UV intensity specifically-linked to ozone depletion in the atmosphere and its impact on skin cancer incidence in the Member States.

Nevertheless, according to measurements of solar UV radiation over Europe carried out by the Joint Research Centre, the UV intensity does not show a substantial variation among the Member States. However, "Mini ozone holes" can be regularly observed over parts of Europe, which lead to locally/regionally higher UV intensity.

In addition, recent scientific evidence shows that increases in bromine, which is a component of many ozone-depleting substances, in the Arctic combined with very cold winters have resulted in high UV conditions being recorded in many parts of Europe. As high UV exposure increases the risk of skin cancer, eye cataracts, damages the human immuno-suppression, and reduces the production of some crops, the Commission is strongly encouraging the recovery of the ozone layer through the implementation of Regulation (EC) No 2037/2000 in the European Community which reflects actions agreed internationally in the Montreal Protocol.

Last, but not least, the Commission tabled a proposal for a Directive on the minimum health and safety requirements regarding the exposure of workers to the risks arising from physical agents (optical radiation). It should be pointed out, however, that the Parliament asked for natural radiations to be excluded from the scope of this Directive.

Question no 96 by Vytautas Landsbergis (H-0760/05)
 Subject: Aid projects relating to the Kaliningrad enclave
 

Does the Commission intend to support the Kaliningrad enclave as an isolated European region bordering on Member States, or would it like to carry out more extensive projects which would involve Lithuania and the Polish administrative regions (voïvodi) concerned?

 
  
 

(EN) The Commission supports the balanced socio-economic development of Kaliningrad so that it does not fall behind its neighbours or indeed become isolated. Indeed, the Commission supports projects intended to further Kaliningrad’s integration with its EU neighbours, since this is one of the best ways of ensuring balanced socio-economic development.

Twelve Neighbourhood Programmes are due to be implemented on the EU’s eastern border in 2004-2006, including a Programme for Kaliningrad, Poland and Lithuania. Under the Russia-Lithuania-Poland Programme, Kaliningrad will benefit from a budget of €9.5 million for the next two years.

The Commission is making available more than €30 million to border crossing infrastructure in Kaliningrad. A number of crossings have been considered as priority. Bagrationovsk is the freight link between Kaliningrad and Poland, where construction was completed in Autumn 2003. Mamonovo II is another potentially important border crossing with Poland. Chernychevsky connects Kaliningrad to the rest of Russia through Lithuania and Belarus. Sovetsk lies on the Riga-Kaliningrad corridor.

Post-2006, Kaliningrad will be eligible for the increased funding proposed under the New Neighbourhood Instrument, demonstrating the importance the EU attaches to cross-border and regional co-operation on the external borders of the EU. The Commission will welcome projects which promote economic and social cohesion across borders, as well as cultural and educational exchange.
