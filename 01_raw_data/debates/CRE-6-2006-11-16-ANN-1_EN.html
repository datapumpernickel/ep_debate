Question no 17 by Hélène Goudin (H-0915/06)
 Subject: Cod fishing in the North Sea, the Skagerrak and Kattegat
 

The International Council for the Exploration of the Sea (ICES) has presented a report emphasising that there is a need to introduce a total ban on cod fishing in the North Sea, the Skagerrak and Kattegat during 2007. The Swedish Board of Fisheries gives its backing to the report and maintains that a total ban is essential if cod stocks are to recover. Criticism has also been levelled at the current quota system. The Swedish Fishermen's Federation and the recently appointed Swedish Minister for Fisheries advocate fishing days rather than fish quotas. At the present time, large quantities of cod are thrown overboard in order to comply with the quota rules.

Will the Presidency advocate a total ban on cod fishing in the North Sea, the Skagerrak and Kattegat? What are the Presidency's views on the proposal to introduce specific fishing days instead of a quota system?

 
  
 

(FI) This answer, which has been drafted by the Presidency, and which in itself is not binding on the Council or its members, was not presented orally in Council question time in the part-session held by the European Parliament in Strasbourg in November 2006.

The situation regarding cod stocks in the areas which the honourable Member mentions has been a matter of concern for all relevant parties for many years now, and has resulted in several measures being adopted to restrict fishing. Although there are some positive signs in sight, more work is clearly needed for cod stocks to recover to a sustainable level in the long term. The measures introduced so far have been the North Sea cod recovery plan, reducing annual quotas, increasing net mesh size, fishing ban zones, fishing ban times, especially the spawning season, and lowering the number of fishing days.

The Council will be taking a decision on the Commission’s proposal for the annual management of cod fishing. Towards the end of November, the Commission is expected to put forward a proposal, based on opinions submitted by the International Council for the Exploration of the Sea (ICES), which endorses Total Allowable Catches (TACs), quotas and other measures relating to the issue for the year 2007. Consequently, the Council cannot say what this proposal will consist of or how the Member States will react to it.

The Presidency proposes to steer discussions, on what is normally a troublesome subject, with the positive and constructive aim of reaching a decision at the Council sitting in December.

 

Question no 18 by Avril Doyle (H-0916/06)
 Subject: Effective smoke-free areas
 

Tobacco is the leading preventable cause of death in the world, with an estimated 4.9 million deaths a year. In the European Union, diseases and deaths due to smoking cost the Member States € 100 billion a year. Smoking also seriously endangers the life of non-smokers, with 79 000 non-smoking adults dying annually in Europe due to passive smoking.

Does the Council agree that in line with their Treaty obligations, to ensure a high level of human health protection in Community policies, Member States need to do more to encourage effective smoke-free environments?

Does it also agree that Member States need to do more to create adequate smoking cessation interventions in accordance with their WHO obligations to take effective measures to promote cessation of tobacco use?

 
  
 

(FI) This answer by the Presidency, which is binding neither on the Council nor its Members, was not given orally during Question Time to the Council at the European Parliament's November 2006 part-session in Strasbourg.

Preventing and combating smoking is already a priority health-policy objective in the Member States and the European Community.

In its recommendation of 2 December 2002 on the prevention of smoking and on initiatives to improve tobacco control(1) the Council notes that, given the health risks associated with passive smoking, Member States should aim to protect smokers and non-smokers from environmental tobacco smoke. The Council recommends that the Member States should:

enact or implement legislation and/or other effective measures to protect people from exposure to environmental tobacco smoke in indoor workplaces, enclosed public places and public transport;

continue to develop strategies and measures to reduce tobacco consumption, such as strengthening overall health education, peculiarly in schools, and general programmes to discourage the initial use of tobacco products and to help people overcome tobacco addiction.

The recommendation calls on the Commission to report on the implementation of the proposed measures, on the basis of information supplied by the Member States. The report to be submitted in 2007 will assess the effectiveness of the measures set out in the recommendation and the need for further measures.

The aim of the World Health Organisation (WHO) Framework Convention on Tobacco Control, which came into force on 27 February 2005, is to protect current and future generations against tobacco consumption and prevent environmental pollution through tobacco use. The Commission and all the Member States with the exception of Italy and the Czech Republic have ratified the Framework Convention, which lays down a specific obligation, binding on the parties, requiring them to protect people against exposure to tobacco smoke. Pursuant to Article 8 of the Framework Convention, each contracting party must enact and implement effective measures providing for protection from passive smoking in indoor workplaces, public transport, indoor public places and, as appropriate, other public places.

At the first conference of the contracting parties to the Framework Convention, held in Geneva in February 2006, the Community and its Member States made a significant contribution to the discussion of the decision on the framing of guidelines for the implementation of the Framework Convention as regards protection from passive smoking. A start has now been made on drawing up the guidelines, which will specify all the factors fundamental to the implementation of Article 8.

It is solely a matter for the competent Member State authorities to enact measures such as a ban on smoking or the provision of counselling programmes for people who give up smoking.

 
 

(1) OJ L 22, 25.1.2003, p. 31.

Question no 20 by Diamanto Manolakou (H-0926/06)
 Subject: Missing people in Cyprus used as human guinea-pigs by the Turks
 

A report by an American firm conducting strategic analyses and studies states that Greek Cypriots and Greek soldiers who have been missing since the invasion of Northern Cyprus by Turkish troops in 1974 were used as human guinea-pigs in industrial laboratories run by the Turkish armed forces between 1984 and 1988.

Given that the tragedy for the families of those missing has now lasted for more than three decades and Turkey, which continues to occupy the northern part of Cyprus, has never provided any information on those missing, will the Council take the necessary initiatives to demand that Turkey provide all the information in its possession on these missing people, however distressing that information might be, so that this tragedy can be brought to an end?

 
  
 

(FI) This answer, which has been drafted by the Presidency, and which in itself is not binding on the Council or its members, was not presented orally in Council question time in the part-session held by the European Parliament in Strasbourg in November 2006.

The Council cannot adopt a position on individual reports, about which it has no first-hand knowledge. We are obviously aware of the human dimension of the consequences of the tragic events of 1974 with regard to the entire population of the island. The Committee on Missing Persons in Cyprus was set up in 1981 to investigate cases of missing Greek and Turkish Cypriots. The work of this committee is an important part of the action taken to try and create an atmosphere of trust and cooperation between the island’s two communities.

The European Union continues to support efforts made within the framework of the United Nations that aim to achieve a comprehensive solution to the Cyprus problem.

 Subject: Statements by Álvaro Uribe in support of an exchange of prisoners in Colombia
 

On 4 October 2006, the President of Colombia, Álvaro Uribe, issued statements acknowledging his willingness to meet with the guerrilla leaders of the Revolutionary Armed Forces of Colombia (FARC) in order to achieve peace for his country. This stance marks a new departure in his approach to the conflict in that it opens up the possibility of starting talks with the guerrilla movement aimed at seeking a humanitarian agreement, with the freeing of 59 kidnap victims in exchange for 500 rebel prisoners.

Three weeks later, following the explosion of a car bomb outside military buildings, the President withdrew his offer of dialogue with the FARC. In the light of this news, does the Council intend to make a statement supporting the exchange of hostages and prisoners and the beginning of a potential peace process in Colombia?

 
  
 

(FI) This answer, which has been drafted by the Presidency, and which in itself is not binding on the Council or its members, was not presented orally in Council question time in the part-session held by the European Parliament in Strasbourg in November 2006.

Question no 22 by Athanasios Pafilis (H-0930/06)
 Subject: Unacceptable new counterterrorism legislation in the USA
 

The Military Commissions Act 2006 was signed a few days ago in the USA. Under this law, torture is essentially permitted, habeas corpus is abolished for people being held 'on suspicion of terrorism ', as is the rule that they must have access to a defence lawyer, and the US President has been granted the power to order that people be detained indefinitely without being charged, and to set up special courts which will be able to pass sentences on the basis of undisclosed and unsubstantiated information and statements obtained by force.

Does the Council condemn this unprecedented legislation which abolishes fundamental human rights and elementary procedural guarantees for detainees? Does it believe that this law violates international treaties and the principles of international law on human rights and the rights of prisoners? Does it reject the adoption of such laws by any state in the international community? Will the Council annul the relevant agreements with the US which may lead to this law being applied to citizens of Member States of the European Union or people resident on its territory?

 
  
 

(FI) This answer, which has been drafted by the Presidency, which in itself is not binding on the Council or its members, was not presented orally in Council question time in the part-session held by the European Parliament in Strasbourg in November 2006.

On 15 September 2004 Foreign Ministers reached agreement on the following press release:

“Ministers reiterated their commitment to combating terrorism effectively, using all legal means and instruments available. Terrorism is itself a threat to a system of values based on the rule of law.

They reiterated that, in combating terrorism, human rights and humanitarian standards have to be maintained. Accordingly, they acknowledged the intention of the United States administration to treat all detainees in accordance with the provisions of the Geneva Convention and the assurances about ICRC (International Committee of the Red Cross) access.

The existence of secret detention facilities where detained persons are kept in a legal vacuum is not in conformity with international humanitarian law and international criminal law.

Ministers noted that they would continue their dialogue with the US, focusing on safeguarding human rights in the fight against terrorism.”

The Council has not completed its examination of the Military Commissions Act.

 

Question no 23 by Gay Mitchell (H-0933/06)
 Subject: Council's role in making the world a safer place
 

Given the situation in Iraq, North Korea and Iran, for example, would the Council indicate what effective role it is playing in making the world a safer place?

 
  
 

(FI) This answer drawn up by the Presidency, which is not in itself binding on the Council or its members, was not given orally at Question Time to the Council at the November part-session of the European Parliament in Strasbourg in November 2006.

The Council’s objective remains a safer Europe in a more just world. The European security strategy adopted in December 2003 set out the means by which the EU seeks to attain this objective.

As regards the examples cited by the Honourable Member, the Council can reply as follows:

Iraq

The European Union continues to support the government of national unity headed by Prime Minister Nouri Al-Maliki in implementing its programme, including the government’s proposed national reconciliation and dialogue plan. The EU is actively involved in drafting the International Compact together with the Iraqi government, the UN and other partners in the international community. This compact is an important opportunity for the Iraqi government to commit itself to difficult but necessary reforms in the political, security and economic fields and for the international community to commit itself to supporting these reforms.

The European Union supported Iraq’s referendum on elections and the constitution and intends to support the democracy process in Iraq in future too. The EU is continuing with its comprehensive aid programme supported by Iraq and has also continued, at Iraq’s request, with its Integrated Rule of Law mission for Iraq (EUJUST LEX). Thanks to this mission, training in the Rule of Law has so far been given to some 800 Iraqi police officers, judges and prison officers. There are plans to begin negotiations in the near future on a trade and cooperation agreement with Iraq. The EU’s close political dialogue with Iraq is also continuing.

Iran

The Council has repeatedly expressed its readiness to develop sustainable long-term relations with Iran and has always supported efforts towards a diplomatic solution on the issue of Iran’s nuclear power.(1)

The deadline of 31 July 2006 set in UN Security Council Resolution 1696 has passed, and the EU has expressed its deep concern at the fact that Iran has still not yet halted its uranium enrichment activities.

In his contacts with the Iranian authorities, the EU’s High Representative has continually called on Iran to put into practice the measures referred to in UN Security Council Resolution 1696 and demanded by the IAEA Administrative Council.

Iran has not as yet shown any willingness to comply with the demand to halt its uranium enrichment activities. Halting such activities is a precondition for the negotiations, supported by the EU, for a new initiative on the basis of the offer made by France, Germany, the United Kingdom, China, Russia and the USA.

In its resolution 1696 the Security Council expressed its intention to adopt appropriate measures under Article 41 of the UN Charter if Iran does not comply with its demands. The Council considered that the fact that Iran is continuing its uranium enrichment activities leaves the EU no choice but to support consultations on such measures.

However, on 17 October the Council noted that Iran still has the option to negotiate and strongly urged Iran to choose the positive course of action proposed.

On 6 June 2006 the EU’s High Representative set out to Iran the proposals of six states (France, Germany, the United Kingdom, China, the Russian Federation and the USA). These far-reaching proposals would serve as the basis for a long-term agreement and would give Iran all it needed to develop a modern civilian nuclear energy sector, while also taking account of international concerns.

Opting for such a positive course of action would pave the way for new relations with Iran, based on mutual respect and wider cooperation in the political and economic spheres.

North Korea

When the Democratic People’s Republic of Korea proclaimed a nuclear test, the Council strongly condemned, in its conclusions adopted on 17 October on the Democratic People’s Republic of Korea, the test of a nuclear explosive device, which it considered poses a danger to regional stability and represents a clear threat to international peace and security. It indicated that the EU will fully implement the provisions of all relevant UNSC Resolutions and notably of Resolution 1718 (2006) and of Resolution 1695 (2006).

The sanctions set out in the UN Security Council’s resolution include a ban on the export of sensitive products and sensitive technology which might promote North Korea’s nuclear-related, other weapons of mass destruction-related and ballistic missile-related programmes, and a ban on supplying services relating thereto, a ban on the procurement of sensitive products and sensitive technology from North Korea, a ban on the export of luxury goods and a freeze on the funds and economic resources of persons, communities and bodies which participate in or support the above-mentioned North Korean programmes.

In its conclusions the Council also called on the Democratic People’s Republic of Korea to return immediately to the six-party negotiations.

The Presidency later expressed its satisfaction at the fact that China, the Democratic Republic of Korea and the USA had reached a consensus in Beijing on 31 October on holding a meeting in the near future in the context of the six-party negotiations.

 
 

(1) See also answer to Question H-0707/06.

 

Question no 24 by Rodi Kratsa-Tsagaropoulou (H-0935/06)
 Subject: Liberalisation of postal services
 

The Commission recently (18 October 2006) submitted a proposal for the full opening of the EU's postal markets to competition by 2009 at the latest with a view to completing the internal market, increasing innovation, improving services, meeting the needs of consumers and maintaining Member States' current obligations to provide a high-quality universal service.

To what extent is the Council in agreement with the Commission's proposal fully to open the market in postal services by this deadline? Some Member States have already fully opened their postal market, while others are planning to do so before 2009. Does the Council have any study into the outcome of fully opening up the market to competition in those Member States? Does it share the Commission's view concerning the need to ensure a high-quality universal service together with flexible options for funding that service?

 
  
 

(FI) This answer, which has been drafted by the Presidency, and which in itself is not binding on the Council or its members, was not presented orally in Council question time in the part-session held by the European Parliament in Strasbourg in November 2006.

On 18 July, the Commission put forward a proposal in accordance with the provisions of the current Postal Services Directive 97/67/EC(1), as amended by Directive 2002/39/EC, for a Directive of the European Parliament and Council concerning the full accomplishment of the internal market of Community postal services(2). On 20 October 2006, the Council received the proposal in question, a Commission report on the application of the Postal Directive(3) and the Commission’s prospective study on the impact on universal service of the full accomplishment of the postal internal market in 2009(4). Furthermore, with regard to its Communication entitled ‘Better Regulation for Growth and Jobs in the European Union’(5), the Commission conducted an Impact Assessment (6) and submitted it to the European Parliament and the Council. This sheds light on the economic and social effects of different options on users and all relevant stakeholders.

The Commission only put the proposal forward a short time ago, so the Council has only just started looking at it. The Council of the European Union emphasises its commitment to working closely with the European Parliament in this matter, and would like to state that it has decided to make a thorough analysis, over an appropriate timescale, of all the relevant critical parameters and threshold issues, and our priority aim will be to safeguard an efficient, sustainable and competitive EU postal sector, which would still have to provide a reasonably priced and first-rate service to all European citizens and businesses.

Question no 80 by Monica Frassoni (H-0951/06)
 Subject: European Schools - Complaints Board
 

The Commission is a permanent member of the Board of Governors of the European Schools. How effective, in the Commission's opinion, is the Complaints Board established under the European Schools system? Is the Commission aware that all the decisions handed down by the Complaints Board to date have dismissed the complaints in question as inadmissible on the grounds that the Board did not have jurisdiction? Is the Commission aware that the Complaints Board adjudicators have never met and, apparently, all the decisions taken to date have been adopted on the basis of informal procedures (telephone conversations and e-mails)? Is it true that the Commission, on the advice of its own legal service, opposed, within the Board of Governors, extending the Complaints Board's decision-making powers? What practical action does the Commission intend to take to ensure that the European Schools system is consistent with the rule of law and consequently that provision is made for decisions taken by European School bodies, particularly as regards disciplinary matters and pupils' right to be enrolled, to be subject to judicial review?

 
  
 

(FR) The Complaints Board(1) is a jurisdictional body that is independent of the European Schools’ Board of Governors. Its members are chosen from a list drawn up for the purpose by the Court of Justice of the European Communities, and candidates are completely independent and noted for their legal expertise.

The Commission cannot concur with the honourable Member’s assertion that all decisions handed down to date by the Complaints Board are inadmissible on the grounds of the Board’s lack of jurisdiction. It has dealt with 35 complaints in 2005 and 2006, of which only nine have been rejected due to lack of jurisdiction. In 11 of the cases, the Board took a substantive decision. The other cases are either inadmissible on procedural grounds or pending. It therefore appears from these figures that the Board gives a substantive ruling wherever possible.

It is inaccurate to say that Complaints Board adjudicators have never met and that the decisions taken to date have been adopted on the basis of informal procedures. The Complaints Board operates in accordance with the rules laid down by Chapter IV of its Statute and regularly meets in public hearings, announced on the European Schools website(2), at its Brussels headquarters. Furthermore, it is the prerogative of the Chairman of the Board to use the resources he sees fit to communicate with the other Members outside the sessions held in Brussels.

In the Board of Governors, the Commission stated its opposition to extending the Complaints Board’s jurisdiction to all cases that may arise in the European Schools system. The Commission in fact notes that the signatories to the Convention defining the statute of the European schools did not want to grant the European Schools complete immunity from legal process. An extension of immunity from legal process to issues other than those currently covered can only come from a change to the Convention itself or from an additional agreement between the signatories. It cannot come from a unilateral act agreed upon by the Board of Governors. Furthermore, the Board of Governors and the administration and financial committee have indicated that granting general powers to the Complaints Board would have far-reaching consequences in terms of its structure and its modus operandi, leading to costs on which it is hard to put a figure, but probably comparable with those of the Court of First instance.

To answer the honourable Member’s last question, the Commission would note that the European Schools system is under no circumstances exempt from upholding the principle of legality, given that all cases not falling under the Complaints Board’s competence fall under national jurisdiction.

It should be specified that issues relating to discipline are subject to a procedure that is clearly set out in Article 44 of the general rules of the European Schools, which places great emphasis on the right to defence and any exclusion from the school of more than two weeks can be referred administratively to the Secretary-General of the European Schools and, where applicable, to the Complaints Board. On the other hand, any cases concerning the enrolment of pupils, which is currently the sole responsibility of each adjudicator(3), under Article 46 of the General Rules of the European Schools do not fall under the competence of the Complaints Board but should be brought before the national courts.

(3) In future, in Brussels, under the Central Registrations Authority.
