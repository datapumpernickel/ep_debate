Verbatim report of proceedings
Wednesday, 12 October 2011 - Brussels OJ edition

18. One-minute speeches on matters of political importance
Video of the speeches
PV
 

  President. – The next item is one-minute speeches on matters of political importance.

 
  
 

  János Áder (PPE). – (HU) Madam President, it was one and a half years ago that the European Parliament adopted a resolution on a ban on the use of cyanide mining technologies, and it did so with an overwhelming majority of 488 in favour and 48 against. In that resolution, the European Parliament called on the Commission to put forward a proposal for a decision by the end of 2011, that is, this year. It is now the middle of October and we have yet to see anything. No proposal whatsoever has been submitted to Parliament. There are two questions we must ask: First, when does the Commission intend to make its proposal? Second, will the Commission take full political, moral and financial responsibility if another environmental disaster occurs in Europe due to this delay?

 
  
 

  Monika Flašíková Beňová (S&D). – (SK) Madam President, at last week’s conference on the implementation of the Charter of Fundamental Rights, I talked in my speech about how I see the EU as a union of fundamental rights. These fundamental rights undoubtedly include the right of Romanian and Bulgarian citizens to enter the Schengen area, but, in my opinion, social rights are among the most important.

We must not lose sight of social rights, particularly in this period of economic, financial and commercial crisis. Who pays the highest price for the consequences of the crisis? It is not the bank managers, speculators or financial market traders. It is mainly employees and members of the so-called middle level. The neoliberal market mechanism has got most of these people into a very difficult situation. It is therefore unacceptable, in my opinion, for us to give priority now to the recapitalisation of the banks, and not to talk about the need to improve the social situation in Europe.

 
  
 

  Niccolò Rinaldi (ALDE). – (IT) Madam President, ladies and gentlemen, yesterday, we received a delegation of citizens who described themselves as ‘indignant’. Some have come a long way to get to Brussels, even travelling by foot from Iceland.

Being indignant is the exact opposite of cynicism, indifference and resignation. It is a non-violent, transparent way of asking for the current European crisis to be tackled, rewarding the European institutions for being assertive and playing a leading role rather than being at the mercy of national governments, but, above all, it is a way of tackling this crisis with tools involving the real participation of the people.

The European Union will have to take difficult, even unpopular decisions. Yesterday’s meeting was short but significant, because it showed that we need to exploit this crisis in order to rebel against the tribal fragmentation of Europe, which prevents it from having a shared vision, but, above all, it showed that we need to involve citizens fully in processes from which, to date, they have felt excluded.

 
  
 

  Catherine Grèze (Verts/ALE). – (FR) Madam President, Sortu’s participation and success in the elections have proven that the political expression of the Basque movement is definitely the right way forward. Today, we are at a crucial turning point in the peace process in the Basque Country.

Only two weeks ago, an international verification commission was set up to assess and monitor the ceasefire declared by the ETA organisation, which is determined to cooperate with the commission. The latter consists of high-ranking mediation and disarmament experts internationally recognised for their neutrality.

Ladies and gentlemen, as Europeans, we have a responsibility to do everything we can, to do everything in our power to resolve the latest conflict within the European Union. I should therefore like to question the European Commission and to ask it how it intends to recognise the international verification commission and to support it as a key contributor to the resolution of this conflict.

 
  
 

  Miguel Portas (GUE/NGL). – (PT) Madam President, massive recapitalisation is currently under way, only a few months after the vast majority of European banks passed stress tests with flying colours. One of these – Dexia – was even ranked in 12th place, and its managing director received a EUR 600 000 bonus. It turns out that it was all lies!

For the second time in the space of three years, Dexia is asking to be bailed out with European taxpayers’ money. We must have a clear picture of how lenient we have been with the financial system, and how this has, in all honesty, fully justified the outrage of those protesting from New York to Lisbon, from Athens to Madrid. They may not know much about financial markets, but they have something vital: a thirst for justice.

 
  
 

  Csanád Szegedi (NI). – (HU) Madam President, ladies and gentlemen, you may all recall that some years ago, six or seven years ago, Serb radicals were beating up Hungarians en masse in Vojvodina, currently part of Serbia. When it finally seemed that we could perhaps put this period behind us, threats clearly aimed at forcibly changing ethnic proportions have unfortunately begun to emerge once again this year. It is graffitied on walls, youths are being beaten and it is graffitied on walls that Hungarians should get out of Serbia because it is not their home. A far more serious issue is that the Serbian Government has elevated hate against Hungarians to a state level, and has enacted a law on restitution which rekindles the notion of collective culpability vis-à-vis both Hungarians and Danube Swabians, whom it excludes from the scope of persons entitled to restitution.

I sincerely hope that the European Union will dispense with the double standard it has been applying so far, and will at last look beyond protecting sexual minorities and shedding crocodile tears only when it comes to the Gypsies of Italy or France, and will also step up when such anti-democratic, dictatorial and discriminatory laws are adopted in a country standing on the threshold to accession.

 
  
 

  Elena Băsescu (PPE). – (RO) Madam President, I have chosen to speak about the Yulia Tymoshenko case under the one-minute procedure as a huge number of my colleagues requested the floor during the previous debate.

The illegal sentence passed yesterday on Yulia Tymoshenko confirms that a politically inspired lawsuit was brought against her. What is even clearer is that the aim is to remove the former prime minister from the political scene once and for all. The trial lacked transparency and was not based on genuine evidence. European diplomats were banned from entering the courtroom. In these circumstances, the government in Kiev is no longer credible when it declares its desire to cooperate with the EU.

This will definitely have repercussions for the signing of the association agreement between the EU and Ukraine. Good relations with the EU do not just mean visa liberalisation or facilitating trade. They mean, above all, respect for the principles of democracy and the rule of law on which the Eastern Partnership, which Ukraine is part of, is founded.

 
  
 

  Csaba Sándor Tabajdi (S&D). – (HU) Madam President, the involvement of the social partners in the legislative process is a fundamental norm in the European Union. While employees throughout Europe are being hit extremely hard by policies of austerity, which do not spare Hungary either, the Hungarian Government is making matters worse: it has launched an assault against employees’ rights. Preparations for the new Hungarian labour law regulations are proceeding behind the scenes in a non-transparent manner and without any official consultations with the social partners, which is contrary to European norms. By contrast, it comes as no surprise that the draft EU Working Time Directive is being written by the European social partners. I ask the European Commission to persuade the Hungarian Government to take into account basic European norms when drafting its amendment of the Labour Code, and to respect the fundamental rights of Hungarian employees.

 
  
 

  Marisa Matias (GUE/NGL). – (PT) Madam President, there are, in fact, episodes that really belong in the realm of the absurd, and what happened yesterday was one such episode. A group of young people from various parts of Europe met in Brussels as part of the protest movements, and Members of this House invited us to meet with them and discuss their ideas. What then happened was that, at the European Parliament’s request, Belgian anti-riot police and Parliament’s own security forces mounted a totally disproportionate action to prevent these young people from entering. Such strict security meant that only seven of them met the criteria enabling them to come in and say what they came to say. I do not understand this measure and must express my outrage, Madam President. Parliament is a seat of democracy and it should not be afraid of the people. After all, where was the threat in this case? Are those who think differently from the dominant way of thinking in Parliament a threat? This is not what we call a ‘threat’, it is what we call ‘the wealth of democracy’, and I hope that we will all share a real sense of outrage on 15 October.

 
  
 

  Monika Smolková (S&D). – (SK) Madam President, I would like in my speech to draw the attention of Ms Reding, Commissioner for Justice, Fundamental Rights and Citizenship, to the resolution adopted on 4 September this year by the European Association of Judges and addressed to the Slovak Minister for Justice. This international institution has rejected moves to reform the judiciary. It criticises the method of selecting judges by commissions that are wholly under the control of the governing coalition. It criticises the transfer of the right to submit proposals for disciplinary action against judges to the minister, as well as the criticism of judicial decisions by representatives of Slovak executive and legislative power. The resolution puts the current Slovak Government to shame. Even though the Slovak Parliament yesterday expressed its lack of confidence in the government and we face early elections, the Commission should respond. The constitutional court’s suspension of the law on the office of public prosecutor, which was intended to bring the independent prosecutor completely under the control of the minister, also deserves a response.

 
  
 

  Krišjānis Kariņš (PPE). – (LV) Madam President, we often assert in this Chamber that fairness should prevail in this or that country or corner of the globe. However, ladies and gentlemen, we must ensure that there should be fairness in our own European Union as well. To be specific, the European Commission’s offer as regards agricultural and cohesion policy in the next multiannual financial framework outright discriminates against Latvia and the Baltic States. For example, whereas growth of 8% is planned for cohesion policy in Europe as a whole, Latvia faces a reduction in cohesion policy of 20% – a whole fifth! In agricultural policy, similarly, only one half. Ladies and gentlemen, we are all in a single market. We cannot allow such a distortion of the market. We must introduce fairness in cohesion and agricultural policy for Latvia and for the other Baltic States. Thank you for your attention.

 
  
  

IN THE CHAIR: ISABELLE DURANT
Vice-President

 
  
 

  Petru Constantin Luhan (PPE). – (RO) Madam President, it is stated in the proposal tabled by the European Commission for the 2014-2020 cohesion policy that its new features include focusing on vulnerable groups and assigning 20% of the European Social Fund allocations to actions targeting social inclusion.

Social exclusion, lack of employment and poverty are directly interlinked. However, we have seen that, as part of centralising the targets set for each Member State, which were established by them within their national reform programmes in April this year, it is stated that the results for the reduction in the number of people exposed to the risk of poverty cannot be calculated at European Union level due to differences in the methodologies used by Member States.

Therefore, we need common guidelines, methodologies and tools to make social inclusion possible. Failure to have them will lead to this subject being avoided, especially by local and regional authorities, which are the first agencies to have to manage these funds. Therefore, I call on the Commission to produce such tools as soon as possible, in good time.

 
  
 

  Kinga Gál (PPE). – (HU) Madam President, I would like to comment briefly on the report on Serbia that was published today by the European Commission. The report ignores one of the most problematic aspects, namely, the property restitution act passed by Belgrade in September. The act, which is otherwise of particular importance, includes a provision on restitution, which appears to strengthen the principle of collective culpability and collective liability as opposed to individual liability. However, as we know, the cornerstone of the European justice system is individual liability. Unfortunately, this fact has apparently passed unnoticed by the Commission. According to the provisions, all persons who were part of the occupying forces stationed in Serbia during World War II, as well as their successors, are excluded from the scope of the act irrespective of whether any form of individual liability can be proven in their case or not.

As it is, almost the entirety of the Hungarian minority in Vojvodina, who had been drafted against their will and under coercion, have been excluded. This passage of the act sheds a negative light on Serbia and will definitely hinder its acquisition of candidate status, but, at the same time, it also calls into question the Commission’s attitude.

 
  
 

  Dominique Riquet (PPE). – (FR) Madam President, the European Commission and France had previously agreed a measure to award EUR 24.5 million in European Globalisation Adjustment Fund (EGF) aid to 3 580 workers affected by a redundancy plan at Renault.

The change to the retirement age in France, following the negotiations concerning the fund, has left 72 workers in limbo. The Minister for Labour, Xavier Bertrand, is currently reviewing this problem.

The socialist group, acting on a request made by the Committee on Employment and Social Affairs, has tabled two amendments in the Committee on Budgets calling for an audit and for part of the 24.5 million to be placed in reserve. The permanent rapporteur, Barbara Matera, and the European Commission have confirmed the inadmissibility of these amendments, which are contrary to the EGF Regulation and which have therefore been thrown out. Nevertheless, the left, joined in an unnatural alliance by those sworn opponents of the EGF, the European Conservatives and Reformists Group and the liberals, has voted against the 24.5 million allocation, and the mandate has been rejected in full.

Ultimately, a partisan scheme designed to inconvenience the French Government has put the company and 3 500 workers in an unprecedented and disastrous position. Europe is once again stigmatised. I hope that this very unfortunate measure can be reconsidered.

 
  
 

  Antonio Masip Hidalgo (S&D). – (ES) Madam President, our British colleagues in the House of Commons, in keeping with their country’s historical parliamentary and democratic tradition that is so admirable, have highlighted the intolerable practices of the Murdoch media group. The courage shown by The Guardian newspaper is also commendable.

However, Rupert Murdoch’s aberrant tentacles reach even further than that. His payroll includes the former Prime Minister of Spain, José María Aznar, who commands a six-figure sum and is still very keen to maintain his influence over Spanish and, indeed, European politics.

An election campaign is about to begin in Spain. Before this campaign gets under way, the Popular Party and its leader, Mr Rajoy, should make clear to the European people what form of support they receive from Mr Murdoch and his group; what it is that the magnate demands in exchange for his financial support of Mr Aznar and his foundation; and whether or not that plot has anything to do with what has taken place in the United Kingdom, now revealed so laudably.

 
  
 

  Kristian Vigenin (S&D). – (BG) Madam President, the Bureau of the Group of the European People’s Party (Christian Democrats) will meet in Sofia in two days’ time. The guests who are going to support the ruling GERB (Citizens for European Development of Bulgaria) party, just a week before the presidential and local elections, are expected to include Mr Barroso, Mr Buzek and EU Commissioners. I am not going to address the issue of whether such a political engagement is acceptable for the persons mentioned. However, I would like to ask them, from this Chamber, to use their visit to ask some questions as well. Why were current mayors and candidates subjected to all kinds of pressure by the authorities to step down from taking part in the elections? How can the interior minister run the ruling party’s pre-election headquarters using its entire human resources against its political opponents? Why was the opposition’s voice stifled by dissolving parliament in the run-up to the elections? This is setting a precedent in the country’s 20-year democratic history. Why were thousands of Bulgarians living abroad deprived of the opportunity to vote in the presidential elections, while the vote there is being organised not by the constitutionally appointed ambassadors but by proxies appointed by the foreign minister? I can go on with this list. I hope that the international observers will give an objective assessment of the state of Bulgarian democracy.

 
  
 

  Charalampos Angourakis (GUE/NGL) . – (EL) Madam President, Palestinian political detainees started a hunger strike two weeks ago in protest against the inhumane conditions in which they are being held. Yesterday, they were joined by another 2 000 fighters. The protest comes in the wake of insulting statements by Israeli Prime Minister Netanyahu, who, in his own words, considers the conditions in which they are being held to be extremely generous.

The hunger strikers are demanding access to health, especially for ill and injured detainees. They have denounced their collective punishments, which include a ban on visits by their families, humiliating body searches and inhumane isolation which, in some cases, has lasted nearly 10 years, as in the case of the Secretary General of the Popular Front for the Liberation of Palestine.

The people need to step up their fight against the imperialists and their fight for the unconditional recognition of an independent, sovereign Palestinian state, within the 1967 borders, and with its capital in East Jerusalem, which is a full member of the UN.

 
  
 

  President. – That concludes the one-minute speeches.
