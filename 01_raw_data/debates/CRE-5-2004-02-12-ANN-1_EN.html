QUESTIONS TO COUNCIL
Question no 18 by Roseline Vachetta (H-0009/04)
 Subject: Public subsidies and redundancies at the ST Microelectronics group
 

The ST Microelectronics group, 34% of whose capital is held by Italy and France, had a turnover of about 3.8 billion euro and a net profit of 187 million euro in the first half of 2003. The group apparently received 542.6 million euro in public subsidies from France and Italy and 90 million euro from the European Union. Despite its good economic performance and public financial support, the group's management has decided since summer 2003 to relocate some production activities to Singapore, close the Rennes site in France (600 jobs) and reorganise these activities by closing down the production site at Castelletto in Italy and shifting the work to Agrate, with the loss of 321 jobs at Agrate and 115 at Castelletto.

In this specific case, what checks will the Council make and what penalties will it impose on a group which, despite receiving public subsidies from Member States and making substantial profits, is sacrificing hundreds of jobs and industrial know-how?

 
  
 

(FR) The Council shares the honourable Member’s concerns regarding the social effects of relocations by industrial groups which, moreover, have benefited from significant public subsidies at national level.

However, the honourable Member is not unaware that it is the European Commission which has competence to establish whether State aid granted by a Member State, favouring certain undertakings or the production of certain goods, is compatible with the internal market (Article 87 of the Treaty). It is therefore not for the Council to make checks or impose penalties on industrial groups such as the one mentioned by the Honourable Member.

Given the importance of this question, in the coming months the Commission is to submit a communication on the social dimension of external policy, which will address the problems raised by globalisation.

 

Question no 19 by Patricia McKenna (H-0011/04)
 Subject: Secret use of death penalty in Vietnam
 

In a decision signed by the Vietnamese Prime Minister on 5 January, the reporting and dissemination of statistics on the use of the death penalty in Vietnam has been made a state secret. This is in contravention of the United Nations resolution of April 2003 on the use of the death penalty, which calls for ‘information with regard to the imposition of the death penalty and to any scheduled execution’ to be made ‘available to the public’. In Vietnam, even the relatives of those facing execution are not informed beforehand.

Will the Council clearly condemn such a policy and will it take steps to promote both the revocation of such a policy and the abolition of the death penalty altogether?

 
  
 

(EN) The Council follows the human rights situation, including the use of death penalty, in Vietnam very carefully, and has repeatedly raised concerns in this context in its contacts with the Vietnamese Government, inter alia in the structured EU-Vietnam human rights dialogue at the level of EU missions in Hanoi.

The recent decision on the death penalty, as mentioned by the Honourable Member and reported by human rights organisations, is a cause of concern. The EU missions in Hanoi are currently gathering information on this issue and will make recommendations for EU action in accordance with the EU guidelines on the death penalty, if need be.

 

Question no 20 by Martin Kastler (H-0017/04)
 Subject: Embryo research under the Sixth Research Framework Programme
 

No agreement was reached by the Council of Ministers in December on funding for embryonic stem cell research from the EU budget. The Parliament is also split on this issue. Commissioner Busquin has interpreted this to mean that in principle funding can now be granted. Is this correct?

Is there still any way to prevent destructive embryo research, which is banned in several Member States, receiving financial support from the EU?

What are the plans of the Council of Ministers as regards future work on this controversial issue?

What steps will the Irish Presidency take to prevent research projects which are banned in certain Member States (including Ireland) receiving EU research funding in future?

 
  
 

(EN) As the Honourable Member correctly points out, the Council did not reach agreement on the Commission proposal of July 2003 which aimed at establishing detailed implementing provisions for Community funded research activities involving human embryos and human embryonic stem cells. Any decision to fund such research projects are therefore based on the European Parliament and Council Decision of 27 June 2002 on the Sixth Framework Programme, as well as on the Council Decision of 30 September 2002 in respect of the relevant Specific Research Programme, ‘Integrating and strengthening the European Research Area’.

These Decisions provide the framework for the examination by the Commission of proposals for research projects and exclude certain activities, such as reproductive cloning and modification to the genetic heritage of human beings, from Community funding. Moreover, research banned in a given Member State may not receive Community funding in that Member State. However, the Decisions adopted in 2002 do not explicitly ban research activities involving human embryonic stem cells, provided that such activities fall within the scope of the specific programme.

As to the other specific question raised, the honourable Member will be aware that the research programme is managed and implemented by the Commission. In this role the Commission is assisted by the Programme Committees composed of representatives of Member States. In particular, proposals for research projects involving human embryonic stem cells are dealt with under the regulatory committee procedure in accordance with the Council Decision of 28 June 1999. Under this procedure, the Commission refers proposals for projects to the Council if there is no agreement at the Committee level and the Council may, by a qualified majority, oppose such proposals.

Over and above the committee arrangements, the Council has no competence in the management of the research programme and the honourable Member is advised, therefore, to turn to the

Commission for any further information on the Commission’s intentions on stem cell research. For these reasons the Council has no plans to come back to this issue in the near future.

Nevertheless, as the honourable Member will be aware, the current programme Decisions expire in 2006, after which subsequent programmes will be subject to Decisions by the European Parliament and the Council on the basis of proposals from the Commission. The debate will most probably be renewed in that context.

 

Question no 21 by Anne Elisabet Jensen (H-0023/04)
 Subject: Protection of commercial drivers against violent assault
 

(EN) At the Council meeting of 14 October 2002, the EU's Justice and Home Affairs Ministers adopted a joint declaration on the protection of commercial drivers against violent assault. The Ministers were seeking appropriate measures to ensure that commercial drivers engaged in export trade do not fall victim to organised crime, by providing the necessary protection and, where necessary, by mapping the incidence of crime against commercial drivers and taking appropriate measures to fight these types of crime.

The means of achieving this goal consist in improving security on particularly dangerous stretches of road and parking areas, implementing specific crime-prevention initiatives in the sector, introducing centralised statistics on reports of violent assaults on drivers, and the exchange of information between the Member States to prevent and combat the problem.

Would the Council provide details of the stage reached in implementing the initiatives referred to in the joint declaration?

 
  
 

The Joint declaration by the Ministers of Justice and Home Affairs of the Member States and the Candidate Countries in association with the European Commission on the protection of commercial drivers engaged in export trade from becoming victims of organised crime to which the honourable Member refers, does not as such impose an obligation on Member States to inform the Council on the steps they have taken or intend to take to honour this declaration. Furthermore, in accordance with the declaration it is up to the Member States to assess the need for national measures in relation to the implementation of the declaration. Finally, it should be noted that the declaration is a non-binding instrument, which is not as such provided for in the TEU.

Within the framework of the Council there is, however, a continuos debate regarding the implementation of relevant instruments, including non-binding instruments. The Council regrets, however, that it is not in a position to provide any information on the implementation of this declaration.

Question no 24 by Monica Frassoni (H-0032/04)
 Subject: Elimination of voters from Maltese electoral lists
 

On 21 March 2003, the Constitutional Court of Malta, at the request of an injured party, gave a definition of 'residence' (the Cassola Case, Judge Tonio Mallia, Case No 6/2003/1: Harry Vassallo vs the Electoral Commission and John Mary Magro). The definition given by the Malta Constitutional court reads: ‘the word 'residence' does not mean physical presence in the country’.

Moreover, the new law on European elections in Malta, (Act No XVI of 2003, published in the Malta Government Gazette of 26 November 2003) explicitly states that all Maltese citizens living in the 25 EU countries have a right to vote and stand for election in Malta.

Is the Council aware of the fact that, despite the constitutional definition and the existing legislation on European elections, the Malta Labour Party has again applied to have 1684 Maltese citizens struck off the electoral register? These court cases will begin to be heard by the Maltese courts in the last week of January, and Maltese employees with the EU institutions are amongst those being targeted.

What does the Council intend to do against this illegal attempt by the MLP to prevent Maltese citizens taking part in the upcoming European elections in Malta?

 
  
 

(EN) It should be recalled that the Maltese constitution recognises all fundamental human and civil rights. This being said, the right to vote at general elections in Malta is restricted by criteria of residence laid down in the constitution, while the right of vote at European elections is regulated by a specific law and is not subject to such restrictions.

The Malta Labour Party's application to have an important number of Maltese citizens taken off the electoral register is being dealt with by the competent Maltese Court.

As regards withdrawing voting rights on the grounds of mental infirmity, this can only be decided by an electoral commission after advice by a medical board. The Council has no reason to believe that these procedures do not offer sufficient guarantees against possible abuses.

The Council does not wish to interfere in the work of the Maltese judiciary, neither by commenting on the steps taken by a specific political party of an Acceding State, nor by any other kind of action. Finally, it is for the Maltese legislator to see whether current restrictions to the right to vote in general elections should be reconsidered.

 

Question no 25 by Lucio Manisco (H-0035/04)
 Subject: Task force to investigate letter bombs
 

In the wake of the recent spate of letter bombs targeting the European institutions, it was decided in Rome on 5 January 2004 to set up a European task force to monitor the phenomenon of anarchist insurrection in all its forms.

The Council is aware that Giovanni Luperi, who has been appointed to head the task force at the suggestion of the Italian Government, is being investigated for serious offences committed in the course of his duties at the G8 Summit in Genoa in July 2001, including the fabrication of evidence against numerous demonstrators. Given the European interest of the initiative, would it not therefore be advisable to appoint someone else to lead this delicate task?

The Task Force, composed of representatives of the law enforcement authorities of several Member States, operates under the sole responsibility of Italy and is not linked to any of the ongoing inter- institutional efforts to protect the physical security of the EU institutions and bodies.

The Council can therefore not take any position in respect of the composition of the Task Force.

Question no 27 by Paulo Casaca (H-0047/04)
 Subject: Continuation of the Iranian regime's nuclear enrichment programme
 

A number of EU Member States, as well as the Council Secretariat, have been claiming some degree of success in blocking the Iranian regime's pursuit of its nuclear programme, while also affirming their support for democratising that regime.

However, the real evidence points to exactly the opposite. The EU's leaders have in fact systematically facilitated the rise of prominent members of the religious hierarchy or their spokesmen, to the detriment of the 'reformists' who are supposedly responsible for political leadership.

In addition, reports of the International Atomic Energy Agency to which press organs such as 'European Voice' have had access confirm that Iran is continuing with the construction of centrifuge plants for uranium enrichment.

Does the Council not consider that the encouragement of the Iranian regime's hardline wing and the toleration of Iran's pursuit of its nuclear programme are positions totally contrary to the guiding principles of the EU?

 
  
 

(EN) Following the commitments undertaken by Iran relating to measures aimed at the settlement of all outstanding IAEA issues with regard to its nuclear programme on the occasion of the visit by the Foreign Ministers of France, Germany and the United Kingdom to Tehran on 21 October 2003, the Council reiterated in the conclusions of its meeting of 8 December 2003 the EU‘s readiness to explore ways to develop wider political and economic cooperation with Iran. The Council emphasised that this can only be achieved through full international confidence in Iran’s adherence to non-proliferation and, in particular, in the peaceful nature of Iran’s nuclear programme, as well as improvements in the areas of human rights, fight against terrorism, and Iran’s position on the Middle East Peace Process. High Representative Solana visited Iran on 12/13 January, to discuss the modalities of taking forward the EU’s dialogue with Iran in all areas, as requested by the Council in December. It was agreed to continue discussions on how to take forward dialogue in all areas.

The Council noted that the EU followed the political situation in Iran with great attention and encouraged a resolution of the situation which would allow free and fair elections. Finally, the Council announced that it would continue closely to follow these issues and awaited in particular the next report on Iran's nuclear programme by the IAEA Director-General, which is expected later this month.

Question no 29 by Malcolm Harbour (H-0050/04)
 Subject: Sales promotions
 

On 4 September 2002 Parliament agreed a compromise position on the proposed Regulation on sales promotions. On 13 February 2003 in its response to the Communication on the Review of the Internal Market Strategy, Parliament reaffirmed its commitment to this Regulation and called upon the Council 'to submit a common position on the sales promotion regulation to Parliament without delay'. (EN) The Council thanks the honourable Member for his attentive follow-up to this proposal for a Regulation.

What are the issues still pending concerning the establishment of a Euro-Mediterranean Foundation? What is the current state of affairs and the timetable for the establishment of this Foundation? How does the Council evaluate the latest Report of the High-Level Advisory Group on Dialogue between Peoples and Cultures in the Euro-Mediterranean Area, and will its recommendations be considered in the procedure for the establishment of the Euro-Mediterranean Foundation?

 
  
 

(EN) One of the priorities of the Irish Presidency is to make progress in working towards agreement on the establishment of the Euro-Mediterranean Foundation. Regarding the Headquarters, the Euromed Committee, as instructed by the Naples Ministerial, will continue work with a view to a decision to be taken at the next Mid-Term Ministerial to be held in Dublin on 5-6 May 2004.

As indicated in the Crete Mid-Term Ministerial Conference conclusions and acknowledged by the Naples Ministerial conclusions, the discussion on the objectives and activities of the Foundation will take into account the recommendations of the latest report of the High Level Advisory Group on Dialogue between Peoples and Cultures in the Euromed area.

 

Question no 31 by Ioannis Souladakis (H-0053/04)
 Subject: Relations between the European Union and Syria
 

There has recently been a perceptible general improvement in relations between the European Union and countries in the Middle East and North Africa (e.g. Libya).

What initiatives will the Council take to improve the European Union's relations with Syria?

What in the Council's view are the prospects for those relations?

 
  
 

(EN) Negotiations on an Association Agreement have been underway since 1998 between the European Union and Syria. The Council is hopeful that the agreement can be finalised in the very near future, although difficulties still need to be overcome on certain political aspects of the agreement.

Syria is the only Euro-Mediterranean partner which has not yet concluded an Association Agreement with the European Union. Once an Agreement has been signed, ratified and entered into force, it would complete the network of Association Agreements concluded with the Euro-Mediterranean partners, who are members of the Barcelona Process, bringing us closer to shaping the Euro-Mediterranean area envisaged in the Barcelona Declaration.

In comparison to the existing Co-operation Agreement from 1977, this Euro-Mediterranean Agreement would considerably enhance bilateral relations between the European Union and Syria. As with all of the Union’s Association Agreements, it would cover cooperation in a broad number of areas, political, economic, social and cultural. It should lay the basis for economic and political reform and prepare Syria for integration into the world economy.

An agreement would also establish an appropriate framework for conducting a regular political dialogue with Syria. The standard provision on respect for the democratic principles and fundamental human rights established by the Universal Declaration of Human Rights would be an essential element of the Agreement. In addition, cooperation on counter-terrorism would be provided for. In line with the Council’s decision of 17 November 2003, it would also include, as another essential element, a clause providing for cooperation in countering the proliferation of weapons of mass destruction and their means of delivery. All these elements would lay the basis for engaging in a real dialogue.

On that basis this agreement would foster the political, economic and social development of Syria, as well as encourage cooperation both within the Euro-Mediterranean framework and at the sub-regional level.
