Questions to the Council
Question no 14 by Anna Karamanou (H-0288/00)
 Subject: Albanian prostitution rings in Europe
 

The French newspaper 'Le Monde' recently featured a report stating that Albanian 'white-slavers' had taken advantage of the war in Kosovo and the illegal immigration networks operating between Albania and Italy and were sexually exploiting some three hundred thousand women - mostly from Kosovo, but also from Moldavia, Romania and Bosnia - in France, Germany and Belgium. The women are forced into prostitution by threats and violence, and the Albanian pimps often pretend to be Kosovars in order to acquire refugee status. Will the Council say what measures it is taking and what policies it is promoting to combat this phenomenon, whether it has any data on the increased movement of women from Eastern European countries, what results have been achieved by various programmes and what is being done to promote the harmonisation of the penal legislation of the Member States and to coordinate efforts to combat the trade in women?

 
  
 

1. On 1 January, the Council and the European Parliament adopted a Community action programme on preventive measures for combating violence against women, known as the Daphne programme 2000-2003. This programme, based on Article 152 of the Treaty establishing the European Community, is another measure to be added to the series of measures that the Council has adopted since 1996 to combat the sexual exploitation of and the trafficking in human beings. Most notably, the Council has extended its definition of the crime of ‘trafficking in human beings’ in the Europol Convention, to include the exploitation of prostitution, other forms of sexual exploitation and the production, sale and distribution of child pornography.

2. With regard to cooperation with third countries, I would draw the honourable Member’s attention to the 1950 United Nations Convention, designed to suppress trafficking in human beings and the exploitation of prostitution. I must also point out that the International Organisation for Migration, based in Geneva, is extremely active in the prevention of trafficking in women and of violence against women throughout the world, including Kosovo.

3. The Council is fully aware of the problems the honourable Member has raised and of the need to take account of the extremely vulnerable position of victims and witnesses. Progress has been made in the Member States in reviewing current legislation and practices and in classifying these forms of behaviour as crimes, punishable by sentences which are specific, proportionate and which will act as a deterrent. It is hoped that the Convention on Mutual Assistance in Criminal Matters, which is due to be signed shortly, will be a factor in making further progress.

4. The future stabilisation of the situation in the Balkans will contribute towards resolving problems connected to trafficking in women, to their sexual exploitation and to their illegal immigration.

5. The Member States and the European Commission are playing an active role in the negotiations under way at the United Nations for a protocol on the prevention, suppression and punishment of trafficking in human beings, particularly of women and children, which will complement the United Nations Convention against Transnational Organised Crime.

 

Question no 15 by Gorka Knörr Borràs (H-0290/00)
 Subject: A Macedonian-based Party unconstitutional
 

On 29 February, the Constitutional Court in Bulgaria declared a Macedonian-based political party, OMO 'Ilinden' – PIRIN – unconstitutional. This party was registered in 1999 and took part in the municipal elections in October last year, when it elected five local officials. This decision could lead to an effective ban of the party, deprivation of its juridical person status, confiscation of its property and the impossibility to take part in elections. This party has always declared that it would respect the constitutional and legal system of Bulgaria and carry out activities peacefully.

Is the Council of the opinion that this decision violates the right to freedom of association and constitutes a discrimination against a national minority? Has the Council asked for any explanation to the Bulgarian authorities? If not, is the Council going to intervene?

Does the Council consider that such a decision is compatible with the respect of the democratic criteria established in Copenhagen?

 
  
 

The Council has been informed of the decision of the Bulgarian Constitutional Court to declare the party OMO ‘Ilinden’, PIRIN, unconstitutional. The official translation of the text is not yet available, however. The Council further noted the decision by Mr Singartiiski, the chairman of the political party concerned, to appeal against the decision of the Constitutional Court before the European Court of Human Rights, which has specific jurisdiction in the matter.

At the present time, for its own part, the Council can only reiterate the need for candidate countries to comply with all the political and economic Copenhagen criteria for accession to the European Union, and repeat its intention to continually monitor the way in which candidate countries apply these criteria and to take appropriate action should it consider that the principles of democracy and respect for human rights and minority rights have been contravened.

In this context, it should also be pointed out that the Commission will take all the political events of the previous year into account when drawing up its next periodic report, which will be presented to the Council, on the progress which Bulgaria has made in the pre-accession process.

 

Question no 16 by Karin Riis-Jørgensen (H-0296/00)
 Subject: Cooperation with Austria
 

Further to the Council's very clear answer to my question at the part-session in March 2000, in which the President of the Council confirmed that Austria is a full member of the EU and continues to take part on an equal footing in EU cooperation, will the Council say why the Austrian Chancellor had to meet the Presidency in Brussels prior to the Lisbon Summit instead of the customary practice of the President of the Council visiting the capital city of the Member State?

If words are to be followed by action and Austria is to be treated as an equal in EU cooperation, the President must travel to Vienna himself prior to the next summit. Can the Presidency give an assurance that it will abide by the rules hitherto applied in EU cooperation?

 
  
 

In response to Mrs Riis-Jørgensen’s (also very clear) question, it is important to point out that the arrangements for any bilateral contact are, by definition, jointly agreed between the two interested parties, in this instance the Portuguese Presidency and the Austrian Government. Indeed, this meeting was no exception to the rule.

It must, however, be stressed that the government of the Member State holding the presidency is responsible for setting its own agenda and may organise the work for this six-month period according to its own priorities and other priorities identified in prior discussions with the other Member States.

The Council is not, therefore, in a position to provide the guarantee requested by the honourable Member in her question because there are no set rules on the subject.

 

Question no 17 by Giuseppe Di Lello Finuoli (H-0300/00)
 Subject: Peace process and aid to Colombia
 

Colombia has submitted a proposal – known as the ‘Colombia Plan’ – concerning a contribution from the European Union to the Colombian Government’s efforts to secure a peace agreement with FARC and ELN, the guerrilla organisations in that country.

The United States is about to grant about USD 1.6 million in military aid to Colombia, principally for campaigns in the south of the country, where small-scale coca growers and FARC operate, but not in the north, where, according to the DEA, paramilitary groups and the local mafia gangs process the drug and ship large quantities of it to the United States and Europe.

In giving significant aid, too, is not the European Union running the risk that, as was the case in Kosovo, it will be paying to repair the damage caused by the American war machine, the US having important strategic interests in the region? What guidelines will the EU lay down for the aid it provides to Colombia? Will the aid be managed jointly by the two negotiating parties? To what conditions, in terms of institutional and social reforms by the Colombians, will it be subject, with a view to its contributing to a lasting peace? What precautionary measures will be taken to ring-fence it so that it will make a genuine contribution to peace?

 
  
 

The European Union has, on several occasions, stated to the democratically elected authorities of Colombia its intention to support, according to its means and using the most appropriate methods, efforts to secure a peace agreement based on respect for human rights and fundamental freedoms.

The European Union is closely monitoring developments regarding the ‘Colombia Plan’ drawn up by the Colombian Government. It has also noted, in particular, the decision by the House of Representatives of the United States Congress on 29 March to grant Colombia USD 1 700 million in additional aid, intended, among other things, for the purchase of equipment to combat drugs and militarisation.

To date, the European Union has received no specific request from the Colombian Government to fund actual projects. When this happens, and this is the case throughout the world, the European Union will respond and will ensure that European aid makes a contribution to the observance and promotion of human rights, which are a key element in cooperation with third countries. The European Union may, if necessary, offer Colombia aid in areas in which it has extensive experience, such as the reform of the State and democratic institutions, the protection of human rights and economic reform.

Question no 19 by Antonios Trakatellis (H-0308/00)
 Subject: Human rights and the impending trial of thirteen imprisoned Iranian Jews in Iran
 

More than a year has passed since the Iranian authorities imprisoned thirteen Iranian Jews without trial on charges of espionage. According to recent reports, most of them are not being allowed a free choice of their defence ahead of their trial, which is scheduled to open on 13 April. The thirteen prisoners have suffered abuse and have also been subjected to degrading treatment on a daily basis.

What action can the European Union take, in the context of its 'critical dialogue' with Iran, to secure a fair and open trial, and the possibility of free and unhindered preparations for the accused? Will the Council request access for international observers to monitor the proceedings, as called for by Parliament in its resolution R5-0029/1999?

In view of the fact that the death penalty continues to apply in Iran and that many prisoners on the same charges were executed in 1998, what steps will the Union take to secure the abolition of the death penalty?

 
  
 

1) The European Union has raised the matter with the Iranian authorities on several occasions. The same message has been transmitted in discussions between Member States of the EU and Iran. The Iranian authorities, including President Khatami, have stated that the accused will get a fair and open trial.

2) The trial is about to begin (13 April) and the European Union will continue to follow the situation closely to ensure that the Iranian authorities indeed live up to the commitment that they gave for a fair and open trial.

3) The position of the European Union on capital punishment is clear: the EU is against the death penalty as a matter of principle, regardless of who the defendant is and what the offence may be. This position has been made clear to Iran within the framework of our constructive dialogue.

 

Question no 20 by Brian Crowley (H-0312/00)
 Subject: Coordinating the activities of police forces in the applicant countries with Europol
 

Will the Council indicate what new initiatives are proposed for the coordination of the activities of the police forces in the applicant countries with the system of cooperation in Europol?

 
  
 

In respect of the coordination between the accession countries and Europol, may I draw the honourable Member's attention to the following activities undertaken within the framework of the Council:

Article 42 of the Europol Convention provides that in so far as it is relevant for the performance of its tasks, Europol shall establish and maintain relations with third countries. The Council has adopted a number of legal instruments in which the rules applicable to these relations are laid down. Those instruments are:

a) the Council Act of 3 November 1998 laying down rules concerning the receipt by Europol of information from third parties;

b) the Council Act of 3 November 1999 laying down rules governing Europol's external relations with third States and non-European Union related bodies; and

c) the Council Act of 12 March 1999 adopting the rules governing the transmission of personal data by Europol to third countries and third bodies.

On the basis of these instruments the Council, at its meeting of 27 March 2000, authorised the Director of Europol to start negotiations in order to establish formal relations with a number of third countries and bodies.

The accession countries are among those third countries listed in the aforementioned authorisation. In the annexed declaration the Council calls upon the Director of Europol to give top priority to negotiations with the accession countries.

 

Question no 21 by Bernd Posselt (H-0316/00)
 Subject: EU and Croatia
 

What progress has been reached in negotiations with Croatia and what is the timetable for future negotiations between the EU and Croatia?

 
  
 

I am glad to be able at last to give a very positive report to the honourable Member, who, as we all know, has followed developments in EU-Croatia relations very closely over the last few years. Let me just underline that the change of policy on the part of the Union towards Croatia has been triggered by the recent political change in Croatia following democratic elections, and the firm commitment of the new Croatian leadership to push ahead with political and economic reform, and to respect fully its international commitments. We think that Croatia can become a catalyst for the region, and the Union has already taken very concrete steps to show its support for the new approach in Zagreb.

Hence, following the positive outcome of the free and fair elections in early January 2000, the Council, on 26 January and 14 February, when the new Croatian Prime Minister, Mr Racan, met EU Ministers, decided:

1) to invite the Commission to prepare a study on the feasibility of opening negotiations with Croatia on a Stabilisation and Association Agreement;

2) to put in place rapidly a joint EU-Croatia Consultative Task Force which will provide Croatia with expertise and technical assistance in all areas of common interest;

3) to send the Ministerial Troika to Zagreb as a further sign of support for the new leadership - the Troika was in Zagreb on 29 March and came back with good news on plans for the reform of election laws, the media, the return of refugees and cooperation with the War Crimes Tribunal.

In conclusion, EU-Croatia relations are presently being substantially upgraded, which is what both sides want. If the Commission's study, which is expected to be presented to the June GAC is positive, the Council will ask the Commission to prepare a mandate for negotiating a Stabilisation and Association Agreement with Croatia.

 

Question no 22 by Gerard Collins (H-0319/00)
 Subject: Promoting social inclusion
 

At the recent summit in Lisbon, the Council committed itself to combating social exclusion. Given the large numbers of people, in both rural and urban areas, who are living close to poverty, perhaps because their skills are no longer required, what specific measures would the Council envisage to facilitate the full integration of the socially excluded into the workforce and to allow them to participate fully in the advantages of a knowledge-based society?

 
  
 

As the honourable Member pointed out, Heads of State or Government meeting in Lisbon on 23 and 24 March 2000 undertook to combat social exclusion. In the presidency conclusions various specific measures were cited, and, in particular, the following objectives were set:

- a substantial annual increase in per capita investment and human resources;

- the number of 18-24 year-olds within lower-secondary level education who are not in further education and training should be halved by 2010;

- Internet access for all schools and training centres;

- a European framework should define the new basic skills to be provided through life-long learning: IT skills, foreign languages, technological culture, entrepreneurship and social skills;

- promoting digital literacy throughout the Union;

- defining the means for fostering the mobility of students, teachers and training and research staff;

- developing a common European format for curricula vitae.

With regard to fighting social exclusion, the European Council deemed ‘the number of people living below the poverty line and in social exclusion in the Union unacceptable’; for that reason, the Council provided for steps to be taken to make a decisive impact on the eradication of poverty by setting ‘adequate targets to be agreed by the Council by the end of the year’. The Council further observed that ‘the best safeguard against social exclusion is a job’, and highlighted the need to ‘improve skills, promote wider access to knowledge and opportunity and fight unemployment’. Finally, the European Council believed that ‘policies for combating social exclusion should be based on an open method of coordination combining national action plans and a Commission initiative for cooperation in this field to be presented by June 2000’.

In particular, the European Council invited the Council and the Commission to:

- promote a better understanding of social exclusion through continued dialogue and exchanges of information and best practice, on the basis of commonly agreed indicators;

- mainstream the promotion of inclusion in Member States’ employment, education and training, health and housing policies;

- develop priority actions addressed to specific target groups (for example, minority groups, children, the elderly and the disabled).

Finally, the Council ‘will pursue its reflection on the future direction of social policy on the basis of the Commission communication, with a view to reaching agreement on a European social agenda at the Nice European Council in December, including the initiatives of the different partners involved’.

 

Question no 23 by Niall Andrews (H-0326/00)
 Subject: Preventing arms sales to third countries
 

In May 1999, the Development Council adopted a resolution in which it confirmed its sustained interest in fulfilling the European Union’s commitments to make a contribution to combating the destabilising accumulation and spread of small arms and light weapons, pointing out that a lack of control has fuelled interstate conflict and constituted an obstacle to peaceful economic and social development. Furthermore, the Council made several recommendations relating to development cooperation, such as the inclusion of the small arms issue in the political dialogue with the ACP and the need to reverse the ‘cultures of violence’. Will the Council indicate what is happening with regard to the review it promised to undertake in its resolution and how is it ensuring that EU rules are not circumvented in the export of arms to third countries?

 
  
 

1. I can confirm to the honourable Member that the Council remains extremely concerned about the destabilising accumulation and proliferation of small arms and light weapons. As the future agreement with the ACP countries, on which negotiations have been completed, provides for enhanced dialogue with these countries, the issue of small arms and light weapons could be part of the political dialogue with the ACP. I can confirm that the Council intends to review the progress made in implementing the Development Council Resolution adopted on 21 May 1999, on the basis of the Commission's report on the issue of peace building and conflict prevention as soon as this report is available.

2. As the honourable Member is aware, the EU Code of Conduct on arms exports was adopted on 8 June 1998 with a view to setting high common standards to be regarded as the minimum for the management of, and restraint in, conventional arms transfers by all Member States, and strengthening the exchange of relevant information between them with a view to achieving greater transparency.

Control of the end-use of arms delivered is provided for in Criterion Seven of the Code. A number of points are set out which Member States are to consider when assessing the risk that exported goods might be diverted to an undesirable end-user. The implementation of arms export policies, and therefore of the Code, remain the responsibility of the Member States.

Question no 26 by Pernille Frahm (H-0337/00)
 Subject: EMU
 

Can the Council confirm that EMU can only be changed through amendment of the Treaty, supported unanimously by all Member States, and not at meetings of the governing board of the Central Bank?

Joint Answer

According to Article 48 of the Treaty on European Union, it is for the governments of Member States to determine by common accord the amendments to be made to the Treaty on which the Union is founded, subject to ratification by all Member States in accordance with their respective constitutional requirements.

The EC Treaty provisions on Economic and Monetary Union obey the same rules as any other Treaty provision. Accordingly, and as mentioned above, amendments to the Treaty provisions on EMU can only be made by common accord of the governments of Member States and need to be ratified in accordance with their respective constitutional requirements.

Question no 28 by Ioannis Marinos (H-0338/00)
 Subject: Use of all the official languages of the EU
 

The Guardian newspaper reported on 10 March 2000 that Spain had complained at the fact that the EU's Kosovo reconstruction agency based in Thessaloniki uses only French, English and German and not the other eight official EU languages.

Will the Council say why the three languages in question are being privileged in this way, what the legal basis for this discrimination is, whether Greece has reacted officially (since Greek is not used in the above agency, even though it is based on Greek soil) and what measures it intends to take to prevent any undermining of the fundamental right of every citizen of the Union to communicate with the decentralised EU institutions and agencies in his or her mother tongue?

 
  
 

In replying to the honourable Member's question, I would like to recall that the European Reconstruction Agency was set up by Council Regulation No 2454/1999 of 15 November 1999, the so-called ‘OBNOVA’ Regulation dealing with EC assistance to South-Eastern Europe.

Article 17 of the Regulation sets up a Governing Board, which consists of one representative from each Member State and two from the Commission. The Governing Board is chaired by the Commission and takes decisions by two-thirds majority. The President does not vote.

As far as the language arrangements for the Agency are concerned, these are, however, according to Article 17, decided unanimously by the Board.

Question no 81 by Gerard Collins (H-0320/00)
 Subject: Public access to the Internet at affordable prices
 

The Commission has recently issued a communication on Electronic Communications (COM (1999) 539). In the context of ensuring general public access to the Internet at affordable prices, can the Commission state whether it would support a universal service obligation with a view to ensuring the establishment of adequate infrastructure in order to allow the more peripheral regions of the Community to derive equal benefit from the new technologies?

 
  
 

The Commission attaches the utmost importance to the inclusiveness of the Information Society.

The current legal scope of universal service includes the possibility of Internet access in terms of dial-up access over the telephone line. At the moment, this is the most common access method for individuals and households. In addition to the cost of the telephone call, this involves using an Internet Service Provider (in most Member States free Services are offered).

This guarantee is in all regions as universal service covers the whole of the national territory. In most Member States (all except Finland) the telephone tariffs are common within a country.

Prices for using the Internet are continuing to decrease, irrespective of the type of user group and geographic location, due to liberalisation of telecommunications markets. Although there may be more choice of Internet Service Providers (ISPs) in urban and denser populated areas, the bigger operators and Internet Service Providers offer nationwide service. Fierce competition in urban areas puts pressure on incumbent operators and leads to lower prices nationwide.

In January 2000, the Commission launched a public consultation (based on COM (99) 539) on all aspects of telecommunications regulations including universal service. The consultation has revealed that there is a broad support for the Commission’s proposal not to extend for the moment the current scope of universal service to broadband Internet access. This extension would, at the present point of market development, risk constituting a cross-subsidy from ordinary telephone users to the early adopters of broadband technologies, who usually tend to be wealthier.

The Commission will continue to monitor and review this scope of universal service so that the concept keeps pace with market and technological developments. The Commission intends to include a specific review clause in the new legal framework.

Question no 93 by Inger Schörling (H-0349/00)
 Subject: MTBE in petrol
 

A debate is taking place in the USA on MTBE, a substance which is added to petrol and which has polluted groundwater, in particular in California. It is added to petrol everywhere and began to be used as an octane booster when lead-free petrol was introduced. Under Swedish rules, petrol may contain 15% MTBE, but on average it contains 2-3%. The problem with MTBE and closely related ETBE is that they take longer to break down in water than the corresponding alcohols, methanol and ethanol. MTBE and ETBE, like ethanol and methanol, have been proposed as low-level petrol additives as a first step towards introducing renewable fuels into our petrol tanks without the need to change engines.

Is the Commission aware of the increasing use of MTBE and ETBE and their relatively little known impact on the environment? Does it intend to regulate the quantity of these substances permitted in petrol?

 
  
 

The Commission is well aware of the political and public concern about the contamination of water supplies in the United States and recent statements by the US EPA and will keep abreast of developments.

MTBE is on the Third Priority List, published in 1997, of priority substances for assessment, as foreseen under Council Regulation 793/93 on the evaluation and control of the risks of existing substances(1)1 (Commission Regulation (EC) 143/97)(2)2. Finland is the Member State ‘Rapporteur’ responsible for the assessment of the substance, but all Member States are represented in the competent Group of Experts which discusses and finalises the risk assessment report for MTBE. The Community risk assessment will address the possible environmental and human health concerns in the Community, taking into account the US findings. When the risk assessment report is finalised, it will be presented to the Scientific Committee for Toxicity, Eco-toxicity and the Environment for independent peer-review. Risk reduction measures will be recommended in those areas where concerns have been indicated and legislative measures taken, if appropriate.

In August 1999, the Commission asked Member States for information on whether they have encountered any problems with (water) pollution due to MTBE. Once such information is available from a number of Member States, the Commission will provide complementary information to the MEP.

MTBE is a blending component (oxygenate) used in petrol to varying degrees by the Member States. In this context, it should be pointed out that there is a fundamental difference between American and Community legislation. EU Directive 98/70 relating to the quality of petrol and diesel(3) 3 allows a maximum oxygen content in petrol of 2.7% by volume (which equates to a maximum MTBE content of 15%), whilst not setting a minimum content. The American legislation, on the other hand, requires a minimum oxygen content of 2% - which equates to a minimum MTBE content of 11% - in certain geographic areas. This minimum oxygen content has given rise to a much higher MTBE percentage in petrol in some parts of the United States, as in California, than is generally the case in the Community.

MTBE is the most commonly used ether. By contrast, ETBE has found limited use.
