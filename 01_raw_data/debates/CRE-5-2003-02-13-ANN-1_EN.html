QUESTIONS TO THE COMMISSION
Question no 48 by Claude Moraes (H-0028/03)
 Subject: National action plans against racism
 

One of the key recommendations at the third UN World Conference Against Racism held in Durban, South Africa, in September 2001 and of the Commission's contribution to those discussions was that states should develop, in consultation with non-governmental organisations, national action plans to mainstream the fight against racism into all relevant policies and programmes. Stemming from the European NGOs present, the idea was supported by the European Union and the governments of Member States.

What specific plans are envisaged for the EU in monitoring the progress of the Member States party to the conference in the identification and implementation of such national strategies, as well as for maintaining constructive dialogue on the relevant issues with Member States?

 
  
 

(EN) As the Honourable Member rightly points out, the Programme of Action adopted at the United Nations (UN) World Conference Against Racism includes a recommendation that States should establish and implement national action plans to fight against racism, racial discrimination, xenophobia and intolerance.

The main responsibility for monitoring these national action plans falls upon the newly-created anti-discrimination unit within the Office of the UN's High Commissioner for Human Rights. At regional level, it has been agreed that the Council of Europe is the most appropriate forum to discuss the follow-up to the World Conference, as was the case with the preparatory process.

Action taken at Union level to combat racism and racial discrimination certainly complements and reinforces the efforts of individual Member States in this field. The Commission has provided the UN High Commissioner's Office with written information on relevant Union initiatives. (A copy of this information will be sent directly to the Honourable Member.) The Commission has also participated actively in discussions in the Council of Europe framework concerning follow-up the World Conference.

The Commission will continue to engage in a constructive dialogue with the Office of the High Commissioner for Human Rights, the Council of Europe and other actors involved in the follow-up process.

Question no 50 by Christa Prets (H-0040/03)
 Subject: Commission proposal for a directive on discrimination based on sex (Article 13)
 

In autumn 2002 I was appointed rapporteur for the Committee on Women's Rights and Equal Opportunities on the Commission proposal for a new directive on discrimination based on sex (Article 13). This directive is urgently needed as an effective measure by which to achieve equal opportunities between men and women in areas other than employment. I would therefore ask you to explain to the members of the Committee on Women's Rights and Equal Opportunities and myself why the Commission has taken so long to draw up this document (the report was scheduled for publication in September 2002), and when we can expect to have the final version of this report.

 
  
 

(EN) A wide-ranging consultation with different stakeholders on the issue of the possible scope of a directive based on Article 13 of the EC Treaty to fight discrimination on the basis of sex has been launched. Since a comprehensive legal framework in the fields of employment and occupation already exists, the consultations are aimed at exploring possible domains to be covered. These are currently being discussed at all levels with the different services of the Commission concerned by them.

The Commission services are equally following current work in the framework of the Convention on the future of Europe. Whether or not to incorporate the Charter of Fundamental Rights into the Treaties is one of the topics for discussion and there seems to be a strong tendency favouring this incorporation. In such a case, careful attention should be paid to the legal effects of the Charter's article on equality between men and women.

In the light of all these elements, the best approach to follow, in particular as to the added value of a proposal in this field, will have to be decided on. Taking decisions which could be counterproductive to the overall area of equal opportunities must be avoided.

Concerning in particular the prevention of domestic violence, a communication on strengthening the coherence of action at Member State and European level aimed at preventing and eliminating domestic violence is due to be finalised in the near future.

 

Question no 51 by María Rodríguez Ramos (H-0896/02)
 Subject: Increase in the number of cases of BSE in Spain
 

As the Commission pointed out in its answer to Oral Question H-0827/02(1), Spain is one of the Member States of the EU where the number of cases of BSE has increased, contrary to the general trend.

In view of this, how is it possible that in the context of the 2002 surveillance programme to monitor transmissible spongiform encephalopathies the Commission decided to reduce the allocation for Spain from EUR 11.24 million to EUR 8 369 625, although the objectives for 2002 will not have been achieved?

Would it not be more logical to oblige the Member States to abide by a strict surveillance plan to ensure that consumers need have no doubts about the resources and determination directed towards combating this disease?

 
  
 

(EN) Since the beginning of 2001, all Member States are carrying out systematic bovine spongiform encephalopathy (BSE) testing of bovine animals. The testing programme covers all bovine animals over 30 months of age slaughtered for human consumption, all bovine animals over 24 months of age subject to special emergency slaughter or slaughtered under specific conditions because of general disease symptoms, and all dead-on-farm bovine animals over 24 months of age. The testing programme was reviewed last year but only minor technical changes were made to the programme.

The objectives of the testing programme are to provide an accurate picture of the BSE situation in the Member States and to prevent bovine animals in the clinical phase of the disease from entering the food or feed chain. Those objectives were achieved in 2002. The testing programme will in itself not lead to a reduction in the number of new BSE cases. Other measures, such as destruction of specified risk material and the feed ban are in place to prevent new animals from becoming infected, thus reducing the number of BSE cases. However due to the long incubation period of the disease that reduction will only occur four to six years after the implementation of the measures.

Current legislation does not stipulate that the BSE testing programme shall be co-financed by the Community. The legislation does however provide for the possibility of a financial contribution of the Community, within the limits of the appropriate budget line.

Since 2001, the Community has participated in the costs of the test kits used in the monitoring programme. Originally the financial participation was limited to EUR 30 per test, which was reduced to EUR 15 per test after six months. The maximum financial participation was again reduced to EUR 10.5 per test in 2003. The reason for decreasing the maximum contribution per test is that the average cost per test is lower than originally estimated, around EUR 18 per test, and still decreasing.

The reductions in the maximum amounts allocated to each Member State for 2003 are a consequence of the reduction in the amount allocated to each test. The total number of tests being co-financed remains unchanged.

 
 

(1) Written answer of 17.12.2002.

 

Question no 52 by Brian Crowley (H-0905/02)
 Subject: Recognition of professional qualifications
 

Will the Commission explain why in its proposal for a directive on the recognition of professional qualifications it is calling for the elimination of the expert advisory committees which can provide invaluable and unique advice to the Commission on the wide range of issues relating to the recognition of professional qualifications?

 
  
 

(EN) As the Honourable Member points out, the Explanatory Memorandum to the Commission proposal for a Directive on the recognition of professional qualifications explains the intention of the Commission to introduce a new, flexible and modern method of consultation to replace the advisory committees operating alongside the sectoral directives. This intention has been further explained in the letter of the Commissioner in charge of the Internal Market of 30 September 2002 addressed to Parliament and Council. This mechanism is designed to ensure the regular exchange of information and ideas between the Commission and the professions and educational establishments most directly concerned with the sectoral directives. It will ensure that these interested parties have the possibility of presenting their ideas to an expert group of national government representatives and the Commission. This function has previously been performed by the advisory committees. In a Joint Declaration of July 2002 all of the European professional associations concerned indicated support for a consultation method along these lines.

Question no 54 by Seán Ó Neachtain (H-0909/02)
 Subject: Developing the EU's cultural dimension in 2003
 

Apart from the adoption of the Decision of the European Parliament and the Council establishing a programme for the enhancement of quality in higher education and the promotion of intercultural understanding through cooperation with third countries 'ERASMUS WORLD', the Commission has not indicated its intention to bring forward any worthwhile initiatives in 2003 aimed at helping the Member States to develop their cultural dimension, which includes not only art and literature but also languages and music. Will the Commission bring forward an initiative for 2003 aimed at supporting the Member States’ efforts in the cultural field, including the interests of the 10 enlargement countries, and will the Commission support cultural exchanges between secondary schools throughout the EU and the enlargement countries?

 
  
 

(FR) The aim of the Culture 2000 programme is, in accordance with Article 151 of the EC Treaty, to support Member States' actions to develop European cultural cooperation. Every year, support is granted to hundreds of cooperation projects throughout Europe, involving thousands of participants from 30 different countries (including 12 applicant countries).

This programme will come to an end on 31 December 2004. The Commission is currently preparing initiatives aimed at ensuring the continuity of the Community's action in this field. In 2003, it will submit a proposal for a Council and Parliament decision extending the present programme for the period 2005-2006, and will, in parallel, also prepare a proposal for a new programme for the following years.

The Community's actions in the sphere of culture cover an extremely wide range of fields: its education, research, employment and regional policies, as well as its external actions (to cite only some of the areas concerned), are involved by means of numerous instruments in the development of the cultural dimension in Europe.

For instance, the proposal for a decision establishing the Erasmus World programme constitutes a large-scale initiative for the promotion of the Union's culture through the opening-up of higher education in the Union to third countries. The extension of the Tempus programme to the southern and eastern Mediterranean countries, proposed in February 2002 and in force since June of that year, is already contributing to better mutual understanding between the cultures of a region which is of crucial importance for the Union.

In the context of the Community's Socrates programme for European cooperation in the field of education, the Comenius scheme proposes Community support for educational partnerships. The Socrates programme was gradually opened up to the applicant countries over its first phase (1995-1999). The decision establishing its second phase makes express reference to the participation of all the applicant countries. Educational partnerships under Comenius are open to all schools, from nursery to secondary level. The programme's objectives are: a) improving the quality of schooling and developing its European dimension; b) the learning of languages; and c) promoting the intercultural dimension of schooling in Europe. The educational partnerships, which may be either multilateral or bilateral, will enable schools to achieve those objectives thanks to financial support for transnational cooperation projects, including joint educational activities in the participating establishments combined with inter-establishment mobility for the teachers and pupils concerned.

 

Question no 55 by Gerard Collins (H-0003/03)
 Subject: Ratification of EU-South Africa Trade and Development Cooperation Agreement
 

By July 2002 only six Member States had ratified the EU-South Africa Trade and Development Cooperation Agreement. The other Member States were in the process of ratifying the Agreement. Can the Commission provide up-to-date information on the ratification process and does it consider that failure to ratify the TDCA has hindered the implementation of its provisions?

 
  
 

(EN) On 17 January 2003 only seven Member States had deposited ratification instruments of the Trade Development and Cooperation Agreement (TDCA) with the Council: Germany, Denmark, Spain, Ireland, the Netherlands, Sweden and Finland. A few other Member States, Belgium, France and the United Kingdom, have informed the Commission that the process is well advanced.

The parties to the Agreement have agreed that pending ratification certain specific sections of the TDCA would provisionally apply. These include the provisions on development cooperation and on trade liberalisation.

Implementation of both these areas has been very successful. More recently, both sides have entered into structured political dialogue on the basis of article 8 of the Cotonou Agreement.

Fruitful cooperation is taking place in a number of other fields such as statistics, phyto-sanitary measures, etc. There is no doubt, however, that ratification would provide the appropriate legal framework for full cooperation in all other areas mentioned in the Agreement: economic cooperation, cooperation in certain trade related areas, and cooperation in other fields such as the environment, culture, human resources, social issues, media and information, the fight against drugs and money laundering.

 

Question no 56 by Niall Andrews (H-0005/03)
 Subject: Cooperation between the customs authorities
 

What new initiatives does the Commission consider could be usefully taken to strengthen and develop cooperation between the customs authorities in the Member States, taking into account the initiative by three Member States to amend the Convention on the use of information technology for customs purposes as regards the creation of a customs files identification database?

 
  
 

(FR) The Commission has expressed its full support for the initiative of France, Germany and Belgium concerning a draft protocol amending the convention on the use of information technology for customs purposes and the creation of a customs files identification database. Such a database would certainly be a useful complement to the signalling/display database already existing under the centralised Third Pillar Customs Identification System (CIS).

Nonetheless, the Commission, which is responsible for managing the technical infrastructure of the CIS (first and third pillars), has always considered that, in the absence of additional human resources, having the CIS up and running must be a prior condition for the development by its services of any new applications, such as the customs files identification database (CFID).

As the CIS will be implemented as of 24 March 2003, the Commission can now envisage a feasibility study on the technical development of the CFID (third pillar), and is now also in a position to take legal measures under the first pillar to complement the initiative of the three above-mentioned Member States with a view to covering all illegal activities in the customs area.

With this in mind, the Commission has included in its programme of work for 2003 a legislative proposal amending Council Regulation (EC) No 515/97 of 13 March 1997 on mutual assistance between the administrative authorities of the Member States and cooperation between the latter and the Commission to ensure the correct application of the law on customs and agricultural matters(1).

A further objective of this proposal for amendment is to complement the existing signalling database and the proposed identification database (both under the CIS system), in particular by introducing databases for strategic analysis (of fraudulent tendencies) and profiling-risk analysis for frauds and irregularities relating to customs. This information, with additions where relevant by OLAF, would be made available to the Member States; under certain conditions, other bodies such as Europol and Eurojust could also have access. The aim is to achieve substantially greater efficiency in customs cooperation.

The Commission further stresses that efficient cooperation is at present impaired by the fact that customs cooperation is split between the first and third pillars and, therefore, between two different legal frameworks. The need to operate two separate (and similar) IT applications for the first and third pillars entails an additional cost of approximately 30% to OLAF's budget for anti-fraud IT tools in the customs field.

The Commission hopes that the Convention on the Future of Europe will examine the possibility of more closely linking the intergovernmental and the Community modes of customs cooperation.

 
 

(1) OJ L 82, 22.3.1997

 

Question no 57 by James (Jim) Fitzsimons (H-0007/03)
 Subject: Annual Young Consumer Competition
 

As the Commission pointed out in its reply to my question on ‘consumer strategy’ (H-0378/02(1)), a European Young Consumer Competition has been held each year since 1993 in which 1200 schools have participated including, since 2001, a total of 56 Irish schools.

Will the Commission provide an overall assessment of this Euro Competition, outlining the trends it has noticed over the last ten years and will it state what it hopes to achieve in 2003 in relation to young consumers and potentially harmful product advertising?

 
  
 

(EN) Directorate General Health and Consumer Protection has organised the European Young Consumer Contest (EYCC) involving all Member States every school year since 1993 (except for 1997-1998), with the help of several private contractors. The objective is to encourage young people to become more aware of consumer affairs and become responsible consumers, by getting them to work on different consumer-linked themes under adult guidance. The contests have taken place around different themes (nutrition, sustainable development, the Euro, etc.).

Before launching the 2002-2003 contest, however, given the limited number of participating schools in 2001/2002 (less than 2000), the Commission has sought to determine whether certain elements of the contest should be modified, or whether it had to be completely revamped or possibly abandoned altogether if no alternative solutions could be found to improve the cost/efficiency ratio for the contest.

The overall conclusion of the internal appraisal was that the European Young Consumer Contest is an action that should be continued, provided one can improve its cost/efficiency ratio. The Commission has found, in particular, that the increase in the budget did not correspond to an equivalent increase in the number of schools participating and that the costs of personnel and administration were particularly high.

Therefore, in order to try and increase the teachers’ interest for the competition and reduce the overall cost, the Commission has asked the European Schoolnet (EUN), a joint venture of twenty-three Ministries of Education from around Europe, to organise the 2002/2003 EYCC via the Internet. The two themes for this year are :

- How to make labelling more user-friendly?

- How to promote a healthy lifestyle through a balanced diet?

Potentially harmful product advertising could be considered as a possible theme for the next issue of the contest, provided of course that the interest for the contest among the tens of thousands of schools in the Union is maintained.

 
 

(1) Written answer, 11.6.2002.

 

Question no 58 by Nuala Ahern (H-0010/03)
 Subject: Structural integrity safety assessments
 

In correspondence dated 19 December 2002, and made public last month, between the UK minister for energy (Brian Wilson) and the UK minister for employment safety ( Ian McCartney) it is revealed that in 2001 BNFL conducted two safety assessments on the structural integrity of its building B30 at Sellafield, which stores long-lived, high activity radioactive waste (HLW).

The UK Government has refused to make public these reports on the grounds of 'national security', and is only prepared to make public a censored version of the reports 'excluding information that is of a nature that cannot be disclosed for security reasons.'

Have the Commission and Euratom nuclear safety authorities been provided with the full reports? And will the Commission send a safety evaluation team to Sellafield to assess whether the B30 HLW storage building presents any environmental and health threat to Ireland and the people of northern England?

 
  
 

(EN) The Commission has not received the report on the structural integrity of the B30 building at Sellafield. Safety assessments of nuclear installations are the responsibility of the operator and of the national competent authorities and it is in principle for the Member States to decide whether or not to give public access to the relevant information in accordance with their national legislation.

Article 37 of the Euratom Treaty allows for the Commission to assess the environmental impact of a nuclear installation on another Member State. On the basis of this Article of the Treaty the Commission has assessed in the past the possible impact of many installations at Sellafield. This procedure, however, cannot be applied to those installations that already existed prior to the accession of the United Kingdom to the European Economic Community. This is the case for the B30 installation.

 

Question no 59 by Phillip Whitehead (H-0011/03)
 Subject: Eurovelo cycle network and the revision of the TEN-T network
 

If it is the case that the Commission is not currently planning to integrate the Eurovelo cycle network into the Trans-European Transport, or TEN-T network, currently under review, simply because cycling is not considered to be a long-distance mode of transport, would the Commissioner consider secondary ways of linking these networks, on the basis of intermodality? Does the Commissioner agree that publicising such links would have beneficial consequences for environmental and health awareness throughout the enlarged Community?

 
  
 

(FR) As stated in the reply to Mr Helmer's written question E-3486/02, trans-European networks policy seeks to facilitate the establishment of the internal market and, to this end, concentrates on infrastructures routing traffic over long distances – clearly not applicable in the case of the bicycle. Accordingly, there are no plans to include a Europe-wide network of routes for cyclists in the forthcoming review of Community guidelines for the development of the trans-European transport network.

On the other hand, the Commission believes that action to promote non-motorised forms of transport such as bicycles could have beneficial consequences for both health and the environment in the context of sustainable mobility policies at local level. For this reason, as part of the Civitas initiative, the Commission is supporting innovative urban mobility initiatives, including moves to promote cycling which could be relevant to secondary access links to trans-European network axes and hubs. The Commission is also currently preparing a thematic strategy on the urban environment, in accordance with the Sixth Environment Action Programmes. Urban transport, which includes cycling, will be looked at in connection with this work

 

Question no 60 by Per-Arne Arvidsson (H-0012/03)
 Subject: Measures to combat primary immune deficiency
 

Primary immune deficiency is a relatively unknown disease which is under-diagnosed and often results in unnecessary suffering for the individuals affected. The number of people in the EU who have a primary immune deficiency is estimated to be at least 1.5 million. In severe cases, the condition can result in premature death. Infant mortality can be caused by primary immune deficiency. Knowledge within this field must be improved among healthcare workers, the general public and decision-makers throughout Europe. Networks for the exchange of knowledge and research should be developed.

Can the Commission envisage including primary immune deficiency in the general public health strategy and the research framework programme?

 
  
 

(EN) Although the topic of Primary Immune Deficiency was not specifically selected in the first call for the Priority 1 ‘Genomics and health’ under the 6th Framework programme, quite a lot of research covered by this priority will be directly beneficial to research on Primary Immune Deficiency (PID), its diagnosis and treatment. Indeed several topics covered in the work programme in different sections of this priority are directly relevant for research into PID and its treatment and will allow progress to be made. These topics certainly offer opportunities for the scientific community involved in PID research to apply for support in their field. Examples are for instance:

- Functional genomics approaches in animal models to study human disease of the immune system; ---- Optimised allogeneic stem cell transplantation for haematological and neoplastic diseases;

- Gene therapy of inherited diseases;

- Large epidemiological studies of X-linked syndromes;

- Genome-based individualised medicines;

- Medicines for paediatrics;

- Development of genetic tests allowing for harmonisation, validation and standardisation;- Development of a European databank comprising recent advances in genomics, proteomics and cell biology for immunotherapies;

- Co-ordination of rare disease research in Europe, with various stakeholders from research, small and medium-sized enterprises and patient organisations;

- Molecular markers of congenital anomalies.

In the course of the 6th Framework Programme the Commission will continuously re-examine all available scientific elements as a basis for the selection of priority topics for future calls under Priority 1. In that context the Commission will certainly carefully reconsider, in collaboration with the scientific Advisory Committee and the Priority 1 ‘Genomics and Biotechnology for Health Programme Committee’, the need for further research on Primary Immune Deficiency.

In the framework of the Community Programme for rare diseases, a project was supported in 2002 entitled ‘Information Network for Immunodeficiencies’ (N° 2000/RD/10024). The project partner was the University of Tampere. This network is still functioning. It is one of the activities of the European Society for Immunodeficiencies (ESID).

The Work Plan for 2003 within the Community Public Health Programme 2003-2008 will be published shortly together with a call for proposals. This will include the area of rare diseases. The projects proposed for financial support will be selected according to criteria defined for the Programme, which will also be published.

 

Question no 61 by Manuel Medina Ortega (H-0017/03)
 Subject: Special supply arrangements for the outermost regions
 

In view of the recommendations put forward on 19 December 2002 concerning supply plans and Community aid for the supply of certain essential products for human consumption, for food processing and for the supply of eggs and live animals to the outermost regions, and in view of the most recent amendments adopted in respect of those proposals, will the Commission explain the methodology which it is using (or which it intends to use) for those plans and that aid? Will it also explain why it set such a late date as 19 December 2002 for proposing a regulation which was to come into force on 1 January 2003? Is it intending to put forward its 2004 proposals more promptly?

 
  
 

(FR) On 20 January 2003, the Commission adopted Regulation 98/2003 (EC) establishing the supply balances and Community aid for the supply of certain essential products for human consumption, for processing and as agricultural inputs and for the supply of live animals and eggs to the outermost regions.

The subsidies were calculated on the basis of EC Regulations 1452/2001, 1453/2001 and 1454/2001, and also on that of Implementing Regulation 20/2002 (EC).

They were calculated primarily by observing extra supply costs and, as regards the agricultural inputs and products intended for processing, extra costs incurred as a result of regions being insular or on the outermost borders of the EU, as well as the prices customarily charged when comparable products were exported to third countries. The Commission then identified the extra costs eligible for Community finance and determined the appropriate aid.

The exercise referred to above, in which the Member States involved took an active part, was lengthy and complicated. It is for that reason that it was only on 19 December 2002 that the text was submitted to the joint management committee for its opinion. The committee was nevertheless willing to give its opinion on that date, but, at the request of a delegation, the vote was deferred to 9 January 2003.

In order not to disrupt supplies to the outermost regions, the Commission caused Regulation 98/2003 (EC) to enter into force with effect from 1 January 2003.

The Commission will have to repeat this exercise in 2003 in order to determine the amounts of aid applicable with effect from 2004 onwards. It intends to bring forward the date on which the draft text of the Regulation is submitted to the competent committees; as it is not, however, in sole charge of the exercise, this presupposes close and fruitful cooperation with the authorities of the Member States.

 

Question no 62 by Catherine Stihler (H-0018/03)
 Subject: Alcohol logos and under-18s
 

The Scottish Parliament's health committee has been asked to look at the issue of alcohol adverts in children's replica football strips following the high-profile announcement of a sponsorship deal reported to be worth a total £12 million. Multinational brewers Coors will sponsor both Rangers and Celtic to wear the Carling brand on their strips in seasons 2003 to 2006. There is growing evidence of alcohol problems among Scotland's youngsters, and so the Scottish Executive's Plan for Action on Alcohol has made tackling young people's drinking a major priority. The whole thrust of this strategy is undercut if the replica shirt market produces thousands of under-18s wearing beer adverts.

What action is the Commission taking to monitor the use of alcohol logos on child and youth wear across the EU? Will the Commission recommend that no alcohol logos be placed on children's sized shirts?

 
  
 

(EN) In several Member States the problems due to the drinking of alcohol by children and adolescents are rising in recent years. Referring to this, the Council Recommendation on the drinking of alcohol by young people(1) proposes that Member States should encourage, in co-operation with the producers and retailers of alcoholic beverages, the establishment of effective mechanisms to ensure that alcoholic beverages are not promoted to appeal to children and adolescents, paying particular attention to avoiding the implication of sporting success linked to alcohol promotion.

For the time being, the accent lies on self-regulation. In recent meetings with representatives of the alcohol industry the Commission has, however, very strongly stressed the need for the industry to comply with the Council Recommendation and with the existing codes of conduct. The Commission had replied that there was no reason for reviewing the CSF or calling into question its fundamental objectives and that these funds would continue to support the development of the Greek provinces.

Can the Commission now confirm that all these commitments have been honoured without exception and that no CSF funds have been channelled, either directly or indirectly, to projects and enterprises with a view to holding the Olympic Games in 2004?

 
  
 

(FR) The Commission can confirm that no change has been made to the current Community support framework programmes for Greece in 2000-2006 in terms of the amounts involved and the programmes' priorities. The Structural Funds of this Community support framework will not be used for projects directly connected with the Olympic Games.

The Commission can, however, accept the Greek authorities' cofinancing urban and transport infrastructure projects in the Attiki region with the help of the Structural Funds. Whilst these projects contribute to a policy of developing Greece's regions, they do in fact have a bearing on the success of the Olympic Games. The Commission supports the efforts that are being made to this end by the Greek authorities and by the International Olympic Committee.

Question no 78 by Ioannis Patakis (H-0055/03)
 Subject: Devastation of infrastructure and farms in Thessaly and other parts of Greece by widespread flooding
 

The heavy rain which swept across Greece recently has given rise to flooding in many areas of the country, particularly Thessaly and Attica, causing considerable damage to homes and businesses, livestock and crops, and totally devastating agricultural production. These floods have demonstrated the deficiencies or unsuitability of the anti-flooding works carried out in these areas and the population is deeply concerned about a possible recurrence of such events.

What measures will the Commission take towards compensating those affected and creating the necessary infrastructure to protect the areas concerned from flooding?

 
  
 

(EN)

1. The Commission has not yet received any official information or request from Greece concerning the damage to infrastructure and concerning the destruction of the agricultural production caused by extensive flooding that took place recently in Thessaly, Attica and other regions of Greece.

2. On the basis of the current regulatory framework, the Commission would like to inform the Honourable Member that possible Community Funds that might be necessary to overcome the specific problems of Greek urban and rural areas might be taken from the appropriations of the third Community Support Framework for Greece (2000-2006), under condition that the actions to be co-financed are in conformity with the eligibility rules and the development aims of the approved Operational Programmes. It should be emphasised that European Agriculture Guidance and Guarantee Fund intervention will be limited to the reconstitution of production potential and not to compensate income losses. For the latter, as well as for the reconstitution of production potential, assistance may be given by national funding, under the prerequisite of the notification to and approval by the Commission of such State Aid, in line with the relevant State Aid rules.

Within this framework, it is up to Greece and the regions affected to submit to the Commission such requests for compensation of the affected population and areas and to include proposals for the support of appropriate flood protection projects and to ensure the flood protection of the country. This responsibility falls entirely on the Member State concerned.

The Commission, Member States and Candidate Countries have in 2002 started comprehensive practical cooperation on the issue of flooding. Beyond the formal scope of the Water Framework Directive, exchange of information, knowledge and experience on flood prevention and flood protection will, during 2003, lead to a joint document on best practices in flood prediction, prevention and mitigation. At the same time the Commission is working on a horizontal initiative addressing environmental risks (forest fires, earthquakes, flood events, and technological risks), with a Commission Communication foreseen during the first half of 2003. Following discussion on this Communication and the collating of best practices, the Commission will consider need and scope of possible legislative frameworks.

 

Question no 79 by John Walls Cushnahan (H-0056/03)
 Subject: Human rights in Iran
 

The Commission supported the opening up of political dialogue with Iran, with the aim of strengthening reformist elements and improving Iran's human rights record. What is the Commission's view on the progress to date in this respect given that stoning has merely been abolished temporarily, a number of newspapers have been closed down and that the number of executions in 2003 appears to have increased when compared to the same period last year?

 
  
 

(EN) The current political dialogue with Iran – referred to as the Comprehensive Dialogue – is in effect since 1998. One of its aims is, indeed, to bring about an improvement in the Human Rights situation in that country.

The Commission shares the concern at the lack of progress, and has therefore advocated and encouraged the establishment of an enhanced and more effective dialogue on Human Rights. To that end, a first Union-Iran Human Rights troika meeting coupled to an non governmental organizations roundtable was held in Iran 16-17 December 2002. This emerging dialogue on Human Rights held specific discussions on discrimination and prevention against torture. It took notice of the Iranian Government’s willingness to receive a number of United Nations Thematic Rapporteurs, and will continue to assess progress on the basis of concrete benchmarks.
