Verbatim report of proceedings
Thursday, 13 September 2012 - Strasbourg OJ edition

16.3. Azerbaijan: the case of Ramil Safarov
Video of the speeches
 

  President. – The next item is the debate on Azerbaijan and the case of Ramil Safarov.

 
  
 

  Charles Tannock, author. – Mr President, the ECR Group regards the granting of a pardon by President Aliyev, and the hero’s welcome given to Ramil Safarov immediately after his return home to Azerbaijan, as deplorable.

This is contrary to the reported diplomatic understanding by Hungary, where he lived because he was convicted and serving a life sentence in prison for carrying out the brutal murder of an Armenian citizen. It is also against the spirit of the European Convention on the Transfer of Sentenced Persons, which is supposed to mean that returned prisoners serve out their sentences in their home country in order to be closer to their families and in their home culture and language.

Although what was done was, strictly speaking, legally permitted within the laws of Azerbaijan, politically it sends all the wrong signals to Armenia by escalating tensions in an already fraught situation. Azerbaijan is a member of the Council of Europe and participates in Euronest, the EU Eastern Partnership and various other bodies, like the OSCE, which all purport to uphold democratic values and human rights. Clearly, this case is illustrative of the increasingly bellicose attitude taken by Baku over the Nagorno-Karabakh territorial dispute.

Renewed efforts must now be made by the European Union, through the Minsk Group, to try to resolve this frozen conflict and establish a long and lasting peace between Armenia and Azerbaijan and allow the whole of the South Caucasus to develop and prosper.

 
  
 

  Véronique De Keyser, author. – (FR) Mr President, I share many of the views expressed by Mr Tannock on the shocking way in which Azerbaijan has pardoned and promoted Ramil Safarov.

However, I would like to explain the position of my group. We have kept our signature at the bottom of this compromise resolution, but we have one question: if Hungary agreed to return Mr Safarov to Azerbaijan, it seems that it must have received assurances that his sentence would still be served, and that has not been the case. Why is the Hungarian Government not strongly protesting today?

We have therefore called, in our resolution, for an amendment, which is very prudent with regard to the Hungarian Government, and which simply says that its attitude is a little surprising and foolhardy.

We will approve the resolution on the condition that this amendment is adopted, because I think that this is a game of three halves.

 
  
 

  Marie-Christine Vergiat, author. – (FR) Mr President, the resolution before us today is one of these texts on which we have had rather surreal negotiations. For reasons that I fail to understand, the Group of the Alliance of Liberals and Democrats for Europe has withdrawn its signature.

Yet the facts speak for themselves. The Hungarian Government has given in to the age-old pressures of the Azerbaijani authorities by agreeing to transfer Ramil Safarov, who committed an horrific crime against a young Armenian lieutenant, Gurgen Margaryan, and who was sentenced to life imprisonment for this crime. When he arrived in his own country, Mr Safarov was considered a national hero and was immediately pardoned and even rewarded. Relations between Azerbaijan and Armenia certainly did not need this.

The European Union should not allow one of its Member States to act in this manner: adding fuel to the fire instead of entering into negotiation efforts, as others have, in order to finally find a solution to the Nagorno-Karabakh conflict.

I am not as diplomatic as Ms De Keyser, and I would say that unfortunately, we are used to seeing Mr Orbán only showing concern for the values and positions of the European Union when it suits him.

 
  
 

  György Schöpflin, author. – (HU) Mr President, Commissioner, the story of today’s debate has three actors: Azerbaijan, Armenia and Hungary, and it runs along two courses: a legal and a political course. Out of the three actors, the role of Hungary is marginal, as it was merely following the norms of international law. In the light of this, I find the motions for amendment submitted by the left-wing puzzling, as they are heating up a frozen conflict in order to condemn Hungary. One could say that this is a well-known left-wing technique of obfuscation. Azerbaijan, on the other hand, clearly violated the spirit of the rules by immediately granting a pardon to the returned Mr Safarov.

It is not surprising for Armenia to attack Azerbaijan, but the anti-Hungarian outbursts are an indication of a heated state of emotions. The reason why all this is alarming is that the overexcited reaction on the part of Armenia and the questionable step taken by Azerbaijan explicitly endanger the already unstable security of the region.

(The speaker agreed to take a blue-card question under Rule 149(8))

 
  
 

  Véronique De Keyser (S&D), Blue-card question. – (FR) In the resolution that we signed together, in paragraph 4 – if I can still read properly – I see that diplomatic assurances were given to the Hungarian authorities with regard to this transfer.

I will admit that the letter of the Convention on the Transfer of Sentenced Persons was respected, but the spirit was not. If this transfer was so above board, then why ask for assurances? My question is therefore as follows: were assurances given to the Orbán government or not? The question is clear, it is simple and it is easy to answer.

 
  
 

  György Schöpflin (PPE), Blue-card answer. – Assurances were given, the text is available and I, too, have said that the Azerbaijanis effectively did not act in the spirit of the convention.

 
  
 

  Kinga Göncz (S&D), Blue-card question. – (HU) I have two short questions. Mr Schöpflin made a distinction between the legal and political aspects here; I would be curious as to the motivation behind this. Hungary’s previous governments resisted this request and this pressure for years, for the very reason that we were aware that if we gave in, we would be intervening in a very delicate situation. So we did not give in. Beyond the issue of guarantees, which Ms De Keyser enquired about for the very reason that contradictory statements had been issued at home, my question is whether there had been any preparations for this. If you did not prepare for this, was it because you had laid off everyone in the Ministry of Foreign Affairs who would have known what was going on in the region? Or was it perhaps because you did not ask for the opinion of the Ministry of Foreign affairs or decided to not take it into account?

 
  
 

  György Schöpflin (PPE), Blue-card answer. – (HU) This is once again a disturbing statement. Mr Safarov served his sentence, and was on probation for attacking a prison guard. This is why he could not be extradited under the socialist government. As far as the rest is concerned, please check the media.

 
  
 

  Ulrike Lunacek, author. – Mr President, as we have just been hearing, this urgent human rights matter relates to a case that mingles several levels. It involves the unresolved conflict and the current stalemate in the peace process on Nagorno-Karabakh and the surrounding occupied territories, as well as an act of a brutal personal revenge in the form of a pre-meditated murder and the deplorable glorification of a murderer. It also involves the economic crisis in the new EU Member State and irresponsible acts by Heads of Government and State respectively.

Let us start at the beginning. Mr Safarov, as we have heard, was transferred to Azerbaijan from Hungary long before his sentence was completed. The European Parliament strongly condemns that. He was granted a presidential pardon by President Aliyev there, and he has been honoured as a national hero and promoted to major in the Azeri military. All despite the fact that he has not repented of the brutal murder of an Armenian soldier that he committed in Budapest in 2004.

I acknowledge the legitimate frustration in Azerbaijan about the lack of substantial progress in the peace process, but acts of brutal revenge, and turning murderers into national heroes, is an appalling way of reacting and risks further escalating – not just rhetorically – the ethnic conflict between Armenia and Azerbaijan. Mr Schöpflin, Hungary has not played a merely marginal role in that.

Let us look at Hungary. Hungary’s Prime Minister, Viktor Orbán, has a huge share of responsibility here. Just at the beginning of this week, he admitted that he himself gave way and said ‘yes’ to transferring Safarov back to Baku, that he ignored the warnings from his own administration, and that he himself – that is the interpretation – is responsible for heating up the conflict in the Caucuses.

This brave admission gives rise to the interpretation that, following his recent visit to Baku, Mr Orbán knowingly and deliberately helped to pardon a murderer, and sacrificed diplomatic relations with Armenia for a highly questionable economic deal to help his own country out of the crisis. I hope our amendments on Hungary will also be put to the vote here, because we have to be critical both of the Azeri President and of the Hungarian Prime Minister.

(The speaker agreed to take a blue-card question under Rule 149(8))

 
  
 

  Kinga Gál (PPE), Blue-card question. – (HU) Ms Lunacek, Azerbaijan is a member of the Council of Europe, and when Hungary extradited the prisoner, it was adhering to the relevant convention of the Council of Europe, following every article to the letter. And what Azerbaijan undertook as a member of the Council of Europe it even confirmed in an official letter to the Hungarian authorities. In that letter, it undertook to ensure that the prisoner would serve his sentence, and that no additional procedure would be conducted. We have this on paper.

 
  
 

  Ulrike Lunacek (Verts/ALE), Blue-card answer. – I know about this letter. I have the letter from the Ministry of Justice of the Republic of Azerbaijan, but it can be interpreted in various ways. It does not give total assurance. Even Mr Schöpflin has acknowledged that, while it can be interpreted in that way, it leaves leeway to interpret it in a way that does not give assurances. The Hungarian Prime Minister should have listened to his Minister for Foreign Affairs and others in his administration who warned him about transferring Safarov back to Baku.

 
  
 

  Norica Nicolai, author. – (RO) Mr President, a French minister recently made derogatory remarks about the Roma community in Romania. I feel a sense of indignation, but my indignation cannot give rise to an emergency resolution. This is because any self-respecting parliament abides by the rules provided for in the Rules of Procedure. According to Rule 122 of the Rules of Procedure, this situation cannot give rise to an emergency resolution. It is not a matter concerning the rules of law, democracy or human rights.

Raising this matter in the relations between two countries, when we all acknowledge that this involved a completely legal agreement and both sides, Hungary and Azerbaijan, respected each other’s rights, may cause a sense of indignation, but cannot give rise to a political resolution in a circumstance where the security situation in the region might be affected to a fair extent by promoting such actions.

I would like to ask a rhetorical question: how do you explain that it is already known in the Armenian press that this resolution was adopted?

I would like us to stop trying to play games in this case, favouring one side or the other, where we are managing to destabilise a situation in which the European Union ought to be playing a decisive role. This is why the Group of the Alliance of Liberals and Democrats for Europe has withdrawn its signatures from this resolution and why the ALDE Group is not going to vote for it.

 
  
 

  Laima Liucija Andrikienė, on behalf of the PPE Group. – Mr President, Ramil Safarov, a lieutenant of the Azerbaijani armed forces who was convicted of murder and sentenced to life imprisonment in Hungary, was transferred to Azerbaijan in order to serve the sentence imposed on him, at the long-standing request of the Azerbaijani authorities.

Instead, Lieutenant Safarov received a glorious welcome in Azerbaijan and was granted a presidential pardon, set free and promoted to major. The European Parliament could only deplore the hero’s welcome and presidential pardon granted to Safarov because – and I fully agree with my colleague Mr Schöpflin – it runs contrary to the spirit of the international agreement. The lack of any substantial progress as regards the peace process in Nagorno-Karabakh does not justify either acts of revenge or provocation, which only add tension to an already tense and sensitive situation in the region.

 
  
 

  Edit Herczog, on behalf of the S&D Group. – (HU) Mr President, ladies and gentlemen, according to the Hungarian Government, this is about a legal act between two sovereign countries. However, if that is so, why did the Group of the European People’s Party (Christian Democrats) initiate that the matter be discussed here at the Strasbourg session of Parliament, among urgent humanitarian issues? After all, it was the PPE Group that instigated this initiative, while the Socialists disagreed. Murder, ladies and gentlemen, is murder. A judgment is a judgment. In the case of a transfer between two countries, there is a joint responsibility to ensure this on the basis of mutual guarantees. This is the responsibility we are looking for. International organisations, including the European Parliament, must make serious efforts to strengthen trust in the region and to ensure that the issue is settled as soon as possible.

There is no alternative to a peaceful process of negotiation in the region. I sincerely hope that bilateral relations will be restored to their original state as soon as possible.

 
  
 

  Kristiina Ojuland, on behalf of the ALDE Group. – Mr President, the ALDE Group did not sign the resolution on the case of Ramil Safarov, and I shall vote against this text today. It did not sign because the text is something that may escalate the existing conflict between Azerbaijan and Armenia over Nagorno-Karabakh.

The territory of Nagorno-Karabakh and seven surrounding regions is internationally recognised as belonging to the Republic of Azerbaijan, and that should also be the basis for the position of the European Union. The absence of any results after 20 years of work indicates that the Minsk Group needs a reshuffle. In order to find a balance in the negotiation process, France should be reinforced by the European Union, which, in my view, has an historic chance to try to resolve this very frozen conflict, showing its knowledge and experience in negotiations.

The European Parliament remains committed to the developments in the South Caucasus, and its Member States are aware of the huge potential of Azerbaijan. The constructive partnership between Brussels and Baku entails reciprocity and commitment to shared values.

 
  
 

  Carl Schlyter, on behalf of the Verts/ALE Group. – (SV) Mr President, the resolutions that we debate here usually deal with things that happen outside Europe’s borders, but we also have to discuss what happened in Hungary. The focus is nevertheless on the fact that, when people are transferred, it is usually agreed that they will remain in prison. After a number of years, they then often get the chance to be pardoned or released.

In this case, it was decided on day one to honour a murderer as a kind of hero and reward him financially in order to exacerbate the conflict. There can be no purpose to this other than to foster hatred and exacerbate the conflict. It seems as though they have succeeded in doing this. That is why it is important now for us to help Armenia and Azerbaijan to achieve a peaceful solution to the conflict and to ensure that this type of policy of hatred cannot benefit in any way those who pursue it.

 
  
 

  Jaroslav Paška, on behalf of the EFD Group. – (SK) Mr President, the release of the murderer sentenced to life imprisonment by the Azerbaijani President in August this year was shocking news for the civilised world.

In doing so, Ilham Aliyev indirectly told his citizens and the world that any citizen of Azerbaijan who murders an Armenian shall be a hero in his eyes, and he would be exalted and rewarded for such a murder. The Azerbaijani President thus behaves like a mafia boss who rewards his stooges for the murder of his enemies.

We should note that a sense of frustration hangs over Azerbaijan due to the occupation of Azerbaijani territories by the Armenian military. However, Ramil Safarov murdered his Armenian colleague during a course being held as part of the Partnership for Peace programme held in Budapest, and not somewhere on a battlefield in the border zone.

I do not wish to comment on the professionalism of the Hungarian administration which permitted this criminal to return home, but I understand the actions of the Armenian Government, which broke off diplomatic relations with Hungary. I firmly believe, however, that our generous accommodating policy towards Azerbaijan is not conducive to the cultivation of a political environment in that country and, in the future, we should be more cautious in our expectations that the Armenian and Azerbaijani governments might agree on a peaceful resolution of the Nagorno-Karabakh problem.

 
  
 

  Jiří Maštálka, on behalf of the GUE/NGL Group. – (CS) Mr President, the statement made by the European Parliament on the Ramil Safarov case is, in my opinion, very appropriate. The support expressed for the mediators in the peace process between Armenia and Azerbaijan is of fundamental importance, I believe.

I discern in the wording of the resolution, however, a rebuke for an EU Member State over the failure of its diplomats and legislators to foresee the presidential pardon, or rather for seeing it as a way of getting rid of a problem. As the President of Azerbaijan clearly did not have much of an option, the reaction of the Armenians who suspect Budapest of doing a deal is not to be wondered at. On the other hand, I would expect a promise that the European Parliament will help to settle relations between Armenia and Hungary.

Most welcome of all would be for the European Parliament to actively join in with the states seeking a peaceful resolution to the disputes over Nagorno-Karabakh. I would like to say that many Member States export large volumes of arms and ammunition to this area.

 
  
 

  Krisztina Morvai (NI). – Mr President, punish or extradite? This is the fundamental principle of international criminal law in matters of mutual cooperation in criminal justice matters between states. In this case, Hungary used a combination of these two. We enforced eight years of Mr Safarov’s sentence and then extradited him – at the request of Azerbaijan – and allowed his transfer to Azerbaijan.

As a criminal lawyer, I think there should have been more careful consideration in terms of making sure that Azerbaijan did not use Article 12 of the relevant European Convention and did not grant a pardon or amnesty to Safarov. As a Hungarian criminal lawyer, I am not proud of this particular decision, and I think there is room for improvement.

Having said that, the European Union is not a role model for us. The European Union should not instruct us in matters of international cooperation in criminal cases when there is a shameful, shocking case pending in the European Union in which we are on the receiving end of a miscarriage of justice, and you had better solve that case before you instruct us. It is the Tobin case. Have you ever heard about this case? If not, that is really sad; you should have heard about it.

An Irish banker in Hungary killed two Hungarian children in an extreme case of reckless driving. He was sentence to three years’ imprisonment by a Hungarian court. He is now in Ireland, in his country of citizenship, and do you know what? Ireland refuses both to extradite him to Hungary and to enforce his sentence. What does the European Union do? It sits back and keeps silent. Why is it that when we are on the receiving end of these miscarriages of justice, you never raise your voices? Ms Hall said earlier this afternoon that there should be no cherry picking in matters of human rights. Please use this principle when Hungary is on the agenda.

(The President cut off the speaker)

 
  
 

  Elisabeth Jeggle (PPE). – (DE) Mr President, ladies and gentlemen, as a member of the Council of Europe and of the Eastern Partnership, Azerbaijan needs to heed its freely undertaken commitments in respect of human rights, democracy and the rule of law in the Safarov case. International treaties and agreements absolutely have to be complied with. The EU should support Azerbaijan in realising these aspirations as far as is possible.

I am committed to ensuring that the EU intensifies its efforts to mediate in the Nagorno-Karabakh conflict, thus getting involved and helping to bring peace to the region. Active participation and political will on the part of both the Azerbaijanis and the Armenians are a prerequisite for this. Talks have to be held in this regard at the highest political level, while broader civil society must also be involved at the same time. The European External Action Service (EEAS) and the Commission must work closely with the local, regional, national and international actors on the ground in order to obtain synergies. For me, the question arises as to whether it is helpful to look to lay blame at the door of the Hungarian Government, or whether we do not instead need to be very clear in what we say to the Azerbaijani Government.

 
  
 

Catch-the-eye procedure

 
  
 

  Sari Essayah (PPE). – (FI) Mr President, as parliamentarians, we have been under quite a lot of pressure to abandon this resolution. I cannot quite understand the view of the Group of the Alliance of Liberals and Democrats for Europe that we should not say anything about this issue, because it is extremely important, given the unrest in that region. Furthermore, this is an urgent matter and we should specifically endeavour to say something that will ease the situation there.

The Hungarian Government has posted a statement on its website to the effect that Azerbaijan’s Ministry of Justice had assured Hungary that Safarov would continue to serve the remaining part of his sentence in Azerbaijan. Unfortunately, that was not the case and, instead, he was pardoned and promoted, and he became a sort of national hero. It is extremely important that we condemn the way in which Safarov was treated in Azerbaijan. We must also do everything to ensure that relations between the countries in the region, Armenia and Azerbaijan, are restored peacefully and that neighbourly relations can continue between them.

 
  
 

  Lidia Joanna Geringer de Oedenberg (S&D). – (PL) Mr President, during an international training course organised in 2004 by NATO in Hungary, an Azeri soldier, Ramil Safarov, killed an Armenian officer with an axe and said that the victim had shown disrespect both to him and to the Azeri flag. Acting in accordance with the 1983 Strasbourg Convention on the Transfer of Sentenced Persons, signed both by Budapest and Baku, Hungary agreed to transfer the prisoner to Azerbaijan. As everyone has said here, Safarov was greeted as a hero, released from the penalty of life imprisonment that had been imposed by Hungary, and he was then promoted for his heroic deed to the rank of major. This incident has aroused considerable and widespread concern. First of all, according to the provisions of the convention on the transfer of sentenced persons, Azerbaijan was under an obligation to ensure that the entire penalty imposed by Hungary was carried out and this it did not do. Secondly, President Aliyev gave the world a very bad example by glorifying a brutal crime and treating the murderer as a national hero. For this reason, I appeal to the Azeri authorities to act in accordance with international standards as regards the justice system.

 
  
 

End of the catch-the-eye procedure

 
  
 

  Androulla Vassiliou, Member of the Commission. – Mr President, honourable Members, I welcome your initiative to raise the issue of Mr Safarov’s extradition and pardoning. I am fully aware of the very strong reactions prompted by this development, not only in Armenia, but also far beyond.

Ramil Safarov was sentenced in Hungary to life imprisonment for the brutal murder of Gurgen Margaryan, an Armenian officer and fellow NATO course participant. From our contacts with the Hungarian authorities, I understand that the decision to extradite Mr Safarov was based upon written assurances received from the Azerbaijani Government that Mr Safarov would serve the remaining part of his life sentence there.

The subsequent Azerbaijani decisions, including the pardoning of Mr Safarov and steps which have widely been seen as rewarding his crime, are deeply regrettable. The handling of this case has the potential to further complicate the peaceful settlement of the Nagorno-Karabakh conflict, as well as – potentially – the Eastern Partnership agenda.

Despite this setback – and I think we cannot pretend that it is otherwise – the EU remains committed to, and persistent in, its support to the Nagorno-Karabakh conflict settlement process in the framework of the Minsk Group. The EU does not see – and would absolutely reject – any alternative to peaceful conflict settlement. The Safarov case should not be used by anyone to deviate from this.

Intense contacts with all actors to establish the situation have been deployed by the EU, and there was a good discussion on this at the informal Foreign Ministers’ meeting last weekend in Cyprus. Moreover, Baroness Ashton has instructed the EU Special Representative for the South Caucasus and the crisis in Georgia to visit Yerevan and Baku and convey our messages, which he is doing as we speak.

Last but not least, there is also a lesson for the Eastern Partnership, in which both Armenia and Azerbaijan participate. Without a community of values, without the rule of law and respect for human rights and commitments taken, there can be no reconciliation or peace. In this respect, the implementation of the Eastern Partnership Road map becomes a key element for regional stability.

More than ever, our efforts in support of peace in the region are needed. The Commission welcomes President Schulz’s statement and the European Parliament’s commitment in focusing the attention of partners on this objective.

 
  
 

  President. – I have received six motions for resolutions(1) to wind up the debate.

The debate is closed.

The vote will take place at the end of the debates.

Written statements (Rule 149)

 
  
 		 

  Jacek Olgierd Kurski (EFD), in writing. – (PL) The case of Ramil Safarov should be viewed in the wider context of the ever deepening conflict between Armenia and Azerbaijan. Every day, the situation becomes more complicated. The rhetoric on both sides is increasingly aggressive and does not hide the fact that steps are being taken towards an armed conflict. It is enough to mention today’s declaration by the Azeri authorities stating that the visit by the Armenian President to the ‘occupied territories’ of Nagorno-Karabakh would be treated as a hostile act against Baku. Yerevan has been using similar rhetoric: a few months ago came the declaration that, in the coming conflict with Azerbaijan, the Armenian army should create a new frontier with Russia. The main allies of the two parties, Russia and Turkey, are well aware of the situation and have been massing their armies on the border with the Southern Caucasus. The only thing that we can now do is to debate, following another blunder of EU diplomacy, which was to strengthen foreign policy activity in the light of the ‘Arab Spring’ to the detriment of the Eastern Partnership and the Black Sea Synergy. The European Union does not play any significant role in this region, which is vital for our energy security. We have handed the area over to Moscow and Ankara, who are not concerned about peace but about extending their territory of influence. What is worse, there are still no signs of any change in the policy conducted by Brussels.

 
  
 		 

  Tadeusz Zwiefka (PPE), in writing. – (PL) In today’s resolution, we have once again had to speak critically about the behaviour of the Azeri Government. It should be noted that this country is in the process of negotiating an association agreement with the EU and is actively involved in the concept of the Eastern Partnership and the Euronest initiative. One might therefore conclude that it aspires to modify its legal order to the order on which the European Union is based. Respect for democracy and the rule of law are fundamental for European integration. The case of Ramil Safarov clearly breaches not just agreements, but also generally accepted standards of international law. It is unimaginable that a person who has been handed down a legally binding sentence of life imprisonment should, immediately following a transfer under the European convention on the transfer of sentenced persons, be pardoned, promoted to the status of a hero and rewarded. While working on diplomatic steps to bring about a peaceful resolution to the conflict in Nagorno-Karabakh, I believe that the European Union cannot remain quiet on the way in which Azerbaijan has behaved, not just publicly glorifying a crime, but also consciously risking an escalation in the conflict.
