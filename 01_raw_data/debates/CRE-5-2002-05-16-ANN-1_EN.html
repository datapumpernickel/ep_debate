QUESTIONS TO COUNCIL
Question no 18 by Konstantinos Alyssandrakis (H-0316/02)
 Subject: Statements by Mr Javier Solana concerning accession to the European Union of the Greek Cypriot part only of Cyprus
 

In an interview with the newspaper To Vima tis Kyriakis, the High Representative for the CFSP, Mr Javier Solana, stated on the subject of enlargement to incorporate Cyprus that if there is no agreement (to resolve the political problem), the Greek Cypriot part of Cyprus will accede. In addition, he recognised the occupied territories as a political entity in pointing out that the northern part of Cyprus had not negotiated at all with the Union and therefore did not fulfil the criteria for accession.

Are Mr Solana's statements an expression of the Council's own position? Are they contrary or not to the decision taken at Helsinki, which refers to the 'accession of Cyprus' and not a part thereof, and to international law, the resolutions of the UN and the Member States, which recognise a single government only in Cyprus and, therefore, a single interlocutor? Does the position adopted at Helsinki, that a settlement of the Cyprus problem is not a precondition for accession, apply only to the free part of Cyprus and thus in effect entail definitive partitioning?

 
  
 

In the interview the Honourable Member of Parliament refers to, Secretary-General / High Representative Solana was misinterpreted. He expressed concern about the possibility that the Cyprus talks would not be completed succesfully before the end of Cyprus' EU accession negotiations. In this case, the acquis communautaire could not be implemented in the part of the island which is not under the effective control of the government of Cyprus.

The Council position on EU accession of Cyprus as established in the Helsinki European Council conclusions has not changed.

 

Question no 19 by Michael Gahler (H-0317/02)
 Subject: Extending EU sanctions against Zimbabwe
 

On 15 April 2002, the General Affairs Council deferred further decisions on additional targeted measures against the Government of Zimbabwe until May although the domestic situation has been increasingly deteriorating since the March elections. In the meantime, countries such as the US and New Zealand have extended their lists. What is the Council's interim assessment of sanctions to date? How many visa applications have been rejected by the embassies of the 15 Member States? Given the exceptional events taking place, have the same criteria been applied by the 15 Member States? On what grounds are certain Member States preventing or delaying the extension of targeted sanctions? Are those Member States afraid that identical criteria could be applied in their spheres of influence in Africa and that, as a result, they themselves could come under political pressure to account for their actions? Is the Council prepared, at its May meeting, to extend the present list of 20 persons in line with what individual EU states and other states are doing in practice and on the basis of Parliament proposals?

 
  
 

The Council confirms that on 15 April it decided to defer consideration of additional targeted measures against the Government of Zimbabwe until its meeting in May. This discussion, when it takes place, will of course also imply an interim assessment of the sanctions.

The issuance of visas is the responsability of the Member States. The Council does not dispose of any information concerning the number of visas issued. It has no reasons to believe that Member States do not apply the same criteria to the restrictive measures. The Council is not aware of any Member Sates preventing or delaying a possible extension of the targeted sanctions either.

The European Council in Barcelona on 15-16 March decided to dispatch a high level troika in the future to confer with countries in the SADC region about the EU’s concerns regarding Zimbabwe. When the Council in April decide to defer its discussion till May, it was under the assumption that the troika would have taken place before the 13 May. The troika will be finally be carried out from 20 to 22 May leaded by the Spanish Secretary of State of Foreign Affairs. Therefore, the Council will not be able to discuss the question of Zimbabwe at its meeting in May.

 

Question no 20 by John Bowis (H-0323/02)
 Subject: Independence Day in East Timor
 

Who will represent the Council at the Independence celebrations for East Timor on 20 May 2002 and what message of continued support from the European Union has been sent to newly elected President Xanana Gusmão?

 
  
 

The President-in-Office of the Council will be represented at the celebrations by the President of the Spanish Senate, Mrs Esperanza Aguirre, who will assure President Xanana Gusmão of the Union's continued support for East Timor. This support will entail the Community's highest level of aid per capita to anywhere in Asia. It will also pay tribute on this occasion to the United Nations Transitional Administration in East Timor (UNTAET), and to the people of East Timor, who have now realised the dream of independence for their nation.

 

Question no 21 by Concepció Ferrer (H-0336/02)
 Subject: Human rights in Guinea
 

During the UN Assembly meeting in Madrid on ageing, Mr Aznar met Teodoro Obiang, the President and dictator of Equatorial Guinea, apparently promising to continue to send aid to Guinea.

While the most recent UN report on human rights did indeed note an improvement in the human rights situation in Guinea, it needs to be stressed that those rights continue to be violated there. The most recent wave of over 100 arbitrary arrests is a glaring example; eliciting from the UN Human Rights Commission’s special representative for Guinea, Mr Gustavo Gallón, the statement that ‘the people of Guinea live in complete judicial insecurity’.

Can the Council confirm that the European Union is not going to change its policy towards Equatorial Guinea as long as the most basic rights continue to be systematically violated in that country, and tell me what measures it intends to take to ensure that all those arrested in Guinea in recent weeks will enjoy a fair trial?

 
  
 

The Council is aware of the recent arrests of oppositions politicians in Equatorial Guinea and the dubious circunstances of their detention. Following reports in the international press the European Union requested a more detailed report from the EU Heads of Mission on the political situation in the country with a particular focus on human rights issues, expecting to get a clearer picture about the situation and the extent of human rights’ violations as a bassis for discussing the future course of action towards Equatorial Guinea.

The report was issued on 10 May and following its conclusions a demarche troika level will be carried out in Malabo to express the deep concern of the European Union with regards to the situation of human rights and to request the Government of Equatorial Guinea guarantees of transparence, humanitarian treatment, and fair and public judgement for the arrested and remind its engagement of advancing in the present process of administrative and judicial reforms in order to enforce civil and political rights.

The Council can assure the Honourable Member of the European Parliament that the EU is not going to change its policy towards Equatorial Guinea as long as there are no sustained efforts by the Government to improve the human rights situation.

 

Question no 22 by Pedro Marset Campos (H-0338/02)
 Subject: Situation in Palestine
 

The EU has been asking Yasser Arafat to postpone the proclamation of the Palestinian State. A perverse effect of this postponement has been the dramatic deterioration in the situation and the continued Israeli aggression against Palestine and against the Palestinian Authority and its President, Yasser Arafat.

The end-result of the EU's mistaken attitude has been the continuation of the military occupation and the further spread of illegal settlements in Palestine.

Would the Council not agree that it should without delay tackle the issue of the proposed recognition of the State of Palestine so as to enable it to enjoy the support, protection and rights afforded by international law, and allow an intervention force to be sent, dialogue to be resumed on a basis of equality, etc.

Would it not agree that it should suspend the association agreement between the EU and the State of Israel with a view to compelling Israel to comply with international law, international agreements and the UN resolutions on Palestine?

 
  
 

The Council does not share the perception of the Honourable Member that delays in the proclamation of a Palestinian State have led to the violence of the last few months or to the extension of Israeli settlements, however worrying both of these phenomena may be. Nor does the Council think that the proclamation and recognition of a Palestinian State at this point would contribute to solving the current problem. The Council does, however, believe in the vision shared by other members of the Quartet and spelled out in UNSCR 1397 (2002), namely two states, Israel and Palestine, living side-by-side within secure and reorganised borders.

The Council informs the Honourable Member that it does not intend to suspend the Euro-Mediterranean Association Agreement with Israel.

 

Question no 23 by Raimon Obiols i Germà (H-0344/02)
 Subject: Breaking the deadlock in international adoptions in Romania
 

During the last part-session in Strasbourg the Council representative gave a personal undertaking to make new efforts within the Council to break the deadlock in the adoption procedures that are currently blocked.

What new action has been taken in this matter?

 
  
 

The Council is aware of the sensitive nature and human interest involved in this issue, which has already been addressed several times in this forum. Indeed, when this issue was last discussed, in April, the Presidency made it clear that the European Union continues to monitor the situation and to address the issue in the context of the bodies created within the framework of the Europe Agreement, most recently at the EU-Romania Association Council on 12 March 2002, as well as in bilateral contacts with the Romanian authorities. Furthermore, the Presidency undertook to take further action in this area, should this prove to be necessary.

At the same time, the Presidency recalled that, in the revised Accession Partnership for Romania that was adopted by the Council on 28 January 2002, the priorities and intermediate objectives included the stipulation that Romania should "maintain the moratorium on international adoption in place until new legislation compatible with the best interests of the child and Romania's international obligations is adopted and the administrative capacity to implement the new legislation is ensured". Whilst maintaining the general moratorium, in December 2001 the Romanian Government took measures to enable the completion of adoption procedures for those cases that were already subject to court procedures when international adoptions were suspended. In addition, procedures exist to process pending cases that can be judged as exceptional circumstances.

As for the most recent developments, in April an independent working group presented a report on inter-country adoptions to the Romanian Government. Prime Minister Nastase has welcomed the report as a basis for future policy on international adoptions and provided an assurance that the current moratorium will not be lifted until there is adequate administrative capacity in place to implement revised legislation. He has indicated that the package of laws proposed by the independent working group will be adopted by the end of 2002 and that, in the meantime, steps will be taken to improve administrative capacity in order to implement this legislation. These appear to be positive developments.

 

Question no 24 by John Walls Cushnahan (H-0345/02)
 Subject: Football hooliganism
 

Following the recent approval of the Deprez report on football hooliganism by the European Parliament (A5-0047/2002 - T5-0148/2002), would the Council encourage cooperation between Member States and the authorities in South Korea and Japan to establish 'national information points' to prevent football hooligans marring the World Cup?

 
  
 

Following the opinion of the European Parliament on football hooliganism the Council has adopted, on 25 April 2002, the Decision concerning security in connection with football matches with an international dimension (Council document 8131/02 ENFOPOL 55, not yet published in the Official Journal): each Member State will set up or designate a national football information point of a police nature. Between these points police information will be exchanged for the purposes of preventing and combating football-related violence and of allowing the competent authorities to make proper preparations and to provide an appropriate response to the possible risks.

In 2001, the Belgian Government, in preparing its initiative for the above Council Decision, organised an International Seminar on football hooliganism, in which participated not only experts from the EU Member States, but also a large number of experts from South Korea and Japan in view of the World championship 2002 in these countries. During this Seminar contacts have been established for the future cooperation between the EU Member States and the authorities in South Korea and Japan.

The Council Decision of 25 April 2002 in an important contribution to the efforts for international cooperation to establish security at football matches.

 

Question no 25 by Eija-Riitta Anneli Korhola (H-0346/02)
 Subject: Fiscal impact of EU emissions trading
 

The EU is consistently seeking to reduce greenhouse gas emissions in accordance with the targets agreed at Kyoto. One important means of doing so is the introduction of emissions trading in the EU. An essential element in emissions trading is that it takes the place of energy taxation so as to avoid imposing a double burden on operators. Thus ‘bad’ businesses will be compelled to transfer revenue to ‘good’ ones. However, there will be a corresponding decline in the taxes and levies which businesses have previously paid to the state, and this could significantly affect two other aspects of sustainable development, namely the social and economic. These adverse side-effects make emissions trading significantly different from environmental taxation, for example, on which it has not yet been possible to reach a decision within the EU despite the fact that it could be an equally effective way of reducing greenhouse gas emissions.

Has this matter been debated within the Council, and along what lines does the Presidency believe that a solution could be arrived at?

 
  
 

Emission trading is expected to be an efficient and cost effective way towards helping the EU to meet its commitments under the Kyoto Protocol. Social and economic considerations are among the key issues of the Sixth Environmental Action Program. The Council is well aware of the importance of working towards, and integrating, aspects of sustainable development in its work.

The Council is still examining the Commission's proposal for a greenhouse emission trading scheme, and at this stage it would be premature to prejudge the outcome of these discussions. I am convinced, however, that the social and economic concerns highlighted by the Honourable Member will be adequately addressed.

Question no 39 by Astrid Thors (H-0308/02)
 Subject: Children's rights in the applicant countries
 

To what extent will the Commission's progress report on enlargement take account of how the applicant countries have honoured their commitments under the UN Convention on the Rights of the Child and of the reports received by the Committee on the Rights of the Child pursuant to that Convention?

In reply to question H-0915/97(1) tabled in 1997, the Commission pledged that the annual reports on the Phare programme would show the amounts planned for national programmes to improve the situation of children in the applicant countries. To what extent is that the case?

 
  
 

Respect for human rights, including the rights of the child, is a key element of the political accession criteria laid down by the European Council at Copenhagen in 1993. The Commission provides a comprehensive assessment of progress made by each of the candidate countries in implementing the Copenhagen criteria in its Regular Reports. In this framework, the Commission draws on all available sources of information, including the reports of the United Nations Committee on the Rights of the Child or other competent bodies as well as Non Governmental Organisations, to examine progress made by candidate countries in this area.

The Commission gives high importance to projects that aim at improving the situation of children in the applicant countries of Central and Eastern Europe. In the years 1997-2001 about EUR 70 million have been provided for this purpose, on average EUR 14 million per year. Actions financed through the Phare national programmes aim inter alia at the reform of childcare and education. In some cases projects target specifically children from disadvantaged groups such as the Roma. Moreover, the Commission has funded and continues funding through its Delegations in the candidate countries a great variety of small projects in favour of children under the Lien, Democracy, Access and Small Projects Programme as well as the European Initiative for Democracy and Human Rights.

In Turkey, the Commission contributes inter alia EUR 100 million over five years to a Reform Programme for Basic Education.

The Commission follows a standard external aid classification of sectors of intervention in its reporting on the Phare programme. Projects in favour of children fall under several generic sectors, the main ones being “Education, training and research”, “Social development and employment” and “Civil society and democratisation”. In order to provide specific information on projects for children, an ad-hoc research through our Delegations in the candidate countries is necessary which has been undertaken for the answer to this question. Due to these methodological difficulties the Commission did not systematically report on children’s projects. It will further elaborate on this issue in order to include an ad hoc section on children in the 2001 Phare report.

Question no 40 by Gary Titley (H-0313/02)
 Subject: Power plant in Ignalina
 

In its report on the financial perspective for enlargement, the Commission indicated that support for the closure of the Ignalina power station would be required for decades to come. On the other hand, the Council is not prepared to make any commitment beyond the end of the current financial framework in 2006. The Lithuanians are reluctant to commit themselves to closing the second reactor without a clear indication of future support.

How does the Commission propose to resolve these contradictory positions so that the energy chapter can be closed in time for possible Lithuanian accession in 2004?

 
  
 

The Union's position on the closure of the Ignalina Nuclear Power Plant should first be recalled.

Concerning the closure of the plant, the Union requires a clear and binding confirmation of Lithuania's commitment regarding the final closure of Unit 1 before 2005 and a clear and binding commitment by Lithuania to close Unit 2 by 2009 at the latest.

Regarding external financial assistance for the closure and decommissioning, the Commission would like to recall the substantial Union assistance already provided, notably through Phare. Furthermore, the Union has expressed its readiness to explore with Lithuania further financial support. Moreover, the Commission has been very much encouraged by the Member States, which generally have welcomed the proposals in this regard presented in the Commission's paper on the Common Financial Framework in January 2002. The Commission indeed considers that the decommissioning of the Ignalina Nuclear Power Plant is an extra-ordinary burden for Lithuania in view of the plant’s economic importance relative to the country’s size and that this situation renders necessary additional support, which - as indicated by the Honourable Member - should continue for the next decades.

In general one should however notably underline that nuclear reactors of the RBMK type(1), including the two reactors of the Ignalina Nuclear Power Plant (NPP), suffer from fundamental design failures, so that - despite any short-term safety improvements - they cannot realistically be upgraded to an internationally acceptable high safety level. The Commission recalls that the decision to build up such an NPP in Lithuania was made under particular historical circumstances.

The Commission is furthermore encouraged by the ongoing work in Lithuania to prepare the necessary decisions. The highest political level, including the President, the Government and the Parliament, are all engaged in this very difficult but important process.

Lithuania is fully aware that the accession negotiations are based on the present acquis including the limits set by the current financial perspectives.

On the basis of the above, the Commission is confident that all parties involved will find in time an adequate and well-balanced solution, based on Community Solidarity and mutual trust. The fact that the European Parliament as budgetary authority continues to attach high importance to this matter only increases confidence in this regard.

Question no 41 by John Joseph McCartin (H-0314/02)
 Subject: Civil rights abuses
 

Is the European Commission aware of complaints from Amnesty International, about abuses of civil rights in Turkey and the application of inhumane treatment to female prisoners in detention (information on recent specific cases forwarded directly to Commissioner for External Relations) and has the Commission raised this question with the Turkish Government? Is the Commission satisfied that the Turkish Government has complied and is complying with the conditions set out for candidacy of the European Union, specifically in relation to their human rights record?

The improvement of detention conditions and the prevention of torture in Turkey are amongst the short- and medium-term priorities of the Union’s Accession Partnership with Turkey.

The Commission will continue to discuss these issues with the Turkish authorities.

The Commission has reported in detail on Turkey’s compliance with the Copenhagen criteria for accession in its regular report of November 2001. A new report will be published in October 2002, and particular attention will be paid to developments in Turkey in relation to the political criteria of Copenhagen.

 

Question no 42 by Eija-Riitta Anneli Korhola (H-0347/02)
 Subject: Support for European federations representing particular industries in the context of enlargement
 

Significant pan-European federations exist which represent particular industries and do vital work to disseminate among their members best practice and know-how in their field as well as ethical traditions relating to it. This is very important in the context of enlargement, as industries in Eastern European countries seek to establish a new business ethos and to bring their know-how better into line with that of their counterparts in the EU countries. At the same time they have an important part to play in implementing the acquis communautaire in their fields.

However, some industries are primarily made up of SMEs, whose European organisations lack funding, although they are important from the point of view of the wide range of know-how which they possess. For example, the organisation representing the locks industry can promote awareness of the importance of security in society at large.

So far, businesses in the applicant countries have only limited resources for participating in the work of such organisations, although links with them are essential. Similarly, organisations representing SMEs have only limited opportunities to foster the participation of their new members.

Is the EU supporting the work of such small organisations representing industries in order to facilitate the participation of businesses from applicant countries? If the EU is providing support, or is planning to do so, is the Commission taking measures to provide appropriate information to the organisations concerned?

 
  
 

The Union has supported and intends to continue to support the work of organisations representing businesses in the candidate countries through the Phare Business Support Programme.

The Business Support Programme is a multi-beneficiary Phare programme supporting the corporate restructuring and acquaintance with the acquis of industrial business representative associations in the candidate countries. It relies on typical institution building activities such as training, internships and placement actions delivered by sister business associations in the Union.

The existing Business Support Programme (BSP)–Phase 1 (EUR 20.9 million) has focused on assisting business representative organisations (BRO) in the Central and Eastern European Countries (CEECs) in strengthening and acquainting them with the acquis.

A new decision on a Business Support Programme-Phase 2 (EUR 15 million) was adopted by the Commission for both horizontal and sectoral CEECs associations and a call for proposal is due to be published soon.

The BSP Phase 2 will focus on the following objectives: full familiarisation of target business representative organisations with the relevant acquis and its implications vis-à-vis their company members; the strengthening of target business representative organisations in the candidate countries of Central and Eastern Europe.

As regards Union small business organisations, the Commission is committed to ensuring an on-going dialogue with them. Addressing the specific concerns of small enterprises and taking their views into account is one of the key recommendations of the European Charter for Small Enterprises.

In this context, in December 2001 the Commission appointed an "SME envoy" to make sure the Commission listens carefully to small business’ needs, and to give them a single access point to the Commission’s services. The Commission has also intensified contacts with small businesses’ horizontal organisations and is increasing the frequency of its regular information and consultation meetings with them.

 

Question no 43 by Jan Andersson (H-0349/02)
 Subject: Timetable for enlargement
 

For most of the applicant countries, the accession process is approaching its final phase. The target is that ten countries should have completed their negotiations by the end of the current year.

In the Commission’s estimation, how many countries will reach the final negotiating stage during the current year? How realistic is the target that all ten countries should reach that stage?

 
  
 

The Laeken European Council on 14 and 15 December 2001 confirmed its determination to meet the objective it had set at Gothenburg, i.e. to bring the negotiations with those candidates that are ready to a successful conclusion by the end of 2002, so that these countries can take part in the Parliament elections of 2004 as members.

The European Council at Laeken also listed those countries that could be ready to meet the Gothenburg objective, provided the present rate of progress of negotiations and reforms in the candidate countries is maintained.
