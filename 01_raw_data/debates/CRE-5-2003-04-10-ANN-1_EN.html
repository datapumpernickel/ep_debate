QUESTIONS TO COUNCIL
Question no 23 by Alain Krivine (H-0173/03)
 Subject: Franco-German charter flight
 

On Monday 3 March 2003 France and Germany collectively expelled 54 Senegalese and Ivorian nationals on a Euralair Horizons charter flight. On board the aircraft hired by the two governments were 30 Ivorians and 23 Senegalese, who had not been admitted to France or whose request for asylum in France had been rejected, and who had been detained in the transit area at Roissy airport, and a Senegalese who had come from Germany by road, having been expelled from that country.

Does the Council consider that Article 4 of Protocol No 4 to the European Convention for the Protection of Human Rights and Fundamental Freedoms and the first paragraph of Article 19 of the Charter of Fundamental Rights, as ratified at the Nice Summit, have been respected?

Does the Council consider that the second paragraph of the same Article 19 of the Charter of Fundamental Rights has been respected with regard to the Ivorians rejected to a country currently in a situation of unrest and that the asylum procedures guaranteed by the Geneva Convention of 28 July 1951 and the protocol of 31 January 1967 relating to the status of refugees have been respected?

What measures does the Council intend to take to ensure that the treaties, in particular the provisions thereof on the right of asylum and protection against removal, expulsion and extradition, are respected?

 
  
 

(FR) The Council does not have information on the decisions taken by the Member States regarding removal from their territory of illegal nationals from third countries.

Simply the fact that those people have been removed using charter planes does not enable us to conclude that these are collective deportations without an individual decision having been made with regard to each of those people.

With regard to the application of asylum, immigration and removal regulations already established in Community law, it is up to the Commission to assess whether the measures taken by the Member States in these areas conform to those regulations.

 

Question no 24 by José Ignacio Salafranca Sánchez-Neyra (H-0175/03)
 Subject: Abolition of the scheme of generalised tariff preferences for some sectors in Central America and the Andean Community, through graduation
 

Does the Council take the view that abolishing tariff preferences (through the adoption by the EC of proposal (COM/2003/0045 final) for a Council regulation implementing Article 12 of Council Regulation (EC) 2501/2001(1)) for the sector live plants, flowers, edible vegetables and fruit in Colombia, as a consequence of applying the graduation mechanism, will help a country in which there are around 26 million people living in poverty, almost 30 000 violent deaths each year and around 10 kidnappings per day, in its fight against drug production and trafficking?

Does the Council not take the view that this measure may jeopardise the fragile economic, social and environmental progress made by Costa Rica in a sector which is concentrated in disadvantaged areas of the country that are highly susceptible to natural disasters, and which provides employment above all for women breadwinners and for immigrants from Nicaragua?

Does the Council not believe that applying the graduation mechanism to the beneficiaries of the 'Drugs' GSP may distort the objectives which led to the creation of what is probably the most successful trade mechanism the EU has ever adopted vis-à-vis developing countries, a mechanism which is vital for Andean and Central American countries at a time when they are facing a regional crisis?

Does the Council believe that the situation in these countries is likely to improve over the months by which the entry into force of the Regulation has been delayed?

Does it not take the view that this may send a discouraging signal to the countries concerned at a time when the Madrid Summit held out the prospect of free trade for Central America and the Andean Community? What reactions has it received from the beneficiary countries?

 
  
 

(EL) On 13 February 2003 the Council received the Commission proposal for Council Regulation referred to by the honourable Member and is in the process of examining it. It aims to take a decision on it before 14 May in line with the timetable set out by the regulatory procedure.

Sectoral graduation is a feature of the European Union's Scheme of Generalised Tariff Preferences. Such graduation is meant to target preferences on those countries other than those which turn out to be able to face international competition without preferential market access. In 2001 the Council decided that as a matter of principle it should also apply to the special arrangements to combat drug production and trafficking, as it already applied to the GSP in general.

The EU remains committed to the commitments made on a possible future agreement, including a Free Trade Agreement, made at the EU-Latin America and Caribbean Summit.

The EU has been informed about the position of some Latin American countries on the proposed application of the graduation scheme within the GSP.

 
 

(1) OJ L 346, 31.12.2001, p. 1.

Question no 28 by Rodi Kratsa-Tsagaropoulou (H-0204/03)
 Subject: War in Iraq and Turkey
 

Some Member States of the European Union (the Netherlands, for instance) recently expressed their concern about the dispatch of Turkish troops to northern Iraq, while others (Belgium and Germany) have stated, through their foreign ministers, Louis Michel and Joschka Fischer, that such a move would lead to a review of all the positions so far adopted by the 15 as regards Turkey's bid for accession to the European Union.

Does the Council have any information about the intentions of the Turkish authorities, and what policy does it intend to pursue over this issue which will seriously exacerbate the crisis in the region? Does it share the views and concerns of the Member States mentioned above? Does it intend to take practical initiatives and measures with a view to the adoption of a common position by the 15 in the event that Turkey pursues such a policy?

 
  
 

(EL) At its meeting in Brussels on 20/21 March, the European Council adopted conclusions calling on all countries of the region to refrain from actions that could lead to further instability in the context of the Iraq war, and which were endorsed by Turkey together with all other acceding and candidate States. By this, the European Council made it clear that the EU expects Turkey to apply restraint. At the same time the European Council expressed readiness to assist those countries that are faced with problems and risks as a result of the conflict, including possible refugee flows.

Turkey has shown awareness of the risks carried by a dispatch of troops, justified according to Ankara, for the purposes of safeguarding its interests inter alia as regards massive flow of refugees; it appears now that Turkey, following recent consultations with the United States in particular, is committed to refrain from such a move. Until these days as a matter of fact no discernible refugee movement has been recorded.

The EU follows very attentively the situation and is staying in close contact with the Turkish authorities on this. The EU trusts in Turkey to address its concerns by other ways than military intervention. Moreover the EU via the Presidency and the Commission has been in contact with the Turkish authorities in order to co-ordinate humanitarian help needed in Iraq through Turkey.

 

Question no 29 by Ioannis Patakis (H-0206/03)
 Subject: Provocative US contempt for treaties on chemical and biological weapons
 

Chemical and biological weapons represent an immense threat to humanity. The USA, the biggest producer and stockpiler of vast quantities of these weapons of mass destruction, refused in 2001 to sign the Geneva Treaty banning the manufacture, stockpiling and use of biological weapons.

The USA is currently carrying out a criminal and barbaric military assault on Iraq, with hundreds of casualties, on the pretext that Iraq has chemical and biological weapons in its possession. What is the Council's position in the face of this provocation and what steps will the Council take to confront the USA with its responsibility for its criminal acts and its obligation to respect international law, which that country is heinously and systematically violating with incalculable consequences for the whole of humanity?

 
  
 

(EL) In February, the European Council unambiguously expressed its concern about the situation in Iraq and about weapons of mass destruction. It urged the Iraqi authorities to cooperate fully and actively with the UN. The Council regrets that, in the end, it was not possible to resolve the situation peacefully.

The Union’s position on the military conflict in Iraq is clearly set out in the European Council Statement on 20 March. On that occasion, the Union decided to intensify work for a comprehensive, coherent and effective multilateral policy of the international community to prevent the proliferation of weapons of mass destruction. As to the Iraqi conflict, the European Council hoped that it would end with the minimum loss of human life and suffering. The honourable Member is certainly well aware that the Council has not taken a position on the question of the legality of the war with Iraq.

The immediate priority must be the welfare of all the Iraqi people. That is why the Council has supported that the UN’s ‘Oil for Food Programme’ be resumed so that humanitarian and medical assistance can continue to be met through that programme. It welcomed very much that the Security Council has unanimously adopted a resolution to that end on 28 March. About 60 % of the Iraqi population depend on the ‘Oil-for-Food’ programme, so it is important that it is not interrupted for a long time.

On the UN’s role in general, the Council remains firmly of the view that the UN must play a central role during and after the conflict, and that the UN system has a unique capacity in coordinating assistance in post-conflict States.

 

Question no 30 by Efstratios Korakas (H-0207/03)
 Subject: Flagrant and provocative violation by the USA of the fundamental rights of detainees
 

Six hundred people suspected of involvement in the al Q'aida terrorist networks have been held without trial at the US base of Guantanamo since the end of 2001. A US Court of Appeal ruled on 12 March 2003 that the 'suspected terrorists' could not invoke any of the rights enshrined in American legislation as they are considered to be aliens who are not on US territory. The Court also ruled that 'they cannot seek release based on violations of the Constitution or other treaties; the courts are not open to them.'

What international initiatives does the Council intend to take and does it intend to raise this matter in the context of the Euro-Atlantic dialogue with a view to stopping the USA's flagrant and provocative violation of the detainees' fundamental rights, which has been condemned by humanitarian and international organisations and gives the USA licence to imprison foreigners extrajudicially without bringing charges and without access to justice on the pretext of fighting terrorism?

 
  
 

(EL) The Union remains strongly committed to the fight against terrorism. As the Council has made clear on a number of occasions in 2001 and 2002, the fight against terrorism must respect human rights and the rule of law.

The Council does not possess the necessary information to determine the status of those held in Guantanamo by the United States. However, the Council believes that, while their status may be in doubt, this does not mean that they can be deprived of their human rights. Equally, there should be no distinction on the basis of nationality, religion or origin. The United States has a responsibility to ensure that these rights are respected.

The Council will continue to make use of the various meetings it has with the United States, within the framework of the New Transatlantic Agenda, to make known its concerns and to urge the United States to act accordingly.

There are reports that certain Members States have refused to issue a visa to the President of Taiwan on the grounds that they are acting in accordance with a common position. Question no 43 by Claude Moraes (H-0193/03)
 Subject: Progress on the Seville European Council conclusions on illegal immigration, external border controls and the proposed European border police
 

What progress has the European Commission made, and what difficulties have been encountered, in relation to the conclusions of the Seville European Council on the question of illegal immigration, external border controls and the proposal for a European border police?

 
  
 

(EN) According to the Seville European Council conclusions and following the Road Map established by the Council, the Commission, the Council and the Member States have carried or are carrying out the actions provided for.

As regards the execution of the initiatives conferred upon the Commission, the Commission wishes to inform the honourable Member that either they have been achieved or they are in progress. They will be completed according to the timing provided for by the road map established by the Justice and Home Affairs Council, following the Council's Plan of 28 February to combat illegal immigration and trafficking of human beings in the Union and the Council's Plan of 13 June 2002 for the management of the external borders of the Member States of the Union.

For example, the review of the list of third countries, whose nationals require visa or are exempt from that requirement has been carried out through the amending Council Regulation EC n°453/2003, adopted by the Council 6 March 2003(1).

And the feasibility study on a common identification system for visa data is almost finalised and the report will be ready soon.

As regards the feasibility of establishing a European Corps of Border Guards, this is a very sensitive, political issue.

The Commission has envisaged the possible creation of such a body as the final target of a dynamic process aimed at developing in stages an integrated management of the external borders.

The Council's Plan, adopted on 13 June 2002, retained this approach and structured it in providing a series of measures and actions to be performed with a tight calendar.

The Council has placed ‘a possible decision on the setting up of a European Corps of Border Guards, composed of joint teams’ of Member States guards and with the sole function of supporting the national services’ at the end of a long process.

The development of joint operations and projects carried out by the Member States would constitute indispensable progress towards the integrated management.

Only after these experiences and an in-depth evaluation of their impact, such an important step might be considered.

The Commission shares this point of view. The Commission is confident of the positive impact of the numerous common activities, such as pilot projects and joint operations, which the Member States are carrying out in order to develop common practices at the external borders and to adopt a common model for the risk analysis and a core curriculum for the border guards training.

The European Council of Thessaloniki of June 2003 will examine various reports in the field of migration and the management of external borders and will provide the Commission with its appreciation of these experiences.

The Commission is sure that its efforts to build up a common integrated policy for the management of the external borders will receive the necessary political support.

 
 

(1) OJ L 69, 13.3.2003

 

Question no 44 by Michl Ebner (H-0194/03)
 Subject: Discrimination - Convention
 

In its answer to my Written Question E-0159/03 the Commission writes that it supports the inclusion of the principle of non-discrimination in the future Constitution of the European Union in the framework of the work of the Convention. In addition, it supports the incorporation of the Charter of Fundamental Rights in the text of the Constitution, whereby Article 21 of the Charter would be elevated to constitutional status. It further agreed with me completely on the issue of taking account in its initiatives of the need to prevent and combat discrimination on grounds of language or membership of a national minority, as well as of the other cases listed in Article 21 of the Charter.

Can the Commission now tell me whether it has already taken action on this matter in the Convention?

Does it happen that ‘small’ matters such as these are overlooked in the heat of the debate on ‘big’ issues?

To what extent is this question being borne in mind in preparing the preliminary drafts of articles for the constitutional treaty?

The Commission, and preferably a member of the Commission taking part in the Convention, is requested to state in detail its position on this.

 
  
 

(FR) First of all, it should be pointed out that the Commission does not have the right of initiative for the Convention, as it does for Community legislation. It takes part in the debate through two members of the Convention, Mr Barnier and Mr Vitorino, and their two alternates. This low numerical representation should be compared to that of Parliament, which has 16 members in the Convention. It is, however, true that both the Commission and the European Parliament have two representatives in the Praesidium of the Convention (which has 12 members) which produces the initial drafts of articles.

Regarding the subject with which the honourable Member is concerned, the Commission has already made its view known in the answer to written question no. E-0159/03. The Commission in no way considers the issue of discrimination to be a secondary one.

The Commission:

- supports the incorporation of the Charter of Fundamental Rights into the Constitution, which will make the Charter legally binding so that it can be directly invoked by citizens. Article 21 of the Charter forbids any type of discrimination, expressly citing discrimination on grounds of language or membership of a minority;

- also supports the inclusion of an additional provision generally forbidding discrimination, in addition to discrimination on grounds of nationality. Including this type of provision in the Constitution is necessary in order to create a legal basis to enable the Union to combat discrimination, insofar as it has been recognised that even if the Charter is incorporated into the Constitution that does not create new powers for the Union.

These opinions have been expressed both within the Praesidium and at the plenary sessions on the first articles of the Constitution on 27 February by Mr Barnier. At that plenary session, Mr Barnier supported the full incorporation of the Charter into the Constitution, covering all types of discrimination. He also suggested that the Constitution should contain a specific provision banning all types of discrimination.

 

Question no 45 by Joachim Wuermeling (H-0202/03)
 Subject: EU rules relating to immigrants on national labour markets
 

A newspaper report (in the Süddeutsche Zeitung of 20 March 2003) attributed to Antonio Vitorino, Member of the Commission, a statement on immigration policy to the effect that no Commission proposal forces any Member State to admit ‘one single additional immigrant into the country’.

Is it true that the proposals on the status of asylum-seekers and the mass influx of refugees provide that immigrants are entitled to access to employment? Is it true that the directive on the access to employment of self-employed third-country nationals includes a provision that immigrants who meet the conditions laid down in the directive are entitled to a work permit?

How can the Commissioner’s statements be reconciled with those proposals? Will the Commission support a clarification in the future Constitutional Treaty which states that decisions about the number and kind of immigrants allowed on the labour market are the prerogative of the Member States?

 
  
 

(EN) The press statement quoted by the honourable Member refers to the debate on the Commission proposal for a Council Directive on the conditions of entry and residence of third country nationals for the purpose of paid employment and self-employed economic activities(1).

When the Parliament discussed Ms Cusi´s report on this proposal(2) on 11 February 2003, the Commission made it clear that it does not intend to decide by directive the numbers of immigrants that Union economies and societies are prepared to absorb. The proposed directive leaves that decision with the Member States; only they, together with their civil societies and local authorities know how much their societies are capable of integrating.

Article 26 of this proposal explicitly acknowledges this fact and provides an option for Member States to adopt horizontal measures (e.g. national ceilings, temporary suspension of issuance of residence permits) in order to limit the admission of third country workers, if Member States consider this necessary, taking into account the overall capacity to receive and to integrate third country nationals on their territory or in specific regions thereof.

The Commission considers that immigration for the purpose of exercising employed or self-employed economic activities is a cornerstone of immigration policy. This is presently enshrined in Article 63 of the EC Treaty, and is also reflected in the draft Treaty text under discussion in the Convention, which would provide for the adoption of measures on the conditions of entry and stay of third country nationals.

The two other Directives, to which the honourable Member refers, deal with asylum policy, not with economic migration:

- Council Directive 2003/9/EC of 27 January 2003 laying down minimum standards for the reception of asylum seekers(3) lays down minimum standards for the reception of asylum seekers in Member States. Regarding admission to work, its Article 11 provides that ‘Member States shall determine a period of time, starting from the date on which an application for asylum was lodged, during which an applicant shall not have access to the labour market. If a decision at first instance has not been taken within one year of the presentation of an application for asylum and this delay cannot be attributed to the applicant, Member States shall decide the conditions for granting access to the labour market for the applicant. Access to the labour market shall not be withdrawn during appeals procedures, where an appeal against a negative decision in a regular procedure has suspensive effect, until such time as a negative decision on the appeal is notified. For reasons of labour market policies, Member States may give priority to Union citizens and nationals of States parties to the Agreement on the European Economic Area and also to legally resident third-country nationals.’

- Directive 2001/55 of 20 July 2001 on minimum standards for giving temporary protection in the event of a mass influx of displaced persons and on measures promoting a balance of efforts between Member States in receiving such persons and bearing the consequences thereof(4) establishes minimum standards for giving temporary protection in the event of a mass influx of displaced persons from third countries who are unable to return to their country of origin. Regarding admission to work, its Article 12 provides that ‘The Member States shall authorise, for a period not exceeding that of temporary protection, persons enjoying temporary protection to engage in employed or self-employed activities, subject to rules applicable to the profession, as well as in activities such as educational opportunities for adults, vocational training and practical workplace experience. For reasons of labour market policies, Member States may give priority to Union citizens and citizens of States bound by the Agreement on the European Economic Area and also to legally resident third-country nationals who receive unemployment benefit. The general law in force in the Member States applicable to remuneration, access to social security systems relating to employed or self-employed activities and other conditions of employment shall apply.’

Question no 46 by Bart Staes (H-0134/03)
 Subject: Copyright and related rights in the information society
 

Directive 2001/29/EC(1) tries to strike a balance between, on the one hand, watertight protection of copyright and, on the other, free development of high-technology equipment, even if that may be used improperly in order, for example, to make illicit copies of works protected by copyright. Article 5(2)(b) of the Directive provides for exceptions in respect of reproductions made for private use ‘on condition that the rightholders receive fair compensation…’. It is principally, but not exclusively, the opportunity provided by those exceptions which distributors of content, such as record companies and software firms, seize in order to (a) stop supporting authors financially and promoting them and (b) keep the price of CDs, inter alia, at an especially high level.

Is the Commission aware of this problem, and can it indicate what measures it is thinking of taking in order, on the one hand, to protect the rights of authors and their legal representatives and, on the other, to defend honest consumers against having to pay excessive prices for, inter alia, original music and software packages?

 
  
 

(FR) Article 5(2)b of Directive 2001/29/EC enables Member States to provide for exceptions or limitations to the exclusive right to reproduction established in Article 2 of the Directive and granted to authors for their work and performers, record producers and film producers for the other items protected. The exception established in Article 5(2)b allows private copying, for which the legal provisions are not harmonised at Community level. This is permitted on the condition that the rightholders receive ‘fair compensation’. It is the job of the Member States to ensure that the fair compensation is effectively distributed to the different rightholders. The form, arrangements and level for that fair compensation are given in Recital 35 of the Directive, which points out the quite broad degree of evaluation reserved for the Member States in order to determine it according to ‘the particular circumstances of each case’. However, the system of exceptions must comply with the three-stage test established in Article 5(5) of the Directive: the exception must apply to certain special cases, must not conflict with normal exploitation of the work or other protected items and must not unreasonably prejudice the legitimate interests of the rightholder. The Member States are currently transposing this Directive and the Commission is actively ensuring that this complies fully with the text of the Directive. Would the Commission also agree that the public sector needs to strengthen its commitment and re-focus its resources if progress on this issue is to be made?

 
  
 

(EN) Recognising the importance of the water challenge, and in particular of providing access to safe water as a basic need, the Union launched the Community Water Initiative during the World Summit on Sustainable Development in Johannesburg.

The Community Water Initiative builds on the policy defined in a Communication from the Commission and the resulting Resolution adopted by the Council in May 2002. Within the over-arching framework of ‘Integrated Water Resources Management’, based on a river basin approach, a first priority of Community action is to ensure a supply of sufficient, good quality drinking water, adequate sanitation and hygiene to every human being, especially the poorest and with a clear focus on the needs of women and children, with the general objective of reducing poverty and improving people's health, quality of life and livelihood opportunities.

In order to achieve these goals, good governance, political and sectoral reforms are necessary. Awareness raising, institutional strengthening and capacity building activities must be supported, while ensuring participation of all stakeholders and ownership of policies and strategies. Partnerships between public, private and civil society actors have to be promoted, ensuring that those partnerships remain equitable and transparent, allowing free and reversible choice on water services management, safeguarding consumers' and investors' interests and maintaining high standards of environmental protection. Pricing of water services is important in ensuring financial sustainability, although meeting the basic needs of poor and vulnerable groups requires the design of appropriate tariff structures and collection systems.

The question of private sector involvement refers to a larger one, that of the reform of state-owned enterprises. While recognising that public companies needs to strengthen its commitment and re-focus its resources, there is a growing awareness that an adequate contribution is needed for supporting the Community Global Water Initiative. However, before deciding on a particular solution (including those involving the private sector), there is a need to objectively examine all the options in order to select the most appropriate one. There is a clear need to keep an open mind on these reform issues, by putting in place adequate regulatory frameworks and establishing monitoring mechanisms to ensure the protection of public interest.

The deadlines for the implementation of two very important Directives by the Member States fall in 2003. Under the Community Action Programme to Combat Discrimination, a Legal Working Group has been established, made up of representatives of Member States in charge of the transposition of both directives. This Group provides Member States with the opportunity to exchange information and good practice regarding the transposition of the two directives.

The Ministry of Labour has organised a working group on the transposition of Directive 2000/78/EC.

Question no 79 by John Walls Cushnahan (H-0209/03)
 Subject: Car prices in the EU and Irish Vehicle Registration Tax
 

The latest report from the Commission on car prices within the EU shows that some convergence in price differentials is taking place, particularly within the eurozone, although significant price disparities still persist. However, in Ireland, car prices are massively increased by the imposition of the Vehicle Registration Tax which totally undermines any price convergence that is occurring across the EU. What measures will the Commission take to force the Irish Government to reduce this tax so that Irish car buyers may finally benefit in real terms from the Single Market?

 
  
 

The Commission would like to inform the honourable Member that, according to the Commission's latest report of 27 February 2003 on car price differentials within the European Union on 1 November 2002, Ireland is the fifth cheapest market in the Euro-zone as regards car pre-tax prices.

As far as retail prices for passenger cars are concerned, it is true that these prices are substantially affected by both the level of pre-tax prices and the existing taxes on passenger cars, which are very diversified throughout the Community in terms of structure and levels. So far there is little Community legislation or harmonisation of national fiscal provisions applied by the Member States. Approximation of vehicle taxation levels proved very difficult to achieve, due to the unanimity rule needed for decisions on fiscal matters.

The Commission presented in 2002 a Communication on taxation of passenger cars in the European Union(1), which intended to launch a discussion with the Council, the Parliament, and main stakeholders on future options for action at Community and national levels. This Communication is currently under discussion within the Parliament, and hopefully, the Greek Presidency will bring it for discussion to the Council.

One of the main policy recommendations in this Communication is that Registration tax levels should be gradually reduced, with a view of Registration Tax stabilisation at low levels, and preferably with a view to its total abolition. To ensure the feasibility of this recommendation, the Commission suggested that the reduction of Registration Tax should take place in parallel with a gradual transfer of revenue from Registration Tax to Annual Circulation Tax, and to some extent to fuel taxes, over a transition period of five to ten years.

The Commission, based on the principles of the Communication, and in the light of the results of the consultation process, will consider submitting proposals for Community legislation, which would, inter alia, establish a Registration Tax refund system and general rules with regard to the method of calculation of Registration Tax on used cars, which are transferred between Member States.

These measures would, if adopted by the Council, on one hand provide definitive solutions to problems relating to double payment, or to disproportionate payment of Registration Tax, and on the other hand, would contribute to a considerable convergence in retail prices, both for new and second hand passenger cars.

Question no 80 by Bastiaan Belder (H-0210/03)
 Subject: Information programmes concerning HIV/AIDS in developing countries
 

Is the Commission aware of the encouraging results achieved by information programmes concerning HIV/AIDS in Uganda and Zambia, programmes which make marital fidelity and abstinence integral components of the fight against the disease(1)?

To what extent does the Commission see the good results achieved by these information programmes as a legitimate contribution to efforts to reduce the number of abortions?

Do the concepts of marital fidelity and abstinence have a place in EU activities in the sphere of development cooperation, given that sexual behavioural models play an important role in many information programmes? After all, handing out contraceptives in itself never proves effective in combating HIV/AIDS.

Would the Commission prefer to integrate the concepts of marital fidelity and abstinence into all relevant EU aid measures and programmes, or will it acknowledge the relevance of these values only on the basis of agreements with all the local groups involved?

 
  
 

(EN) The Commission takes note of the reference to programmes concerning human immunodeficiency virus (HIV)/acquired immune deficiency syndrome (AIDS) in Uganda and Zambia and would welcome further information and evidence based results on these particular programmes before commenting further.

The Programme for Action adopted at the International Conference on Population and Development held in Cairo in 1994 lays down clear principles regarding sexual and reproductive health and rights. The Commission’s actions and policies in reproductive and sexual health are firmly rooted in the Programme for Action which states in paragraph 8.31 that:

‘Programmes to reduce the spread of HIV infection should give high priority to information, education and communication campaigns to raise awareness and emphasise behavioural change. Sex education and information should be provided to both those infected and those not infected, and especially to adolescents. Health providers, including family-planning providers, need training in counselling on sexually transmitted diseases and HIV infection, including the assessment and identification of high-risk behaviours needing special attention and services; training in the promotion of safe and responsible sexual behaviour, including voluntary abstinence, and condom use’.

The Commission supports this approach and emphasises the need to provide information that is clear, objective and which presents all the choices available.

Education is an important component of comprehensive programmes for the prevention of HIV/AIDS and improving reproductive and sexual health. A recent Union statement delivered at the United Nations Commission on Population and Development on 1 April 2003 endorses the need for good quality sex education that informs and enables young people to make responsible choices.

The values within the Programme for Action are about ensuring rights and promoting choices to make private decisions about one’s lifestyle and sexuality. It is not Commission policy to advocate one lifestyle choice over another.
