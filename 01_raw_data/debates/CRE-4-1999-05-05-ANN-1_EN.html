1. Questions to the Council

 

Question No 33 by Karin Riis‐Jørgensen (H−0388/99)

Subject: German taxes on Danish coach companies

Danish coach operators are experiencing major problems in securing approval for their fare scales when transporting tourists in Germany and are therefore required to pay VAT according to the higher German market price.

As a result, Danish coach companies are having to pay an unjustifiably high level of VAT. In practice, it is an unreasonable burden on coach companies in Denmark to require them to ascertain how they are to calculate their price per kilometre since, for example, the tax office in Flensburg concludes individual agreements with coach operators, refuses to approve operating accounts approved by Danish state‐authorised auditors and often changes practice as far as the basis of calculation and administrative procedures are concerned. As a result, Germany has been holding back sums due to Danish operators, in one case for up to one and a half years, without paying interest on them.

Does the Council take the view that the above situation is in conformity with the rules of the internal market? If so, will it explain why in detail?

Answer

I must inform Mrs Riis‐Jørgensen that the Council has not yet had any cause to deal with the problem she raises in her question.

I would like to take this opportunity to point out that this is a problem relating to the smooth functioning of the internal market. Mrs Riis‐Jørgensen also assumes this in her question. It would therefore primarily be the responsibility of the Commission to check compliance with current rules and if necessary to take the appropriate measures.

Question No 34 by Heidi Hautala (H−0392/99)

Subject: Democratic control over the interception of telecommunications

The draft Convention on Mutual Assistance in Criminal Matters contains some particularly sensitive provisions with regard to the interception of telecommunications. It is technically feasible to listen to a subject who is in a different country from the listening authority. Differing views prevail in the Member States firstly as to whether this should be permitted, and secondly as to whether a licence from the target country is required or if a notification to this effect is sufficient. In Finland there has been public concern about the interception of telecommunications, because all the preparatory documents concerning police cooperation and this Convention are secret. Can the Council guarantee the democratic control of the interception of telecommunications and the legal protection of the subjects of such interception? Does the Council intend to define the offences it regards as justifying the interception of telecommunications? How does the Council propose to guarantee that such interception will remain, in practice as well as in theory, the exclusive preserve of the competent authorities of the subject's own country? Does the Council propose to publish a summary of the differences between the EU Member States' provisions governing the interception of telecommunications? How does the Council propose to consult Parliament on the draft Convention pursuant to Article 39 of the Treaty of Amsterdam (former Art. K.11)?

Answer

The purpose of the draft Convention on Mutual Assistance in Criminal Matters between the Member States of the European Union is to complete the European Convention of 20 April 1959 on Mutual Assistance in Criminal Matters. The Council is examining in particular the inclusion of rules on the interception of telecommunications for the purpose of criminal investigations in EU Member States. Agreement on provisions in this area is not only in the interests of effectively combating serious crime but is particularly urgently needed to guarantee the legitimacy of cross‐border interception measures:

‐ Requests for telephone interception have hitherto been approved on the basis of the 1959 Convention, although this does not contain any specific rules concerning the interception of telecommunications. For the new satellite telecommunications systems there are no provisions whatsoever concerning interception. What makes such situations unusual is that for interception purposes a link‐up may be required in a ground station in one Member State while the interception subject is in another Member State.

There is agreement between the Member States that where the Member State on whose territory the interception subject is located is, for specific technical reasons, not required to provide technical assistance for the interception, that Member State must nevertheless be involved by the Member State carrying out the interception. Such involvement is needed so that the Member State concerned can ensure that the interception of a person on its territory is in line with its domestic laws, and in particular with fundamental rights. The details of a regulation to this effect are currently being examined by the Council.

‐ The draft does not contain a definition of the criminal offences that justify the issuing of an interception order, as it deals exclusively with relations between the Member States in cross‐border matters. The Council agrees, however, that the interception of telecommunications is to be judged in particular in relation to the European Convention for the Protection of Human Rights and Fundamental Freedoms and the principle of proportionality.

‐ There are no plans to limit interception measures to the authorities in the subject"s own country, nor would this be appropriate in view of the particular nature of the new, modern satellite telecommunications described earlier. This would mean that a criminal who uses modern satellite telephones could not be intercepted, in contrast to a criminal who uses a standard telephone. The planned rules will guarantee that satellite telecommunications can also be intercepted.

‐ National provisions on the interception of telecommunications in the Member States have been examined in the context of the European judicial network. An appropriate form of publication is being considered for the results. In view of the different national regulations the Council will examine in accordance with Article 131 of the Treaty of Amsterdam whether it will be necessary to bring Member States" provisions into line with each other in order to improve cooperation.

‐ The European Parliament was consulted on the draft Convention on Mutual Assistance in Criminal Matters under the UK Presidency. Following the entry into force of the Treaty of Amsterdam the Council will consult the European Parliament once again in accordance with the new rules.

Subject: Kashmir

What steps is the Council going to take to ensure compliance with UN resolutions on a referendum on Kashmir? The presidency shares the view of the honourable Member that the conflict in Kashmir is the most intractable problem besetting relations between India and Pakistan. The results of the meeting in Lahore were welcomed by the European Union, which now urges both parties to implement the Lahore agreement as soon as possible.

Question No 38 by Richard Howitt (H−0399/99)

Subject: Millennium Round

Can the Council tell Parliament which Member States have initiated, supported, or spoken in opposition to the European Union's proposal for a new and comprehensive Millennium Round of negotiations in the World Trade Organisation and on which occasions they have done so? How does the Council expect to consult the European Parliament on this question?

Answer

All WTO member states are contractually committed to hold further negotiations in the WTO from 1999/2000 in important areas (including the agricultural and services sectors). The EU Member States and the Commission agreed some time ago that it was in the interests of the EU to conduct negotiations about these sectors together with other matters of interest in a comprehensive WTO round. The Commission and the EU Member States coordinate the EU's WTO policy in committee 113.

The EC Treaty makes no provision for official consultation of the European Parliament by the Council in this field. However, the Council sets great store by the fact that the Commission as the negotiating body should report to the European Parliament comprehensively and in good time about the forthcoming EU negotiations.

Question No 40 by Mark Watts (H−0331/99)

Subject: Outdoor religious slaughter in France

Over 40 000 sheep were ritually slaughtered outdoors in France this March during this year's Eid‐el‐Kabir festival. This was done in defiance of the law as both EU and French law prohibit outdoor religious slaughter.

The animals were roughly dragged – often with their front and back legs tied together – to the slaughter point. There they were held down on wooden trestles on the ground and their throats were cut while they were fully conscious. Often the slaughter was carried out by untrained people who sawed backwards and forwards across the animals' throats sometimes with blunt knives. In many cases the animals took several minutes to die, often in great agony.

Will the Commission now take action to require France to enforce the prohibition in Council Directive 93/119/EC(1) on outdoor religious slaughter and thereby prevent further barbaric killings taking place at next year's Eid‐el‐Kabir.

Answer

Following intervention of the Commission, France confirmed that Directive 93/119/EC on the protection of animals at the time of slaughter or killing has been fully transposed within its territory. However, the French authorities have admitted that in certain urban areas having large Muslim populations there are inadequate slaughterhouse capacities to enable the considerable number of animals required for the celebration of Eid‐el‐Kabir at the time of the festival to be slaughtered in accordance with all the applicable legal requirements.

A number of local authorities have, for reasons of public order and public health, found it necessary to designate certain sites outside slaughterhouses for the purpose of celebration of the festival.

The Commission, taking into account the extreme sensitivity of the issue, has raised the matter within the Standing Veterinary Committee.

Most Member States have underlined that they have transposed the directive in such a way to prohibit slaughter outside the slaughterhouses. Two Member States, Belgium and the United Kingdom, however admitted that they allowed in their national law slaughtering outside slaughterhouses for the celebration of this festival. They realise that they had interpreted Community legislation incorrectly and will change their law in order to bring it back into line with Community legislation.

Apart from France, none of the other Member States seems to have capacity problems with the celebration of the festival Eid‐el‐Kabir or at least could organise the celebration of the festival in such a way that these problems do not arise. The Commission is in favour of a practical solution for the special problem concerning capacity.

Besides the capacity problem the honourable Member refers to the bad treatment of the animals during restraining and slaughtering. This is of course a distinct problem.

Bad treatment of the animals during restraining and slaughtering is not acceptable. This is a problem that can be solved easily if there is efficient control by the authorities. The Commission will therefore contact the French authorities in order to give them the opportunity to react on this problem.

Question No 41 by Jens‐Peter Bonde (H−0352/99)

Subject: Subsidising the destruction of food

Will the Commission propose once and for all that there should be no more subsidies for the destruction of food?

Answer

Short‐term surpluses are an inherent feature of fruit and vegetable production. In a market where small surpluses can cause significant drops in market price, withdrawals are seen as being the only realistic way to stabilise the fruit and vegetable market in the short term.

Withdrawals as such are relatively small, globally never exceeding 10 % of annual Community production in nearly 3 decades. Nonetheless, the Commission recognises the public negative image of withdrawals and addressed two issues in the 1996 reform of the common organisation of the market. Firstly, it strives to reduce the overall level of withdrawals by providing financing to improve the marketing of produce and by making the withdrawal option less attractive, and in any case not profitable.

Secondly, it aims to reduce the level of destruction of withdrawn produce by developing various alternative uses, such as free distribution in schools, charities and humanitarian aid. Other uses include animal feed, non‐food purposes and distillation.

Question No 42 by John Iversen (H−0353/99)

Subject: Shipments of calves

Shipments of calves from Denmark to the Netherlands must comply with the provisions of Directive 91/628/EEC(2) on the protection of animals during transport.

However, many reports suggest that this is not the case. Has the Commission checked whether the Member States are complying with the directive? Will it indicate the result of any inspection visits?

Does it have current plans to propose a total ban on the transport of calves under three months old, for example?

Answer

Transport of calves is permitted in accordance with Directive 91/628/EEC on the protection of animals during transport. The point 1 of the Annex of the above‐mentioned directive states that new‐born animals in which the navel has not completely healed shall not be transported.

The Commission did not receive any complaint in respect of transport of calves from Denmark to the Netherlands. As long as calves are transported according to the requirements of the directive, there should be no objection from the point of view of animal welfare to transport these animals. The aim of Community legislation in this field is to improve the condition of transport of live animals and not to ban this practice.

With regard to enforcement of the rules, the primary responsibility remains with the Member States. The Commission can nevertheless, verify the correct application of the rules within the Community and a number of inspections in the Member States concerning this specific field, have been carried out. The reports on these inspections are also available on the Commission website.

In cases where it is discovered that the directive is not being properly enforced by the authorities of the Member States, the Commission normally intervenes with those authorities and if the matter is not satisfactorily resolved, the Commission may open the infringement procedure laid down in Article 226 of the EC Treaty (ex Article 169).

Question No 44 by Mihail Papayannakis (H−0376/99)

Subject: ‘Mad Cow Disease'

According to studies by British research institutes and concurring reports in The Lancet, Liberation and Le Monde, 'mad cow disease' continues to exact a tragic and alarming toll.

According to 1998 statistics, in the United Kingdom the number of fatal cases among young people of new variant CJD has increased steeply. At the same time it now seems certain that ‘mad cow disease' is crossing over to sheep. Is the Commission aware of this, and if so what is its position? Is it satisfied with the measures taken so far and the way they have been implemented in the United Kingdom?

In view of the above developments, does it intend to propose new measures or a radical overhaul its policy so far which is closely linked to the protection of public health in the EU?

Answer

The Commission is aware of the information to which the honourable Member is referring and shares his concerns on the implications of an increase in the number of deaths caused by variant Creutzfeldt‐Jakob Disease (vCJD), and the possible crossing over of bovine spongiform encephalopathy (BSE) into small ruminants. Its position was and is firmly based on this possibility of a cross over; the current preventive measures with respect to the animal waste treatment standards, the feed ban and the specified risk material elimination rules all address cattle, as well as sheep and goats. In addition the mandatory Community surveillance programme covers in principle all animal transmissible spongiform encephalopathies (TSEs) in all animal species.

There is to date no evidence neither from the regular inspections carried out by the Food and Veterinary Office of the Commission in the United Kingdom, nor from the scientific committees, requiring the Commission to amend its policy with respect to the United Kingdom.

The Commission is following carefully the evolution of the situation as regards the incidence of vCJD, and will take any necessary public health measures, if justified. Should such recommendations be issued in future, the Commission will adapt its measures to continue to ensure the attainment of a high level of health protection based on the best available scientific evidence.

Question No 45 by Karin Riis‐Jørgensen (H−0389/99)

Subject: Export of halal meat products to Norway

If a Danish company wishes to export halal meat products to Norway, it encounters trade barriers through being unable to obtain the necessary important licence in Norway. The fact is that it has still not been possible for the company in question, despite several attempts with the ‘Eggscentralen' in Norway including using a third party in Norway, to obtain an import licence. The company meets all Danish requirements concerning halal meat products. Should the company one day manage to obtain a licence, their products will nevertheless be subject to high customs duties.

Does the Commission consider that Norway is acting in accordance with the EEA agreement? Furthermore, does it know of any other areas where Norway is preventing EU Member States from exporting to Norway?

Answer

In general, Norway has taken over the veterinary provisions incorporated in the acquis communautaire in the context of the EEA Agreement. Annex I of this Agreement contains a list of all the directives concerning meat and meat products as well as Council Directive 93/119/EC on the protection of animals at the time of slaughter or killing. The Commission has not been informed of any general veterinary problems relating to trade between Norway and the Member States.

The particular matter mentioned by the honourable Member relates to trade in meat from animals slaughtered according to certain religious rites, known as ritual slaughter.

This type of trade has not been formally regulated by Community legislation. In the first instance, the Commission believes that this type of trade should be possible if the meat is being sold to meet the requirements of certain religious groups.

The Commission will contact the Norwegian authorities to find out why this trade is not being allowed and will set out its position after having received an explanation from the Norwegian authorities.

Question No 46 by Nikitas Kaklamanis (H−0321/99)

Subject: Wilful omission of Greek islands from euro coins

In its answer to my Oral Question H−0138/99 the Commission stated for the first time that there are after all two criteria with regard to the depiction of the islands of Europe on the new euro coins: an area of at least 2500 km2 for each island and an area of more than 5000 km2 for a group of islands. It is therefore surprising to find that one of the most important archipelagos in Europe, the Aegean islands, which form an area much larger than 5000 km2, has been completely omitted from the new coins, and that islands such as Lesbos, Chios, Rhodes, Samos, Ikaria, Thasos, the Cyclades, the Sporades and others do not appear. Can the Commission say why these islands have been omitted, who is responsible and how this astonishing inaccuracy, which constitutes an affront to the nationalist sentiment of all Greeks, will be rectified?

Answer

Leaving aside the issues of European and national symbolism, the depiction of islands and groups of islands on euro coins must comply with certain aesthetic and technical criteria. That is why limits have been set in relation to the sizes of the islands and archipelagos that can be depicted. As a result, islands must have an area of over 2 500 km2 and archipelagos must have an area of at least 5 000 km2 .

The groups of islands mentioned by the honourable Member are clearly too small to meet these criteria: the largest group of islands in the Aegean Sea, the Dodekanissos islands, have a total area of 2 714 km2 , with Rhodes accounting for 1 398 km2 of this. The Cyclades, another large archipelago, has a total area of 2 571 km2 , while the Sporades has an area of scarcely 400 km2 and the island of Skyros represents around half of this, with an area of 200 km2 .

The depiction of Greece and its islands responds to the criteria set out by the Council and has been approved by the Council. There are therefore no plans to change the designs, and the production of coins has begun in most Member States on the basis of the approved designs.

Question No 47 by Concepció Ferrer (H−0322/99)

Subject: Initiatives to assist the textile and clothing industry

On 14 July 1998 the European Parliament adopted a resolution (A4‐0224/98) on the Commission action plan to increase the competitiveness of the European textile and clothing industry.

Can the Commission indicate what action it has taken, or will be taking, to act on the European Parliament's proposals, in particular to stimulate access to third‐country markets, by drafting a regulation accommodating Community operations to promote SMEs' exports or by coordinating national promotion campaigns?

Answer

With a view to full integration of textiles and clothing products into GATT 1994 by 1 January 2005 as a result of the application of the WTO Agreement on Textiles and Clothing (ATC), the Commission's main priority is to prepare the Community for the world‐wide competitive environment in the textiles and clothing sector. In this context the improvement for access of Community textiles and clothing products to third countries' markets is of prime importance.

1999 being at the middle of the transitional period of implementation of the ATC, preparatory work by the Commission is intensifying. As part of the strategy, the likely position of the Community textiles industry in the post−2005 environment will be examined, as well as the main obstacles that should be addressed in key markets. This is to help ensure that when general GATT rules will be applied to the textiles sector from 2005, there shall be a level playing field in textiles trade.

Better access to market of third countries will be sought through bilateral negotiations, a close monitoring of the implementation of third countries' obligations vis‐à‐vis the Community using whenever appropriate the relevant WTO mechanisms, and stepped up efforts in the fight against fraud and piracy. Another priority will be a smooth application of the bilateral and multilateral agreements concluded by the Community with third countries.

Actions for the pursuit of better market access for Community producers to third countries' markets will include the establishment of a list of priority market access objectives along with an assessment of the means to achieve them. These actions may, among others, take place in the framework of the preparation of the third phase of GATT integration, by entering into negotiations with other WTO countries to obtain better market access, in the context of the overall WTO trade negotiations that may start from year 2000 and the continuation of the pending WTO accession negotiations, notably those with Russia and China.

Communication with industry and other interested parties will be strengthened to facilitate the development of an active market access policy and to prepare the Community's positions in the WTO.

Question No 48 by Tony Cunningham (H−0323/99)

Subject: Threat to great apes from illegal bushmeat trade

The mainly illegal bushmeat trade in Africa, contravening CITES regulations, poses a serious threat to the survival of the great apes of Africa, and other CITES‐listed protected species. In view of the links between the rapidly growing timber industry in Africa and the major increases in the commercial bushmeat trade, there is a clear need for responsible action by logging companies to discourage and restrict the hunting of or trade in protected animal species which is specifically outlawed under the international CITES convention.

What actions has the Commission taken to follow‐up the ACP‐EU Assembly resolution passed in March 1996, which called on the EU to require that EU companies involved in logging follow a code of conduct which ensures their activities do not assist or facilitate hunting, killing or trade in protected species? Has the Commission raised this for discussion with any of the 130 EU timber company subsidiaries operating in Africa, and has it drafted a code of conduct based on this resolution?

What further actions does the Commission now intend to take to combat the threat to the survival of these species?

Answer

The Commission is well aware of the grave situation of the Great Apes in Africa. Actions have been taken at several levels:

Commercial trade in parts or derivates of specimens of great ape species is prohibited under the provisions of CITES as it is for all other species threatened with extinction included in its Appendix I and Annex A of Council Regulation (EC) 338/97 (OJ L 61 of 3.3.97), which implements CITES in the Community.

A ‘code of conduct' concerning issues of sustainability including biodiversity in tropical forests is currently developed at the international level. The stewardship principles under development will make clear reference to biodiversity including the Great Apes. The Community supports this international effort in various ways. It finances the Website ‘Certification Information System' (http: /www.efi.fi/cis/). In addition, the Community has funded a study on indicators and criteria of sustainable forest management undertaken by CIFOR for the discussions in the International Forum on Forests. These include biodiversity indicators. Furthermore, practical pilot exercises in Central and West Africa are supported. These include dialogues with various stakeholders including government, NGOs and logging companies. Finally, in Central Africa the Community supports a regional programme called ECOFAC which as one of its components tries to address the problems of hunting and bushmeat trade. The activities include: (i) awareness raising on biodiversity issues among local people; (ii) improvement of surveillance; (iii) development of sustainable natural resource management systems, and (iv) exploration of alternative income sources for the local population.

Question No 49 by Patricia McKenna (H−0326/99)

Subject: Kosovo crisis

What is the Commission doing to relieve the ongoing suffering of the refugees in and around the Kosovo conflict area? Question No 50 by Brian Crowley (H−0378/99)

Subject: Housing and the Kosovo crisis

In view of the Kosovo refugee crisis, will the Commission state if it has any plans to allow EU funds to be used for urgent temporary housing/renovation projects in the Member States?

Answer

On December 16 1998 the Commission proposed a Joint Action to provide practical support to the reception and voluntary return of refugees, displaced persons and asylum seekers. At the request of the special Justice and Home Affairs Council of 7 April, this proposal was amended to also include measures on emergency assistance to those who have fled as a result of recent events in Kosovo.

In addition, the Commission can state that, in the framework of the structural funds, Member States may also, under limited conditions, initiate projects to renovate accommodation when this forms part of a project to renew the fabric of run‐down urban districts.

Question No 51 by Katerina Daskalaki (H−0379/99)

Subject: Danger of environmental damage in the Balkans

Owing to bombing raids on refineries and fuel depots in Yugoslavia, thousands of tonnes of toxic and hazardous substances have escaped into the atmosphere. Moreover, it is reported that the GAU 8/A Avenger weapons system is being used in the bombing: its projectiles are reinforced with radioactive uranium and have the potential to cause catastrophic environmental damage. The radioactive uranium which is released into the atmosphere creates a toxic cloud which can travel quite far and falls to earth as soon as it rains. What measures will the Commission take to protect those living in the EU and uphold their right to balanced information and the re‐launching of MED‐TV?

Answer

The honourable Member has raised the issue of the closure of MED‐TV, a satellite channel licensed by the United Kingdom authorities.

Community Law applicable in this area is the Television Without Frontiers Directive (Council Directive 89/552/EEC as amended by Directive 97/36/EC of the European Parliament and of the Council). This directive lays down the legal framework for television broadcasting activities within the Union.

The directive provides that each Member State shall ensure that all television broadcasts transmitted by broadcasters under its jurisdiction comply with the rules of the system of law applicable to broadcasts intended for the public in that Member State. This obligation of ‘home country' control is the basis for granting ‘freedom of reception and retransmission' across the Union.

According to the information available to the Commission, the Independent Television Commission, which is the licensing authority in the United Kingdom, revoked the licence of MED‐TV on 23 April 1999. This was pursuant to its duty to ensure compliance with United Kingdom Law. According to the United Kingdom authorities, the channel was in breach of its licence requirements, and also in breach of UK Law. In particular, the United Kingdom authorities have informed the Commission that MED‐TV broadcast programmes which encouraged acts of violence and which were likely to encourage or incite to crime or lead to disorder; the revocation of the licence followed a period of monitoring by the ITC which had issued a number of warnings to the channel that it was in breach of its licence requirements and the ITC code.

Under these circumstances, the United Kingdom authorities appear to have been executing their obligations under the Television Directive and thus under Community Law.
