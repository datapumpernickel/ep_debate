QUESTIONS TO COUNCIL
Question no 17 by Astrid Thors (H-0552/03)
 Subject: Convention on Consent to Marriage, Minimum Age for Marriage and Registration of Marriages
 

The human rights dimension has become increasingly important in EU policy, both externally and internally. An important but seldom highlighted element of human rights is equality in marriage and the right to enter into marriage. Information in the public domain indicates that infringements of this right occur even in the Member States of the European Union. Has the Council taken into consideration the Convention on Consent to Marriage, Minimum Age for Marriage and Registration of Marriages at all?

According to the information available, only 9 Member States and 4 applicant countries have ratified this Convention.

Has the Council taken this Convention into account in relation to the applicant countries even though it is not among the conventions which the Commission has taken into consideration in the progress reports?

 
  
 

(IT) The Council reminds the Honourable Member that Article 5 of the Accession Treaty states that the new Member States undertake to accede to the conventions provided for in Article 293 of the EC Treaty and to those that are inseparable from the attainment of the objectives of the EC Treaty.

When the acquis communautaire was defined, the Convention mentioned by the Honourable Member was not considered to be an instrument that needs to be included at present in the conventions that are inseparable from the attainment of the objectives of the EC Treaty.

The Council is prepared to look, within the framework of the provisions laid down by the Treaty, into any proposal that may be presented regarding the issues mentioned by the Honourable Member.

Question no 19 by Efstratios Korakas (H-0571/03)
 Subject: Immediate measures to revoke the decision to 'remove' Yasser Arafat
 

Israel's provocative decision to expel Yasser Arafat, the Chairman of the Palestinian Authority, is the signal for another bloodbath. It will exacerbate the situation created by Israel's continued occupation of territory. The repeated murderous military operations by the Israeli army, its intimidating presence on Palestinian territory and the arbitrary arrests together with the crude and crushing conditions in which people are held in detention, the denial of the existence of a Palestinian state and the continuous violation of a people's right to self-determination have created an explosive situation in the Middle East and a general climate of instability and insecurity.

What initiatives and action will the Council take to ensure that the decision to expel Chairman Arafat, which is jeopardising peace in the region, is revoked, that Israel alters its intransigent stance, that it withdraws its troops and stops its murderous attacks, and recognises the existence of an independent Palestinian State as the only solution to the problem in the Middle East?

 
  
 

(IT) The issues raised by the Honourable Member of the European Parliament have been discussed in depth by Foreign Ministers in the Council most recently on 29 September and 13 October 2003.

As to the specific point regarding President Arafat, the Council has urged the Israeli Government to refrain from executing its decision in principle to remove the elected President of the Palestinian Authority, which would be counter-productive to the efforts at reaching a peaceful solution to the conflict. The EU will remain in contact with all Israeli Government and Palestinian Authority interlocutors.

While recognising Israel’s legitimate right to self-defence in the face of terrorist attacks against its citizens, the Council also urged the Government of Israel to exert maximum effort to avoid civilian casualties, to put a halt to the practice of extra-judicial killings which are contrary to International Law, to reverse the settlement policy and activity, to dismantle settlements built after March 2001 and to freeze the construction of the separation fence, which results in confiscation of Palestinian land, restricts movement of people and goods and hinders a just political solution to the conflict.

The Council also made clear that there is no alternative to a swift and full implementation, in good faith by the two sides, of the roadmap. Together with other members of the Quartet, the EU remains committed to the roadmap and the common goal of two states living side by side in peace and security.

 

Question no 20 by Ewa Hedkvist Petersen (H-0575/03)
 Subject: Trafficking of human beings
 

Trafficking of human beings, primarily women and children, is a major problem in the EU. There is also public concern that this abominable trade will grow when enlargement becomes a reality. What does the Italian Presidency intend to do to strengthen anti-trafficking measures ahead of enlargement and to set up better protective and support mechanisms to assist the victims?

 
  
 

(IT) The Council agrees with the Honourable Member that trafficking in human beings is an important issue. It is also a complex issue. It needs to be addressed both at national and at international level. And it needs to be dealt within a broader context, which goes beyond criminal law and also involves asylum and immigration policies and preventive measures.

With regard to the assistance of victims of trafficking in human beings, it is recalled that the Council bodies are currently considering a proposal for a Directive submitted by the Commission. Its objective is to offer to the victims who show their willingness to co-operate and effectively co-operate with the authorities in the proceedings initiated against the traffickers, a certain number of benefits, and in particular the possibility to remain legally in the territory of the Member States for the entire duration of the relevant proceedings. Such treatment is intended to help the victims to recover and to escape from the influence of the traffickers. As it clearly appears, the approach adopted by this instrument offers a two-fold advantage: for the Member States, in their fight against the traffickers and for the victims, who may be granted a legal status under certain conditions and for a period linked with the duration of the proceedings.

As far as criminal law is concerned, the EU has in particular adopted the Framework Decision of 19 July 2002 on combating trafficking in human beings. On the basis of this instrument Member States must provide for effective, proportionate and dissuasive penalties regarding trafficking in human beings for the purpose of labour exploitation or sexual exploitation. For defined aggravating circumstances, the maximum penalty provided by Member States must be of at least 8 years imprisonment. The instrument also contains provisions on liability of legal persons and on jurisdiction and prosecution. It must be implemented by Member States at the latest by 1 August 2004.

The Council has also adopted measures on trafficking in human beings, namely the Directive of 28 November 2002 defining the facilitation of unauthorised entry, transit and residence and the Framework Decision of the same date on the strengthening of the penal framework in that regard. These measures must be put in place by the Member States at the latest by 5 December 2004.

A number of more general criminal law measures taken will also contribute to the fight against trafficking in human beings. Examples are the European arrest warrant, Eurojust, the EU Convention on mutual assistance in criminal matters and the European Judicial Network.

 

Question no 21 by Othmar Karas (H-0576/03)
 Subject: Europe-wide VAT system
 

According to the country-of-origin principle regarding VAT, all transactions are taxed in the country where the goods or services are provided, with the recipient then being able to have the tax paid deducted in his or her own country. It is generally felt that a Europe-wide system of VAT following the country-of-origin principle would make the current VAT system less susceptible to fraud. However, there is at present a tendency for individual Member States to develop their own anti-fraud rules. What is the Council’s position on harmonising rules at EU level with a view to, for example, developing the VAT system further along the lines of the country-of-origin principle?

Large, international companies could soon be faced in the EU with 25 different systems to determine the tax basis. What is the Council’s opinion of the creation of a single European tax basis for companies operating throughout the EU?

 
  
 

(IT) In accordance with the new strategy for VAT presented by the Commission in June 2000, the Council will focus its work on the simplification and modernisation of current VAT rules, more uniform application of current rules and a new approach to administrative cooperation.

The Council strategy in the area of company taxation consists of focussing attention first on those individual issues which give rise to the greatest problems.

Only at a later stage and, in particular, on the basis of a communication from the Commission expected during 2003, will the Council be in a position to discuss the possibility of a more ambitious approach to harmonisation in the area of company taxation and VAT.

 

Question no 22 by Rodi Kratsa-Tsagaropoulou (H-0580/03)
 Subject: Destruction of precious artefacts in the Hagia Sophia
 

The new director of the Hagia Sophia museum, Mr Seratzetin Sahin, recently stated in a report to the Turkish Directorate-General for museums and cultural goods that 550 priceless icons and cultural artefacts (bibles and sacerdotal vestments) have been destroyed by humidity in the Hagia Sophia storerooms. These cultural treasures had been collected from a large number of Orthodox churches on Turkish territory when the churches concerned were abandoned.

Given that Hagia Sophia has been classed as a world heritage site since 1985 and Turkey's latest national programme to implement the acquis communautaire states that universal cultural goods are protected under Law 3386 of 17 June 1987 so that they can be handed down to future generations in perfect condition, will the Council take up a clear position on this matter?

Does the Council believe that Turkey's action in this regard constitutes a step in the wrong direction in implementing the acquis communautaire in the field of culture and international commitments? Does it consider it appropriate to take joint action with UNESCO in order to tackle this situation, and in particular to prevent similar problems from arising in the country concerned? How will it respond vis-à-vis the Turkish Government?

 
  
 

(IT) The Council shares the concern about the state of the cultural treasures mentioned by the Honourable Member, which form part of the world heritage. The Council recalls to the Honourable Member that in a resolution adopted on 6 May 2003, the Council confirmed that a basic principle ruling the EU, as a community of cultural values and sensitivities, is the respect and protection of world cultural heritage. It also recalls that in another resolution on horizontal aspects of culture, adopted on the same day, the Council stressed that an extra effort is needed to be made to include culture in other sectors with the aim of placing culture at the heart of European integration.

The Council has not discussed this specific issue and has no indication of the extent of the damage and resources needed to support conservation and protection of these pieces of art or on the way the Turkish authorities are taking the matter into consideration. However, the Council recalls that the accession partnership includes a reference indicating that membership of the European Union requires inter alia compliance with the Copenhagen criteria. These aims therefore include the community of cultural values. Furthermore, the Council recalls that incorporation of the ‘acquis communautaire’ into legislation is necessary, but not in itself sufficient as it is necessary to ensure that it is actually applied.

 

Question no 23 by Bill Newton Dunn (H-0582/03)
 Subject: Council press conferences
 

The Council is unwilling, as ever, to deal with the problem it has created of taxpayers having to pay vast amounts of money to keep the European Parliament travelling between Brussels and Strasbourg. Therefore, to assist the media in reporting the proceedings of the Parliament while it is in Strasbourg, and thus informing the public better about the Union, will the Council kindly decide not to hold any press conferences of its own in Brussels while the Parliament is in Strasbourg - a practice which the Council used to operate? If not, why not?

 
  
 

(IT) The Council would point out that the location of the seats of the EU institutions is covered by a Protocol annexed to the Treaties.

The Council makes every effort to take account of the European Parliament's wishes. It is, however, inevitable, given their respective activities, that the EU institutions conduct their business simultaneously in the appropriate seats, as laid down by the Treaties.

As the Honourable Member points out, it is extremely important to inform the public better about the European Union. The various institutions' activities directed at the press contribute to pluralism of information. The Council, like the other institutions, has a duty to provide the public and the press with information and explanations, a duty to which it attaches the utmost importance.

The 'press and information' aspects cannot be seen as separate from these activities, given that these are political in nature. One or more Presidency press conferences and national media briefings are therefore held on the occasion of each Council meeting, whatever the date on which it is held. To ensure that information on the Council's business and decisions is as rapid and exhaustive as possible, such press conferences must be held as soon as possible and as close as possible to the Council meeting.

Moreover, the Council has noted the European Parliament's agreement on the conclusion of the interinstitutional agreement on 'Better Lawmaking', which provides for the three institutions to hold a joint press conference whenever negotiations reach a successful conclusion under the codecision procedure, irrespective of whether this occurs at first or second reading or even as the successful outcome of conciliation.

The Council will abide by this undertaking as soon as the interinstitutional agreement is signed and enters into force. In the meantime, it has asked the competent services to make whatever arrangements are necessary for this purpose.

 

Question no 24 by Bernard Poignant (H-0584/03)
 Subject: Manufacture of nuclear weapons by the USA
 

The American Senate recently authorised research into and the manufacture of nuclear weapons designed to destroy bunkers. This has been severely criticised as setting a bad example to the whole world, since the aim of the United States, like that of the international community, is to do everything possible to prevent the production and spread of weapons of mass destruction. This latest unilateral decision taken by the Americans will increase the hostility of nations throughout the world. Does the Council intend to criticise the American Senate's decision? What actions and measures does it intend to take in order to exert pressure on the American Administration to rescind this decision?

 
  
 

(IT) On discussions or decisions within the US administration regarding the research or development of nuclear weapons aimed at destroying bunkers the Council’s attention has not been raised.

The EU is fully committed to the Treaty on the Non-proliferation of Nuclear Weapons (NPT) and supports its effective implementation, as well as the implementation of the objectives laid down in the Resolution of the 1995 Review and Extension Conference and in the Final Document adopted by the 2000 Review Conference.

The Final Document which was adopted by consensus at the Review Conference, to which the US participated as a State Party to the NPT, concluded in Article VI, paragraph 14: ‘The Conference notes the nuclear-weapons States' declaration that none of their nuclear weapons are targeted at any State [Party].’

The EU has repeatedly stated that the NPT is a cornerstone of the global non-proliferation regime and the essential foundation for the pursuit of nuclear disarmament. In its statement at the Second Preparatory Committee of the 2005 Review Conference of the Parties to the NPT (Geneva, 28 April-9 May 2003), the EU recalled the special importance it attaches to achieving universal adherence to the NPT in order to reinforce the global non-proliferation and disarmament objectives, which are at the core of the Union's policy.

 

Question no 25 by Niall Andrews (H-0597/03)

On 18 March this year the European Council agreed its negotiating position regarding the conclusion of an agreement on political dialogue and cooperation between the European Union and the Andean countries (Bolivia, Columbia, Ecuador, Peru and Venezuela).

Question no 26 by Brian Crowley (H-0598/03)
 Subject: Closure of chat rooms
 

As the Council is aware, Microsoft has decided to close down its unmoderated Internet chat room service because of its concerns over the safety and vulnerability of children using them. Access to the service in the US, Canada and Japan in future will be on a subscription fee basis and will ensure identification of subscribers.

What are the Council’s views on this development, and does it believe that the closure of all chat rooms should be considered or does it favour a subscription-only policy in order to protect children?

 
  
 

(IT) The Council reminds the Honourable Member that any enterprise offering electronic services such as chatrooms is free to provide those services either free of charge or on a paid-for (subscribers-only) basis.

Whether a specific enterprise chooses anonymous chatrooms, with or without monitoring, or subscription/identified chatrooms, all of these commercial choices must be operated in a way which satisfies Community data protection law.

In general terms anonymity does protect privacy, but this must, where appropriate, be balanced against other public policy concerns, including the prevention and detection of crime and the protection of minors. Community law provides a framework which allows private-sector companies to make commercial choices which reflect this balance.

Question no 29 by Ioannis Souladakis (H-0606/03)
 Subject: Efforts to combat the circulation of counterfeit euro banknotes
 

There have been lurid reports in the European press that in some applicant countries and in countries of the western Balkans counterfeit euro notes are being produced and then distributed in the European Union, creating extensive problems for business transactions.

What will the Council do to combat the circulation of counterfeit euro notes in the European Union?

Will the Council ensure that the applicant countries undertake to control and combat this illegal activity?

 
  
 

(IT) According to the report of the Commission on the practical experience gained with one year of circulation of Euro banknotes and coins (doc. COM (2002) 747 final), counterfeiting of Euro notes and coins has, since their introduction as from 1.1.2002, taken place at a much smaller scale than what was previously experienced with national banknotes and coins.
