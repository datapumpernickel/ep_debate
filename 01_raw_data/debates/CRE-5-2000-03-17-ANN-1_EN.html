Questions to the Council
Question no 10 by María Izquierdo Rojo (H-0172/00)
 Subject: Libya and the Charter for Stability and Security in the Mediterranean
 

Is the Council planning to include Libya in the major project represented by the Charter for Stability and Security in the Mediterranean?

 
  
 

Libya will take part in the work of the “Charter for Stability and Security in the Mediterranean” as soon as it becomes a full participant in the Barcelona Process, which is dependent on Libya providing written confirmation of its complete and unreserved acceptance of the terms of the Barcelona Process and of the indivisible nature of the partnership.

 

Question no 11 by David W. Martin (H-0177/00)
 Subject: Attacks on UN officials
 

Is the Presidency aware of the disturbing number of attacks on United Nations personnel in the field over the past months and years? Since 1992, 180 UN officials have been killed on duty. Eighty staff members remain missing. Does the Council have any plans to help in this situation?

 
  
 

The Council is deeply concerned at the increasing number of attacks against civilian personnel working for the United Nations, which resulted in the death of 184 United Nations staff members between 1 January 1992 and 31 January 2000, and fully supports the condemnations of these acts of murder and other violence against UN staff members, as expressed by the UN Security Council, most recently on 9 February 2000.

The protection of United Nations and associated and humanitarian personnel is a fundamental responsibility for United Nations missions in situations of conflict. Violence against them is unacceptable and cannot be tolerated under any circumstances. Without their safety and security, missions and operations could not function, let alone succeed.

The Union has expressed a firm position on this question during the Security Council open debate held on 9 February 2000. On this occasion, the EU stressed that the responsibility for the safety of personnel on the ground falls, in the first instance, on the governments hosting peacekeeping and humanitarian operations. The Union urged all parties to conflict to take all steps necessary to ensure the safety and security of United Nations and associated, as well as humanitarian personnel, including locally-recruited personnel.

Furthermore, the Union expressed the view that perpetrators of crimes against such personnel must be brought to justice. Governments must act forcefully to prevent these acts of violence and to punish those responsible for them. In this context, the Statute of the International Criminal Court is also essential. The inclusion in the Statute of the war crime of intentionally attacking personnel involved in humanitarian assistance or peacekeeping missions will help to bring perpetrators to justice.

Finally, the Union stated that the United Nations Secretariat must ensure that United Nations and associated personnel are adequately protected. The Union supports the Secretary-General’s continued strengthening of the Office of the United Nations Security Coordinator with personnel dedicated to security in peacekeeping operations.

The Union's positions are largely reflected in the Statement made by the President of the UN Security Council on behalf of the Council on 9 February 2000.

Question no 14 by Karin Riis-Jørgensen (H-0209/00)
 Subject: Sanctions against Austria
 

In the Council President’s view, have the sanctions against Austria, adopted by the 14 Heads of State and Government and announced by the Portuguese Presidency, had the desired effect? Can the Council President state whether his view on this matter will have any influence on the maintenance of these sanctions by the Heads of State and Government?

What requirements will have to be met in order for the sanctions to be lifted?

Joint Answer

1. As I pointed out in a written answer to a question put by Mrs Pernille Frahm in the framework of the Question Time of February last, the position expressed by the President of the European Council, in the name of fourteen governments of the Member States of the European Union, regarding the inclusion of the Freedom Party in the Austrian Government, does not represent a Council position.

Any such action is a matter for the governments of the Member States concerned. The use of the provisions of Article 7 of the EU Treaty is a separate issue. The mechanisms provided for under this Article have not been invoked nor has the possibility of having recourse to them been discussed by the Council.

 
 

Question no 15 by Nelly Maes (H-0180/00)
 Subject: Political agenda for respect for minority rights in Turkey
 

Turkey's application for membership of the EU has prompted questions from people concerned about democracy, human rights and minority rights. What strategy has the Council defined on spelling out a route for Turkey towards satisfying the Copenhagen criteria (which specify respect for minorities as an accession requirement)? What changes to the law do legal experts in the Member States feel are necessary? Is there any consultation on this issue? Has a clear agenda been drawn up on this question? How constructive is the attitude of the Turkish authorities, and what contribution are they themselves making?

Is there also a discussion with the Turkish authorities to encourage them to engage in peace talks on the conflict concerning the rights of the Kurdish minority, as has been the case in Northern Ireland for some time now? Is there to be a European peace fund, modelled on the fund for Ireland, to build bridges between the communities involved? It is obvious that this is the only way to stabilise the situation in Turkey.

 
  
 

1. With regard to Turkey, the pre-accession strategy will outline the EU’s priorities for satisfying the so-called Copenhagen criteria. Accession negotiations will not be able to start until Turkey satisfies these criteria, which include human rights and the protection of minorities. In this context, it should be made clear that Turkey does recognise the minorities referred to in the Treaty of Lausanne of 1923, specifically the Greek, Armenian and Jewish minorities. The pre-accession strategy for Turkey will probably be adopted in October or November 2000.

2. On 24 February, the current Chairman of the HADEP (the pro-Kurdish party) and his predecessor, together with 16 other members of the party, were given prison sentences, some of more than three years, for allegedly supporting the PKK terrorist group. The support in question involved hunger strikes in support of Abdullah Öçalan during his stay in Italy at the end of 1998, before his arrest in February 1999.

3. The elected leaders of the councils in Diyarbakir, Siirt and Bingöl, who are all Kurds and members of the HADEP, had been arrested a few days previously by the security forces in spectacular circumstances. They were then also accused of supporting the PKK and were remanded in custody pending trial. The three council leaders have since been released, following intervention by the EU.

4. Human Rights organisations have condemned the recent actions carried out against Kurdish politicians. The European Union stressed – through the confidential work of a troika in Ankara – the importance of non-interference in democratic institutions in the context of the Copenhagen criteria for accession and of the proper treatment of the arrested men. The EU also expressed its “surprise” at these arrests.

5. With regard to the peace fund, the Council has not received any proposal on this matter.

Question no 17 by Pedro Marset Campos (H-0252/00)
 Subject: Human rights in Chechnya
 

The reason given for the EU’s approval of NATO’s bombing of Yugoslavia at the Berlin Summit on 25 March 1999 was to stop human rights violations in Kosovo.

In the light of the human rights situation before, during and after the intervention by Russia in Chechnya, what is the difference in the quality, scale or nature of the human rights violations that led the EU to support the bombing of Kosovo, but do nothing in Chechnya other than allow the Russian Government to act as it sees fit?

Joint Answer

The Council has repeatedly condemned Russia’s military intervention in Chechnya and considers it to be completely unacceptable. It has stressed the fact that the fight against terrorism does not justify, under any circumstances, the indiscriminate use of force. The Council has made it quite clear that this behaviour contravenes the basic principles of humanitarian law, the commitments given by Russia to the OSCE, as well as its obligations as a Member of the Council of Europe.

The Council has on several occasions urged Russia to end its military offensive against the Chechen people, to allow the delivery, in safe conditions, of humanitarian aid, and to immediately enter into political dialogue with Chechen representatives. The Council is convinced that only a political agreement can provide a lasting solution to the complex underlying causes of the conflict. The Council therefore fully supports the efforts of the OSCE and the Council of Europe. Any political agreement would clearly have to give the Chechens a sufficient degree of autonomy, without threatening Russia’s territorial integrity.

In addition to this, the Council is deeply alarmed at the increasing number of reports on human rights abuses, such as torture and executions. In their meeting on 2 March with the Foreign Minister, Igor Ivanov, the EU Troika urged Russia to end these abuses and to bring those responsible to trial. The Troika appealed to Russia to allow independent investigations to take place in order to ensure complete transparency. In addition to this, at the trilateral ministerial meeting of 3 March in Lisbon, the EU and the US together put pressure on Russia to do so. The Council will continue to follow this issue very closely, and to put pressure on Russia if necessary.

In this context, we must emphasise that the Council has already urged Russia on several occasions to allow a permanent presence in the region of competent international organisations such as the Council of Europe, the OSCE and the United Nations. The Council supports the efforts of the President-in-Office of the OSCE, Benita Ferrero-Waldner, who will shortly be travelling to the region, as well as the commitment of Mary Robinson and of the Council of Europe Commissioner for Human Rights, Gil Robles. The Council will continue to exert pressure on Russia so that the OSCE Assistance Group can return quickly to the region.

As a result of the situation in Chechnya, the Helsinki European Council decided to refocus the Joint Strategy for Russia, to redirect the TACIS programme towards certain priorities, to adopt a strict approach to trade matters, if Russia violates the PCA and to end cooperation under the PCA in certain areas. The EU is currently working on the implementation of these measures.

Furthermore, the Council is particularly concerned at the treatment of journalists working in Chechnya. In light of the case of Mr Babitsky, the Council has urged Russia to adopt all necessary measures to guarantee basic conditions for free and independent media to operate, as well as an unhampered flow of information.

Under growing international pressure, Russia has accepted the participation of experts from the Council of Europe in Vladimir Kalamanov’s team, and has stated that it is willing to study the possibility of other interested organisations participating. On its own initiative, Russia has invited the president of the ICRC to travel to Moscow, and he will probably also visit Chechnya. The new head of the OSCE Assistance Group travelled to Chechnya on 11 March.

The Council will continue to closely follow the human rights situation in Chechnya and to take every appropriate opportunity to express its concerns to the Russians.

Question no 20 by Ole Krarup (H-0196/00)
 Subject: The EU's defence and security policy
 

According to the Global Intelligence Unit () the President of the Commission has stated that any attack on an EU Member State is an attack against the whole EU. He described this as ‘the ultimate guarantee’.

This remarkable statement, which was made to a delegation from one of the candidate countries, demonstrates – taken in connection with the gradual expansion of the EU’s military capacity, and most recently with Mr Prodi’s statements of 4 February (reported in The Independent) – that the EU is in the process of developing into a military union.

Does the Council share the Commission President's view? If not, has the Council moved to dissociate itself from his statement? What reservations does the Council have with regard to the participation of neutral and candidate countries in the ‘commitment’ postulated by the Commission President?

 
  
 

Article 17 of the Treaty on European Union states that “the Common Foreign and Security Policy shall include all issues relating to the security of the Union, including the progressive framing of a common defence policy, in accordance with the second subparagraph, which might lead to common defence, should the Council so decide”. Common defence is envisaged in the Treaty as a possible long-term objective, which will in any event require a specific decision by the European Council and the subsequent adoption of a decision by the Member States in accordance with their respective constitutional laws.

There is currently no “supreme guarantee” within the Union. This guarantee of assistance against external aggression is laid down by the North Atlantic Treaty and by the amended Treaty of Brussels (the Western European Union) in relation to the States that are party to these Treaties.

The purpose of developing the Union’s military capabilities is to provide the Union with the necessary means for crisis management, in other words, to satisfy the so-called “Petersberg Tasks”, which are humanitarian and evacuation tasks, as well as tasks aimed at maintaining and re-establishing peace, to which specific reference is made in Article 17(2). This fact was clearly stated by the Cologne European Council in June 1999 and confirmed in Helsinki in December 1999. In addition to this, it has also been specified on several occasions that this process “does not imply the creation of a European Army”.

Given that common defence is not in question, the participation of neutral Member States in this process and in crisis management does not raise any problems. Under the single institutional framework, all of the EU’s Member States, whether they are neutral, non-allied or Nato members can participate on equal terms in all preparatory stages and in decision making with regard to EU-led operations.

With regard to the participation of applicant countries and countries which are Nato allies but not EU Members, the Helsinki European Council decided that, in crisis management operations launched by the EU, appropriate structures will be created for dialogue and the exchange of information with those countries. These structures will be used for the purpose of consultation when the Council has not reached a decision. The possible forms of these structures are currently under discussion in Council forums.

The Commission believes that the lack of consumer confidence in on-line payments is a major barrier to the development of electronic commerce. That is why it intends to elaborate further on this issue in the context of overall strategy on electronic commerce.

Question no 109 by Roy Perry (H-0239/00)
 Subject: Environmental impact assessment in the UK
 

Would the Commission agree, given the size and possible impact of certain developments such as those outlined in the Crowe Report (which proposes to build over 1 million new homes in the south east of England, over 100 000 of which will be in Hampshire), that an environmental impact assessment should be carried out on the entire planned venture, rather than on the separate and specific unit development sites, in order that we might gain a more accurate picture of its potential impact on the environment?

 
  
 

Member States are required under the Environment impact assessment (EIA) Directive (Directive 85/337/EEC(1)1 on the effects of certain public and private projects on the environment, as amended by Directive 97/11/EC(2)2), to ensure that an environmental impact assessment is carried out before development consent is given for projects likely to have significant effects on the environment. For the purpose of the Directive, project means the execution of construction works or of other installations or schemes or other interventions in the natural surrounding.

For projects falling within Annex II of the Directive Member States have to ensure that an environmental assessment is carried out where they consider that the projects will be likely to have a significant environmental effect (Articles 2(1) and 4(2)). The developments identified in the Crowe Report would presumably fall under category 10(b) of Annex II, urban development projects. In determining whether an Environmental Impact Assessment would be required for the entire planned venture rather than for separate and specific site developments, it is necessary to consider whether the entire venture would constitute one single project for the purpose of the EIA Directive. According to the information in our possession, this would not be the case. In this respect, it should be noted, however, that the Directive requires to evaluate in the environmental assessment process the cumulative effects (where they are significant) of individual projects on the environment.

According to the Commission's information, the Crowe Report does not constitute an application for development consent for a project. In that context it would not appear that the adoption of the Crowe Report requires an Environmental Impact Assessment.

As far as plans/programmes are concerned, the future Strategic Environmental Assessment Directive will expand the principle of Environmental Assessment to the planning level. However, since this instrument is still under discussion, it is not yet a legal requirement in Member States.

Question no 114 by Pedro Marset Campos (H-0253/00)
 Subject: Effective measures to promote full employment
 

The main issue to be dealt with at the forthcoming Lisbon Summit will be the fight against unemployment. According to data compiled by the Commission itself, in the last eight years since the entry into force of the Maastricht Treaty and with the broadening of the criteria for convergence and the Stability Pact, we have been witnessing a stabilisation of monetary conditions, a growth in GDP and in productivity per man-hour, but at the same time there is a worrying increase in unemployment, labour deregulation, insecure jobs and social exclusion, combined with a reduction in social security benefits.

In view of these contradictory developments, with positive economic figures and negative unemployment figures, does the Commission not consider that the criteria laid down in the Maastricht Treaty and the Stability Pact should be revised?

 
  
 

The employment situation has improved considerably in recent years, with the average unemployment rate in the 15 Member States at its lowest level since 1992.

This is partly due to the framework of macroeconomic stability that has been established with Economic and Monetary Union. It is also partly due to policies to improve the functioning of labour markets that Member States have pursued. These policies go beyond deregulation of labour markets to embrace a comprehensive approach, including in particular a shift from passive measures to active measures aimed at improving employability.

More active employment policies and reforms in labour markets - in line with the Broad Economic Policy Guidelines and the Employment Guidelines - have given rise to improved labour market performance. In particular, through a comprehensive, preventive approach to employment policies and through contributing to a progressive shift from passive spending to active spending measures, as mandated by the Florence European Council in 1996.

However, unemployment, much of it structural and long-term, remains unacceptably high, and is the prime cause of social exclusion in the Community. Therefore, one of the Commission's proposals for the Lisbon European Council is that Member States commit themselves to a return to full employment. The main policy strands for achieving this should be the pursuit of economic reforms and the strengthening of the European social model through investing in people.

The Convergence Programmes and the Stability and Growth Pact have played and will continue to play a crucial role in establishing a stable macroeconomic environment, which is a necessary condition for achieving sustainable growth and employment. Therefore the Commission does not consider that the convergence criteria or the Stability and Growth Pact should be revised.
