Question No 1 by Bernd Posselt (H-000195/11)
 Subject: EULEX in Northern Kosovo
 

What is the Council’s assessment of the performance of the EU Rule of Law mission EULEX in Kosovo, particularly in Northern Kosovo and Mitrovica, and what measures is it taking to improve the work of this important body?

 
  
 

The present answer, which has been drawn up by the Presidency and is not binding on either the Council or its members as such, was not presented orally at Question Time to the Council during the June 2011 part-session of the European Parliament in Strasbourg.

On 26 April 2010, the Council noted with satisfaction the results achieved so far by EULEX Kosovo in a difficult environment assisting judicial and law enforcement agencies and in promoting rule of law reforms, including in the prosecution and adjudication of sensitive criminal cases. The Council expressed its support for the mission's approach to intensify its efforts in the fight against organised crime and corruption.

Since reaching its initial operational capability in December 2008, the EULEX mission has significantly contributed to an environment where the Kosovo population is increasingly demanding that nobody should be above the law. By investigating senior ministers, politicians, senior officials, including the Governor of the Central Bank, former KLA Commanders and business men the mission has seriously challenged the perception of impunity.

Several hundreds of investigations are on-going. More than 180 verdicts have already been handed down by EULEX judges in a wide range of cases including corruption and organised crime. Around 1150 hearings have been held and EULEX prosecutors have been involved in more than 1300 cases, including around 50 cases of organised crime and close to a 100 cases on corruption.

The mission has also made significant strides in fighting trafficking of human beings and organs, most concretely through major cases such as the Tisza (smuggling of human beings) and the Medicus case (trafficking of organs).

There are a number of investigations ongoing related to criminal activities in the north. Since EULEX reopened the Mitrovica court in December 2008, after nearly 9 months of closure, international judges and prosecutors, supported by a multi-ethnic team of Kosovo-Serb and Kosovo-Albanian local staff, have completed no less than 35 trials. Criminal acts falling into EULEX’s mandate are properly investigated and adequately adjudicated by a team of EULEX judges and prosecutors even if local judges and prosecutors have not yet returned. A local multi-ethnic inventory team have made great progress in finding, re-arranging and registering thousands of case files.

There is a constant presence of EULEX Customs and Border Police Officers at Gates 31 and 1. Since the deployment of EULEX, smuggling in the north has been reduced by as much as 60%, thus resulting in a real increase in revenues for both Belgrade and Pristina. All data collected at the gates is being shared with both Belgrade and Pristina and registration of people entering is introduced in a centralised data base.

Recently and in line with the priorities of the new Head of Mission, Xavier Bout de Marnhac, the mission has established the Task Force Mitrovica (TFM), a unit of EULEX police and customs investigators and prosecutors. The TFM’s objective is to fight serious crimes using intelligence led policing in order to enhance the rule of law both north and south of the Ibar. The arrest in Zubin Potok on 17 May of a person alledgedly involved in organised crime is a further testimony to EULEX activities.

EULEX activities in the north are also coupled with active public relations, for instance through the launch of anti-drug campaigns.

 

Question No 2 by Jim Higgins (H-000198/11)
 Subject: Road safety
 

In the programme of the Hungarian Presidency, the Presidency outlined that it would make ‘all possible efforts to speed up the on-going negotiations on the cross-border exchange of information on road safety-related traffic offences’.

As the Hungarian Presidency comes to a close, what steps has the Council taken to ensure that the above will be realised?

 
  
 

The present answer, which has been drawn up by the Presidency and is not binding on either the Council or its members as such, was not presented orally at Question Time to the Council during the June 2011 part-session of the European Parliament in Strasbourg.

As the honourable MEP is well aware of, the adoption of a Directive facilitating cross-border exchange of information on road safety related traffic offences is a priority for the Hungarian Presidency.

The competent Council preparatory bodies have examined the above draft Directive and the Presidency has held several informal meetings with the EP rapporteur and the shadow rapporteurs with the objective to explore, informally, possible areas for compromise in order to make a second reading agreement possible before the end of June 2011.

The Hungarian Presidency, as previously announced, will do its utmost to reach an agreement with the EP on this file before the end of its term.

 

Question No 3 by Vilija Blinkevičiūtė (H-000202/11)
 Subject: Free and unfettered access for disabled people to elections to express their will as citizens
 

Hungary, whose turn it is to hold the EU Council presidency for the current six months, includes as one of the main priorities in its programme the European Disability Strategy (2010-2020), which notably aims to combat discrimination against disabled people and ensure that they are able to exercise their fundamental and civic rights.

As the country holding the presidency, does it not consider that there is a need in the Member States to remove the obstacles preventing disabled people from having free and unfettered access to elections, to express their will as citizens and exercise their civic rights? Unfortunately because of an unsuitable physical environment only a small percentage of disabled people have the opportunity to turn out and vote.

 
  
 

The present answer, which has been drawn up by the Presidency and is not binding on either the Council or its members as such, was not presented orally at Question Time to the Council during the June 2011 part-session of the European Parliament in Strasbourg.

The specific arrangements put in place to ensure that all citizens can vote is a matter that falls under the competence of the Member States. However, the Council attaches great importance to the full participation of persons with disabilities in public life on an equal basis with others. The Resolution(1) adopted by the Council and the Member States on 7 and 8 June 2010 identified access to public life as a prerequisite for the full inclusion and participation of persons with disabilities in society. Similarly, the Resolution(2) adopted on 29 February 2008 identifies improving accessibility as a precondition for autonomy, inclusion and participation, and as a way of enabling persons with disabilities to enjoy human rights and fundamental freedoms.

The Council is discussing the Commission's proposal(3) for a new anti-discrimination Directive, which also contains provisions for improving accessibility.

Furthermore, the Council has repeatedly invited(4) the Member States and the Commission to promote the ratification and application of the UN Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities (UNCRDP) which specifically(5) addresses the issue of effective and full participation in political and public life, by ensuring appropriate and accessible voting procedures, facilities and materials, by facilitating the use of assistive and new technologies and by allowing personal assistance in voting.

Finally, in the context of the Commission's new European Disability Strategy 2010-2020(6), which largely focuses on the implementation of the UNCRPD, the Hungarian Presidency has prepared a set of draft Council Conclusions, which are currently being discussed by the Council preparatory bodies.

Question No 4 by Gay Mitchell (H-000204/11)
 Subject: Road safety
 

This summer will once again see many British and Irish families travel to southern Europe on their holidays. Many Irish tourists will take the boat from Cork or Rosslare to Roscoff and Cherbourg and many British tourists will take the Channel Tunnel from Dover to Calais. These travellers will spend a couple of weeks in France, Germany, Spain and other EU countries that drive on the right-hand side of the road. As the Irish and British drive on the left with no experience of driving on the right, there is cause for worry. Accidents are far more likely because of the impairments of driving a left-hand-drive car on the right-hand side of the road. Has the Council a strategy to improve the safety of road users who travel abroad in this way?

 
  
 

The present answer, which has been drawn up by the Presidency and is not binding on either the Council or its members as such, was not presented orally at Question Time to the Council during the June 2011 part-session of the European Parliament in Strasbourg.

In its conclusions on Road Safety(1) adopted during its meeting of 2 and 3 December 2010, the Council has considered that the level of road fatalities and injuries remained unacceptably high and has stressed the importance of adapting motorways, roads, streets and vehicles to human capacity.

The Council has furthermore considered that infrastructure and vehicles should be designed as to prevent and limit the consequences of human error and inappropriate behaviour and has recognised the need for building road infrastructure and developing road network fulfilling high road safety standards.

The Council, however, has not examined the specific issue raised by the honourable MEP.

Question No 5 by Marian Harkin (H-000213/11)
 Subject: Bringing Europe closer to its citizens
 

What initiatives has the Council undertaken to achieve one of its priorities, i.e. bringing Europe closer to its citizens?

 
  
 

The present answer, which has been drawn up by the Presidency and is not binding on either the Council or its members as such, was not presented orally at Question Time to the Council during the June 2011 part-session of the European Parliament in Strasbourg.

Article 10 of the Treaty on European Union states that 'every citizen shall have the right to participate in the democratic life of the Union'. Decisions shall be taken as openly and as closely as possible to the citizens.” According to Article 11 TEU “the institutions shall ... give citizens and representative associations the opportunity to make known and publicly exchange their views in all areas of Union action”.

In order to realise these goals and enhance participatory democracy, Article 11 TEU provides for the citizen's initiative, which is one of the main innovations of the Lisbon Treaty. This Treaty provision was implemented through a Regulation of the European Parliament and of the Council(1). Member States and the Commission are to take the measures necessary for its implementation by 1st April 2012, when the Regulation will become applicable. Beyond all these measures, Article 12 TEU also provides for the involvement of national parliaments in the decision-making process of the Union.

Furthermore, the Council has welcomed and supported the initiative undertaken by the European Parliament and the Commission to establish a joint "Transparency Register" and will make a unilateral statement in June, when the Transparency Register is scheduled to be launched, stressing the value of this new tool and the Council's support for this initiative.

The Council is ready to start tripartite discussions in order to examine the modalities of the Council's possible role in the Register.

In addition, in accordance with Article 15 of the Treaty on the Functioning of the European Union, all Council's deliberations and votes on draft legislative acts are public and accessible on the Internet.

As regards public access to documents, the Commission proposed a recast of Regulation (EC) 1049/2001 of 30 May 2001 regarding public access to European Parliament, Council and Commission documents(2) in order to extend the public right of access to documents of all the Union institutions, bodies, offices and agencies. The proposal is being discussed within the framework of the ordinary legislative procedure. Furthermore, the increasing number of consultations of the public register of Council documents demonstrates that this instrument continues to provide an important tool for citizens wishing to follow the activities of the European Union.

More generally, the aim of the Council is to communicate with the citizens in a clear and transparent manner. In this context, the Council continues to be actively involved in the implementation of the political declaration "Communicating Europe in Partnership" signed by the European Parliament, the Council and the European Commission on 22 October 2008(3).

According to this declaration, whilst respecting the individual responsibility of each EU institution and Member State for its own communication strategy and priorities, the three Institutions will, in the framework of the Interinstitutional Group on Information, identify yearly a limited number of common communication priorities.

In establishing its position on this issue, the Council conveys Member States views to ensure that the communication activities and information campaigns have the necessary national, regional and local interest to reach out to all citizens.

 
 

(1) OJ L 65, 11.3.2011, p. 1.
(2) OJ L 145, 31.5.2001, p. 43.
(3) 13712/08.

Question No 16 by Liam Aylward (H-000207/11)
 Subject: Workplace safety and fatalities at work
 

In the EU-27 several thousand workers die in fatal accidents at work annually and approximately 2.9 % of workers have had an accident at work with more than 3 days of absence.

Can the Commission comment on what measures are in place to address this tragic situation?

What is the Commission’s assessment of the existing 2007 - 2012 strategy? Has the strategy made progress towards achieving its aims and objectives and lowering the risk of fatalities at work across the EU?

What is the current status of the consultation process on a new Health and Safety at Work Strategy for Europe for 2013 - 2020?

 
  
 

While EU statistics point to a substantial decline in the number of fatal accidents and serious accidents at work(1), the Commission agrees that there is room for improvement in terms of reducing the number of accidents at work and cases of work-related disease across the EU.

The European Strategy on health and safety at work for 2007-12(2) aims for a 25% reduction in the incidence of accidents at work over the period, an ambitious target given the overall reduction in the incidence of accidents at work over that covered by the previous EU strategy(3). The national strategies which the Member States are to adopt under the Strategy for 2007-12 play a key role in meeting that target, and should give the Member States and national authorities direction in their efforts to prevent accidents at work. In addition, a Council Recommendation on the prevention of injury and the promotion of safety, also calling upon Member States to set up national plans for preventing accidents, was adopted in 2007(4).

The mid-term review(5) confirms that the current Strategy on health and safety largely meets the purpose, despite changes that have taken place in the EU since 2007, and in particular the impact of the financial and economic crisis. A final assessment of the Strategy’s effectiveness in achieving that 25% reduction will only be possible once official data for the reference year 2012 are available(6), but provisional estimates will be provided in the interim. According to data for 2007 to 2009(7), the figure is down in 15 Member States, unchanged in four and up in three, while the variation in the remaining five is not significant.

A broad consultation is under way to identify the priorities for the strategy for 2013-20, while a dedicated working party of the tripartite Advisory Committee on Safety and Health at Work is assisting the Commission in carrying out the final evaluation of the current Strategy and developing the future strategy.

Question No 17 by Jutta Steinruck (H-000211/11)
 Subject: Situation of trainees in Europe
 

In the recent study by the German Trade Union Federation (DGB) the precarious situation of trainees is exposed again. 28% of graduate students work in internships of which 40% are unpaid. It is an urgent situation, because companies are establishing the habit of replacing regular workers by highly skilled interns without paying adequate wages. This is a problem all over Europe. In Italy almost half of the interns have a master’s degree, and the situation is similar in other Member States such as France, Austria and the UK: it seems that the number of badly paid internships is growing constantly.

Apart from the current comprehensive study, is there anything the Commission is doing in the short term to stop the exploitation of young workers in Europe?

How can the Commission request that Member States create greater clarity in their legal provisions and simplify exchanges for trainees performing their training in other Member States?

The initiative ‘Youth on the move’ mentions a ‘quality framework for traineeships’. When exactly can this framework be expected? Are other initiatives planned to support the integration of young workers into the labour market?

 
  
 

The Commission is aware of the issue raised by the Honourable Member and that traineeships are often used to replace regular jobs. At the same time, the Commission considers that good quality traineeships can contribute to bridging the gap between theoretical knowledge acquired in education and the skills and competences needed at a workplace. As such, as part of the Europe 2020 flagship initiative 'Youth on the Move', the Commission has committed itself to presenting a quality framework for traineeships, including addressing the legal and administrative obstacles to transnational placements, in 2012.

In addition, 'Youth on the Move' includes a series of initiatives to promote the integration of young people into the labour market, including an invitation to the Member States to consider introducing open-ended single contracts in order to tackle labour market segmentation; the 'Your first EURES job' initiative and the new European Vacancy Monitor to promote intra-EU mobility of young people; the invitation to Member States to develop so-called 'Youth Guarantees' ensuring that young people are in a job, further education or benefit from activation measures within four months of leaving school; a new mutual learning program of the European Public Employment Services to help them reach out to young people; and making use of the recent European Progress Micro-Finance Facility to increase access to financing for new entrepreneurs, including young people.

 

Question No 18 by Marian Harkin (H-000214/11)
 Subject: Merging the EGF and ESF
 

In light of certain suggestions to merge the European Globalisation Fund with the European Social Fund, could the Commission outline the essential differences between the two funds and could it also elaborate on its perspective with regard to such a proposal?

 
  
 

With a view to the post-2013 review of the European Globalisation Adjustment Fund (EGF), the Commission is looking at various options for resolving the two problems affecting the Fund's current financing mechanism, namely the length of time the decision-making procedure takes and the risk that appropriations may turn out to be insufficient.

The Commission recalls that the European Social Fund (ESF) was established to contribute to strengthening economic, social and territorial cohesion by improving employment and job opportunities, encouraging a high level of employment and more and better jobs. It supports the European Employment Strategy as well as Member States' policies aiming to achieve full employment, improve quality and productivity at work, promote social inclusion, and reduce national, regional and local employment disparities. The main difference with the EGF is that the ESF consists of multi-annual programmes in support of strategic, long-term goals – notably anticipation and management of change and restructuring. The EGF, in turn, provides one-off, time-limited individual support, geared directly to workers affected by redundancies as a result of trade globalisation or the financial and economic crisis.

If it were decided to establish the EGF within the ESF for the period from 2014, it would be still possible to safeguard the identity, visibility and objectives of the EGF. This could be achieved by setting up within the ESF an EU level strand with a specific budget allocation. Incorporating the EGF into the ESF would simplify management of individual applications, as it could take advantage of the ESF management and control systems already in place. It would also enhance coherence between preventive (ESF) and curative (EGF) support.

The Commission will present its proposals on the successor to the EGF as part of the next multi-annual financial framework, due to be adopted at the end of June.

 

Question No 21 by Giovanni La Via (H-000217/11)
 Subject: Timely use of structural funds in European regions
 

Many European regions’ official data on the spending of funds from the ESF, the ERDF, etc., show that the spending targets laid down in Regional Operational Programmes are reached late and inefficiently.

In the light of these delays recorded by the relevant monitoring authorities in the Member States, what lessons learnt in the last financial programming period could be applied to the 2013-2020 period in order to speed up spending and implement more precise controls on the fulfilment of objectives?

 
  
 

The Commission shares the Honourable Member's view that ensuring the efficient absorption of cohesion policy funds, without jeopardising the effective use of resources, is indeed a very important issue. The Commission has reflected on the reasons why some Member States face difficulties in absorbing funds and is currently examining ways in which to address this issue.

The automatic decommitment rule aims to ensure that projects are implemented within a reasonable timeframe and to encourage financial discipline. The rules in force for 2007-2013, and the possibilities for exceptions to the rule, have become more complex, and the original simplicity behind the rule has been reduced. A clear, unambiguous automatic decommitment rule, without exemptions or derogations, would simplify financial management for both the Member States and the Commission. In order to allow for a reasonable start-up and launch period for programmes, the Commission is reflecting on ways in which the automatic decommitment rule could begin from the second year for all programmes.

The Commission considers that simplifying the automatic decommitment rule could ensure a balance between ensuring the quality of investments and smooth and rapid implementation.

 

Question No 22 by Silvia-Adriana Ţicău (H-000218/11)
 Subject: EU strategy for Danube region - stage reached in identifying strategic projects and financial resources
 

In line with the requests made by the European Council, in its conclusions of 18 and 19 June 2009, and the European Parliament, in its resolution of 20 January 2010, on 8 December 2010 the Commission presented the EU's strategy for the Danube region. The Council adopted the strategy on 13 April 2011. Parliament, in its resolution of 15 February 2011, called on the Member States and the Commission to begin implementing the strategy and thus enable the regions' inhabitants to benefit from a series of common strategic projects which will create jobs and stimulate economic and social development in the region. On 3 February 2011 in Budapest, the Commission named the localities and regions which will be responsible for coordinating the strategy's priority actions. The role of the coordinators is to define, together with the Member States concerned, measurable objectives and strategic projects for the implementation of the strategy, as well as, and above all, the financial resources required for that implementation.

Can the Commission provide information on the stage reached in identifying the above measurable objectives and strategic projects, and also on the financial resources required for their implementation? Can it give a date for their adoption and publication?

 
  
 

The preparation for the implementation of the EU Strategy for the Danube Region is well under way. After its adoption by the Commission on 8 December last year, Priority Areas have been attributed to Danube countries in February. The General Affairs Council endorsed Conclusions on the Strategy on 13 April. A first meeting with Priority Area Coordinators and the Commission took place in Hungary on 8-9 May. In June, kick-off meetings for each Priority Area will be held. Most of the Steering groups have been set up. Work programmes will now be drafted.

The successful implementation is in the hands of the Member States and the Commission will help facilitate the process. It is the role of the coordinators to identify, together with the involved states, the quantifiable objectives and the strategic projects for the implementation of the Strategy and, above all, the financial resources necessary. Priority Area Coordinators will, for the accomplishment of their tasks, avail of technical assistance budgets. It would involve in particular cohesion policy programmes, IPA Coordinators, representatives of ENPI programmes, national funding sources and the main International Financial Institutions. An amount of over EUR 100 billion is available in the Danube Region for the period 2007-2013 stemming from Structural funds and Cohesion fund programmes. The strategy itself does not provide specific funding.

In relation to targets, the Commission takes the view that setting specific targets will improve the implementation process. The examples proposed are open for discussion. The deadline for establishing targets is end of June 2011. The work done on the targets by the Priority Area Coordinators will be communicated by the Commission to the High level group of EU-27 Member States and the other participating countries in September for further discussion. They should be in place by end-2011.

 

Question n° 23 de Gilles Pargneaux (H-000201/11)
 Subject: Transposition of Council Directive 2009/71/Euratom of 25 June 2009 establishing a Community framework for the nuclear safety of nuclear installations
 

At present, nuclear safety is within the exclusive competence of the Member States. In 2009, a Directive aiming to increase nuclear safety in the Member States was adopted. It obliges Member States to establish and continuously improve national frameworks in the field of nuclear safety. The text reinforces the role and independence of national regulatory authorities and confirms that licence holders have prime responsibility for nuclear safety. The Member States are obliged to encourage a high level of transparency in regulatory action and to ensure that independent safety assessments are regularly carried out. This text must be brought into force by the Member States by 22 July 2011.

Can the Commission say whether the process of implementing this Directive is under way? If so, can the Commission confirm that the Directive will have been transposed in all the Member States by 22 July 2011?

 
  
 

The formal deadline by which the Member States have the obligation to bring into force the laws, regulations and administrative provisions necessary to transpose the Nuclear Safety Directive(1) is 22 July 2011.

The Commission is not in position to comment on the final transposing measures before the end of the transposition period.

 
 

(1) Council Directive 2009/71/Euratom establishing a Community framework for the nuclear safety of nuclear installations (OJ L 172, 2.7.2009).

 

Question No 24 by Laima Liucija Andrikienė (H-000210/11)
 Subject: Impact of the Traditional Herbal Medicinal Products Directive on traditional herbal medicines in the EU
 

Directive 2004/24/EC regarding traditional herbal medicinal products (THMPD) was adopted on 31 March 2004. THMPD established a number of tough requirements to be met in order for traditional medicinal products to be authorised (related to GMP practices, etc). These requirements have created serious obstacles to the production and use in the EU of herbal substances especially from different traditional cultures.

How does the Commission evaluate the implementation of the THMPD as from 31 March 2004? How does the Directive affect SMEs producing traditional herbal medicinal products and does the Commission in any way help such SMEs to achieve authorisation? How will the said authorisation requirements affect prices of herbal medicinal products? Does the Directive allow the use (i.e. via injections) of medications produced from herbs which are successfully used in treating patients with cancer and other malignant diseases? Will this Directive affect the production and use of homeopathic medicine?

 
  
 

Before entering into the detail of each question there are two points that the Commission would like to highlight.

All medicinal products, including herbal medicinal products, need a marketing authorisation to be placed on the EU market. This is a fundamental requirement of European pharmaceutical legislation and a key lesson learnt from the Thalidomide incident of the 60's.

Traditional herbal medicinal products have particular characteristics, notably their long tradition of use. To take account of this, the EU has introduced a lighter, simpler and less costly registration procedure for them, while providing the necessary guarantees of quality, safety and efficacy.

In addition, the Commission would like to stress that the registration of traditional herbal medicinal products is a national procedure under the responsibility of the competent authorities of the Member States.

Let the Commission now turn to the individual questions of the Honourable Member:

1. The European Medicines Agency has prepared a report with the information provided by the Member States on the number of authorised or registered herbal medicinal products at the end of 2010. This data shows an increasing number of herbal medicinal products registered at the end of 2010 and of applications for registration received by the competent authorities of the Member States. In the second half of 2011 a new report will be prepared to assess the situation after the end of the transitional period.

2. The Directive on traditional herbal medicinal products alleviates the burden on companies which is particularly relevant for Small and Medium-sized Enterpreises (SMEs). The Commission wants to stress again that a simplified registration procedure is introduced to facilitate the placing on the market of traditional herbal medicinal products for all companies, including small and medium-sized enterprises (SMEs).

The simplified procedure allows the registration of traditional herbal medicinal products, without requiring particulars and documents on tests and trials on safety and efficacy, which the applicant is otherwise obliged to provide under the marketing authorisation procedure. Instead the applicant only has to provide sufficient evidence of the medicinal use of the product throughout a period of at least 30 years, including at least 15 years in the European Union.

The procedure is further facilitated by the publication of monographs on a number of substances to which companies can refer in order to complete their application for registration in the Member States to comply with the requirements of the simplified procedure.

3. As regards the cost of registration or authorisation, the Commission needs to point out that it is the responsibility of the Members States to determine the fees that companies pay for the registration of traditional herbal medicinal products which takes place at national level.

4. Directive 2004/24/EC does not apply to medicinal products administered by injection as the scope of this Directive is limited to traditional herbal medicinal products which are oral, external and/or inhalation preparations.

5. The Directive does not affect production and use of homeopathic medicine. A specific simplified registration procedure for homeopathic medicines is also foreseen by the EU pharmaceutical legislation.
