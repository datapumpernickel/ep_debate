Question no 8 by Esko Olavi Seppänen (H-0447/03)
 Subject: Legal basis of the nuclear safety directive
 

The Commission’s proposal CNS/2003/0021 for a Council (EURATOM) directive on nuclear safety proposes harmonising the administration of funds for decommissioning nuclear power stations. Does the Commission have the appropriate legal basis to make such a proposal?

 
  
 

(IT) The Council informs the honourable Member that the question of the legal basis is still being examined by the competent Council Working Group. At this stage, therefore, the Council is unable to predict the outcome of the discussions.

 

Question no 9 by Lennart Sacrédeus (H-0450/03)
 Subject: The EMU Stability Pact and active measures to combat the recession
 

On 2 July, speaking on behalf of the Italian Presidency, Prime Minister Silvio Berlusconi admitted in the European Parliament that the members of the Euro zone no longer had any financial and monetary means of combating recessions and that instead there was a need for ‘European measures’, an active European financial policy and measures by the European Investment Bank. The Council’s new President also called for a relaxation of the EMU Stability and Growth Pact for the euro.

What is the Council’s view of the proposal made in June/July by France’s Defence Minister, Michèle Alliot-Marie, partly with the support of her German counterpart Peter Struck, that purchases of armaments should be regarded not as expenditure but as investment, so that they would have no bearing on the balanced-budget requirement, and the suggestion by Italy’s Minister for European Affairs, Rocco Buttiglione, that research funding should be treated in the same way? What active ‘European measures’ are envisaged in order to overcome the recession?

 
  
 

(IT) The Stability and Growth Pact provides for assessment of Member States' general government budgetary positions, as set out in the national accounts. Detailed provisions for the establishment of the national accounts, including the concept of 'general government', are laid down in Council Regulation No 2223/96 of 25 June 1996 on the European System of National and Regional Accounts in the Community (commonly referred to as ESA 95).

The established figures for 'general government' budgetary positions include both defence and research expenditure, so these are taken into account when considering national budgetary positions in the context of the Stability and Growth Pact. The Council has not discussed any proposal to alter ESA 95 definitions for the purposes of surveillance under the Stability and Growth Pact.

In respect of the content of public expenditure programmes, the Council has always emphasised the importance of the quality of public expenditure when assessing Member States' annual Stability and Convergence Programmes in the context of the Stability and Growth Pact. This was reflected in the ECOFIN Council's report to the Spring 2003 European Council on 'Strengthening the co-ordination of budgetary policies', which was endorsed by the European Council. This was the most recent discussion in ECOFIN of the budgetary coordination framework and concluded unequivocally that 'there is no need to change either the Treaty or the Stability and Growth Pact, nor to introduce new budgetary objectives or rules'. The report did, however, note that 'greater attention should be paid, within the overall constraints of the Stability and Growth Pact, to the quality of public finances with a view to raising the growth potential of the EU economies in conformity with the Lisbon agenda'. Thus the quality of public finances and their contribution to raising potential growth will be assessed in the context of the Stability and Growth Pact without any change to the parameters of the Pact itself.

The 'European measures' which might be envisaged to promote growth are presented both by the Italian Presidency in its 'European Action for Growth' and by the Commission in its 'Initiative for Growth'.

How the EIB might contribute is described in the EIB's Memorandum to the ECOFIN Council: 'The EIB's role in promoting long-term growth through investment in TENs and major R&D projects'. All three of these documents were discussed by the ECOFIN Council on 15 July, at which it adopted Conclusions giving a mandate to the Economic and Financial Committee for further work. Those Conclusions clearly note that further discussion of the issue is expected at the ECOFIN Council and the European Council in October, then at the ECOFIN Council in November and the European Council in December. Other Council formations will pursue work on the particular aspects of concern to them in order to inform discussions at the European Council.

 

Question no 10 by Carlos Carnero González (H-0453/03)
 Subject: Murder of the Spanish citizen Ana Isabel Sánchez Torralba in Equatorial Guinea
 

According to the information available at the time of writing, the Spanish citizen Ana Isabel Sánchez Torralba was murdered yesterday in Equatorial Guinea by means of gunshots fired by local military personnel at the coach in which she was travelling through the mainland part of the country. She had travelled to Guinea (together with various companions) in order to work for Misioneras Escolapias. This is a brutal act which has cost a young Spaniard her life and which was committed by people who, in a premeditated, unjustifiable and criminal fashion, opened fire on a civilian vehicle which had just passed a military checkpoint. Such a deed illustrates the behaviour of representatives of the forces of order who are accustomed to attacking and mistreating members of the general public. What information is the Council to seek? How does it intend to protest? How will it insist that justice is done in the case of the murder of a European citizen? What effect will this crime have on relations between the EU and the dictatorial regime in Equatorial Guinea?

 
  
 

(IT) The Council does not have information on the alleged murder of the Spanish citizen Ana Isabel Sánchez Torralba in Equatorial Guinea and in these circumstances it is not in a position to identify specific steps that could be undertaken. The Council is of the opinion that the problem of violence and crimes should be addressed in general terms and not only in relation to EU citizens.

The EU is deeply concerned about the deterioration of the internal political situation in Equatorial Guinea with regard to the human rights record and the democratisation process. Following last year's elections (December 2002) the EU expressed its concern over the political development in the country and in particular on the increased human rights violations. The EU urged the government and the political opposition to hold an all-inclusive political dialogue on the process towards democratisation and on establishing guarantees for the respect of human rights. It will continue to do so and in any case to purchase any action within its contracts with the relevant authorities to prevent and clear up dramatic situation as the one referred to by the Honourable Parliamentarian in his question.

 

Question no 11 by Paulo Casaca (H-0455/03)
 Subject: List of terrorist organisations
 

At Question Time on 4 June the Council stated that lists of terrorist organisations were drawn up by independent investigating magistrates(1), which constitutes a gross distortion of the letter, form and practice of legislation on the part of the Council.

In July and for the first time in Europe an independent judicial organisation in a Member State - France - had the opportunity of expressing its views on the accusations of terrorism made against the Iranian resistance. Will the Council not consider putting into practice what it stated as a fact?

Has the Council already taken into consideration the Argentine judiciary’s decision to issue a series of international arrest warrants against officials of the Iranian regime for acts of terrorism carried out in Buenos Aires, Berlin and Paris, which claimed dozens of lives?

Why does not the Council hesitate to replace the Iranian opposition organisations on the list of terrorist organisations by the Iranian regime itself?

Does the Council not consider that its attitude constitutes a blatant violation of the rules inherent in the rule of law, that it is putting the lives and safety of European citizens at risk and that by not telling the truth about terrorism it is failing to treat Parliament with respect?

 
  
 

(IT) The Council would like to remind the Honourable Member that the decision taken by the Council on 2 May 2002 to include the 'Mujahedin-e-Khalq' in the EU list of persons and groups involved in terrorist acts was based on a detailed and in-depth examination of available information fully complying with the criteria laid down in Article 1(4) of Common Position 2001/931/CFSP of 27 December 2001. These criteria were set out to ensure that all cases comply with the fundamental principles and procedures of the rule of law and respect human rights.

The Council would like to clarify once again that the inclusion in the list of a person or group is subject to a prior decision taken by a competent authority. The competent authorities in question are those addressed in Article 1(4) of the above mentioned Common Position.

In connection with the raid undertaken on 17 June 2003 by the French authorities against the MKO, the Council is not aware of any decision by the French judiciary stating whether or not the MKO is a terrorist organisation.

As regards the Argentine judiciary's decision to issue a series of international arrest warrants against officials of the Iranian regime, the Council has not taken a position on this matter.

The EU is currently negotiating with the Iranian authorities a Trade and Co-operation agreement and, in parallel, an agreement on political dialogue and co-operation on counter-terrorism. The Council has underlined that all these agreements form an indissociable whole as regards their entry into force, application and denunciation.

The EU also takes advantage of all political dialogue meetings with Iran to press for further progress in democratic reforms. In this regard, a specific dialogue on Human Rights has been set up with Iran; two meetings have already taken place and a third one is scheduled for September.

The EU has always made clear in its dialogue with all third countries that fighting terrorism must be done in full respect of human rights and the rule of law, and that it must by no means serve as a pretext to enforce or maintain restrictions on fundamental freedoms.

 
 

(1) H-0312/03 – oral answer of 4.6.2003.

 

Question no 13 by Brian Crowley (H-0460/03)
 Subject: Ensuring the future development of the western peripheral countries
 

With the geographic centre of Europe moving further east once the next enlargement process has been completed, will the Council state the ways in which it will ensure that the interests of peripheral countries in the west continue to be developed and promoted?

 
  
 

(IT) According to the EC Treaty, economic and social cohesion is both a mission and a policy for the European Community. The economic and social cohesion objectives designed to promote the harmonious development of the Community as a whole, set out in Title XVII of the Treaty, will remain valid in the context of enlargement.

First of all it will be up to the Commission, in the context of its proposals - expected in 2004 - for new regulations governing the Structural Funds for the 2007-2013 programming period, to assess the needs of both the new Member States and the existing ones. It will then be up to the European Parliament and the Council to draw up provisions to take account of the interests of the various regions concerned, in particular in the light of their peripheral location. A number of regions in western Europe are in this situation, but they are not the only ones.

Question no 15 by Seán Ó Neachtain (H-0465/03)
 Subject: Training for specialists treating drug misusing patients
 

The Council adopted in June 2003 a series of resolutions(1) on tackling the problems associated with drug abuse. In its resolution on ‘Drug dependencies within the national health care’, the Council stressed the importance of ‘ensuring that medical and other professionals undergo appropriate training to enable them to acquire the necessary skills for providing quality services to drug misusing patients.’

Does the Council consider that the European Social Fund can be used to train the medical experts and other professionals referred to in the Council’s resolution and indeed other relevant actors in local communities and schools to help them develop the necessary drug prevention and counselling skills?

 
  
 

(IT) At the 11-26 June 2003 Council meeting a series of relevant texts was indeed adopted, including a recommendation on preventing and reducing the damage to health caused by drug dependency, an EU action plan on drugs and a series of Justice and Home Affairs resolutions. The resolutions relate to drug dependents within the national healthcare system, to education curricula on substance misuse disorders and to drug use amongst young people.

All of the texts place particular emphasis on the importance of properly integrating health and social services in order to provide greater continuity in the treatment of drug dependents, and on the development of training courses for medical professionals with a view to enabling drug-dependent patients to be offered high-quality services through improvements to both diagnosis and treatment.

To this end the representatives of the Member States' governments meeting within the Council have stressed, in the resolution on education curricula on substance misuse disorders, that specific curricula aimed at students and professionals in the medical and other healthcare sectors must be drawn up.

In reply to the precise question raised by the honourable Member, the Council recalls that under the current regulation on the European Social Fund (ESF), which was adopted by the European Parliament and the Council on 12 July 1999, one of the eligible activities is, in fact, education and vocational training, including the provision and upgrading of basic skills. It is, however, of the responsibility of the national and regional authorities to present projects in accordance with the main priorities of a development programme defined in cooperation with the Commission. Local actors in economic and social affairs are also involved in the exercise. The eligibility of the training activities mentioned by the Honourable Parliamentarian is to be considered in the light of those elements and rules.

 
 

(1) 2516th Council meeting, Luxembourg, 11, 12, 17, 18, 19-25-26 June 2003.

Question no 17 by Pedro Marset Campos (H-0469/03)
 Subject: Failure by the Spanish Government to fulfil its commitments to SINTEL workers
 

Almost two years have passed since the proposal and conditions put forward by the Spanish Government were accepted in the form of the agreement of 3 August 2001, the sole aim of which was to implement the package of measures for resolving the crisis at SINTEL (an enterprise declared bankrupt on 28 June 2001). The problem is that the representatives of the workers and creditors have not been called to a meeting of any kind either by the bankruptcy bodies or by the management or Telefónica, and this is impeding implementation of the agreement. In view of the helplessness of the workers and the Spanish Government’s failure to deliver on its commitments, does the Council not see a need for that government to intervene and fulfil those commitments, thereby resolving the SINTEL dispute once and for all?

 
  
 

(IT) The Council is aware of the fact that the implementation of the rescue package in respect of the failed telecommunications company SINTEL is a particularly sensitive subject in Spain.

Community legislation adopted in recent years demonstrates that the information and consultation of employees and their representatives is an important issue for the Council.

Provisions on employee information and consultation are contained in Council Directive 94/45/EC of 22 September 1994 on the establishment of a European Works Council or a procedure in Community-scale undertakings and Community-scale groups of undertakings for the purposes of informing and consulting employees (1), Council Directive 98/59/EC of 20 July 1998 on the approximation of the laws of the Member States relating to collective redundancies (2) and Council Directive 2001/23/EC of 12 March 2001 on the approximation of the laws of the Member States relating to the safeguarding of employees' rights in the event of transfers of undertakings, businesses or parts of undertakings or businesses (3). Another key instrument in this context is Directive 2002/14/EC of the European Parliament and of the Council of 11 March 2002 establishing a general framework for informing and consulting employees in the European Community (4), which the Member States should implement no later than 23 March 2005.

However, it is the Commission, which is responsible for monitoring and, where appropriate, enforcing the application of the relevant directives by the Member States. The Council is consequently not in a position to comment on this specific case.

 
 

(1) OJ L 254 of 30.9.1994, p. 64.
(2) OJ L 225 of 12.8.1998, p. 16.
(3) OJ L 82 of 22.3.2001, p. 16.
(4) OJ L 80 of 23.3.2002, p. 29.

Question no 20 by Myrsini Zorba (H-0478/03)
 Subject: Hydrogen
 

The hydrogen economy was one of the items on the agenda of discussions at the EU-US summit in June. According to the joint statement, cooperation between the two parties forms 'part of our broadening cooperation on energy ' and concerns the 'technical, legal, and commercial basis'.

Will the Council provide further information and data concerning cooperation, especially in the field of research, and the fostering of public-private collaboration? Will it also provide the timetable and action plan relating to 'International Partnership for the Hydrogen Economy'?

 
  
 

(IT) The honourable Member is no doubt aware of the long-term nature of developing the potential of hydrogen as an energy carrier in the interests of sustainability. It should be noted that the Community has funded research on hydrogen and fuel cells since the 1970s and this funding has been substantially increased within the current sixth framework programme as compared to its predecessor. Given the size of the task of developing the hydrogen economy, international co-operation is considered to be necessary, indeed vital, to achieving progress. The recent agreement with the US should be seen against this background; it results in fact, from an amendment to an existing implementing agreement between the Commission and the US Department of Energy (DOE)(1).

The honourable Member will also be aware that implementation of the Science and Technology agreement concluded between The European Community and the Government of the United States of America in 1998 falls under the responsibility of the Commission; the Council has no direct involvement in its implementation.

The Council, therefore, has no specific tasks in formulating the timetable and action plan relating to the 'International Partnership for the Hydrogen Economy', which is a US initiative, and which is being developed during the course of 2003. It will, of course, as part of the long-term policy development in energy and research matters follow progress very closely.

For more detailed information regarding the timetable and action plan relating to the 'International Partnership for the Hydrogen Economy', the honourable Member is advised to take up these questions with the competent authorities.

There are, for the moment, various means for fostering public-private collaboration on research and development, the most obvious one being the implementation of the sixth framework programme within the context of the European Research Area. Various recent initiatives for increasing investment in R&D to reach the 3% GDP objective have underlined the importance of this collaboration(2), the content of which could be better detailed by the Commission.

 
 

(1) 'Amendment to the Implementing Agreement between the Department of Energy of the United States of America and the European Commission, for Non-nuclear Energy Scientific and Technological Co-operation relating to Co-operation in the Area of Fuel Cells.'
(2) See Communications from the Commission:
- 'Investing in Research: An Action Plan for Europe' (doc. 8860/03 RECH 75)
- 'An Initiative for Growth: investing in Trans-European Networks and major R&D projects' (doc. 11343/03 ECOFIN 216 FIN 321 ECO 153 TRANS 187 ENER 217 RECH 121).

 

Question no 21 by Patricia McKenna (H-0484/03)
 Subject: EU Agreement on a Visa Information System
 

EU Governments have unanimously decided on an EU Agreement on a Visa Information System, which will include harmonised biometric identification, including iris scanning and fingerprints, linked to EU databases. Can the Council say what measures are now being taken by Member States to follow on from this decision and what impact will it have on the movement of persons? Also what consultation took place with National Parliaments and the European Parliament prior to this decision being taken?

 
  
 

(IT) At the session on 5-6 June 2003, the Council 'Justice and Home Affairs', adopted 'conclusions on the development of the Visa Information System'. In these conclusions, that were subsequently endorsed by the Thessaloniki European Council on 19-20 June 2003, the Council 'invites the Commission to continue its preparatory work on the development of the VIS in cooperation with Member States' and states that the Council 'will give the necessary political orientation by December 2003 on the basic elements of the VIS, including the architecture, the functionalities, taking into account the financial component, the choice of biometric identifier(s), etc.'.

As the honourable Member will have deducted from these quotations, no final decision has been made neither on the inclusion of biometric identifiers nor on the type of identifiers, if this option is included into the VIS. The Commission is expected, in the light of the political input from Council by the end of the year, to submit appropriate proposals for the legal framework of the VIS in the beginning of 2004, and these proposals will be examined according to the usual procedures laid down in the Treaty establishing the European Community.

 

Question no 22 by Bernd Posselt (H-0486/03)
 Subject: Croatia's application for membership
 

What steps will the Italian Presidency of the Council take before the end of the year with a view to finally granting Croatia candidate status, as called for by the European Parliament?

ensuring more extensive interfacing with programmes and actions conducted at national level,

developing software enabling parents and/or teachers to limit children’s access to inappropriate material and content on the Internet,
