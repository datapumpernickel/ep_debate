QUESTIONS TO COUNCIL
Question no 18 by Cecilia Malmström (H-0652/02)
 Subject: Membership of the United Nations for Taiwan
 

It is currently being debated in Taiwan whether the country should apply for membership of the United Nations. This would be an interesting international indication of how the people of Taiwan view its future as an independent state and would probably entail a change in relations with the People’s Republic of China. Taiwan is currently one of the few democratic countries in South-east Asia and therefore deserves active support from the European Union, inter alia with the aim of persuading the Chinese regime to introduce democracy and respect for human rights.

How will the Council respond if Taiwan applies for membership of the United Nations?

 
  
 

(EN) The Union has repeatedly, and for the last time in the joint Summit statement issued at the last EU China Summit in Copenhagen on 24 September 2002, underlined the EU's hope for a peaceful resolution of the Taiwan question through constructive dialogue and reiterated the Union's attachment to the "One China Principle".

The Council has not discussed the question of a possible Taiwanese membership in the UN.

 

Question no 19 by Rodi Kratsa-Tsagaropoulou (H-0658/02)
 Subject: Combating paedophilia
 

The lenient sentences recently given to people charged with taking part in a major paedophile network in France frustrated public opinion. In view of the fact that none of the existing programmes on combating organised crime or on police and judicial cooperation is directly concerned with paedophilia, will the Council draw up a proposal to harmonise the Member States' legislation in regard to the definition of the offence, penalties, the age of the victims and their care.

Does the Council believe that Europol and Eurojust have the appropriate resources to track down and crack down on such networks?

 
  
 

(DA) As was pointed out in the answer to question H-0431/01 from the honourable Member, combating child pornography is a top priority for the European Union. The Council would point out to the honourable Member that the European Parliament has been consulted on a Draft Council Framework Decision on combating the sexual exploitation of children and child pornography(1), which the Commission submitted on 22 January 2001. Parliament gave its opinion on this proposal on 20 September 2001(2).

It is anticipated that the Justice and Home Affairs Council will reach a general consensus on this draft at its meeting on 14–15 October 2002.

Europol has made the fight against paedophilia one of its priority objectives, as can be seen in its work schedule for 2003(3).

Although Eurojust has previously assisted the national judicial authorities in legal cases pending in this field, the Council is not authorised to comment on the substance of a judgment given in a Member State.

Among other things, a coordinators' meeting was held on this subject in June 2002, with the participation of Eurojust, the national judicial authorities and Europol. At present, it is difficult to establish whether this body, which became operational in March 2002,(4) has adequate resources at its disposal.

 
 

(1) OJ C E of 27.2.2001, p. 327.
(2) PE 309.466.
(3) Document 8385/02 EUROPOL 27 (can be found on the Council’s website).
(4) Council Decision of 28 February 2002 setting up Eurojust with a view to reinforcing the fight against serious crime (OJ L 63, 6.3.2002, pp. 1–13).

 

Question no 20 by Torben Lund (H-0665/02)
 Subject: Repatriation to Afghanistan
 

In connection with the repatriation of Afghans, will the Council comment on the Amnesty International report(1) in the light of the EU’s ongoing discussion of a common repatriation policy with regard to Afghans?

The report documents the serious problems that such repatriations may entail and Amnesty International calls on the EU to refrain from encouraging or forcing Afghans to return to their home country. The report also contains a number of recommendations that ought to be followed in the event of voluntary return. As I am very concerned at the uncertain situation facing these people, will the Council explain in particular how future repatriation policy relates to these recommendations?

 
  
 

(DA) Since the collapse of the Taliban regime and the establishment of new political institutions in Afghanistan, preparations have been under way, both in the Member States concerned and at EU level, for the repatriation of Afghans illegally resident in EU Member States. Some Member States have already adopted programmes such as this; where appropriate, in cooperation with the Office of the United Nations High Commissioner for Refugees.

As regards EU action in this field, I would like to draw attention to the fact that the conclusions from the Seville European Council urged the Council to adopt a repatriation programme based on the Commission Green Paper on repatriation by the end of the year. This programme shall also aim to provide ‘the best possible facilities for early return to Afghanistan’, as stated in point No 30 of the conclusions.

In order to implement the conclusions from Seville, the Danish Presidency has presented a draft plan specifically dealing with repatriation to Afghanistan. This draft was discussed at the meeting of the Council of ministers with responsibility for asylum and immigration last week, where it met with widespread approval. The Presidency anticipates that the plan will be adopted at the next Council meeting involving this group, which is on 28 and 29 November. Subsequently, it is anticipated that the plan can be implemented as swiftly as possible in 2003.

The plan envisages voluntary repatriation as its preferred model, but also presupposes that, after a reasonable period of time, expulsion can be resorted to if the persons concerned will not return of their own volition.

The Presidency is of course familiar with the Amnesty International report from July of this year to which the questioner refers, and its recommendations. We also agree wholeheartedly with Amnesty that returning to Afghanistan at the present time is not without problems. This is one of the reasons why the plan also contains provisions on counselling of returnees. Furthermore, the plan envisages the establishment of a coordination group under the auspices of the Commission, one of whose tasks will be to ensure the smooth implementation of the plan, taking into account such factors as reception conditions and capability in Afghanistan. Lastly, the plan also envisages the involvement of relevant organisations such as the Office of the United Nations High Commissioner for Refugees and the International Organisation for Migration, and of course the Afghan authorities.

 
 

(1) Afghanistan: Continuing need for Protection and Standards for Return of Afghan Refugees (AI Index: ASA 11/014/2002).

 

Question no 21 by Jonas Sjöstedt (H-0668/02)
 Subject: Salmonella in marinated meat
 

In conjunction with five Swedish municipalities, the Swedish National Food Administration has taken some 40 samples of imported meat preparations, approximately one third of which tested positive for salmonella. Chicken products were the worst offenders.

In November 2001, the Commission decided that the rule concerning compulsory salmonella testing does not apply to meat preparations. However, when meat preparations are imported from a country outside the EU, the Swedish guarantee applies.

The tests conducted in Sweden show that meat preparations must be subject to compulsory salmonella testing if the salmonella guarantee is to be effective in that country.

What does the Council intend to do to ensure that meat preparations are subject, as soon as possible, to compulsory salmonella testing before being exported to Sweden?

 
  
 

(EN) The Council is currently discussing two Commission proposals that are relevant to Sweden’s salmonella guarantees.

The first proposal is for a Regulation on the control of salmonella and other zoonoses. Its aim is to reduce the prevalence of salmonella within the Community, particularly in primary production. It would not result in any reduction of the guarantees that Finland and Sweden currently enjoy with respect to live animals. The provision of the Commission’s proposal concerning salmonella guarantees has not given rise to controversy during the discussions within the Council.

The second proposal concerns the hygiene of food of animal origin. The Commission’s proposal would have preserved Finland and Sweden’s current salmonella guarantees, but would have permitted their modification through comitology. The European Parliament has endorsed the maintenance of the status quo for Finland and Sweden, but proposed that it not be possible to modify the guarantees other than through the co-decision procedure. The Council has yet to conclude its discussions on this point.

On the occasion of its meeting on October 15, the Council held a policy debate on food safety and zoonoses and it agreed to instruct its preparatory instances to pursue the in-depth examination of the dossier in order to report back to the Council at its forthcoming session with a view to reaching a possible political agreement.

 

Question no 22 by Esko Olavi Seppänen (H-0671/02)
 Subject: The Statute for Members of the European Parliament
 

A Statute for Members of the European Parliament has been in preparation. The Council has not reached agreement on it. Does the Danish Presidency intend to bring the matter to a conclusion?

 
  
 

(DA) Article 190(5) of the EC Treaty stipulates that it is up to the European Parliament to lay down the regulations and general conditions governing the performance of the duties of its Members, after seeking an opinion from the Commission and with the approval of the Council acting unanimously.

As the result of the Irish referendum on 19 October 2002 was positive, the Treaty of Nice will enter into force very soon. In accordance with this Treaty, the Council shall take decisions regarding approval on the basis of a qualified majority vote instead of unanimously, except where provisions in connection with the tax scheme are concerned.

It is true that the European Parliament adopted a draft Statute on 3 December 1998; that is to say, before the Treaty of Amsterdam – which established the required legal basis – entered into force on 1 May 1999. It is also true that Parliament formalised the draft on 5 May 1999 and subsequently confirmed this with a vote on 27 October 1999, following the elections in the same year. Since then, however, new ideas have come to the fore in Parliament, which have been discussed, in particular, in the Committee on Legal Affairs and the Internal Market, on the basis of, among other things, the conclusions reached by the High Level Group.

The Council would draw attention to the fact that a dialogue has been taking place the whole time between the various Presidencies and the representatives nominated by Parliament, in contact groups, for example.

For its part, the Council, on 4 December 2000, stated its position on some of the main elements of the Statute that Parliament had proposed, by endorsing a letter from the President of the Council, Mr Moscovici, to Mrs Fontaine.

With regard to the tax scheme, the Council reached unanimity on 29 October 2001.

Lastly, the President of the Council, Mr Piqué, sent a letter to Mr Cox on 8 May of this year, reminding him that the Council is prepared to work closely with Parliament to bring about a compromise which is acceptable to both institutions and which can be adopted well in advance of the next European Parliament elections, in 2004.

The Council is of the opinion that a common statute will undoubtedly contribute to conferring greater dignity on the office of Member of the European Parliament by generating greater openness in the general conditions for the exercise of this office.

 

Question no 23 by Antonios Trakatellis (H-0676/02)
 Subject: Detectability of genetically modified DNA or genetically modified protein in products produced from GMOs
 

With a view to the publication of the Council’s common position concerning traceability and labelling of genetically modified organisms and traceability of food and feed products produced from genetically modified organisms(1) and amending directive 2001/18/EC(2), what is the Council’s position regarding the detectability, by scientific methods, of genetically modified DNA or genetically modified protein in products produced from genetically modified organisms? How will it protect consumers from fraud, product adulteration, distortion of competition and barriers to trade when analytical methods are not used to detect the difference in products, some of which, despite being produced from GMOs, are ultimately identical in all respects to the corresponding conventional products, i.e. do not contain genetically modified DNA or protein and do not exhibit any other characteristics to distinguish them from the conventional products?

 
  
 

(EN) The oral question H-0676/02 of the Honourable Member of the European Parliament, Prof. Antonios Trakatellis, regards two aspects: Firstly the Council's position on detectability of genetically modified DNA or protein, and secondly consumer protection in cases where products produced from GMOs are analytically identical to conventional products.

Detectability as such is a phenomenon that depends on scientific facts and the availability of technical solutions. It is therefore up to the Commission and not to the Council to scrutinise and assess detectability and to suggest appropriate solutions.
With regard to genetically modified DNA or protein in products produced from genetically modified organisms, it is a well known fact that there are cases where products produced from GMOs cannot be analytically distinguished from conventional products.

In cases where analytical methods cannot be used to distinguish conventional products from GMO-derived products, the Council is determined to ensure a high degree of consumer protection by providing for the necessary rules in the relevant legislation. The Council and the European Parliament currently discuss a regulation concerning traceability and labelling of products consisting of or containing GMOs and traceability of food and feed products produced from GMOs and amending Directive 2001/18/EC (2001/0180/COD)). This proposal contains, as you will well be aware of, the following elements:

Systems and procedures to identify to whom and from whom products are made available, at all stages of placing on the market;

The transmission by operators of information concerning the identity of a product, i.e. that the product "is produced from GMO's" in case of GMO-derived products;

The retention by operators of specified information for a period of at least five years, to be made available to competent authorities upon request.

This envisaged so-called paper trail system has proven to be practicable and safe in similar situations, e.g. for the documentation of products from organic farming. Likewise, it is an essential advantage of the chosen procedure that consumers are offered, by the appropriate labelling as foreseen in the parallel proposal for a regulation on genetically modified food and feed, a choice regarding the production method they wish to support by their purchase.

In parallel to the proposal on traceability and labelling of GMOs, the Council currently discusses a proposal for a regulation on genetically modified food and feed, equally covering derived products ("produced from GMOs").

 
 

(1) OJ C 304 E, 30.10.2001, p. 327
(2) OJ L 106, 17.4.2001, p. 1

 

Question no 24 by Richard Howitt (H-0677/02)
 Subject: Prioritising development issues in the light of the demise of the Development Council
 

In the light of the demise of the Development Council, the General Affairs and External Relations Council, due to be held this November, has been chosen as a forum in which to treat development issues. In what way will the agenda and organisational proceedings of the meeting be adjusted accordingly in the meeting of the GAC/RELEX Council, as is stipulated in the Conclusions of the Seville Summit, so that the attendance and participation of development ministers is facilitated and focused upon?

Does the Council have long-term plans to continue to dedicate one General Affairs and External Relations Council meeting per Presidency to development issues? How exactly will these Council meetings be organised in order to ensure that development remains the priority which Heads of State claimed it would be?

 
  
 

(EN) At the European Council in Seville it was decided to reduce the number of Council formations. And it was decided to integrate all matters related to the Union's external actions through the creation of a new General Affairs and External Relations Council covering inter alia the development cooperation policy. This conclusion was reflected by the relevant amendments to the Council's Rules of Procedure on 22 July 2002. The aim of the reform of the Council is to improve the functioning of the Council in the run-up to enlargement and to enhance the coherence between the components of the Union's external policies.

The Council attaches great importance to the full implementation of these new rules. In order to accomplish the above mentioned objectives of efficiency and coherence, while at the same time avoiding the risk of breaking up policy domains such as development cooperation, the Danish Presidency is proposing carefully balanced agendas for the meetings of the General Affairs and External Relations Council.

Following the recent international conferences and the World Summit on Sustainable Development in particular, development-related issues are at the forefront of the EU agenda. The Danish Presidency emphasised the importance it attaches to development issues when it included a public and broadcasted debate on the key issue of Trade and Development on the agenda of the meeting of the new General Affairs and External Relations Council on 30 September 2002.

For reasons of consistency and in order to facilitate the participation of the Ministers for development cooperation, the Danish Presidency is aiming to deal with a number of development related items in one session at the Council meeting on 18-19 November 2002. The development issues will inter alia include Trade and Development, Reform and Efficiency of the Community’s Development Policy and Follow-up on international conferences.

In order to facilitate the participation of the Ministers for development cooperation, the Presidency will try -insofar as possible- to place the development-related issues together on the agenda of one of the days of the Council meeting. This is done without prejudicing the organisational proceedings to be decided upon by the future Presidencies.

However, it should be noted that the Seville conclusions do not contradict the rule established by the Treaty (article 203) which stipulates that Member States are competent to define the person who will represent them within the Council, being assumed that this person should be entitled to engage the Member State Government.

Discussions on the practical implementation and the long-term implications of the Conclusions from the European Council in Seville are still ongoing. The Council is ready to review and if necessary adjust the organisation of the proceedings of the Council on a continuous basis in order to ensure that all aspects of EU’s external relations incl. development cooperation is dealt with in an appropriate manner.

As a conclusion, the Council confirms that development remains a priority for the Council's agenda and that the organisational proceedings decided by the European Council do not detract from its importance. On the contrary, they are intended to enhance the development dimension of EU related policies and to meet the overarching objective of promoting comprehensive EU external action.

Question no 26 by Marit Paulsen (H-0685/02)
 Subject: Imports of organic foodstuffs
 

In order to import goods into an EU country, an import authorisation is required. On 1 November, EU bureaucracy will further increase with the introduction of a requirement for a ‘certificate of inspection’. Such a certificate must accompany every consignment of organic products imported from countries outside the EU.

Organic foodstuffs afford poor countries an opportunity to break into the world market. However, the complicated paperwork involved will create a less advantageous situation for growers. This will hamper and perhaps even prevent exports of organic food to the EU.

At the UN Environment Conference in Johannesburg, participants agreed that exports from the Third World should increase. Organic products were particularly singled out in this context.

The new requirement renders liberalisation of the world market illusory and constitutes a hidden non-tariff barrier to trade. Does the Council intend to adopt this approach systematically?

 
  
 

(EN) The Council is fully aware that organic foodstuffs provide an opportunity for poor countries to break into the world market. This is why, at the UN World Summit on Sustainable Development in Johannesburg, the EU backed the text in the Plan of Implementation on supporting voluntary WTO compatible market-based initiatives for the creation and expansion of domestic and international markets for environmentally friendly goods and services, including organic products.

The Council, in many of the decisions it takes, has to balance competing constraints and ensure coherence of its policies. This is at the heart of the public debate it held on trade and development on 30 September. The issue raised falls within this context: balancing the protection of consumers and the requirements of food safety with the opportunity that the EU market provides to developing countries.

Helping developing countries to meet EU requirements, such as for organic food and sanitary and phyto-sanitary measures, is part of the measures the Commission has put forward in its proposed action plan set out in its Communication on trade and development as compliance with such standards has emerged as one of the major constraints on the increase of exports by developing countries to industrialised markets. The Council is currently examining this Communication and will take a position on trade and development in the conclusions it plans to adopt on this subject at its session on 18-19 November.

The Council would reiterate that the inspection certificate mentioned by the honourable Member is part of the legislative provisions introduced by the Commission in accordance with Council Regulation No 2092/91 on organic farming. Its aim is to safeguard the equivalence of production standards for organic products available to the European consumer, regardless of their origin.

 Subject: Russian Federation - expulsion of Catholic priests
 

Is the expulsion of Catholic priests compatible with freedom of religious expression in the Russian constitution and with the international obligations of the Russian Federation in respect of religious freedom?

To this end, on 21 October 2002, the Council took stock of preparations for the EU-Russia Summit to be held in Copenhagen on 11 November 2002.
