QUESTIONS TO COUNCIL
Question no 10 by Ilda Figueiredo (H-0395/04)
 Subject: Situation of the 'Miami Five'
 

Over three years since the verdict of a Miami court in the case of five Cuban citizens unjustly accused of spying, the US Administration continues to deny their human rights in an unacceptable manner. The list of violations is considerable: they have been denied trial in a place chosen by themselves (their right under the UN conventions), as well as their rights as defendants under the US Constitution; their relatives, even those with US nationality, have been refused visiting rights; and their 'crimes' have attracted punitive and discriminatory sentences by comparison with similar cases involving US citizens or other third-country nationals.

Is the Council aware of the circumstances of these Cuban citizens? What action will it take to ensure that the fundamental rights of these five Cuban citizens are respected?

 
  
 

(EN) I would like to reiterate the major elements of the answers given by Council to similar questions put in the past, notably to Question 743/03, in December 2003:

"As a general comment, the Council would like to emphasise that it deplores any situation where human rights and respect of persons are not sufficiently guaranteed. As already stated in its answer to questions H-544 to 549/03, the Council is informed of the trial and imprisonment of five Cuban citizens resident in Florida, since 2001, and of the concerns expressed by HR NGOs about their imprisonment conditions. However, the Council is not competent to discuss the conditions of the trial.

The treatment of Cuban prisoners and their families is a bilateral issue between the USA and Cuba, since the protection of the rights and interests of States' nationals abroad is, pursuant to the Vienna Convention on Consular Relations, the individual responsibility of the State concerned. Moreover, the Geneva Conventions are laws governing only the treatment of persons captured during armed conflict, not of civilians, such as these two persons, who are being held pursuant to alleged breaches of criminal law.

As concerns the more general issue of respect for human rights, the Council underlines that it takes advantage of any meeting at whatever level with third countries to focus on the need for it to be ensured and promoted. In particular, the Council would like to stress that in its conclusions after its meeting in Brussels on 12 December last, the European Council released a declaration on transatlantic relations in which it insists on the necessity for the EU and its transatlantic partners to defend a common agenda based in particular on the promotion of the rule of law, democracy and human rights.

In the present case, nevertheless, the Council informs the Honorable Parliamentarian that the "Miami Five" case has not been discussed with the United States, being a bilateral problem between the USA and Cuba."

 

Question no 11 by Sérgio Ribeiro (H-0396/04)
 Subject: Worsening of the situation in the Middle East
 

The situation in Palestine remains unstable and disturbing. Despite the demonstrations and international protests, the same things continue: the building of settlements on the West Bank; the military occupation; the destruction of infrastructures (some of them built with EU funds), agriculture, housing and other assets; 'selective' assassinations, in reality not limited to their targets; constant violations of the fundamental rights of the Palestinian people by means of full-blooded state terrorism; the building of a quite unacceptable wall, which the UN special rapporteur on human rights in the occupied Palestinian territories has said must by now be condemned as an illegal act of annexation; and defiant and aggressive gestures against UN and Red Cross personnel who do not condone Israel's terrorist practices. It is vital to take a clear and objective stand against Israel's persistent violations of the Palestinians' rights and in favour of the Palestinians' right to form a sovereign state.

What initiatives will the Council take to ensure the application of the successive UN resolutions on the Middle East? Does it not consider that it has long since been desirable to suspend the Euro-Mediterranean association agreement between the EU and Israel, as called for in Parliament's resolution on the situation in the Middle East of 10 April 2002(1)?

 
  
 

(EN) The question of the honourable Member of Parliament includes a whole list of different items and developments which cover the Israeli-Palestinian conflict.

The EU is in continuous contact with the relevant partners in the international community and with the parties concerned in order to bring about a lasting peace in the Middle East. The last European Council of 4-5 November 2004, recalling the established EU positions, confirmed its commitment to the two-State solution as laid out in the Roadmap and agreed between the parties, which would result in a viable, contiguous, sovereign and independent Palestinian State existing side by side in peace with an Israel living within recognised and secure borders.

With respect to the application of UN resolutions, as specifically raised by

the honourable Member, it is up to the political bodies of the United Nations to decide how to act upon these resolutions.

 
 

(1) OJ C 127 E, 29.5.2003, p. 584

 

Question no 12 by Gay Mitchell (H-0402/04)
 Subject: Israel's withdrawal from Gaza
 

Will the Council give an opinion on Israeli Prime Minister Ariel Sharon's planned withdrawal of Jewish settlers from Gaza, and specifically whether the Council is of the opinion that this move is beneficial to the roadmap for peace?

 
  
 

(EN) At its meeting of 25-26 March, the European Council noted - what were then - the proposals for an Israeli withdrawal from the Gaza Strip and part of the northern West Bank to which the honourable Member of Parliament referred to in his question. In the view of the European Council, such a withdrawal could represent a significant step towards the implementation of the Roadmap, provided that, in accordance with the deliberations of the Council (External Relations) of 23 February 2004:

it took place in the context of the Roadmap;

it was a step towards a two State solution;

it did not involve a transfer of settlement activity to the West Bank;

there was an organised and negotiated handover of responsibility to the Palestinian Authority;

and Israel facilitated the rehabilitation and reconstruction of Gaza.

The European Council of 4 -5 November 2004 welcomed the Knesset vote on 26 October to support an Israeli withdrawal from the Gaza strip and part of the northern West Bank and expressed its willingness to support such a withdrawal as a first step in the overall process, in accordance with the conditions as laid out above, amongst which, that it takes place in the context of the Roadmap.

Question no 14 by Rodi Kratsa-Tsagaropoulou (H-0404/04)
 Subject: Parental leave in the EU
 

According to recent data from Eurobarometer, the majority of male employees in Europe (75%) are aware of their right to parental leave, but 84% of them do not take advantage of it. This is mainly because of financial considerations (42% cite the resultant reduction in income) and because men fear that it may damage their careers (31%). This issue has a very important part to play in increasing both the birth rate and the employment of women, and is directly linked with the Lisbon objectives.

What view does the Council take of the findings of this survey? Is it satisfied with the situation in the Member States as regards the implementation and above all the effectiveness of Directive 96/34/EC(1) (which provides for the entitlement to parental leave and regulates its duration for the whole of Europe)? Will the Council propose that the Commission adopt initiatives and measures relating to this subject in order to inform men and encourage them to make use of this right?

 
  
 

(EN) The Council understands the concern expressed by the honourable Member of Parliament regarding the results of the recent data from Eurobarometer concerning parental leave in the EU. However, as you know, it is not the practice of the Council, as a Community Institution, to formulate opinions on such surveys.

The Council, furthermore points out that the Commission is responsible for monitoring and, where appropriate, enforcing the application of the relevant directives by the Member States. In this respect, the Commission adopted a report in June 2003 on the implementation of Council Directive 96/34/EC which gives an extensive analysis of the situation in the various Member States regarding parental leave.

On a general basis the Council underlines its support to the aim of a balanced participation of women and men in family and working life as expressed in its Resolution of June 2000 in which the Council and the Ministers for Employment and Social Policy encouraged Member States to develop global and integrated strategies to achieve this aim, inter alia by “examining the scope for the respective legal systems to grant working men an individual and untransferable right to paternity leave, maintaining their rights to employment, to be taken at the same time as the mother takes maternity leave irrespective of the length of the periods of maternity and paternity leave.”

 
 

(1) OJ L 145, 19.6.1996, p. 4.

Question no 16 by Dimitrios Papadimoulis (H-0371/04)
 Subject: Tendering procedures for public works contracts in Greece
 

Following the judgment of the Court of Justice in case C-247/02, which was published on 7 October 2004, a debate has ensued in Greece as to whether the new legal framework governing tendering procedures for public works contracts in Greece is consistent with that judgment as regards the criteria for awarding such contracts.

Are the criteria for awarding public works contracts laid down in the new law consistent with the above judgment of the Court of Justice to the extent that the tendering procedures for public works contracts in Greece can be considered to be soundly based in law?

 
  
 

(FR) The new Greek law (3263/2004) on the procedures for the award of public works contracts was adopted and published in the Official Journal of the Hellenic Republic at the end of September 2004 and before the Court of Justice of the European Communities gave judgment in case C-247/02.

The Commission will contact the competent authorities to obtain an official copy of the law in question, as well as any relevant information relating to the application of the system for awarding public works contracts in Greece, with a view to examining the compatibility of this law with the above-mentioned judgment of the Court of Justice.

The Commission will certainly inform the honourable Member of the results of this examination and any action it may take to ensure that the said law is compatible with the Community law on public contracts.

 

Question no 17 by Mairead McGuinness (H-0374/04)
 Subject: Research into non-food uses of agricultural produce
 

In the current climate of concern surrounding the depletion of fossil fuels, reform of the CAP and more stringent rules on food produce and standards, what steps is the EU taking in the area of research to find non-food uses of agricultural produce, for example the use of biomass? Are any funds going into this type of research? If so, how much and what is the policy on such research?

 
  
 

(EN) Research into the non-food use of agricultural products has been a priority for the EU Research and Technological Development Framework Programmes (FP) for many years now.

Under FP5 (1998-2002) the main efforts dealing with the non-food use of agricultural products were funded through the “Quality of Life and Management of Living Resources Programme” and the “Energy, Environment and Sustainable Development Programme”. The main areas of research included the development of improved and sustainable crops, the manufacturing of bioproducts i.e. chemicals, bioplastics, cosmetics, drugs, polymers as well as the energy production (heat, electricity and biofuels for transport) from crops. Many projects are still ongoing and their results will be available within the next two years.

Under the current Framework Programme (2002-2006) funding is focused on non-food uses of agricultural products for energy (Sustainable Energy Systems). The research areas include energy production from biomass (heat, electricity, hydrogen), biofuels (fuels from biomass) for the transport sector as well as biomass use for the integrated production of energy and bioproducts (so called biorefineries). These research areas are in line with the Common Agricultural Policy which provides assistance for crops for energy production as well as with our energy policies concerning sustainable, secure and affordable energy supply for the Union.

The total budget for the Sustainable Energy Systems is € 890 million. From this budget, about 100 M€ will be allocated to biomass. It should be noted that, in addition to the agricultural products and residues, biomass includes forest and biowastes. Therefore, not all this budget is directly linked to the use of agricultural products.

----------------------------

The BIOMAT website funded by the EU gives a very good overview of all the FP5 projects as well as the opportunities open under FP6.

 

Question no 18 by Ilda Figueiredo (H-0375/04)
 Subject: Statement by Commissioner Almunia on the Stability Pact and the Cohesion Fund
 

The Portuguese newspaper 'Diário Económico', in an article of 12 October 2004 entitled 'Almunia threatens to block funding for cohesion states - Brussels admits it only punishes poorer Member States which breach the Stability Pact', reports Commissioner Almunia as stating: 'For both new and old EU Member States which are in receipt of Cohesion Fund monies, we have a powerful weapon which is not in the Pact. If a Member State in receipt of such funds fails to meet its undertakings regarding macroeconomic supervision, we can block its access to the Cohesion Fund'.

Can the Commission confirm this statement? If it is correct, can it explain how it intends to end such discrimination and whether it will block Structural Fund monies for Germany or agricultural funding for France? Does it believe that proposals of this nature represent equal treatment for all Member States?

Does the Commission believe it is rational to continue with a Pact which lacks all economic logic, penalises economic growth and employment and, even worse, seems in reality to apply only to the 'poorer' Member States?

 
  
 

(EN) The possibility to suspend the financing of new cohesion fund projects if a Member State has not implemented its stability or convergence programme in such a way as to avoid an excessive government deficit is foreseen by article 6 of the Council Regulation (1264/99) on the Cohesion Fund.

Macro-economic conditionality is one of the basic principles of the Cohesion Fund laid down in 1994. Additional Community assistance through the creation of the Cohesion Fund was to be provided to less developed countries which fulfilled the conditions for a sound budgetary situation as set out in article 104 of the Treaty, in line with the Protocol on economic and social cohesion.

However, the suspension of the Cohesion Fund assistance is not automatic.

First, though the excessive deficit procedure and conditionality are related, they are two separate procedures with different legal bases. Suspension needs a specific decision from the Council acting upon a specific recommendation from the Commission.

Second, conditionality would not be used as a sanction for past deficits which governments have no possibility to correct but rather as an incentive for the Member States concerned to take corrective action in the future. Thus, suspension would not be recommended at the stage where the existence of an excessive deficit is decided by the Council in accordance with article 104.6 of the Treaty and a recommendation is addressed to the Member State concerned for its correction. Suspension could be implemented if Member States persist in failing to put into practice the recommendation of the Council and do not take the measures for deficit reduction that are judged necessary by the Council. In other words, Member States will be granted the opportunity to take action before being sanctioned. If suspension is implemented, it will cease when effective action has been taken.

Such an approach allows the conditionality provision specific to the Cohesion Fund to be implemented in a manner consistent with the objective of sound public finances and the principle of equal treatment.

Does the Commissioner intend to support moves to revise the Dayton Agreement with a view to facilitating the return of displaced people, to the Bosnian entity Republika Srpska as well, and reforming Bosnia-Herzegovina's constitution so as to create a federation of three peoples with equal rights?

Question no 21 by Catherine Stihler (H-0380/04)
 Subject: Reform of regional state aids
 

The Commission has published proposals on the reform of the regional state aids granted by Member States. The proposals involve substantial reductions in eligible areas and in aid intensity ceilings, especially within the EU 15, and would hamper efforts to promote cohesion.

Could the Commission indicate why, at a time allocations from the Structural Funds are likely to shift eastwards, it deems it appropriate to restrain further the ability of Member States to support their own regions and why the Commission’s approach to seeking ‘less but better’ regional state aid focuses entirely on eligibility and intensity rather than on the more obvious target of value for money?

 
  
 

(EN) The Commission has not adopted yet any proposal on the reform of the Regional Aid Guidelines but is currently reviewing them for the assessments of compatibility of regional State aid for the term after 2006.

The aim is to redefine regional aid policy in an enlarged Union, reconciling the overall reduction of State aid volumes with the Community objective of economic and social cohesion. The reform is inspired by the conclusions of the European Councils of Lisbon and Stockholm, calling upon the Commission to give clear priority to the least developed regions in conformity with the exceptional nature of regional State aid. The underlying economic principle of the reform is that the best way to address regional disparities and increase regional growth rates is by creating the right incentives to economic operators by fostering higher productivity and competitiveness rather than subsidising inefficient firms or causing serious distortions in the internal market.

Accordingly with the spirit of the reform of the Cohesion policy, the rationale is also to shift from geographical to thematic concentration, outside the lagging regions.

In this context, Directorate General Competition has elaborated a working document and transmitted it to all Member States. This document is not binding on the Commission and the remarks from the Member States are being analysed and will be taken into consideration. The guidelines should be adopted by the Commission by the end of 2005.

 

Question no 22 by Josu Ortuondo Larrea (H-0382/04)
 Subject: Need for the Commission to support the European shipbuilding industry: IZAR crisis
 

The Commission's decision declaring illegal the aid received by the IZAR shipyards has raised the spectre of possible job losses and a massive public response in the Ferrol region of Galicia, home to the IZAR-Fene shipyards, and the Sestao region of the Basque Country, home to the La Naval shipyards. In the first of these cases, the continued ban on the construction of merchant vessels and limitation to the construction of offshore platforms to which IZAR-Fene is subject, which has resulted in the loss of thousands of jobs, is contradictory. Has the Spanish Government submitted a request for IZAR to be allowed to recommence construction of merchant vessels? Will the Commission negotiate a transitional phase, within the ambit of the WTO, in which shipbuilding aid could be granted that would enable European shipyards to compete with ones in Korea?

Besides this, the European Parliament adopted a report on the Prestige accident (A5-0278/2003) that included a resolution(1) which advocated promoting the construction of dual-hull tankers in regions seriously affected by maritime disasters, as is the case of Galicia and the Basque Country. What action has the Commission taken in this respect? Should the Commission not be striving for a genuine European shipbuilding policy that punches its weight at world level, under conditions of equality?

 
  
 

(FR) The Spanish government submitted a request for IZAR-Fene to be allowed to recommence construction of merchant vessels on 10 November 2003. The Commission has not yet taken any position on this issue due to the State aid investigations concerning IZAR. In any case, a reopening of IZAR-Fene for shipbuilding seems to be contradictory to the need for a major restructuring of the Spanish public shipbuilding sector.

The Commission already in 2001 proposed a temporary defensive mechanism(2) to help EU shipyards face the unfair competition from South Korean shipyards in market segments particularly hit. This mechanism, allowing aid of up to 6% of the contract value per ship, is available since October 2002 and was recently prolonged until 31 March 2005(3). This mechanism should answer the main concerns of the honourable Member.

Following the Parliament’s Resolution on the Prestige accident a Temporary Committee (MARE) on improving safety at sea was established by the Parliament. The Commission has taken full account of the March 2004 conclusions of this Temporary Committee. In this context the Commission intends to ensure a high standard of the ships registered in the Member States and/or calling into EU ports, but it does not aim at supporting any particular shipyards.

The Community has indeed a coherent shipbuilding policy. This policy consists of four main elements.

World Trade Organisation (WTO) procedure. The Commission launched a dispute settlement procedure in the WTO against South Korea, challenging restructuring aid and export aid and the price depression these measures have caused. This procedure is currently ongoing in Geneva.

Temporary defensive mechanism. (See point 2 above)

InterSHIP is a € 38 million research project, financed jointly by Euroyards and the European Commission and designed to improve the competitiveness of European cruise, ferry and ro-pax shipbuilders.

“LeaderSHIP 2015(4)” is a Commission initiative aiming at strengthening the competitiveness of EC shipyards.

(2) OJ L 172, 2.7.2002, Press release IP/02/945, 27/06/2002
(3) OJ L 81, 19.3.2004.
(4) COM(2003) 717 final of 21.11.2003

 

Question no 23 by Willy Meyer Pleite (H-0384/04)
 Subject: Scheme for refugee camps at the EU's external borders
 

The Ministers of Justice and of the Interior of the 25 Member States are studying the creation of pilot schemes for 'refugee camps' outside the European Union. A number of humanitarian, political and social organisations have unanimously rejected this proposal, as the scheme offers no guarantees of respect for human rights in so-called reception camps which could become true concentration camps.

Does the Commission not think that this implies the resounding failure of European immigration policies? How does the Commission intend to justify the fact that funds from the EU budget are used to contract 'à la carte services' from collaborating countries in the Maghreb in a repugnant commercial exercise, putting the destinies of thousands of people seeking refuge in democratic Europe into the hands of governments who show no respect at all for human rights?

 
  
 

(EN) Migration and asylum are complex and sensitive issues. Referring to “concentration camps” in that context can only give rise to emotional reactions and generate confusion and misunderstanding without helping to find concrete solutions to real problems. There is no intention to create concentration camps whatsoever.

Since last year, various ideas concerning schemes for refugee centres have been presented by certain Member States in the EU context. These ideas aimed either at establishing protected zones in third countries to which those arriving in EU Member States could be directly transferred to have their asylum claims processed, or, more recently, at processing claims of those intercepted at the international waters in the Mediterranean in centres in North-Africa in order to prevent further loss of lives during their crossing of the Mediterranean Sea.

These suggestions have not been endorsed at EU level. Nevertheless, it is almost certain that such suggestions will continue to be presented as long as the problems which they attempt to address remain.

The ideas just described above should however not be confused with the recent Commission’s proposals, endorsed by the Council, aiming at enhancing refugee protection in the regions of origin by establishing EU Regional Protection Programmes.

The objective of such programmes, which should facilitate the Commission in assisting third countries in their efforts to better manage asylum, is to enable the countries in the region of origin to offer effective protection to persons requiring international protection as soon as possible and as closely as possible to the countries of turmoil. Co-ownership with the third countries concerned, close cooperation with United Nations High Commissioner for Refugees (UNHCR) as well as respect of the relevant international legal instruments, in particular the 1951 Refugee Convention, will be the guiding principles for the implementation of these programmes.

Through Tacis, Cards and other thematic budget lines, the Community also provides technical and financial assistance for developing third countries institutional capacities in the areas of migration and asylum. This assistance is certainly not given in exchange of “à la carte services”. It is usually part of a broader approach towards these countries aimed at helping them to better manage migration, enhancing their national asylum systems, bringing them closer to the international standards and developing solutions to refugee problems closer to the regions of origin.

 

Question no 24 by Alain Hutchinson (H-0385/04)
 Subject: Improved provision for the problems of cities and urban areas in Union policies
 

Our cities today, while creating major wealth, also present large concentrations of social distress. Consequently, it appears to me that the Union should give consideration to the urban dimension when drawing up legislation. In the section of the Constitution dedicated to economic, social and territorial cohesion, I am astonished not to see reference to cities and urban areas in Article 220-III, while the text devotes special attention to other areas. Could this omission to give priority consideration to urban policy in the Constitutional Treaty be redressed by introducing a special intervention mechanism in the reform of the Structural Funds?

 
  
 

(EN) The Commission has taken a number of initiatives in the field of urban policy. In particular, in 1999 the Commission adopted a Framework of Action for Sustainable Urban Development in the European Union, which aimed at better co-ordination and targeting of Community action in this regard. The Commission is also responsible, in partnership with the authorities in the Member States, for the management of the Urban Community Initiative, supporting 70 urban programmes across the EUR-15.

Other EU policies also have actions to support urban areas. For example:

The thematic strategy for the urban environment aims to help cities as they plan and implement strategies for meeting EU environmental targets.

The initiative taken by the Greek authorities to reduce VAT on fuels by 30%, with a view to facilitating transport to and from the remote islands, conforms to the provisions of the sixth modified Council Directive of 17 May 1977 on VAT, which authorises Greece to ‘apply VAT rates up to 30% lower than the rates applied in mainland Greece’ for several regions and certain islands in the Aegean Sea.

An exception to this principle of uniformity was introduced by means of Article 28(2f) of the above-mentioned directive.

1.3 Excise duties affecting the consumption of fuels used in air transport

Article 14(1) of Directive 2003/96/EC of 27 October 2003 restructuring the Community framework for the taxation of energy products and electricity, allows for the Member States to exempt the use of ‘fuels for air transport, other than private tourism’. In practice, an exemption of this nature favours access to the Greek remote islands, which also responds to the concern of the Greek authorities which has led them to benefit from a reduced rate of VAT on fuels, as has just been pointed out.

Furthermore, Article 14(2) of the directive allows for the scope for exemption from excise to be reserved for international and intra-Community air transport, which by design implicitly allows the Member States the possibility of taxing the consumption of fuels intended for domestic flights.

2. The system of aid to compensate for the disadvantages suffered by islands

Three aid mechanisms allow the Member States to support financially the development or maintenance of sea and air transport services, in particular destined for or within island regions. These are the awarding of public service contracts, the existence of social aid and finally regional operating aid granted to the least-favoured regions. These three mechanisms are implemented on the initiative of the Member State, under the supervision of the European Commission, and for the latter two categories, following a formal decision by the Commission.

2.1 Public service contracts

Council Regulation No 2408/92/EEC of 23 July 1992, on access for Community air carriers to intra-Community air routes, and Regulation No 3577/92/EEC of 7 December 1992 on maritime cabotage, allow Member States to impose, if they consider it necessary, public service obligations on air carriers, including, amongst other things, tariff obligations, in the case of outlying regions or development within their territory, or regional low-traffic routes. As regards maritime transport, public service obligations may be imposed on shipping companies participating in services to and between islands.

If no air carrier is in a position to perform these obligations on a commercial basis, the Member State in question may award a public service contract guaranteeing exclusive operation of the route for three years to an air carrier selected by means of a European invitation to tender, and may provide it with financial compensation defined by contract for the operation of that route. In the maritime sector, similar contracts may be employed for a period which should normally not exceed six years.

2.2 Social aid

In accordance with the exemption laid down in Article 87 (2a) of the EC Treaty, and in accordance with the guidelines on air transport, a Member State may grant aid for the end consumer of the service, provided that it is granted without discrimination in terms of the origin of that service, that is to say, in this case any carrier involved.

In practice, the social aid only benefits certain categories of passengers, but it may also involve the whole of the population of the region in question. It must therefore be expressly authorised by the European Commission, following notification by the Member State.

2.3 The granting of regional operating aid for the least-favoured regions

This type of regional aid may be granted pursuant to Article 87(3a) of the EC Treaty, and is intended to compensate for certain handicaps suffered by the least-favoured regions.

It is considered compatible with Community law provided that the aid granted is degressive and for a limited time period, in accordance with point 4.15 of the Guidelines on State aid for regional purposes.

 

Question no 37 by Rodi Kratsa-Tsagaropoulou (H-0405/04)
 Subject: Parental leave in the EU
 

According to recent data from Eurobarometer, the majority of male employees in Europe (75%) are aware of their right to parental leave, but 84% of them do not take advantage of it. This is mainly because of financial considerations (42% cite the resultant reduction in income) and because men fear that it may damage their careers (31%). This issue has a very important part to play in increasing both the birth rate and the employment of women, and is directly linked with the Lisbon objectives.

What view does the Commission take of the findings of this survey? Does it have qualitative and quantitative data on this issue, broken down by Member State? (EN) Council Directive 96/34/EC of 3 June 1996 on the framework agreement on parental leave concluded by UNICE, CEEP and the ETUC provides a right to parental leave for all male and female workers in the European Union.

Question no 41 by Georgios Toussas (H-0410/04)
 Subject: Impact of high-voltage power lines on health
 

Research by the University of Oxford, commissioned by the British Department of Health, has shown that children living in the vicinity of high-voltage power lines have twice as high a risk of developing cancer as children in other areas. Moreover, children living within 100 metres of high-voltage power lines are more likely to develop leukaemia. In Greece, the State Electricity Corporation has a number of power stations in residential areas. What will the Commission do to protect the health of children and of the rest of the population?

 
  
 

(EN) The question of the honourable Member refers to the problem of exposure of the general population to electro-magnetic fields. This is a matter dealt with in a Council Recommendation of 12th July 1999 limiting the exposure of the general public to electromagnetic fields.

This Recommendation sets a framework of basic restrictions and reference levels, with a view to providing for a high level of health protection for the public.

It is the responsibility of the Member States to ensure, if these basic restrictions and reference levels are exceeded, to carry out an assessment of the situation and take the appropriate follow-up measures, such as informing those who are exposed or changing the location of the installation, the design or the way in which it is operated.

The Commission keeps this Recommendation under constant review. Considerable research work has been undertaken to investigate more deeply the possible long-term effects of electromagnetic fields on health. The International Electro Magnetic Field (EMF) Project conducted by World Health Organisation (WHO) is one of the major current studies and the Commission is following this project closely.

The residential proximity to electrical power-line installations and risks of childhood leukaemia or any other cancer remains a concern for the Commission and it will react, if necessary, to any new scientific evidence not yet taken into consideration in the present Council Recommendation.
