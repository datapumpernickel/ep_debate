QUESTIONS TO COUNCIL (The Presidency-in-Office of the Council of the European Union is solely responsible for these answers.)
Question no 16 by Claude Moraes (H-0922/05)
 Subject: Disability access and equality legislation
 

Following contact by a constituent of mine who experienced great difficulties when travelling with her guide dog, it is evident that there is very different treatment towards blind and partially sighted people and other assistance dog users across the EU.

What plans does the Council have to ensure that the principles of inclusive design that could be used to guide architects, planners and designers of buildings to make them fully accessible to guide dog owners, other blind and partially sighted people and other assistance dog users are recognised across the EU?

Will the Council work to ensure that guidelines are produced which could be used by European guide dog owners, other blind and partially sighted people and other assistance dog users to help them understand the disparity of disability access and equality legislation that exists between Member States?

 
  
 

(EN)The Council appreciates the concerns raised by the honourable Member but as the honourable Member will know, the Council can only act in a legislative manner on the basis of a proposal from the Commission which, in turn, requires an appropriate legal basis. At the present time, the Council has received no such proposals and there are therefore no specific plans to ensure that the principles of inclusive design are recognised across the EU or to produce guidelines to help European guide-dog owners, blind and partially-sighted people to understand differences in disability access and equality legislation between Member States.

I would, however, draw your attention to the general legal framework which already exists in the area of employment and occupation, for combating discrimination including on the grounds of disability in Council Directive 2000/78/EC(1). This Directive sets minimum requirements which must be respected by all Member States but Member States may also introduce or maintain provisions more favourable to equal treatment than those laid down in this Directive.

Of course, there is also the Commission's recent Communication "Non-Discrimination and Equal Opportunities for All - A Framework Strategy". This applies to all disadvantaged groups including people with disabilities and, amongst other things, encourages dissemination of information, awareness-raising, sharing of experience, training and access to justice. A similar initiative could also be taken in the area identified by the honourable Member.

 
 

(1) Council Directive 2000/78/EC establishing a general framework for equal treatment in employment and occupation (OJ No L 303 of 2.12.2000, p. 16).

Question no 18 by Ilda Figueiredo (H-0929/05)
 Subject: Problems in the footwear industry
 

As is well known, the footwear industry is currently undergoing grave problems thanks to international trade liberalisation. This is primarily affecting those Member States with more fragile economies, including Portugal, some of whose northern areas are now running the risk of high unemployment and the blockage of their development, as the author of this question has recently witnessed. A particularly grave factor has been the removal of quotas on imports from China, one of whose most visible results has been a fall in average import prices of approximately 50%.

Can the Council state what action it is taking on the matter, especially with regard to the European Footwear Industry Confederation's request for an anti-dumping inquiry?

 
  
 

(EN)The honourable Member will certainly be aware of the general legal framework governing Trade Defence Instruments, and in particular anti-dumping proceedings(1). Within this framework it is first up to the Commission to act. It recently initiated two anti-dumping investigations into this matter.

The aim of those investigations is to establish whether the imports in question are dumped and have produced a negative impact on the economic situation of the Community footwear industry. The investigations will also have to assess the impact, and the possible adverse effect, of any measures on the other economic operators in the Community. These are highly complicated cases and the Commission is still carrying out its investigation. Provisional measures, if any, should legally be imposed at the latest 9 months after the initiation of the proceedings. Member States are consulted on the provisional conclusions of the Commission.

At a later stage, when the Commission reaches its final conclusions, and providing there is a need to impose definitive measures, it will submit a formal proposal to the Council, which will have to decide on that basis within one month after reception of the Commission's proposal. The honourable Member can rest assured that the Council follows developments in the footwear industry closely and will, if necessary, act speedily.

 
 

(1) Council Regulation (EC) No 384/96 of 22 December 1995 on protection against dumped imports from countries not members of the European Community ( OJ L 56 of 6.3.1996, page 1).

 

Question no 19 by Péter Olajos (H-0932/05)
 Subject: The directive on mining waste in a newly acceding country
 

The proposal for a directive on mining waste (16075/1/2004 - C6-0128/2005 - 2003/0107(COD); European Parliament report A6-0236/2005) is currently at the conciliation stage between the Council and the European Parliament. The Commission proposed this directive in response to the disastrous cyanide spill at a mine in Romania in 2000. The disaster seriously polluted sources of drinking water and damaged the environment in Romania, Ukraine, Slovakia and Hungary. The purpose of the directive is to prevent any recurrence of such an incident. It is expected to be adopted by December.

Is it possible that the Council may grant Romania any exemption from complying with this directive? What guarantees can the Council give that this will not happen?

 
  
 

(EN)As the honourable Member is certainly aware, Article 55 of the Act of Accession 2005 provides for the possibility for Romania as an acceding State to request, in duly substantiated cases, temporary derogations from acts of the institutions adopted between 1 October 2004 and the date of accession.

It is not for the Council, however, to speculate on whether Romania will request such a temporary derogation for the implementation of the draft directive on mining waste, once it has been adopted, or whether the Commission would subsequently make a proposal to the Council to that effect.

 

Question no 20 by Alain Hutchinson (H-0935/05)
 Subject: Human rights violations in Tunisia
 

The barriers to human rights and the rule of law in Tunisia have regularly been denounced by numerous non-governmental organisations. It seems important for the European Union to take firm action in response to those violations, which run contrary to the Association Agreement it has concluded with Tunisia. Indeed, Article 2 of that Agreement states that: 'relations between the Parties, as well as all the provisions of the Agreement itself, shall be based on respect for human rights and democratic principles, which guide their domestic and international policies and constitute an essential element of the Agreement.' I would accordingly request that the Council harness all the means provided for in the EU-Tunisia Association Agreement to ensure full application of the clauses therein on respect for human rights and fundamental freedoms, and in particular Article 2. What action will the Council take in response to this request?

 
  
 

(EN)The Council shares the concerns of the honourable Member about the human rights situation in Tunisia. In particular the EU is concerned about restrictions on, and the treatment of, civil society and human rights activists. EU Ambassadors in Tunis have discussed human rights issues with Tunisian Ministers and the Tunisian Coordinator on Human Rights.

In September 2005 the EU Troika attempted to deliver a demarche to the Tunisian authorities on the Tunisian Civil Society Organisation. As the Tunisian authorities refused to receive the Troika, the demarche was subsequently delivered to the Tunisian Permanent Representative to the UN in Geneva. The Presidency issued a declaration on behalf of the EU in which it recalled that human rights were a significant element in its political dialogue with Tunisia within the framework of the EU-Tunisia Association Agreement, noted with concern the situation of the Ligue, and expressed its wish that the Ligue would be able to continue its work to promote and protect human rights in Tunisia.

The EU has also made clear to the Tunisian authorities and all states and institutions taking part in the World Summit on the Information Society, to be held in Tunis in November 2005, that it expects them to fully respect the right to freedom of expression.

The meeting of the EU-Tunisia Association Committee, scheduled for 7 December, will provide an opportunity to raise the human rights situation with Tunisia. We also hope to take forward the establishment of a Joint Subcommittee on Human Rights, to which Tunisia has committed itself under the framework of the European Neighbourhood Policy Action Plan.

The Council would like to assure the honourable Member that the EU will continue to monitor closely the human rights situation in Tunisia and where necessary to raise its concerns with the Tunisian authorities.

 

Question no 21 by Mairead McGuinness (H-0937/05)
 Subject: Council's policy towards the sugar markets of the developing world in view of the reform of the European market in sugar
 

In light of the restructuring of Europe's market in sugar, can the Council as the institution that is responsible for both agreeing the EU's budget and defining the EU's mandate for negotiations with third countries, outline what steps it intends to take to offset the potentially damaging consequences these reforms could have for the countries of the developing world? Is EUR 40 million sufficient to allow these countries to adjust to the reforms?

Now, more than ever, there appears to be an absolute need for these countries to develop their own 'Common Agricultural Policy' in order to safeguard the livelihoods of those that work and earn their living in the agricultural sector. There is an onus on the Council to provide the necessary resources, as well as a clear time frame to allow these countries to develop such policies. Can the Council clarify it position in this regard?

 
  
 

(EN)Accompanying measures for Sugar Protocol countries affected by reform of the EU sugar regime include both trade measures and development assistance to help those countries adapt. Trade measures are being taken forward in negotiations on the Economic Partnership Agreements. In terms of development assistance, the Council has supported the Commission’s proposal for accompanying measures to assist the Sugar Protocol countries affected by reform and will seek the agreement of the European Parliament to this proposal

Under the Commission’s proposal the development assistance will be provided over a period of 8 years. In terms of funding, the Commission has proposed EUR 40m of development assistance for the first year (2006) and the Council has supported this amount. A final decision on the funding for 2006 will be taken by the Council and European Parliament as part of the 2006 budget agreement.

A decision on the funding for the remaining seven years of the assistance scheme will be taken by the Council and the European Parliament under the next Financial Perspective. The Commission is proposing that transitional assistance will be delivered through country plans prepared by the ACP countries themselves, in consultation with the Commission. I am not sure that I would wish to comment on extending the CAP but can say that development assistance is provided to the ACP under the framework of the Cotonou agreement though the European Development Fund. This assistance is centred on the objective of poverty reduction, in line with the objectives of sustainable development and progressive integration of the ACP countries into the global economy, and this includes support for agriculture.

The United Nations in October doubled its appeal target for the earthquake in South Asia to USD 549.6 m. In comparison with the immediate response of nations to the tsunami disaster, the response to this catastrophe has been very weak. Will the Council take urgent steps to persuade the EU Member States to step up their contributions, especially in cases where Member States have made no contribution whatsoever?

 
  
 

(EN)The South Asian Earthquake and Indian Ocean tsunami are disasters with very different characteristics. There is no fair way here of comparing like for like. Nevertheless, the Council recognises the very severe humanitarian implications of this latest tragedy. The Commission and EU Member States bilaterally have responded to the urgent appeal. The UN has recently issued a revised Priority Humanitarian Action Plan for November and has identified a funding shortfall of USD 43.5 million. All Member States need to consider carefully how they can respond to this urgent requirement.

Member States have already contributed in a number of ways including through the UN, NATO and directly to NGOs operating on the ground, as well as in kind through the provision of helicopters, aircraft, engineers and rescue teams. The Commission has moved quickly to put together an aid package of EUR 93.6 million, including EUR 40 million drawn from the emergency reserve for 2005.

Question no 25 by Neil Parish (H-0945/05)
 Subject: Export refunds
 

The Commission has made EUR 77 million of taxpayers' money available to encourage the export of live cattle to the Middle East in 2005, by way of export refunds. The Commission's proposed budget for 2006 provides EUR 62 million for the same purpose.

The reported disregard for EU animal welfare regulations during the journey that can take up to ten days and the slaughter methods at their destination compound the stress and suffering on these animals. The practice of using export refunds is also wrong in the context of the reformed CAP.

When will the Council take action to stop the payment of these export refunds?

 
  
 

(EN)The export refunds for live cattle to which the honourable Member refers are laid down in Regulation (EC) No 1254/1999 on the common organisation of the market in beef and veal.

As regards non-compliance with the legislation on welfare, the regulations in force oblige Member States to carry out mandatory veterinary inspections for all transports at the point of exit from the customs territory of the Union, at the place where the means of transport is changed in a third country and at the place of the first unloading in the third country of final destination.

Furthermore, pursuant to Commission Regulation (EC) No 639/2003, the payment of a refund is subject to compliance with the provisions of Council Directive 91/628/EEC on the protection of animals during transport, which provides for a mandatory veterinary check for all transports at the point of exit from the customs territory of the Community. In third countries, the Regulation also provides for an inspection at the place of first unloading of the animals in the third country of final destination in cases where there has been a change of the means of transport in a third country and where the veterinary officer at the point of exit from the Community has deemed it necessary.

Irrespective of any considerations regarding the maintenance or otherwise of export refunds for agricultural products (a consideration not known to be excluded from the current debate on world agriculture in the context of the Doha Round), Community legislation meets the concerns of society with regard to animal welfare.

Thus far, the Council has received no Commission proposal for abolishing the export refund system.

It is for Member States and the sectoral operators to ensure compliance with the rules adopted.

Question no 27 by Seán Ó Neachtain (H-0948/05)
 Subject: Starving people of Zimbabwe
 

Zimbabwean President Robert Mugabe recently addressed the United Nations Food and Agriculture Association in Rome.

Question no 30 by Charles Tannock (H-0956/05)
 Subject: Tension in the Horn of Africa
 

Ethiopia's continuing refusal to accept the ruling of the Ethiopia-Eritrea international border commission – which ruled that the village of Badme is part of Eritrea – is contributing to rising tension in the Horn of Africa and intensifying fears of another disastrous war between the two countries.

What steps can the Council take as guarantor of the Algiers peace agreement (2002) to persuade Ethiopia to honour its commitment to accept the border commission's ruling, which is backed by UN Security Council resolution 1622? To what extent does the Council consider the presence of Ethiopian prime minister Meles Zenawi on the UK government's Commission for Africa to be an obstacle to resolving this issue?

 
  
 

(EN)The Council is deeply concerned at the continuing lack of progress in the Eritrean-Ethiopian peace process, and at the current tension, including the Eritrean ban on UN helicopter flights and restrictions on UN vehicle movements.

The Council remains convinced that the achievement of lasting peace between the two countries and a definitive solution of the outstanding territorial issues must be based on the Algiers Peace Agreement, including the acceptance of the decision of the Ethiopia-Eritrea Boundary Commission on the demarcation of the Eritrean-Ethiopian border as final and binding. Similarly, the Council fully supports the calls made on the parties in various UN Security Council Resolutions on Ethiopia and Eritrea, including Resolution 1622.

The EU continues to call on both countries to accept as final and binding the decision of the Boundary Commission, and to take steps so that the demarcation of the border can proceed.

The Council does not consider that the participation of the Ethiopian Prime Minister in the Commission for Africa, which has now closed and delivered its recommendations, has had an effect on this issue.

 

Question no 31 by Bernd Posselt (H-0964/05)
 Subject: Human rights situation in Russia
 

What is the Council's view of the worsening human rights situation in Russia, and in particular of the conviction of Mikhail Khodorkovsky and the conditions is which he is being held, and what is it doing to counteract attempts by the Russian authorities to silence forces which are advocating a peaceful solution to the Chechnya problem?

 
  
 

(EN)The Council shares the honourable Member’s concerns about the human rights situation in the Russian Federation, in particular the situation in Chechnya and the Northern Caucasus.

The Council has made its concerns known many times about the lack of fairness apparent in the handling of the Yukos case, and the impact this has had on investor confidence.

The Council is not aware that the penal colony in which Khodorkovsky and his co-defendants are currently held violates their human rights.

The Council regularly raises human rights concerns with Russia in the framework of the political dialogue, at a technical as well as a political level. For example human rights issues were raised at the latest EU/Russia summit in London on 4 October 2005 – at which there was also a substantive discussion of the North Caucasus. The EU is also actively engaged in biannual human rights consultations with Russia. The latest round took place in Brussels on 8 September at which, amongst other issues, we raised in detail our concerns about human rights abuses in Chechnya and the Russian judicial system.

The Council continues to support a political solution to the conflict in Chechnya which has the support and confidence of the Chechen population, and has called on the Russian authorities to ensure that the Parliamentary elections in Chechnya at the end of this month are free and fair.

The Council is encouraged by the recent EU Ambassadors troika visit to Chechnya and the North Caucasus. Under the auspices of the Chechen Electoral Commission the troika met representatives of all eight parties standing in the upcoming elections. The Council hopes that these parties can command the broad support of the Chechen population necessary for a peaceful solution.
